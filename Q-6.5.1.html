Was Joseph Smith a true prophet?

The load-bearing question of any conversation with Latter-day Saints — and the corpus answers it by disputing the criterion, not out-arguing the feeling. Two experts, two substantive treatments.

The question

By what criterion do we test the claim that Joseph Smith was a prophet of God — and does that claim survive the test?

The Mormon missionary’s testimony rests on two pillars: the translation feat (an uneducated farmhand rendering “Reformed Egyptian” into English “by the power of God”) and the spiritual witness (the burning-in-the-bosom confirmation promised in Moroni 10:3–5). The apologetic task is not to out-argue the feeling but to dispute the criterion — to show that neither a supernatural feat nor a subjective confirmation is the test Scripture itself prescribes.

The two experts approach from opposite ends. Perry contests the epistemology — what counts as evidence of prophethood — and substitutes the prior-written-word test. McGrew contests the motive — supplying a punchy biographical disqualifier as the rhetorically superior debate move, while conceding the methodologically correct answer is the criterion one.

The convergent argument — both experts

A single deductive spine runs through both treatments: a prophet is tested by a prior, public criterion, not by the impression he creates. They diverge only on which criterion to foreground — doctrine (Perry) or motive (McGrew).

Premise 1

Prophetic legitimacy must be judged by a criterion external to the prophet’s own say-so.

Two criteria offered. Perry: the prior written word — doctrinal alignment with prior revelation (Deut 13 / 18; Gal 1:8). McGrew: the same evidential standard applied to “any historical claim,” which for Joseph Smith surfaces an obvious fabrication motive.

Premise 2

Supernatural feats and subjective confirmation cannot bear the evidential weight placed on them.

Perry: signs and wonders prove nothing (Matt 7:21–23 — the rejected workers really “did these things”); the heart is deceitful (Jer 17:9); “Satan disguises himself as an angel of light” (2 Cor 11:14). McGrew: the apostles’ biography (persecution, martyrdom, no gain) versus Smith’s (at least 40 wives) yields a decisive motivational asymmetry.

Conclusion

Therefore the proper test is doctrinal consistency with prior revelation and the witness’s motive profile — and Joseph Smith fails it.

Perry: Smith preaches “another gospel,” so even an angelic visitation cannot rescue him (Gal 1:8). McGrew: Smith “was in it for the sex,” so his motive disqualifies him as a witness. The internal-critique twist: the LDS members already hold the prior-word criterion — they just decline to apply it to Smith.

How each expert deploys it

Preston Perry 1 transcript
The prophet-test framework — dominant voice
Signature deployment
  • Names the LDS criterion first — the translation feat plus the Moroni 10 spiritual-witness prayer — then dismantles both.
  • Matthew 7:21–23 disqualifies signs and wonders: the rejected workers do not say “I thought I cast out demons,” they say “I did these things.” The works were real; they proved nothing.
  • Jeremiah 17:9 + the fornication-revelation parity reductio: the elders would reject a “God told me to have sex” revelation by appeal to His prior word — so they already hold the prior-word criterion.
  • Deuteronomy 18 (and implied 13): a prophet who leads you after other gods is no prophet — the doctrinal-alignment test.
  • Galatians 1:8 as the angelic-criterion override — even an angel preaching another gospel is accursed; “Satan disguises himself as an angel of light” (2 Cor 11:14).
Signature move

Hard logical pressure cushioned in an honour frame. Perry names the parity test, then immediately softens: “with all due respect... I don’t mean to disrespect my elders.” The voice-over aside (“Did y’all peep what he just said?”) teaches viewers without ever breaking the dignity frame in the live conversation.

Timothy McGrew via McLatchie review
Motivational-asymmetry counter — the debate-floor move
Distinctive moves
  • Quoted inside McLatchie’s review of the McLatchie–Dillahunty debate as a rhetorical alternative to the standard methodological answer.
  • Concedes the dialectically correct reply is McLatchie’s — “the same way we do any historical claim, so just keep it tight.”
  • Argues the rhetorically superior move is to name Smith’s wives — a roll-call ending “there are 40 of these... in it for the sex, can we move on.”

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

The translation feat proves divine power

An uneducated man with a third-grade education could not render Reformed Egyptian into English without the power of God. The feat itself is the first evidence of prophethood.

LDS missionary

Strongest response

Signs and wonders are not proof

Matt 7:21–23 shows real miraculous works performed by those God ultimately rejects — they “did these things.” A deceptive supernatural agency can counterfeit a feat, because “Satan disguises himself as an angel of light.” The feat establishes power, not legitimacy.

Perry Matt 7 signs-disqualifier

Objection 2

The spiritual witness confirms it (Moroni 10:3–5)

I prayed over the Book of Mormon and the Holy Ghost gave me a witness that it is the word of God. That confirmation is how I know.

LDS missionary

Strongest response

The heart is deceitful

Jeremiah 17:9 — the heart is deceitful above all things; a subjective confirmation cannot be the controlling measure. “I can pray a prayer” too. The controlling measure must be the prior written word, not the impression of the prayer.

Perry Jer 17:9 + prior-word test

Objection 3

The spiritual witness is “only a small fraction” of my testimony

“It’s a fraction of my test. A very small fraction of my testimony, but it hinges on this spiritual witness.” The feeling is not the whole basis, so disqualifying it does not defeat me.

LDS Elder (verbatim)

Strongest response

Then apply your own controlling test

Perry concedes it is one measure but presses the parity asymmetry: the elders use the prior-word test against a hypothetical fornication-revelation but not against Smith. The controlling measure must be the prior word, applied consistently.

Perry internal-critique / parity

Objection 4

Moroni was an angel sent to direct Smith

An angelic visitation authenticates the prophet. The angel Moroni directed Joseph Smith to the plates — angelic mediation is part of the testimony.

LDS context (implied)

Strongest response

Galatians 1:8 angelic override

“Even if an angel from heaven should preach to you a gospel contrary to the one we preach, let him be accursed.” An angelic source cannot authenticate a different gospel — and “even Satan disguises himself as an angel of light.”

Perry Gal 1:8 + 2 Cor 11:14

Objection 5

How do you distinguish honest witnesses from lying ones?

If you trust the apostles’ testimony, why not Joseph Smith’s? What principled criterion separates the honest witness from the charlatan?

Matt Dillahunty (in the reviewed debate)

Strongest response

Motive — and the same standard as any history

Methodologically, by the standard applied to any historical claim (McLatchie). Rhetorically, name Smith’s 40 wives — a clear fabrication motive the apostles entirely lack, since they gained only persecution and martyrdom.

Persistent gaps in the corpus

Gap 1

The failed-prophecy test goes unused

Deuteronomy 18:22 — the empirical test of whether a specific prophecy came to pass — is the sharpest form of the prophet-test, and it is never aimed at any specific Smith prophecy.

Deut 18:22 predictive-accuracy limb

Gap — not deployed

Perry stays on the doctrinal limb only

Perry uses Deut 18 for doctrinal alignment but never the predictive-accuracy limb. No specific failed Smith prophecy is cited anywhere in the corpus.

Gap 2

No hermeneutical engagement with Moroni 10:4

A book that commends itself by a prayer offered over that book is circular — the prior-text / self-authentication problem. The promise is never engaged on its own terms.

Self-authentication circularity

Gap — not engaged

Rebutted psychologically, not textually

Perry rebuts the spiritual witness via the deceitful heart, but never names the circularity of the Moroni 10:4 promise itself.

Gap 3

The “40 wives” claim is asserted, not sourced

McGrew’s roll-call is rhetorically effective but historically unargued in the transcript. An LDS interlocutor disputing the marriage record (sealings vs conjugal marriages) gets no response.

Historical-record dispute

Gap — unsourced

A disqualifier, not a documented total

Historians debate the count and the nature of several sealings. The figure should be deployed as a rhetorical disqualifier, not a settled total — the corpus supplies no documentation.

Gap 4

The translation feat is rebutted by analogy, not investigated

“Satan can counterfeit a feat” answers whether a feat proves prophethood; it does not engage the historical case (Anthon transcript, Kinderhook plates, Book of Abraham papyri).

Documentary history of the translation

Gap — no documentary rebuttal

Criterion-level only

The corpus rebuts the feat’s evidential value but never engages the historical artefacts that bear on whether the translation happened as claimed.

Gap 5

The “burning bosom” as veridical experience

The Mormon counter — that the witness is self-authenticating the way regeneration is for the evangelical — turns the parity charge back on the apologist. It is left unanswered.

LDS parity counter

Gap — unanswered

Reverse-parity not addressed

Neither expert addresses how the evangelical’s own experiential epistemology escapes the deceitful-heart objection. The symmetry charge has no rebuttal in the corpus.
