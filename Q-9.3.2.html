The question

Between Calvinism and Arminianism, which better fits the biblical data — and is there a third position that keeps what is true in each?

The two historic systems disagree on whether God’s election of individuals is unconditional (Calvinism: God chooses, then the chosen believe) or conditional and corporate (Arminianism: God chooses the class of those who will believe), and on whether saving grace is irresistible (Calvinism) or resistible (Arminianism).

William Lane Craig’s distinctive move is to refuse the binary. He argues that both systems read part of the data correctly and part incorrectly, and that a Molinist synthesis — predicated on divine middle knowledge — preserves every datum that forces the impasse. The verdict, verbatim: “a Molinist perspective enjoys a considerable advantage.”

Honest density

This is a thin node: two transcripts from a single voice (WLC’s Defenders systematic-theology class), pastoral in register and not philosophically exhaustive. Its argumentative weight is borrowed — from the Calvinist case below it (Q-9.3.1) and from the doctrine of middle knowledge that discharges its verdict (Q-7.4.2). Read it as a junction, not a comprehensive treatment.

The argument structure — inference to the best explanation

This is not a deductive syllogism but a cumulative case: WLC adjudicates two systems by which best fits the whole of the biblical data, then proposes a third that fits it better. Three evidence streams pull in opposing directions; a fourth move resolves them.

Stream A · Corporate texts

  • Romans 9–10 read as a whole
  • Body of Christ; the church
  • Olive tree, branches grafted in (Rom 11)
  • Rom 10:13 — “everyone who calls”
  • Favours the Arminian: corporate election

Stream B · Individual foreordination

  • Acts 13:48 — “ordained to eternal life”
  • Herod & Pilate named (Acts 4:27–28)
  • “Definite plan & foreknowledge” (Acts 2:23)
  • Paul set apart before birth (Gal 1:15)
  • Favours the Calvinist: individual election

Stream C · Resistibility

  • Acts 26:19 — “not disobedient” (could have been)
  • Acts 7:51 — “you always resist the Spirit”
  • The call is real but not coercive
  • Grace is offered, not imposed
  • Favours the Arminian: resistible grace
The resolving move · verdict

Molinism (Luis de Molina, 1535–1600) keeps all three streams at once.

By means of middle knowledge — God’s knowledge of what every free creature would do in any circumstance — God can foreordain and predestine individuals (Stream B) while their response remains genuinely free and resistible (Stream C), supplementing the corporate election that already reads Stream A rightly. Pure Calvinism keeps the individual sovereignty by denying freedom; pure corporate-election Arminianism keeps freedom by under-reading the individual texts. Only Molinism keeps every datum — hence the “considerable advantage.”

How the expert deploys it

Source 1 · Part 4 — the Arminian planks
  • Grounds the sincere call in God’s universal salvific will (2 Pet 3:9; 1 Tim 2:4).
  • Deploys Robert Shank (Elect in the Son) as a hostile-to-Calvinism witness whose sarcastic reductio makes the universal call “a sham” — while explicitly labelling the register: “Obviously, Shank is writing here with considerable sarcasm.”
  • Resistible grace from Acts 7:51 (“you always resist the Holy Spirit”): salvation “is not an irresistible, unilateral work of God.”
  • Handles Romans 9:18 by granting sovereign discretion but relocating its object to a class: God chose to save all who have faith, Jew or Gentile.
Source 2 · Part 5 — the evaluation and verdict
  • Concede the Arminian’s best ground: corporate election “makes very good sense” of Romans 9–10 and Paul’s corporate metaphors.
  • Show where it under-reads: Acts 13:48 “can’t plausibly be construed corporately.”
  • Defeat the Arminian escape — the verb tasso is passive (God is the subject), and the wider Acts theology foreordains named individuals (4:27–28; 2:23; 9:15; Gal 1:15).
  • Concede the Calvinist’s best ground (individual foreordination) but show the over-read: the call is not irresistible — Paul “was not disobedient” (Acts 26:19) implies he could have been.
  • Resolve with Molinism: middle knowledge shows how individuals can be predestined yet free, so “a Molinist perspective enjoys a considerable advantage.”
Verdict by advantage, not proof

Note the modesty of “enjoys a considerable advantage.” WLC adjudicates probabilistically — inference to the best explanation — not apodictically. The signature engine is a double concession: grant each system its strongest text, then expose its weakest reading. The single load-bearing exegetical move is the passive voice of tasso, which defeats the Arminian “disposed” gloss of Acts 13:48.

What this node does not establish

The verdict rests entirely on Molinism, yet middle knowledge is never defended here against the grounding objection. The page asserts the synthesis; it does not ground it. That defence lives at Q-7.4.2 and must be imported before the verdict can be trusted.

Objections handled — master list

Single-voice node: “cross-expert” here means cross-source (Part 4 vs Part 5) and the objections WLC stages on behalf of his opponents. The objections he answers are exegetical; the objections that would actually threaten his verdict are left open — styled as gaps below.

Objection 1

God’s sovereign discretion — Romans 9:18

“He has mercy upon whomever he wills, and he hardens the heart of whomever he wills.” Therefore election is unconditional and reaches individuals directly.

Calvinist (staged by WLC)

Strongest response

Grant the discretion, relocate its object to a class

WLC concedes God’s sovereign discretion but locates its object in a class: God has sovereignly chosen to save all who have faith in Christ, Jew or Gentile (Rom 9:22–24, 30–31; Gal 3:6–9; Rom 10:12–13). The choice is sovereign; the criterion is faith.

Craig Part 4 — sovereign choice of the faithful

Objection 2

“Ordained” in Acts 13:48 means merely “disposed”

“As many as were disposed to eternal life believed.” If you had the disposition, you believed of your own free will — preserving Arminian conditionality.

Arminian (staged by WLC)

Strongest response

Passive voice + the Acts theology of foreordination

Rejected on two grounds: the verb tasso is in the passive voice, making God the agent; and the wider theology of Acts foreordains named individuals (Herod and Pilate, 4:27–28; the “definite plan and foreknowledge of God,” 2:23; Paul “a chosen instrument,” 9:15; “set apart before I was born,” Gal 1:15). “I’m not persuaded that this is a plausible interpretation.”

Craig Part 5 — load-bearing lexical move

Objection 3

The universal call is insincere on Calvinism

God pleads “whosoever will, let him come” while ensuring most cannot come — rendering the call to repentance a sham.

Arminian, via Robert Shank’s reductio

Strongest response

Endorse the reductio; ground sincerity in salvific will

WLC endorses Shank’s reductio — the Calvinist call is “a sham” — while keeping analytic distance by naming it as sarcasm. Sincerity is secured by God’s genuine universal salvific will (2 Pet 3:9; 1 Tim 2:4) plus resistible grace (Acts 7:51).

Craig / Shank Part 4 — hostile-witness reductio

Objection 4

“Desires all to be saved” is the preceptive will, not the decretive will

1 Tim 2:4 / 2 Pet 3:9 express God’s revealed/preceptive will, not his decretive will. So the Calvinist can affirm a sincere call without a universal salvific intent — blunting the “insincere call” reductio.

Calvinist rejoinder (two-wills distinction)

Gap — not engaged

Salvific desire taken at face value

WLC reads 2 Pet 3:9 / 1 Tim 2:4 as God’s actual salvific desire and never addresses the two-wills distinction that would weaken the sincerity argument at its weakest joint.

Aggregation gap

Objection 5

Acts 7:51 concerns common grace, not effectual grace

“You always resist the Holy Spirit” is about resisting the Spirit in general (common grace). It does not show that saving/effectual grace is resistible — the Calvinist distinguishes the two.

Calvinist (common vs effectual grace)

Gap — not engaged

Inference from general resistance left undefended

WLC infers resistibility of saving grace from a text about resisting the Spirit generally, without defending the inference across the common/effectual-grace distinction.

Aggregation gap

Objection 6

Libertarian free will is incoherent or unbiblical

The compatibilist holds that freedom is compatible with divine determination. So Acts 26:19 (“not disobedient”) does not require libertarian freedom — and the whole resistibility leg rests on a contested assumption.

Calvinist / compatibilist

Gap — not engaged

Libertarian freedom assumed, not argued

WLC simply assumes libertarian freedom is required by Acts 26:19; he does not argue against compatibilism in this pastoral setting. The defence lives at Q-7.5.2.

Aggregation gap

Objection 7

The grounding objection to Molinism

Counterfactuals of creaturely freedom have no truth-maker — there is nothing in reality that makes it true what a free creature would do. So there is nothing for God’s middle knowledge to know, and the synthesis collapses.

Anti-Molinist (Hasker, Adams, et al.)

Gap — the headline gap

The verdict’s central liability is invisible here

The whole adjudication is load-bearing on Molinism, yet the most serious philosophical objection to middle knowledge is never raised. The verdict therefore presupposes that Molinism is coherent rather than establishing it. WLC defends middle knowledge against the grounding objection elsewhere — that defence must be imported from Q-7.4.2.
