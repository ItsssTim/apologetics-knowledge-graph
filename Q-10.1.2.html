The question

Does the Holy Spirit possess the full deity of Yahweh — or is he a created agent, a personified attribute, or God’s impersonal “active force”?

This is the second of the two foundational Pneumatology questions. Its sibling, Q-10.1.1, asks whether the Spirit is a person — a “who” not a “which.” This node asks the harder, separable question: granting personhood, is that person God? An opponent can concede the first and deny the second — an exalted or created spirit would be personal without being divine — so the deity claim has to be carried on its own evidential legs.

It is load-bearing against three named adversaries: the Jehovah’s Witness “active force” reading (NWT); the Muslim polemical charge that “the Holy Spirit did not become God for 380 years” (deity invented at Constantinople); and the biblical-Unitarian reading. All three are answered from the same base — divine identity plus divine attributes plus co-equal naming — reinforced, against the Muslim charge, by a pre-Nicene patristic appeal.

The convergent case — three evidence streams, not a syllogism

Like the deity-of-Christ case at Q-3.4.1, this is an abductive cumulative case — not the deductive syllogism of the Kalam. Deity is established by stacking texts across three converging streams. Craig works the case from the New Testament; McLatchie works the identical structure from the Old, then adds a fourth, historical leg the personhood page never needed.

Stream A · Identified with Yahweh

  • Lied to the Spirit = lied to God (Acts 5:3–4)
  • Only God can be blasphemed (Mark 3:28–29)
  • “Sent forth” by Yahweh, yet divine (Isaiah 48:16)
  • Speaker of God’s scripture (Hebrews 10, lay-supplied)

Stream B · Divine attributes

  • Omniscience (Isaiah 40:13)
  • Omnipresence (Psalm 139:7)
  • Creative power (Job 33:4; Psalm 104:30)
  • The eternal Spirit (Hebrews 9:14)
  • Holiness — carried in the very name

Stream C · Trinitarian naming

  • Baptismal formula (Matthew 28:19)
  • Pauline benediction (2 Corinthians 13:14)
  • “Equally divine, equally personal”
  • Distinct — “not the Father or the Son in a guise”
On the “five divine attributes” schema

The settled systematic presentation lists five incommunicable attributes — eternity, omnipresence, omniscience, holiness, love. This corpus grounds three in verbatim argument (omniscience, omnipresence, holiness), plus creative power and a single eternity locus (Heb 9:14). Love is not argued at all, and eternity rests on one undeveloped text. The full schema is the tradition’s; these three transcripts carry only part of it — flagged honestly below, not papered over with a manufactured proof-text.

The node’s distinctive move — hostile witness by chronology

Against Hamza’s “380 years” charge, McLatchie shows the doctrine is pre-Nicene: Justin Martyr’s First Apology Ch. 6 and Ignatius already place the Spirit among the divine persons to be worshipped. Nicaea (325) addressed Christ’s eternity, not the Spirit’s; Constantinople (381) formalised what was already believed. Argument from conciliar silence is not doctrinal invention. This historical leg is what the personhood page never needed.

The New-Testament deity thesis — Defenders class
Deity & distinctness theses
  • Thesis A — the Spirit is God (Acts 5). Ananias lied “to the Holy Spirit,” then in the same breath “to God himself” — therefore “the Holy Spirit is God.” The inference is treated as immediate.
  • Thesis C — co-equal naming (Matt 28:19; 2 Cor 13:14): “three distinct persons of the Trinity, equally divine, equally personal.” The equality is where the third person’s deity is asserted as co-ordinate with the first two.
  • Historical framing: the Spirit as “the forgotten person of the Trinity” — an interesting counterpoint to McLatchie’s use of the same early fathers as positive deity witnesses.
Attribution caution — the Hebrews move is lay-supplied

Craig’s own exposition is pure expository proof-texting with no adversarial tactics. The one tactical move — citing Hebrews 10 against Jehovah’s Witnesses — is supplied by a class member, not Craig. Craig separately flags Hebrews 9:14 (“the eternal Spirit”) as a Trinitarian locus. The two Hebrews references are distinct and should not be merged.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

“The Holy Spirit did not become God for 380 years”

The Spirit’s deity was invented at the Council of Constantinople (381); the early church did not hold it. A doctrine that late is a development, not apostolic truth.

Hamza Myatt (Muslim, Speakers’ Corner)

Strongest response

The doctrine is pre-Nicene

Justin Martyr’s First Apology Ch. 6 (2nd c.) and Ignatius already place the Spirit among the divine persons to be worshipped. Nicaea (325) addressed Christ’s eternity, not the Spirit’s; Constantinople formalised existing belief. Argument from conciliar silence is not doctrinal invention.

McLatchie hostile witness by chronology

Objection 2

The Spirit is God’s impersonal “active force,” not God

The Spirit is God’s dynamic presence or operative power — like radio waves carrying a broadcast — not a distinct divine person. The New World Translation renders it accordingly.

Jehovah’s Witnesses (NWT); biblical Unitarians (Ismail)

Response — cumulative only

Data exceeds mere force

McLatchie: the Spirit is blasphemed against (Mark 3), identified as God (Acts 5), and bears divine prerogatives (omniscience, omnipresence, creation) jointly satisfiable only by deity. The case is the joint weight — but the active-force reading is not dismantled on its own terms (see gaps).

McLatchie joint-attribute argument

Objection 3

“Lied to the Spirit … lied to God” (Acts 5) doesn’t prove the Spirit is God

Lying to the Spirit could be lying to God because the Spirit represents or belongs to God — an ambassador, not the sovereign. The parallel proves agency, not identity.

JW / Unitarian (alternative parsing)

Gap — largely unaddressed

Both experts move straight to identity

Craig and McLatchie both step from the Acts 5 parallel directly to “the Holy Spirit is God” without engaging the representational reading. A Witness interlocutor would press exactly here, and the corpus has no answer.

Aggregation gap

Objection 4

The attribute texts describe God acting through his force

“You send forth your Spirit, they are created” (Ps 104:30) describes God creating by means of his power — not the deity of a distinct person. Read each attribute text this way and the deity inference evaporates.

Jehovah’s Witnesses, biblical Unitarians

Partially addressed

Cumulative weight, not text-by-text

McLatchie’s reply is the combination — identity (Acts 5) + distinctness (Isa 48:16) + attributes together resist the through-a-force reading. He does not rebut the through-a-force parsing of the attribute texts individually, which is the precise move a trained Witness makes.

McLatchie joint-stream defenceGap on individual texts

Objection 5

The language is mere personification (Proverbs 8 Wisdom)

Scripture personifies Wisdom as a woman who was “beside” God at creation without making Wisdom a divine person. Spirit-language is the same poetic device — and pneuma’s grammatical gender proves nothing.

Standard JW / Unitarian

Gap — unaddressed

Named by Craig, rebutted by no one

The Proverbs 8 personification counter is flagged in Craig’s own “objections-not-addressed” note but engaged by no expert in the corpus. It is the central counter to the deity stream exactly as it is to the personhood stream. The most important gap, shared with Q-10.1.1.

Aggregation gap

Objection 6

Singular-pronoun symmetry reductio

If singular pronouns prove the Spirit is a distinct divine person, then the singular pronouns used of the one God prove God is one person — which the Trinitarian denies. The argument cuts both ways.

Ismail (vs McLatchie)

Sidestepped, not dismantled

Relocate to divine prerogatives

McLatchie rests the deity case on divine prerogatives (omniscience, omnipresence, creation) rather than on pronoun grammar — the right ground, since it does not depend on the pronoun premise. But the symmetry reductio itself is not directly answered in the extract.

McLatchie prerogative-based relocation

Objection 7

Grant personhood — but the Spirit is a created spirit

An exalted angelic person could possess delegated authority and be sinned against without being the one uncreated God. Personhood and deity are separable; the corpus grants the first too freely.

Arian / sophisticated Unitarian wedge

Partially addressed

Aimed at deity, but the last step is undefended

Better handled here than on the personhood page: the Acts 5 identity, the Mark 3 blasphemy argument, and the attributes all target deity. But the move from “bears divine attributes” to “is numerically the one God” is not defended against an Arian “highest creature” reading.

McLatchie blasphemy + attributesGap on identity step

Objection 8

Spirit-as-divine-person was once Montanist heresy

The view that the Spirit is a distinct active person was historically suspect — even condemned in Montanist form — before being adopted late. That history undercuts its apostolic credentials.

Ismail (vs McLatchie); church-history reductio

Strongest response

The doctrine predates Montanism

McLatchie: the OT proof-texts and the 2nd-century fathers (Justin, Ignatius) show the Spirit’s deity was held well before both Montanism and Constantinople. He engages the substance and the chronology; the specific heresy-label reductio is not separately rebutted.

McLatchie chronological priority

Objection 9

Mark 3 shows only that the Spirit can be sinned against

Why think blasphemy is restricted to deity? One can blaspheme sacred-but-created things. The unforgivable-sin text proves the Spirit is holy and not-to-be-insulted — not that he is God.

Implicit Unitarian

Gap — asserted, not defended

“Only God can be blasphemed” is treated as self-evident

McLatchie states the premise as obvious. The corpus does not argue why blasphemy proper is restricted to deity, leaving the objection that one can profane created sacred things unhandled.
