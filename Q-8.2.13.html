The question

Is God’s retributive justice — his disposition to punish the guilty as they deserve — essential to his nature, or merely a contingent expression of his free will?

This is the modal hinge of the entire penal-substitution defence. PSA (Q-8.2.1) holds that Christ bore the punishment due to sinners so that justice and mercy could meet at the cross — but that edifice is only necessary if God could not simply have pardoned sin by fiat. The philosophical defence of PSA (Q-8.2.8) reduces the Socinian injustice objection by divine-command theory, then concedes the move only pushes the objection back a notch. This node is the notch. If retributive justice is something God merely commands of us but is not essential to his own being, then his nature does not require that sin be punished at all, and he could have forgiven without the cross.

Two positions divide the tradition. Necessitarianism — retributive (“punitive”) justice is a defining attribute, so given a deserving offender it is essential to God to punish, and satisfaction is essential to remission (Turretin; the post-Reformation Protestants; Craig’s own side). Contingentism — God could have freed humanity by sheer will, so the cross was a chosen means, not a required one (the majority patristic view, voiced bluntly by Augustine and diagnosed by Smeaton). The necessity of the atonement stands or falls here.

Coverage — a single-voice node

Read this as Craig adjudicating an in-house dispute, not a clash of experts

Everything below is William Lane Craig, across two 2017 Defenders lectures and the Philosophia Christi symposium on his book Atonement and the Death of Christ. Turretin, Augustine, Smeaton, Origen, Socinus, Thurow and Jansen appear only as sources and interlocutors within Craig’s treatment, never as independent voices. The corpus’s most important limitation — flagged prominently in the gaps section — is that essential retributivism here is sided-with, not secured: Craig persuasively reconstructs Turretin’s four arguments but candidly concedes that most of the tradition denied exactly this, and supplies no independent defeater of the contingentist majority.

The convergent case — a cumulative case for essential retributivism, in three streams

This is not a deductive syllogism. Craig reconstructs Turretin’s necessitarian foundation (Part 17), sets it against the contingentist majority he reports and diagnoses (Part 14), and adds his own positive / qualified-negative refinement from the symposium. The streams are independent — Turretin’s four arguments each converge on the same conclusion, any one of which could carry weight if another failed.

Strand A · The necessitarian foundation

  • Two virtues: justice & goodness (Turretin)
  • “The constant will of giving to each his due”
  • Deserve punishment ⊃ God must punish
  • Satisfaction essential to remission
  • Could not simply have pardoned

Strand B · The contingentist majority

  • Cross “not actually necessary” (the Fathers)
  • Augustine: “they are fools who say”
  • Smeaton: will severed from moral nature
  • Focus on sin’s consequences, not sin
  • Eastern incarnation-over-cross drift

Strand C · Craig’s refinement

  • Positive retributivism (guilt ⊃ punishment)
  • Only qualified negative retributivism
  • Humans bound; God not bound
  • Prima facie / ultima facie justice
  • Mercy relaxes, never abolishes, justice
Turretin · Argument 1

God’s revealed character — he detests sin and is a just judge.

Hatred of sin is judicial, not a mood. “Sin is not just something that God doesn’t want to put up with he hates it.” A just judge does not waive desert at will.

Turretin · Argument 2

Conscience and the universal consent of mankind testify that the guilty deserve punishment.

Corroborated from outside theology. Craig: “this point is true with respect to 21st century philosophical thinking… the widespread view today is that… evil does deserve punishment” — the legal academy’s renaissance of retributivism vindicates the second argument.

Turretin · Argument 3

The Hebrews argument: animal blood cannot take away sin — which only makes sense if God cannot simply will sin away.

From the impossibility of the blood of bulls and goats. “If sins could be put away simply by God’s will then it is not true… that it is impossible for the blood of bulls and goats to take away sin.” Scripture’s claim presupposes “it’s impossible for God not to punish sin.”

Turretin · Argument 4

The otherwise-unjustifiable cross — absent the necessity of satisfaction, the crucifixion is gratuitous cruelty.

The reductio on contingency. “Apart from the necessity of the satisfaction of divine justice no lawful reason could be given for God’s subjecting his son to such an accursed and cruel death.”

Verdict

“Retributive justice broadly conceived is essential to the very nature of God” — so satisfaction is essential to the remission of sins.

The modal payload. “If a person deserves punishment then it is essential to God to punish that person’s sin”; God “could not have simply chosen to forgive sins without satisfying his essential justice.” Craig sides with this — while conceding it is a Protestant development under Socinian pressure, not the patristic consensus.

How the case is built — William Lane Craig across three settings

The legal-historical architect — situate the dispute, then take a side and mark its cost
Stream A — the necessitarian foundation (Part 17, Turretin)
  • Turretin’s theory of justice. Two “principle virtues in God justice and goodness”; justice is “that by which God… has the constant will of giving to each his due” — i.e. retributive justice.
  • The modal claim. “If a person deserves punishment then it is essential to God to punish” — so satisfaction is “essential to the remission of sins,” and God “could not have simply chosen to forgive sins without satisfying his essential justice.”
  • Four converging arguments that God must punish: revealed character; universal conscience; the Hebrews “blood of bulls and goats” argument; the otherwise-unjustifiable cross.
  • Elenctic theology as a teaching point — theology “developed in conversation with one’s opponents”; Turretin’s whole theory is forged against Socinus, doubling as an endorsement of adversarial method.
Stream B — the contingentist majority, and why it is a weakness (Part 14)
  • The patristic position reported. “One of the most interesting features of the ransom theory… is their widespread conviction that Christ’s incarnation and death were not actually necessary” — an omnipotent God could free man directly.
  • Augustine’s blunt statement — “they are fools who say the wisdom of God could not otherwise free man” than by incarnation and suffering.
  • Smeaton’s diagnosis (the analytic key): the Fathers “separated God’s free will from the moral perfections of his nature rectitude wisdom and goodness” — making redemption look arbitrary rather than a demand of justice.
  • Consequences, not sin. Focused on death and mortality rather than sin itself, “Christ’s death was not required on this view by God’s justice as later thinkers like Anselm and the Reformers were to say.”
  • The necessitarian minority. Asked if anyone took the opposite side, Craig points to Origen (“the world must perforce have perished”) and Joseph Mitros’s review of the literature.
Stream C — the positive / qualified-negative refinement (symposium)
  • Positive retributivist — “punishment of the guilty is an intrinsic good” (Rutledge’s articulation, accepted by Craig).
  • Only a qualified negative retributivist — he “holds humans to it but not himself”; the innocent-must-not-be-punished rule binds us, not God.
  • Prima facie / ultima facie. Intervening moral goods (averting “the destruction of the entire human race”) justify forgoing the prima facie demands of positive retribution.
  • Net effect: the atonement “allows God to relax… far less of his essential retributive justice for the sake of mercy” — mercy and essential justice coexist.
The honest historical concession — Craig against the weight of the tradition

Craig is scrupulous that this is a development, not a consensus: “most of the church fathers did not think that God had to exercise punitive justice… but in response to Socinus’s attacks… Protestant theologians increasingly turned to the view that… punitive justice is necessary for the remission of sins.” He flatly grants “there’s a diversity of opinion among Christian theologians with respect to whether or not God has to exercise punitive justice” — declining to pretend the necessitarian view is the only Christian one.

Density / deep-dive note

The material is unusually technical for a lay catechetical setting — Turretin’s Institutes of Elenctic Theology (Topic 14, Qq. 10–14), positive vs negative retributivism, prima facie / ultima facie justification — and the symposium layer adds professional philosophy-of-law apparatus. Treat the node as a one-side settlement: a persuasive case for necessitarianism, reached on the strength of Turretin plus Craig’s own endorsement and a single Origen counter-instance, without independently defeating the conceded contingentist majority. The PSA defence at Q-8.2.8 leans its full weight on a conclusion this node argues for but does not close.

Objection 1

Contingentism: God could simply have forgiven sins without any satisfaction

The cross was a chosen means, not a required one. An omnipotent, gracious God can remit sin by sheer will — so the atonement is not necessary, and PSA over-reads the need for punishment.

The majority patristic view; Faustus Socinus (as Turretin’s opponent)

Strongest response

Turretin’s four converging arguments

Revealed character (God hates sin and is a just judge); the universal conscience of mankind; the Hebrews “blood of bulls and goats” argument; and the otherwise-unjustifiable cross. Together they establish that God must punish, so satisfaction is essential to remission.

Turretin / Craig Part 17

Objection 2

Augustine’s “fools” — it is foolish to think God could free man only by incarnation and suffering

The most authoritative patristic voice explicitly mocks the claim that God had no other way to redeem than the cross — so the necessity thesis is the eccentric one.

Augustine; the Eastern Fathers

Strongest response

Diagnose the mistake behind the view

Smeaton: the Fathers severed God’s free will from the moral perfections of his nature and focused on the consequences of sin rather than sin itself. Corrected, justice does require satisfaction — as Anselm and the Reformers saw.

Smeaton the will-severed-from-nature diagnosisCraig Part 14

Objection 3

The weight of the tradition backs contingency

If most of the Fathers held God could pardon freely, the burden is on the necessitarian, not the contingentist — necessitarianism looks like a late innovation.

Patristic consensus

Strongest response

Concede the numbers, point to the correction

Craig grants the consensus candidly, then notes the necessitarian minority (Origen: “the world must perforce have perished”; Mitros) and frames the Protestant turn under Socinian pressure as a correction of a defective doctrine of God, not a novelty. Honestly: he grants the count runs against him.

Origen the necessitarian counter-instanceCraig Parts 14 & 17

Objection 4

Retributivism is outdated penology — “desert” is a relic

Modern justice is consequentialist or restorative; punishment is justified by deterrence, reform and protection, not by bare desert. Grounding God’s justice in retribution imports a discredited theory.

Implied 20th-century consequentialism

Strongest response

Burden-flip to the contemporary consensus

“The widespread view today is that… evil does deserve punishment.” Retributivism has been vindicated in 21st-century philosophy of law — external corroboration of Turretin’s second (conscience) argument rather than a relic.

Craig modern legal-theory gloss, Part 17

Objection 5

Mercy is the antithesis of retribution

“The theory of retributive punishment knows no such concept of mercy… mercy would be suspending the intrinsic good of punishment… the concept of mercy seems misplaced indeed it is the antithesis of retribution.” An essential retributivist cannot consistently be merciful.

Danielle Jansen (symposium)

Strongest response

Positive / qualified-negative + prima facie relaxation

God is essentially a positive retributivist but only a qualified negative one; intervening moral goods (averting the destruction of the human race) let him relax the prima facie demand without abolishing essential justice — so mercy and essential retribution coexist. Full reply deferred to the published book.

Craig symposium (partial)Deferred to print

Objection 6

Exempting God just “bites the bullet” of the justice worry

Saying God holds humans to the no-punishing-the-innocent rule but is not bound by it himself does not dissolve the injustice — it simply concedes it and exempts God by stipulation.

Joshua Thurow (symposium)

Strongest response

The asymmetry follows from DCT

Craig accepts the asymmetry as a consequence of divine-command theory: God’s nature is the standard of justice, not human moral intuitions. The rule against punishing the innocent is one God commands of us; it does not bind the standard-setter himself.

Craig DCT grounding, symposium

Objection 7

Retributivists reject strict and vicarious liability

An essential retributivist holds that the guilty must be punished — which is exactly why retributivists standardly deny that an innocent substitute may bear another’s penalty. The view seems to cut against the very substitution PSA needs.

Davis (symposium framing)

Craig is “an odd man out among retributivists”

Craig accepts Davis’s framing that he is “something of an odd man out” on exactly this point, but does not dissolve the tension here; the vicarious-liability / respondeat superior defence is exported to Q-8.2.8.

Objection 8

The deferred linchpin — is retribution essential, or only contingently willed?

If retributive justice is merely something God commands of us, not essential to his being, the DCT defence of PSA collapses: God’s nature does not require punishment, so he could have pardoned without the cross and the atonement was avoidable.

Aggregation gap

Persistent gaps in the corpus

Gap 1

Essential retributivism is sided-with, not secured — the decisive gap.

Q-8.2.8 explicitly defers here: its DCT move dissolves the injustice objection only “a notch,” the remaining notch being whether retributive justice is essential to God. Yet this node delivers Craig’s reconstruction of Turretin’s four arguments plus a frank admission that most of the tradition denied exactly this. The necessity of the atonement therefore rests on the necessitarian side winning an in-house dispute the corpus reports but does not close. If retribution is contingent, the cross was avoidable — and Craig supplies no independent defeater of the contingentist majority.

Gap 2

The contingentist case is diagnosed, not refuted on its own terms.

Craig explains why the Fathers held contingency (Smeaton: will severed from nature) and treats the diagnosis as sufficient. But a contingentist could accept divine simplicity and still hold that which perfections God’s freedom must express is underdetermined — that goodness, not retribution, is the governing attribute. That rejoinder is never engaged.

Gap 3

Jansen’s “mercy is the antithesis of retribution” objection is deferred, not answered.

In the symposium Craig postpones his full reply to the published version of the book. The corpus carries the objection — that an intrinsic-good theory of punishment leaves no coherent room for mercy — but not the resolution.

Gap 4

The vicarious-liability tension with retributivism is conceded, not dissolved.

Craig grants (Davis) that retributivists standardly reject the strict and vicarious liability his substitutionary account requires, leaving him “an odd man out among retributivists.” The acknowledgement is candid but the tension is exported to Q-8.2.8 rather than resolved on this page.

Gap 5

The consequentialist / restorative challenge is noted, not met.

Craig observes that retributivism is “widespread today” but does not engage the contemporary consequentialist or restorative-justice case against retributivism on its own ground. The §8.2.14 and §2.5 nodes should pair the counter from elsewhere in the corpus.
