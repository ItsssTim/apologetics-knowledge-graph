The question

Why did Jesus have to die — and if forgiveness is what God wanted, why a blood sacrifice rather than a simple pardon?

This is the load-bearing soteriological question of §8. Not whether Jesus died — granted by virtually all parties, including the Muslim interlocutors who otherwise deny the crucifixion — but what the death accomplished and why it was necessary at all. The apologist must defend two things at once: that sin carries a real penalty a holy God cannot simply waive, and that the cross satisfies that penalty without God ceasing to be merciful.

This node is scoped to the biblical basis of the atonement — the New Testament’s own motifs (sacrifice, the suffering Servant, divine justice, representation, and above all redemption / ransom) and the cross-vs-resurrection emphasis. The atonement theories that systematise these belong to Q-8.2.3; the deity-and-humanity requirement to Q-8.2.2; the legal-philosophical defence of penal substitution to Q-8.2.4–Q-8.2.8. This page cross-references those rather than duplicating them.

The corpus converges on substitution as the death’s core logic, but is attacked from two flanks: the Muslim/skeptical flank (“why can’t God just forgive?” / “you can’t transfer guilt”) and the liberal-Christian flank (Keith Ward), which keeps the cross but relocates redemption to the resurrection.

The argument — five converging streams

Four experts run a recognisable version of the same justice-mercy dilemma, resolved by substitution and supported by analogy. A fifth stream — the Bible’s own atonement motifs, which Craig reconstructs — supplies the foundation the popular argument presupposes. And a dissenting stream (Keith Ward) relocates redemption from the dying to the raising. These are the streams the node must hold together.

Stream A · The penalty

  • Sin against a holy God carries death (Knechtle)
  • “God cannot acquit the guilty” (McLatchie)
  • “God cannot wink at sin” (Huff)
  • Post-Fall, judgment rightly on our heads (Perry)
  • Gravity of sin warrants gravity of cross (S. Knechtle)

Stream B · The dilemma

  • Every deity is either just or merciful (Huff)
  • Just judge condemns all; merciful judge frees all
  • Islam: Allah “just forgives” (merciful)
  • Buddhist karma: “you get what you deserve” (just)
  • Court-of-law: judge “obliged to pass sentence” (McLatchie)

Stream C · The cost / the ransom

  • Forgiveness is “always always costly” (Huff)
  • Forgiveness = “absorbing the hit” (Knechtle)
  • “A ransom for many” — Mark 10:45 (Craig, Huff)
  • The price was “the blood of Christ” (Craig)
  • The cup of wrath drunk to the dregs (McLatchie)

Stream D · The biblical motifs

  • Redemption = buying back captives (Craig)
  • Levitical sacrifice prefigures the cross (Craig)
  • Offerer kills the animal himself (Craig, Leviticus)
  • Lamb who takes away sin — John 1:29 (Huff)
  • Make peace with all the data (Craig, twin-function)

Stream E · The resolution

  • God both judge and justifier (Knechtle)
  • Wrath fully satisfied on Jesus (Huff)
  • Legal transaction: fine paid, case dismissed (McLatchie)
  • The great exchange: he became sin (Perry, 2 Cor 5:21)
  • Dissent: redemption is the raising, not the dying (Ward)

The convergent spine — from ransom-price to justice satisfied

Premise 1

Sin against a holy God carries a real penalty — death — that justice forbids God to simply waive.

Shared across the corpus. Knechtle: “the penalty for that is death and hell.” McLatchie: God “cannot acquit the guilty… while retaining his just nature, because justice prescribes it.” Huff: “God cannot wink at sin.” Perry: post-Fall, “all of humanity rightfully deserve… righteous judgment.”

Premise 2

God therefore faces a dilemma: a perfectly just judge condemns every defendant; a perfectly merciful judge releases every defendant.

Huff’s fullest form. “All worldviews are either rooted in justice or mercy.” Mercy at the expense of justice, or justice at the expense of mercy. McLatchie’s court-of-law variant: the judge “is legally obliged to pass sentence.”

Premise 3

Forgiveness is therefore never free — someone must absorb the cost; and the cost is a life laid down in the sinner’s place, the ransom-price.

The cost / ransom move. Knechtle: forgiveness means “absorbing the hit that I deserve.” Huff: “forgiveness is always always costly.” Craig supplies the biblical category: redemption “was not costless… that price was the blood of Christ.” Jesus gives “his life a ransom for many” (Mark 10:45).

Premise 4

The Bible frames this death through several motifs at once — sacrifice, suffering Servant, divine justice, representation, and ransom — not one to the exclusion of the rest.

Craig’s twin-function thesis. Levitical sacrifice (propitiation/expiation) prefigured the cross; the ransom motif (Mark 10:45; 1 Pet 1:18–19; Rev 5:9–10) frames it as a liberation-price. Any adequate account “has to make peace with the biblical data” — a methodological discipline that doctrine “begins with biblical texts” (Farmer), not later councils.

Conclusion

At the cross, God incarnate becomes both judge and substitute, paying the ransom and absorbing the penalty himself — so justice is satisfied and mercy is released.

Substitution. The only mechanism in which a holy God forgives without ceasing to be just; trust in the sacrifice (not personal merit) acquits the guilty and ransoms the captive. Huff: “justice and mercy come together in a beautiful display.” The corpus’ portable teaching device: mercy is “not getting what you do deserve”; grace is “getting what you don’t deserve.”

How each expert deploys it

William Lane Craig3 transcripts
The redemption/ransom motif + Levitical sacrifice + twin-function thesis
Biblical-theological anchor
  • The ransom motif: “redemption” meant buying back prisoners and slaves; the payment was a ransom. Jesus gives his life “a ransom for many” (Mark 10:45) — “a payment for our liberation from the captivity of sin.”
  • The price was not costless: it was “the blood of Christ” (1 Pet 1:18–19; Eph 1:7; Heb 9:12; 1 Cor 6:20). The ransom also constitutes the freed as “a kingdom and priests” (Rev 5:9–10), fulfilling Exodus 19:6.
  • Levitical sacrifice as prefigure: the “gracious gift of God,” letting “a holy God live in proximity with a corrupt and unclean people” (kipper; Yom Kippur). The offerer, not the priest, killed the animal; the “gruesomeness is deliberate.”
  • Twin-function thesis: any adequate theory “has to make peace with the biblical data” — sacrifice, Servant, justice, representation, and ransom held together.
  • Type-discipline: the firstborn of Egypt are not a Christ-type (they “fell under the wrath of God for their own sins”); Isaac is.
Methodological hinge

Craig pivots from motif-survey to systematics on William Farmer’s principle that “Christian doctrine begins with biblical texts” — built from the New Testament outward, not retrojected from Nicaea or Chalcedon. He notes Eusebius later read the Fathers’ ransom motif as a literal price (“What is that but the price of our souls?”), seeding the patristic ransom theory at Q-8.2.3.

Wes Huff3 transcripts
The justice-mercy dilemma — fullest popular treatment
Signature delivery
  • Moralism fails because nobody is good — the rich young ruler: “Why do you call me good? No one is good but God.”
  • The judge dilemma: a perfectly just judge throws the book at everyone; a perfectly merciful judge frees everyone. “Religions have a problem in solving the issue of justice and mercy.”
  • Comparative-religion sort: Islam’s Allah “just forgives because he can”; Buddhist karma “is inherently just.” Only Christianity unites the two.
  • Resolution through the ransom: “because he’s made a ransom for us, God is able to give us mercy” (Matt 20:18 mission statement; John 1:29 “the lamb… who takes away the sin of the world”).
  • Costly-forgiveness analogies (BMW, slander), the com-passion etymology (“with-suffering”), and the value-by-price appeal: “what did God pay for you?”
Signature reversal

World religions say “do these things and you’ll be saved”; Christianity says “you’re saved, now you’re able to do these things.” His humour line: “Your application to join the Trinity has been denied — you fail the minimum requirements.”

Cliffe Knechtle3 transcripts
Forgiveness as cost-absorption + judge-justifier
Signature delivery
  • “Absorbing the hit”: every act of forgiveness requires the wronged party to absorb cost rather than exact revenge. Christ’s death is “God in human form absorbing the hit that I deserve.”
  • Judge-justifier duality: “God is the judge… but he’s also the justifier.”
  • Live test cases: Botham Jean’s brother, the Charleston AME forgiveness, and the Jean Simon DeLatel guillotine story.
  • Salvation is reconciliation, not self-improvement: “me being reconnected to God through faith in Christ.”
Stuart Knechtle’s extension

The “why blood?” objection is a post-1700s humanistic artefact: “we are good here now, which has never existed in any civilization… we are like fish in water that don’t even know what water is.” Every pre-modern culture practised sacrifice — “not just the Israelite levitical laws” (the scapegoat); the modern West is the outlier.

Jonathan McLatchie3 transcripts
The cup of wrath + the legal transaction + ransom-priesthood
Signature delivery
  • The cup of wrath, drunk to the dregs: tetelestai — “it is finished” — marks the cup turned over “and not one single drop fell.” Recurs verbatim across all three transcripts. Grounded in 2 Cor 5:21.
  • Legal-transaction model: “Jesus pays our fine with his own life’s blood, meaning God can now legally dismiss our case.”
  • Ransom-priesthood: Christ as mediator (Job 9:33), High Priest after Melchizedek (Ps 110), who “gets to sit down” — the missing temple chair — because his work is complete.
  • Internal critique of Islam: the Sahih Bukhari 100-murderer hadith proves Islamic forgiveness “sweeps sin under the rug.” “For Muhammad cannot save you, but Jesus can.”
Preston Perry1 transcript
Substitution as the cure for felt condemnation
Distinctive move
  • Delivers the substitutionary exchange without ever naming it — as the bare contents of “the gospel,” calibrated to undo a deconstruction wound.
  • The great exchange: God “sent his son Jesus who knew no sin to become sin so that we can become the righteousness of God” (2 Cor 5:21).
  • Severing move: the church that taught her condemnation “misrepresented the god of the Bible.”
  • Confessional register: “I believe… I believe… I believe…” rather than “here is what you must believe.” “No condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus” (Rom 8:1).
Keith Ward1 transcript (via Knechtle)
The resurrection-centred dissent
Distinctive move
  • Plato as hostile witness: “if there was ever a truly just and loving person… they would be crucified” — a fourth-century-BC pagan anticipating the central Christian event.
  • The death’s importance “is that it made possible Jesus’s resurrection… that raising into glory is what the redemption is.”
  • Prefers an empty cross; finds the crucifix “not totally spiritually enlightening.”
  • Christus-Victor-adjacent; floats free of the ransom and propitiation motifs — a Pauline-atonement tension Knechtle never presses.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

“Why can’t God just forgive without a gruesome death?”

If God is omnipotent and merciful, he could simply pardon sin. The cross looks like an arbitrary, even cruel, requirement.

Muslim apologetic (the bismillah model); popular skeptic

Strongest response

Cost-free forgiveness is incoherent

Forgiveness is never free — someone always absorbs the cost, in human and divine cases alike. The BMW and slander analogies show even ordinary pardon has a price the forgiver pays.

Huff BMW / slander analogiesKnechtle “absorbing the hit”

Objection 2

If God is omnipotent (Matt 19:26), why does he need a blood sacrifice?

“With God all things are possible” — so what ethically justifies requiring a death at all?

Majors (vs Knechtle)

Strongest response

Sin equals death; the remedy must answer it

If sin is the equivalent of death, something must redeem us from that brokenness. God being the source of all goodness and holiness, “the appeasement is Jesus Christ himself.”

Knechtle judge-justifier duality

Objection 3

Vicarious redemption is immoral — you can’t transfer moral guilt

You may pay another’s debt or serve their prison term, but you cannot say “I’ll take your sins on me.” That asks the other party into “a very immoral agreement.”

Christopher Hitchens; Randolph Richardson

Gap — largely unanswered

Repentance reply only; Hitchens left unmet

Knechtle requires repentance and reconciliation, not blanket third-party absolution. But the moral-philosophy version goes unanswered: Craig — who has a published vicarious-liability defence (Q-8.2.4–Q-8.2.8) — says nothing on the panel.

Knechtle partialAggregation gap

Objection 4

The death is no rescue: Jesus prayed to be saved (Heb 5:7)

If a rescue happens only after you’re killed, no rescue occurred. “Let them kill you first and then I’ll resurrect you” is incoherent — so Jesus was not crucified for our sins.

Ayoob Karim (self-named “reduce it to absurd arguments”)

Strongest response

The death is the mission, not a failed rescue

Matt 20:18 is Jesus’s own mission statement (“a ransom for many”); tetelestai marks the wrath fully consumed; as mediator (Job 9:33) with two natures, Christ absorbs it deliberately.

McLatchie mediator + mission-statement closing

Objection 5

One cannot be punished for another’s sin (Ezekiel-18 style)

Justice means each person answers for their own crime. Laying one person’s guilt on an innocent third party “does not make any sense.”

Ahmed Sraa; Muslim apologetic

Strongest response

The sins were laid on Christ

Deut 21:23 (“cursed is he who hangs on a tree”) is applied to Jesus because the sins of others were laid on him (Isa 53; John 1:29 “the lamb who takes away sin”). Framed through justice/mercy debt categories.

Huff indirect (debt-category framing)

Objection 6

Belief alone deciding heaven/hell is unjust

Two people commit the same sins; the only difference is one believes in Jesus. Sending “good people” to hell merely for unbelief is unjust.

Majors; live-chat questioner

Strongest response

It is sin, not belief-content, that warrants judgment

McLatchie reframes: eternal destiny turns on sin against a holy God, and the gospel is the offer of acquittal. Knechtle: belief is “a statement about God’s character,” a change of heart — not intellectual gymnastics.

McLatchie reframe to sinKnechtle belief-as-character

Objection 7

Blood sacrifice is a pagan imposition on Christianity

Sacrifice is “a pagan import from previous religions that invaded an otherwise wonderful Christian ethic.”

“Eric” (vs Craig)

Strongest response

Sacrifice is internal and prior to Christianity

The Levitical system is “the gracious gift of God to the Hebrew nation,” older than and untouched by Christianity; the “pagan import” framing inverts the actual textual genealogy. Ganssle adds the general-revelation supplement.

Craig Levitical typologyGanssle general revelation

Objection 8

Were the firstborn of Egypt a type of Christ’s redeeming death?

If the Passover slaying of Egypt’s firstborn prefigured the cross, the typology would make Christ’s death just one more divine smiting — and confuse the picture of substitution.

Student (vs Craig, Doctrine of Christ class)

Strongest response

No — they bore their own sin, not Israel’s

The firstborn of Egypt “didn’t bear substitutionally the punishment due to the people of Israel… they just fell under the wrath of God for their own sins.” Isaac, by contrast, is a genuine type of Christ (per Hebrews). A guard against typological overreach.

Craig type-discipline

Objection 9

The Eucharist celebrates a murder

“Did the Jews kill Jesus, and now you are drinking his blood and eating his flesh — are you benefiting from the proceeds of the crime they committed?”

Shabir Ally

Gap — not answered in-corpus

Raised on the debate stage, left hanging

McLatchie does not engage the “proceeds of the crime” framing. No expert in this corpus answers the Eucharist-as-celebrating-a-crime objection on its own terms (see Q-11.3).

Objection 10

“Created sick, then commanded to cure yourself”

“How moral is it to say you’re created sick and then commanded on pain of horrible torture to cure yourself?”

Christopher Hitchens

Gap — not engaged

The panel does not respond

Hitchens’ created-sick framing surrounds the vicarious-redemption objection; the panel touches the adjacent terrain (Wilson, Dennison) but never the moral-philosophical structure.

Sin still exists after the cross, so it “didn’t work”

“If he died for the sins of mankind, we continue to have suffering, we continue to suffer as human beings.”

Ahmed (Ahmadiyya, vs Huff)

Gap — not directly addressed

Canonical quotes

In the ancient world the notion of redemption had to do with the buying back of prisoners of war or with the buying of slaves out of slavery. The payment that was given to redeem these persons to liberate them was called a ransom. We’ve already seen in Mark 10:45 that Jesus characterizes his mission as giving his life as “a ransom for many.” His life served as a payment for our liberation from the captivity of sin. William Lane Craig · the redemption / ransom motif
Other New Testament authors did not think of Christ’s redeeming act was costless. Rather there was a price that was paid for our redemption… that price was, as we’ve seen, the blood of Christ which was paid to redeem us from the captivity of sin. Any adequate theory of the atonement, if it is to commend itself to us as a Christian theory of the atonement, has to make peace with the biblical data that we have reviewed. William Lane Craig · the twin-function thesis
The priest doesn’t kill the animal — you’re required to do it yourself. The offerer lays his hand on the animal and then he slits its throat and kills it… you’re required to kill that animal, which dies in your place to atone for your sin. The gruesomeness of it is deliberate: it says this is what it costs because of your sin. William Lane Craig · Levitical sacrifice as prefigure
Christ upon that cross drunk that cup right there to the very last drop, such that when he cried out “tetelestai” — it is finished — he turned over that cup and not one single drop fell. In Christianity no sin is ever forgiven; sin is dealt with either on the head of the sinner or on the head of the sinless substitute, namely Jesus Christ. Jonathan McLatchie · the cup of wrath
All worldviews are either rooted in justice or mercy. A judge who is perfectly just will throw the book at the criminal; a judge who was completely merciful will let you go. Religions have a problem solving the issue of justice and mercy — if he forgives he’s merciful but at the expense of his justice, and if he’s just, he’s just at the expense of mercy. Wes Huff · the judge dilemma
The gospel says that, to those who accept Jesus, it is not merely believing that Jesus Christ died on the cross for your sins, but it is literally an exchange — it is God taking away your punishment, God taking away your sin, and giving you his righteousness. Preston Perry · the great exchange
The importance of Jesus’s death is that it made possible Jesus’s resurrection, his raising to glory in God. It was as the crucified that he was raised to glory, and for me that raising into glory, that life with God, is what the redemption is. Keith Ward · the resurrection-centred dissent
