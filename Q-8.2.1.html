The question

Why did Jesus have to die — and if forgiveness is what God wanted, why a blood sacrifice rather than a simple pardon?

This is the load-bearing soteriological question of §8. Not whether Jesus died — granted by virtually all parties, including the Muslim interlocutors who otherwise deny the crucifixion — but what the death accomplished and why it was necessary at all. The apologist must defend two things at once: that sin carries a real penalty a holy God cannot simply waive, and that the cross satisfies that penalty without God ceasing to be merciful.

The corpus converges on one mechanism — penal substitution, framed as the only account in which justice and mercy meet — but it is attacked from two flanks at once: the Muslim/skeptical flank (“why can’t God just forgive?” / “you can’t transfer guilt”) and the liberal-Christian flank (Keith Ward), which keeps the cross but relocates redemption to the resurrection.

The argument — four converging streams

Four experts run a recognisable version of the same justice-mercy dilemma, resolved by substitution and supported by analogy rather than formal proof. A fifth stream — Keith Ward’s — dissents, relocating redemption from the dying to the raising. These are the streams the node must hold together.

Stream A · The penalty

  • Sin against a holy God carries death (Knechtle)
  • “God cannot acquit the guilty” (McLatchie)
  • “God cannot wink at sin” (Huff)
  • Post-Fall, judgment rightly on our heads (Perry)
  • Gravity of sin warrants gravity of cross (S. Knechtle)

Stream B · The dilemma

  • Every deity is either just or merciful (Huff)
  • Just judge condemns all; merciful judge frees all
  • Islam: Allah “just forgives” (merciful)
  • Buddhist karma: “you get what you deserve” (just)
  • Court-of-law: judge “obliged to pass sentence” (McLatchie)

Stream C · The cost

  • Forgiveness is “always always costly” (Huff)
  • Forgiveness = “absorbing the hit” (Knechtle)
  • BMW / slander analogies (Huff)
  • Offerer kills the animal himself (Craig, Leviticus)
  • The cup of wrath drunk to the dregs (McLatchie)

Stream D · The resolution

  • God both judge and justifier (Knechtle)
  • Wrath fully satisfied on Jesus (Huff)
  • Legal transaction: fine paid, case dismissed (McLatchie)
  • The great exchange: he became sin (Perry, 2 Cor 5:21)
  • Dissent: redemption is the raising, not the dying (Ward)

The convergent spine — justice and mercy meet

Premise 1

Sin against a holy God carries a real penalty — death — that justice forbids God to simply waive.

Shared across the corpus. Knechtle: “the penalty for that is death and hell.” McLatchie: God “cannot acquit the guilty… while retaining his just nature, because justice prescribes it.” Huff: “God cannot wink at sin.”

Premise 2

God therefore faces a dilemma: a perfectly just judge condemns every defendant; a perfectly merciful judge releases every defendant.

Huff’s fullest form. “All worldviews are either rooted in justice or mercy.” Mercy at the expense of justice, or justice at the expense of mercy. McLatchie’s court-of-law variant: the judge “is legally obliged to pass sentence.”

Premise 3

Forgiveness is therefore never free — someone must absorb the cost.

The cost-absorption move. Knechtle: forgiveness means “absorbing the hit that I deserve.” Huff: “forgiveness is always always costly” — you hit a BMW with no insurance and the driver eats the cost.

Conclusion

At the cross, God incarnate becomes both judge and substitute, absorbing the penalty himself — so justice is satisfied and mercy is released.

Penal substitution. The only mechanism in which a holy God forgives without ceasing to be just; trust in the sacrifice (not personal merit) acquits the guilty. Huff: “justice and mercy come together in a beautiful display.” The corpus’ portable teaching device: mercy is “not getting what you do deserve”; grace is “getting what you don’t deserve.”

How each expert deploys it

Wes Huff3 transcripts
The justice-mercy dilemma — fullest treatment
Signature delivery
  • Moralism fails because nobody is good — the rich young ruler: “Why do you call me good? No one is good but God.”
  • The judge dilemma: a perfectly just judge throws the book at everyone; a perfectly merciful judge frees everyone. “Religions have a problem in solving the issue of justice and mercy.”
  • Comparative-religion sort: Islam’s Allah “just forgives because he can”; Buddhist karma “is inherently just.” Only Christianity unites the two.
  • Resolution: “the wrath of God was fully satisfied on Jesus, and because of that mercy was due.”
  • Costly-forgiveness analogies (BMW, slander), the com-passion etymology (“with-suffering”), and the value-by-price appeal: “what did God pay for you?”
Signature reversal

World religions say “do these things and you’ll be saved”; Christianity says “you’re saved, now you’re able to do these things.” His humour line: “Your application to join the Trinity has been denied — you fail the minimum requirements.”

Cliffe Knechtle3 transcripts
Forgiveness as cost-absorption + judge-justifier
Signature delivery
  • “Absorbing the hit”: every act of forgiveness requires the wronged party to absorb cost rather than exact revenge. Christ’s death is “God in human form absorbing the hit that I deserve.”
  • Judge-justifier duality: “God is the judge… but he’s also the justifier.”
  • Live test cases: Botham Jean’s brother, the Charleston AME forgiveness, and the Jean Simon DeLatel guillotine story.
  • Salvation is reconciliation, not self-improvement: “me being reconnected to God through faith in Christ.”
Stuart Knechtle’s extension

The “why blood?” objection is a post-1700s humanistic artefact: “we are good here now, which has never existed in any civilization… we are like fish in water that don’t even know what water is.” Every pre-modern culture practised sacrifice; the modern West is the outlier.

Jonathan McLatchie3 transcripts
The legal transaction + the cup of wrath
Signature delivery
  • The cup of wrath, drunk to the dregs: tetelestai — “it is finished” — marks the cup turned over “and not one single drop fell.” Recurs verbatim across all three transcripts.
  • Legal-transaction model: “Jesus pays our fine with his own life’s blood, meaning God can now legally dismiss our case.”
  • Internal critique of Islam: the Sahih Bukhari 100-murderer hadith proves Islamic forgiveness “sweeps sin under the rug.” “For Muhammad cannot save you, but Jesus can.”
  • Typological priesthood: Jesus as High Priest after Melchizedek (Ps 110) “gets to sit down” — the missing temple chair — because his work is complete.
Preston Perry1 transcript
PSA as the cure for felt condemnation
Distinctive move
  • Delivers penal substitution without ever naming it — as the bare contents of “the gospel,” calibrated to undo a deconstruction wound.
  • The great exchange: God “sent his son Jesus who knew no sin to become sin so that we can become the righteousness of God” (2 Cor 5:21).
  • Severing move: the church that taught her condemnation “misrepresented the god of the Bible.”
  • Confessional register: “I believe… I believe… I believe…” rather than “here is what you must believe.”
William Lane Craig2 transcripts
Levitical typology + the Hitchens objection
Distinctive move
  • Levitical sacrifice was “the gracious gift of God,” letting “a holy God live in proximity with a corrupt and unclean people.”
  • The offerer, not the priest, killed the animal — substitution made vivid; the “gruesomeness is deliberate.”
  • Rebuts “blood sacrifice is a pagan import” — sacrifice is internal and prior to Christianity.
  • Frames it as personally transformative (“just came alive to me”) — testimony as much as argument.
Unanswered on the panel

Christopher Hitchens lodges the corpus’ sharpest objection — you cannot transfer moral responsibility — and Craig does not answer it. The detailed vicarious-liability reply Craig gives elsewhere is absent here.

Keith Ward1 transcript (via Knechtle)
The resurrection-centred dissent
Distinctive move
  • Plato as hostile witness: “if there was ever a truly just and loving person… they would be crucified” — a fourth-century-BC pagan anticipating the central Christian event.
  • The death’s importance “is that it made possible Jesus’s resurrection… that raising into glory is what the redemption is.”
  • Prefers an empty cross; finds the crucifix “not totally spiritually enlightening.”
  • Christus-Victor-adjacent; floats free of penal-substitution categories — a Pauline-atonement tension Knechtle never presses.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

“Why can’t God just forgive without a gruesome death?”

If God is omnipotent and merciful, he could simply pardon sin. The cross looks like an arbitrary, even cruel, requirement.

Muslim apologetic (the bismillah model); popular skeptic

Strongest response

Cost-free forgiveness is incoherent

Forgiveness is never free — someone always absorbs the cost, in human and divine cases alike. The BMW and slander analogies show even ordinary pardon has a price the forgiver pays.

Huff BMW / slander analogiesKnechtle “absorbing the hit”

Objection 2

If God is omnipotent (Matt 19:26), why does he need a blood sacrifice?

“With God all things are possible” — so what ethically justifies requiring a death at all?

Majors (vs Knechtle)

Strongest response

Sin equals death; the remedy must answer it

If sin is the equivalent of death, something must redeem us from that brokenness. God being the source of all goodness and holiness, “the appeasement is Jesus Christ himself.”

Knechtle judge-justifier duality

Objection 3

Vicarious redemption is immoral — you can’t transfer moral guilt

You may pay another’s debt or serve their prison term, but you cannot say “I’ll take your sins on me.” That asks the other party into “a very immoral agreement.”

Christopher Hitchens; Randolph Richardson

Gap — largely unanswered

Repentance reply only; Hitchens left unmet

Knechtle requires repentance and reconciliation, not blanket third-party absolution. But the moral-philosophy version goes unanswered: Craig — who has a published vicarious-liability defence — says nothing on the panel.

Knechtle partialAggregation gap

Objection 4

PSA is a reductio: Jesus prayed to be saved (Heb 5:7)

If a rescue happens only after you’re killed, no rescue occurred. “Let them kill you first and then I’ll resurrect you” is incoherent — so Jesus was not crucified for our sins.

Ayoob Karim (self-named “reduce it to absurd arguments”)

Strongest response

The death is the mission, not a failed rescue

Matt 20:18 is Jesus’s own mission statement (“a ransom for many”); tetelestai marks the wrath fully consumed; as mediator (Job 9:33) with two natures, Christ absorbs it deliberately.

McLatchie mediator + mission-statement closing

Objection 5

One cannot be punished for another’s sin (Ezekiel-18 style)

Justice means each person answers for their own crime. Laying one person’s guilt on an innocent third party “does not make any sense.”

Ahmed Sraa; Muslim apologetic

Strongest response

The sins were laid on Christ

Deut 21:23 (“cursed is he who hangs on a tree”) is applied to Jesus because the sins of others were laid on him (Isa 53; John 1:29 “the lamb who takes away sin”). Framed through justice/mercy debt categories.

Huff indirect (debt-category framing)

Objection 6

Belief alone deciding heaven/hell is unjust

Two people commit the same sins; the only difference is one believes in Jesus. Sending “good people” to hell merely for unbelief is unjust.

Majors; live-chat questioner

Strongest response

It is sin, not belief-content, that warrants judgment

McLatchie reframes: eternal destiny turns on sin against a holy God, and the gospel is the offer of acquittal. Knechtle: belief is “a statement about God’s character,” a change of heart — not intellectual gymnastics.

McLatchie reframe to sinKnechtle belief-as-character

Objection 7

Blood sacrifice is a pagan imposition on Christianity

Sacrifice is “a pagan import from previous religions that invaded an otherwise wonderful Christian ethic.”

“Eric” (vs Craig)

Strongest response

Sacrifice is internal and prior to Christianity

The Levitical system is internal to the biblical story and predates Christianity; the “pagan import” framing inverts the actual textual genealogy. Ganssle adds the general-revelation supplement.

Craig Levitical typologyGanssle general revelation

Objection 8

The Eucharist celebrates a murder

“Did the Jews kill Jesus, and now you are drinking his blood and eating his flesh — are you benefiting from the proceeds of the crime they committed?”

Shabir Ally

Gap — not answered in-corpus

Raised on the debate stage, left hanging

McLatchie does not engage the “proceeds of the crime” framing. No expert in this corpus answers the Eucharist-as-celebrating-a-crime objection on its own terms.

Objection 9

“Created sick, then commanded to cure yourself”

“How moral is it to say you’re created sick and then commanded on pain of horrible torture to cure yourself?”

Christopher Hitchens

Gap — not engaged

The panel does not respond

Hitchens’ created-sick framing surrounds the vicarious-redemption objection; the panel touches the adjacent terrain (Wilson, Dennison) but never the moral-philosophical structure.

Objection 10

Sin still exists after the cross, so it “didn’t work”

“If he died for the sins of mankind, we continue to have suffering, we continue to suffer as human beings.”

Ahmed (Ahmadiyya, vs Huff)

Gap — not directly addressed
