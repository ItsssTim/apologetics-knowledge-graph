The question

Was Jesus’s divinity a late ecclesiastical invention — or was high Christology present from the earliest layer of the evidence?

This is the developmental-thesis question, the historical-critical counterpart to Q-3.4.1. Where Q-3.4.1 asks whether the historical Jesus made the claim, Q-3.4.4 asks whether the belief in his divinity was invented by his followers and back-projected. It is the load-bearing premise of the “legend” horn of the Lewis trilemma, the Muslim “the disciples called him a man” argument, and Bart Ehrman’s How Jesus Became God evolutionary thesis.

The apologetic answer is uniform: high Christology is early. The earliest datable material — the pre-Pauline creeds (1 Cor 15:3–8), the Philippians 2 hymn, the Aramaic Maranatha prayer — already worships Jesus as Lord, decades before John and three centuries before Nicaea. The late-invention thesis is treated less as a live possibility than as a chronological impossibility.

The convergent case — evidence streams against late invention

Not a deductive syllogism but a cumulative case. No single stream is decisive; the force is in the convergence — each independently pushes the high-Christology date earlier than the developmental thesis can accommodate.

Stream A · Earliest documents

  • Pre-Pauline creed (1 Cor 15:3–8)
  • Philippians 2 hymn
  • Maranatha prayer (1 Cor 16)
  • Romans 10 — Yahweh proof-text applied to Jesus
  • Colossians 1, Hebrews 1, John 1 converge

Stream B · Enemy attestation

  • Josephus
  • Tacitus
  • Celsus
  • The Talmud (“the townman”)
  • Confirms the Son-of-God framing was contemporary

Stream C · Anti-prior background

  • No divine Messiah expected (Num 23:19)
  • No crucified Messiah expected
  • Trypho (via Justin Martyr)
  • Scribal / Pharisaic formation
  • Patristic orthodoxy settled by 115 AD

  • Oldest recorded prayer: the Aramaic Maranatha (1 Cor 16) is directed to Jesus and addresses him as Lord — a fossil predating the Greek gospels.

Jonathan McLatchie 2 transcripts
The anti-prior Jewish background
Distinctive move
  • No divine, crucified Messiah expected: the expectation was a military deliverer, “not the excruciating and embarrassing death of a criminal.”
  • Trypho as hostile witness: Justin Martyr’s Dialogue with Trypho records a Jewish philosopher objecting that the Messiah could not be a crucified man.
  • Criterion-of-dissimilarity move (unnamed): the disciples’ belief was a sharp discontinuity from their scribal / Pharisaic formation — best explained by Jesus’s claims, miracles, and resurrection, not preconceived theology.
  • Pagan-parallels rebuttal: the parallels run gospel↔Old Testament, not gospel↔pagan; the presentation is “thoroughly grounded in the Old Testament.”
Methodological weight

McLatchie inverts the usual move: rather than stacking early texts, he argues the prior probability of inventing this belief inside Second-Temple Judaism was near zero — so the belief is evidentially weighty precisely because it cut against the disciples’ own formation.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

Christology progresses through the gospels — John is latest and most explicit

Reading the gospels in compositional order shows a development. John, the latest, is most explicit about Jesus’s divinity in his own words and has no parables — suggesting invention, not memory. Licona concedes John restates Jesus’s actions as explicit divinity speeches; Evans, that Jesus may not have said them.

Jon Steingard (deconvert), citing Licona & Evans as hostile witnesses

Gap — largely unanswered

Pivot, not engagement

Stuart pivots to the schizophrenia point, 115 AD patristic coalescence, and Bono rather than engaging the Licona authorial-latitude concession. The latent answer — deity is attested before John, so John’s explicitness is not the origin — is never deployed against this specific formulation.

Aggregation gap

Objection 2

High Christology is only in John (a late gospel)

The seven “I AM” statements and explicit divinity are confined to the latest gospel — so the claim is a Johannine development.

Muslim apologetic (standard)

Strongest response

Paul predates John

Paul’s letters were written before John and are “all about the deity of Christ.” The Maranatha prayer and the 1 Cor 15 / Phil 2 creeds are earlier still — the divinity claim sits in the earliest stratum, not the latest.

Stuart Knechtle eight early sourcesCraig Maranatha + Romans 10

Objection 3

Jesus never said “I am God” or “worship me”

He never uttered the explicit words. His disciples in Acts call him “a man of God” (2:22) and “a servant of God” (3:13). So Christology is read into the text, not out of it.

Bashir Vania (Muslim)

Strongest response

Implicit identity that the figurative reading cannot absorb

McLatchie adduces Matt 11:27 (the “Q logion thunderbolt from the Johannine sky”), Mark 13:32, John 8:58, the wicked-tenants parable, and John 17:5 + Isaiah 42:8. Vania meets each with a blanket appeal to metaphor (John 16:25 “I speak figuratively”), but the John 17:5 / Isaiah 42:8 combination goes unanswered.

McLatchie textual stack

Objection 4

The divinity belief is borrowed from pagan deities

Jesus is a rehashed Osiris or Mithras — the divine-son motif is Mediterranean syncretism.

Mythicist / popular online

Strongest response

The parallels run gospel↔Old Testament

The Messianic presentation is “thoroughly grounded in the Old Testament… if anything the parallels are between the Gospels and the Old Testament.” The pagan-parallel claim ignores the pervasive Jewish context of the New Testament.

McLatchie redirected parallel vector

Objection 5

Jesus is a myth — he may not have existed

The whole figure is a literary construction; there was no historical Jesus to develop a Christology about.

Internet mythicism

Strongest response

No scholar defends this

Craig: mythicism is “poppycock” — the Roman crucifixion is “indisputable,” and even non-Christian and Jewish scholars (Lapide) affirm the historical core. We can turn to scholarship and say it doesn’t believe this.

Craig scholarly-consensus appeal

Objection 6

The gospels are biased (written by followers) so unreliable

The authors were Jesus’s own followers — of course they wrote up his divinity. That’s circular.

Standard skeptical

Strongest response

Bias ≠ unreliability

Stuart: a LeBron James enthusiast can write a reliable LeBron James biography. Enthusiasm and bias do not entail falsehood; it can still be a careful, accurate work.

Stuart Knechtle LeBron analogy

Objection 7

It’s all legend — mystery cults, Zoroastrianism, stolen motifs

The fourth “L”: not Lord, liar, or lunatic, but legend — accreted literary techniques borrowed from mystery cults.

Aaron Adair (atheist apostate), audience question

Strongest response

Too close for legend

Stuart: 16 extraneous sources establish Jesus as a historical figure, and the manuscript witness is “too close” to the events. Legend requires a 150–500-year gap that the evidence does not allow.

Stuart Knechtle proximity argument

Objection 8

Reading Christology in is preconceived Christian bias

No Jew ever believed the Messiah, Son of God, or Son of Man would be God. An objective reader — an atheist — would not reach the same conclusion as a Christian from the same text.

Bashir Vania (Muslim)

Partial response

The disciples were the most biased against the conclusion

McLatchie’s anti-prior background implicitly answers this: the disciples’ formation gave them every bias against a divine, crucified Messiah — so the belief is dissimilar to their expectation and demands an external cause. But it is never turned explicitly against Vania’s bias charge.
