A charged word, worth slowing down on

Isn't Christianity intolerant?

It is one of the sharpest things said about the faith. Christians claim one way is true, so surely they look down on everyone else. Before the charge can stick, one question has to come first. What does intolerant actually mean? Once that word is pinned down, the picture changes.

First / The word
Step 1 of 4
Pin the term down before using it

Define intolerant before you throw it.

It is an emotionally charged word, and a charged word does a lot of damage before anyone checks what it means.

The smear
Intolerant has become a label you stick on a view to discredit it without arguing against it. The word does the work that evidence is supposed to do, and the conversation ends before it starts.
Teresa, not the Inquisition
Then there is the history. Cruelty has been done in Christ's name, and it should be named for what it is. But the Inquisition contradicts the teaching it claimed to serve. Judge the faith by Mother Teresa kneeling in Calcutta, not by those who betrayed it.
The real question
So two things get untangled at once. What the word means, and who actually counts as a Christian. Only then can the charge be tested rather than simply felt.
The theory you will hear
Look at the Crusades and the Inquisition. That is your religion. Those were real, and they were wrong. But a teacher is not judged by the student who ignores him. Measured against what Jesus actually taught, the cruelty is the betrayal, not the faith.
✓
Before you accept the charge, ask what intolerant means and who is being judged. Most of the heat is in the undefined word.
Second / The meaning
Step 2 of 4
Tolerance presupposes disagreement

You only tolerate what you disagree with.

Here is the twist most people miss. Tolerance only makes sense when you think the other view is wrong and bear with it anyway.

The modern myth
Today people assume tolerance means accepting all views as equally true. Because Christians will not say every belief is true, they get charged with intolerance. But that definition is brand new, and it quietly cancels itself.
The older meaning
Historically, to tolerate has meant to put up with what you find disagreeable or false. You cannot put up with something you already agree with. Real tolerance needs a real difference of view to be tolerant about.
So who is tolerant
The sceptic who calls a Christian intolerant is, in that very moment, refusing to accept the Christian's belief as legitimate. So which side is the more tolerant? The charge boomerangs on the one who throws it.
The theory you will hear
A truly tolerant person treats every belief as equally valid. Then no one alive is tolerant, including you. The moment you say my view is mistaken, you are doing what you accuse the Christian of. Tolerance was never about agreeing. It was about how you treat people you think are wrong.
✓
Disagreeing with a belief is the very thing tolerance is built for. Calling that intolerant empties the word of meaning.
Third / Humility
Step 3 of 4
Conviction is not the same as arrogance

Claiming truth is not a boast.

The deeper worry is that saying I have the truth must mean I think I am better than you. The gospel says the opposite.

Beggars and bread
Christianity does not run on the idea that believers are smarter or finer than anyone else. It runs on grace. A Christian is simply one beggar who has been given bread, telling other beggars where the bread is found. There is nothing to boast in.
It condemns pride
Arrogance is not a side effect the faith tolerates. It is something the faith openly condemns. The whole logic is unearned gift received with open hands, which leaves no room for looking down on anyone.
Levels of tolerance
Tolerance also works at different levels. What a church asks of its own members is not what the law asks of a citizen. Paul could call for a man to be disciplined inside the church for adultery without anyone proposing he be jailed for it. Correcting your own is not the same as punishing the public.
The theory you will hear
Saying you have the only true way is the height of arrogance. Only if truth is something you earned. If a dying man is handed the cure and passes it on, that is not pride, it is honesty and care. The Christian claims to have received something, not to have achieved it.
✓
A faith that calls its people beggars and condemns pride is not built on superiority. Conviction and humility can sit together.
Fourth / The person
Step 4 of 4
Love and conviction can coexist

Reject the view, honour the person.

You can be fully convinced someone is wrong and still treat them with genuine warmth. We do it every day. The faith insists on it.

Two different things
Rejecting a belief and disrespecting a person are not the same act. You can think a friend is mistaken about something that matters and still hold them in real regard. Disagreement is not contempt.
Made in God's image
There is a reason the warmth is not optional. Every person bears the image of God, which means each one is owed honour whatever they believe. So far as it depends on you, live at peace with everyone (Romans 12:18).
Is it actually true
Which leaves the one question that matters. Not whether the claim is exclusive, but whether it is true. And this faith does not hide in private feeling. It stands on a public, testable claim. Did the historical Jesus rise from the dead? You can investigate it.
The theory you will hear
Faith is just a private preference, so keep it to yourself. A preference would be private. A claim about whether a man rose from the dead in history is not. It is either true or it is not, and it can be examined. The honest move is not to silence the claim but to weigh it.
✓
Loving the person while rejecting the belief is not a contradiction. The real question was never exclusivity. It was truth.
Putting it together
The bottom line

The charge does not survive the definition.

So is Christianity intolerant? Only if tolerance means never thinking anyone is wrong, which would convict everyone who has ever held a view. On the older, honest meaning, the faith bears with those it disagrees with, calls its own people beggars rather than betters, and asks you to honour the person while you weigh the claim (Romans 12:18). The question worth your time is not whether the claim is exclusive. It is whether it is true.
