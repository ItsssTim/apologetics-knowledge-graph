The Canaanite conquest & the Joshua “genocide”

The question

Did God command genocide? In Deuteronomy 20 and Joshua he orders Israel to “completely destroy” the Canaanites — “do not leave alive anything that breathes.” How can an all-good God command the slaughter of an entire people, including the children?

This is Richard Dawkins’ signature charge and the centrepiece of the New Atheist case that the God of the Old Testament is a “moral monster.” Unlike most apologetic nodes, the richest treatment in the corpus comes from a hostile platform: WLC’s 2024 cross-examination by Alex O’Connor, framed as a “right of reply” to Dawkins’ refusal to debate him.

The objection is unusual in that the defenders concede it is genuinely hard. WLC repeatedly grants O’Connor’s strongest intuitions — the human-initiative version “may well have been immoral,” the children are “the difficult aspect,” the scene “sounds” repugnant — and defends not the comfort of the conclusion but its logical consistency.

The convergent defence — a cumulative argument tree

The defence is layered, not a single move. WLC’s signature is to narrow the question from “is it true / did it happen” to “is it internally consistent” — recasting the conquest as a species of the problem of evil, then proving consistency step by step.

Step 1
Wickedness + a 400-year warning — the judgment was deserved and delayed.

Pre-Israelite Canaan practised temple prostitution, bestiality and child sacrifice, corroborated by extra-biblical ANE literature (WLC’s colleague Clay Jones’ study). God tolerated it for 400 years while Israel was in Egypt — positive evidence that opportunity to repent was given; “no warning was sent” is dismissed as an argument from silence. Cliffe Knechtle independently runs the same judgment-on-child-sacrifice line (Lev 18:21, 24) and adds the non-favouritism point: God judged Israel by the same standard, into Assyrian and Babylonian captivity. (Q-14.3.5)

Step 2
Drive out, not annihilate — the command was expulsion; flight meant survival.

The judgment targeted the clans as nation-states, divesting them of the land, not individuals to be hunted down. No one had to die; those who fled were spared; only those who stayed to resist were destroyed. Textual support: Canaanites still appear later in the narrative, so they were not all killed. WLC concludes it is not even attempted genocide. (Q-14.3.4)

Step 3 · core
Divine Command Theory — God commands, and God wrongs no one.

The load-bearing move. On DCT: the good is grounded in God’s own loving, just nature; moral duties arise from imperatives issued by a qualified authority (the policeman analogy); God issues no commands to himself, so has no duties and cannot do wrong; therefore acts prohibited to us may be open to God, who “takes and gives life as he wills.” The two propositions the skeptic calls inconsistent — God is all-good and God commanded the killing — are consistent, because in issuing the command God wrongs no one. (Q-14.3.16)

Step 4
The children go immediately to heaven — the hard case dissolves.

God may end any life prematurely (children die in infancy all the time), and on WLC’s theology these children go straight to a life “more glorious and more happy than anything conceivable.” So far from being wronged, they are blessed. He concedes it “sounds” repugnant and relocates the repugnance to the hearer’s naturalism, and bites the abortion-parity bullet: abortion is wrong not because it harms the victim but because it transgresses a divine command. (Q-14.3.17)

Step 5
The Israeli soldiers are the only plausibly-wronged party — and even they are not wronged.

Having ruled out adults (corrupt) and children (heaven-bound), WLC’s ironic twist is that the one candidate is the soldiers themselves — the brutalising, PTSD-inducing horror of killing a terrified woman and her children. But there are goods achieved such that God has morally sufficient reasons. Atheist philosopher Daniel Came is cited conceding the children’s case is “entirely reasonable.” (Q-14.3.18)

Step 6 · cap
Worst case, this touches biblical inerrancy — not God’s existence.

Even if the entire defence fails, the conquest is not an objection to God or to the moral argument; at most it touches inerrancy. A non-inerrantist can say the story is legendary, or that Israel’s nationalistic fervour wrongly attributed the command to God. WLC credits Dawkins with grasping this, and bounds the blast radius so the objection cannot reach natural theology. (Q-14.3.19)

Supporting line — McLatchie’s omniscience / Flight 93 analogy (Q-14.3.3). On a separate platform, Jonathan McLatchie adds two less-cited moves: God sent hornets and angels ahead of Israel to reduce the Canaanite population gradually (Exodus 23, 33); and Peter J. Williams’ Flight 93 scenario — pilots ordered to ram the plane would kill innocents intentionally, and the only difference between “hero” and “moral monster” is foreknowledge. Since God is omniscient, moral critiques of his actions face an information gap.

The in-house fork — DCT/expulsion vs the hyperbole reading

The single most important methodological feature of the node. There is a live intra-evangelical disagreement about how to defuse the conquest, and WLC plants his flag firmly on one side of it.

WLC’s route

Divine Command Theory + expulsion frame

Treats the commands as actually given and as literally meaning what they say about killing, then argues they are morally justified because God wrongs no one. The text is defended, not softened.

The route WLC declines

The hyperbolic-conquest reading

Paul Copan, K. Lawson Younger and Nicholas Wolterstorff hold that “utterly destroy / leave alive nothing that breathes” is stock ANE military rhetoric — hyperbole, not a literal order to slaughter non-combatants. WLC does not use it.

Why this matters — the decisive datapoint

O’Connor presses the Deuteronomy 20 language directly (“do not leave alive anything that breathes”) and cites Joshua 8 / Ai chapter-and-verse — exactly where a Copan-style defender reaches for hyperbole first. WLC declines it, defending instead on the expulsion frame plus an “aftermath of a pitched battle / armed resistance” distinction (the pursuit at Ai eliminated retreating armed forces, categorically different from civilians vacating the land). It is rare to catch two leading evangelical defenders giving incompatible accounts of what the conquest text even commands, on the record. This is the high-value evidence for the Copan/WLC methodological-disagreement node.

Objections handled — O’Connor’s cross-examination, in sequence

Objection 1

“Completely destroy… leave alive nothing that breathes” is extermination language, not expulsion

Deut 20 and Joshua 8 / Ai are read aloud, chapter-and-verse. The commanded language is annihilation, not “drive out.”

O’Connor, citing the text

Response

Expulsion frame + “aftermath of battle”

The Ai killings were the aftermath of a pitched battle against an armed resistance, not slaughter of civilians who fled. Contested: WLC concedes no ground but never reconciles the literal Deut 20 wording with “drive out,” and declines the hyperbole escape.

WLC expulsion frame

Objection 2

“You had a choice” is no defence

A foreign nation telling natives to leave or be killed, then saying “I gave you a choice,” just relocates the coercion.

O’Connor

Response

Concession, then pivot to DCT

WLC concedes the human-initiative version “may well have been immoral” and pivots: the divine command changes the act’s moral status. The defence stands or falls on DCT, by his own admission.

WLC Divine Command Theory

Objection 3

Surely not literally everyone was corrupt

Hard to believe not one innocent person remained such that the killing is justified.

O’Connor

Response

Narrows the problem to the children

The corruption claim attaches to the adults (an innocent adult could flee); the children are handled separately by Step 4.

WLC adult / child split

Objection 4

Then child-killing isn’t intrinsically wrong

If killing children is commandable, the very moral intuition the moral argument relies on collapses.

O’Connor, via Randal Rauser

Response

Wrong absent a command; overridden by one

The act is wrong in the absence of a command; God can override that by issuing one, which becomes the duty of those addressed. Intuition affirmed, then qualified — coherent on DCT, but see the deeper-arbitrariness gap.

WLC override structure

Objection 5

Calling the children’s deaths a “blessing” is repugnant

In any other context this is morally revolting; O’Connor says he understands why Dawkins finds it so.

O’Connor, channeling Dawkins

Response

Repugnance is a naturalist’s reflex

It only sounds repugnant if one denies an afterlife; on theism a child going to incomprehensible glory genuinely is blessed. “Modern man doesn’t believe that.” Bullet bitten openly.

WLC think-vs-feel

Objection 6

The disposal problem

If the innocent go to heaven anyway, “they go to heaven” can excuse any slaughter of innocents — it works for the adults too.

O’Connor

Response — not fully defused

Re-defers to the command requirement

WLC reasserts that the action requires a divine command (“don’t go kill children” still stands) and that the children’s case is about whether they are wronged, not a licence. The structural worry is left dangling — he concedes the logical entailment and blocks only the practical inference.

Partial handling

Objection 7

Abortion parity

Your view implies abortion confers a great good on the child — eternal life — rather than harming it.

O’Connor

Response

Bites the bullet

Abortion robs ~50–60 finite years but gives eternal joy that far outweighs the loss; it is wrong because it transgresses a divine command against homicide, not because it harms the victim. A counterintuitive entailment accepted rather than dodged.

WLC command-grounded wrongness

Objection 8

Moral-skepticism / Andrea Yates parallel

If foreknowledge can justify killing innocents, what blocks a mother drowning her children “to send them to heaven”?

O’Connor (implicit); invited by McLatchie’s omniscience move

Response

Only God may issue the overriding command

The divine-command prohibition on homicide blocks the human from acting; only God may issue the command that overrides it. Blocks the practical inference while conceding the logical entailment that the killing was a good for the victim.

WLC command-prohibition

Objection 9

The animals

God orders the cattle, sheep and donkeys killed too (Joshua 6), and punishes failure to kill them. Animals cannot “flee” as a nation, nor go to heaven.

O’Connor — raised twice

Gap — never addressed

No response anywhere in the corpus

The cleanest unanswered objection in the whole exchange. It pressures both the expulsion frame and the children-to-heaven move, and WLC engages it not at all.

Unaddressed gap

Objection 10

Saying the soldiers were the real victims is itself repugnant

To describe the slaughter of innocent children and then say the people who “really suffered” were the soldiers jumps off the page with moral repugnance.

O’Connor

Response

Think, not feel

“A philosopher is required to say what he thinks, not how he feels.” Framed as dispassionate metaethics about who is wronged — which sidesteps, rather than answers, the moral-residue worry.

WLC think-vs-feel reframe

Objection 11

Child sacrifice lacks archaeological support

Molek denotes a sacrifice-type, not a deity, and the Israelites may have practised it to Yahweh; the Carthage/Punic evidence is first-millennium, not the second-millennium narrative setting.

Josh Bowen (revisionist scholar), vs Knechtle

Response — contested premise

Holds the narrative reading; gap not engaged

Knechtle defends the biblical-narrative reading and the non-favouritism point but does not engage the second-millennium-evidence gap directly. This pressures the depravity premise (Step 1).

Knechtle narrative readingpremise contested

Objection 12

No warning was sent (unlike Nineveh)

There is no record of any messenger sent to warn the Canaanites of impending judgment, as God sent to Nineveh.

Listener question, Defenders Q&A

Response

Argument from silence

The 400-year delay implies opportunity to repent; the narrator simply wasn’t recording events back in Canaan. A burden-shift that asserts, rather than proves, warnings were given.

WLC silence rebuttal

Gap 3

DCT’s deeper arbitrariness worry is asserted away

WLC’s escape from arbitrariness (“the constraint is God’s own good nature”) is stated, but the Euthyphro structure is never walked through. More pointedly: the proof turns on “God wrongs no one,” yet wronging is itself a moral concept — on a strict DCT where God has no obligations, God wronging someone may be incoherent, not merely false. A non-DCT standard may be smuggled in.
