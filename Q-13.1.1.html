The question

What does Scripture mean when it says human beings are made in the image of God — and what hangs on it?

This is the foundational claim of Christian anthropology. It answers “what is a human being?” before any sub-question — the soul, the fall, sexual difference, the historical Adam, bioethics — can be posed. Almost every downstream commitment in §13 inherits its weight from here.

The doctrine is load-bearing in two directions at once. Looking up, it grounds the moral argument (§1): if humans bear God’s image, they have intrinsic, non-conferred worth. Looking down, it grounds the sanctity-of-life ethics of §15 — Genesis 9:6 makes the image the rationale for treating the shedding of human blood as uniquely grave — and frames the historical-Adam question of who counts as an image-bearer (Q-13.4.x).

A deliberate division of labour runs through the corpus: this node establishes that humans are imaged, in what vocabulary, and what it secures. The separate question of what the image consists in — rational soul, relationship, or function — is held over to Q-13.1.2.

The convergent doctrine — three evidence streams

Q-13.1.1 is not a syllogism. It is a convergent doctrinal case: three experts, working in different registers — systematic exegesis, university apologetic, street-level evangelism — arrive at the same load-bearing claim from three independent streams that reinforce rather than entail one another.

Stream A · The biblical data

  • selem / demut — image & likeness (Gen 1:26–27)
  • eikon (1 Cor 11:7) / homoiosis (Jas 3:9)
  • Two Testaments, one category
  • Image = likeness (Hebrew parallelism)
  • Recurs: Gen 5:1, 5:3, 9:6

Stream B · What it secures

  • Intrinsic, non-conferred worth
  • The historical foundation of Western morality
  • Sanctity of life (Gen 9:6)
  • “Far more important than the sun”
  • The incarnation as the capstone marker

Stream C · Scope & durability

  • Male and female equally imaged (Gen 1:27)
  • Retained though marred by the fall
  • Universal & cross-culturally portable
  • Indelible worth regardless of capacity
  • Irreplaceable by humanism

How each expert deploys it

William Lane Craig2 transcripts
The biblical-data anchor — data before system
Signature deployment
  • Data before system — refuses to say what the image consists in until every text is on the table; reserves the construals for Q-13.1.2.
  • Anchors the locus classicus (Gen 1:26–27) on two Hebrew terms: selem (image, “a resemblance or a literal image”) and demut (likeness).
  • Reads selem / demut as Hebrew parallelism — one reality, not two faculties. Quietly pre-commits against the Catholic two-tier reading and sides with the Reformers.
  • Corroborating OT data: Gen 5:1; Gen 5:3 (Seth fathered “in his own likeness, after his image”); Gen 9:6 (the sanctity-of-life rationale).
  • NT data: eikon (1 Cor 11:7) and homoiosis (Jas 3:9) — the category carries into both Testaments.
  • Christological terminus: man created in God’s image → Christ is God’s image → man in Christ conformed to the image of Christ.
Aggregator caveat

Craig cites 1 Cor 11:7 flatly as eikon attestation but does not engage the verse’s asymmetry (“but woman is the glory of man”). The corpus answers the male-and-female-equally-imaged worry from Gen 1:27, not from 1 Cor 11 — the creation text governs the Pauline argument-from-order.

John Lennox2 transcripts
The dignity-and-incarnation apologetic
Signature moves
  • Scale-asymmetry (the signature): “The universe isn’t made in his image … You were … you’re far more important than the sun … You know it’s there. It doesn’t know you’re there.”
  • Imago Dei as the historical foundation of Western morality — the explicit bridge to the moral argument (§1); dignity grounded, not asserted.
  • The incarnation as capstone: “A human being is the kind of creature that God can become.” The ceiling of human worth is God’s willingness to assume our nature.
  • 2026 escalation against transhumanism — three concentric limits AI cannot cross: quailia, values, relationship with God. AI’s rise therefore raises the apologetic value of the doctrine.
  • Concession-then-limit: grants GPT’s John 11 output is “absolutely brilliant,” then locates the hard limit at consciousness — “it’s not conscious. So it’s not aware.”

The image as intellectual & moral capacity
Distinctive move
  • Phenomenological opener: “what is it that’s looking at you?” — the subject that sees, interprets, thinks, feels and wills is what Christians have called the soul.
  • Locates the image in shared properties: self-consciousness, abstract reasoning, creative imagination, and moral freedom — “your mind shares some of the properties of God.”
  • Identifies soul with mind (Aristotle and “most traditional Christian thought”); the “intellectual soul” marks human from animal.
  • Modernising aside: analogises angels — other beings with intellectual souls — to extraterrestrials.

The cultural-durability / genealogical argument
Distinctive deployment
  • Holland / Ferry genealogy: being made in the image of a triune God grounds inherent worth — the conviction that historically abolished slavery and sustains care for the disabled.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

“Image” and “likeness” name two different things

Genesis uses two words because they mark two aspects of human nature — natural reason (image) and a supernatural gift (likeness) that can be lost. A two-tier anthropology.

Traditional Roman Catholic reading

Strongest response

It is Hebrew parallelism — one reality, not two

In this Hebrew text there is “probably no difference between these two”; selem and demut describe the single man–God relation twice over, not two faculties. The full Catholic–Protestant dispute is laid out at Q-13.1.2.

Craig parallelism reading

Objection 2

1 Cor 11:7 implies woman is imaged only derivatively

Paul writes that “man … is the image and glory of God, but woman is the glory of man” — so only the male directly bears the image.

Standard exegetical / egalitarian worry

Strongest response

Genesis 1:27 governs the Pauline argument-from-order

“Male and female he created them” — both sexes equally bear the image in the creation text; the systematic reading subordinates Paul’s contextual argument-from-order to it. Reconstructed from the data; no expert states this as a formal rebuttal.

Craig NT eikon data (asymmetry not engaged)

Objection 3

The image was lost at the fall

If humanity fell, the image of God was forfeited — so fallen human beings no longer bear it, and the dignity claim collapses for everyone after Eden.

Implicit theological worry

Strongest response

The image is retained though marred

Genesis 9:6 grounds the post-fall sanctity-of-life command precisely because murderer and victim alike still bear the image; Seth is fathered in Adam’s image after the fall. The image is dimmed, not erased.

Craig Gen 9:6 + Seth data

Objection 4

Humanism grounds dignity just as well

You don’t need the image of God to affirm human worth — secular humanism delivers universal dignity without any theology.

Secular-humanist interlocutor

Strongest response

Humanism “does not travel well”

Stuart Knechtle: humanism failed to produce the abolition and anti-infanticide reforms Christianity did, and clusters culturally in white Western settings. “Being the image of God … is way more durable” (Holland / Ferry genealogy).

Stuart Knechtle cultural-durability case

Objection 5

AI / transhumanism can replicate or surpass the human

If intelligence and capability can be engineered, the human is just an early prototype — AI will match and then exceed everything the “image” was supposed to name.

Transhumanist vision

Strongest response

Three limits AI cannot cross

Lennox: quailia (first-person experience), values (right and wrong), and relationship with God. “No utopia can ever be built without facing the problem of human sin and rebellion against God” — concepts that mean nothing to a machine. The limit is consciousness, not capability.

Lennox quailia / values / God stack

Objection 6

The universe is vast; humans are insignificant

Against the scale of the cosmos, a human being is a speck. Cosmic magnitude makes any claim of special human worth look like vanity.

Cosmic-insignificance intuition

Strongest response

Knowing-consciousness outranks magnitude

Lennox: the universe shows God’s glory but is not made in his image — you are. “You’re far more important than the sun … You know it’s there. It doesn’t know you’re there.” A single image inverts the insignificance intuition.

Lennox scale-asymmetry

Objection 7

Imago Dei smuggles theism into a neutral question

“You said nothing that had anything to do with us actually having a soul … no argument for the existence of a soul. You’ve jumped to your apologetics for a failed belief system.” The dignity case presupposes the very theism in dispute.

Aron Ra (vs Stuart Knechtle)

Gap — largely unaddressed

Never returned to

Stuart pivots to the cultural-durability case and never shows the image / soul exists independent of Christian premises. The corpus argues from theism to dignity, not from the image to theism — so the charge of question-begging stands unmet.

Aggregation gap

Persistent gaps in the corpus

Gap 1

The soul / image-exists-at-all charge

The cultural-durability argument shows the doctrine’s fruitfulness, not its truth. Aron Ra’s “you jumped to apologetics” objection is never met.

Why it matters

No expert here runs an argument from the existence of the image to theism — only the reverse. A hostile interlocutor can grant every social-fruit claim and still deny the doctrine is true.

Unresolved

Gap 2

The 1 Cor 11:7 asymmetry is never formally resolved

Craig cites the verse as plain eikon attestation and moves on; “but woman is the glory of man” is left hanging. Equal imaging of male and female is asserted from Gen 1:27, not defended against the counter-text.

Why it matters

This is the standard objection at this node. The reconstruction (creation text governs Paul) is sound but belongs to no expert in the corpus — a genuine coverage hole.

Unresolved

Gap 3

The content of the image is deferred, not argued

Only Ward’s rationalist construal is stated; it is never weighed against the relational and functional views. A structural deferral to Q-13.1.2, not a failure — but a reader arriving for “what the image is” leaves empty-handed.

Why it matters

The construals are where the doctrine does its most contested work (e.g. who counts as a bearer). Routing them entirely to Q-13.1.2 keeps this node clean but thins its standalone usefulness.

Deferred to Q-13.1.2

Gap 4

No engagement with the image-bearer boundary

Neanderthals, hominins, the severely cognitively impaired — who counts as an image-bearer is never addressed here. Craig’s behavioural-markers criterion (from the historical-Adam lesson) bears on it but is not connected.

Why it matters

If the image is tied to capacities (Ward), the boundary question is acute for those lacking them — and is exactly where the sanctity-of-life payoff (§15) is most tested. Belongs to Q-13.4.x / Q-13.1.5.

Routed to Q-13.4.x

Gap 5

Tom Holland’s argument is invoked but not exegeted

Stuart names the genealogy of Christian human-rights vocabulary as his structural anchor but never works through it. The historical claim (Christianity as the source of our dignity intuitions) is asserted, not demonstrated.

Why it matters

The genealogical argument is the corpus’s strongest consequentialist case for the doctrine, yet it rests on an un-shown historical premise — the easiest point for an interlocutor to contest.

Unresolved
