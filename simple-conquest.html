The hardest Old Testament question

Did God command genocide in the Old Testament?

The conquest of Canaan in Joshua, where Israel is told to clear the land, is the passage sceptics reach for first, and not without reason. It is genuinely confronting. So rather than soften it, here is what it actually was, why it happened, and the part that stays hard.

First / What it was
Step 1 of 4
Drive out, or wipe out?

The command was to drive them out, not wipe them out.

Start with what the texts actually say, because the popular picture, a divine order to exterminate a race, is not quite what sits on the page.

Drive out
As William Lane Craig stresses, the core command was to drive the Canaanite peoples out of the land, not to hunt down a race. Those who fled the advancing army were spared; the fighting fell on those who stayed to resist. The judgment was on these clans as nations holding the land, and the aim was that none remain in the land, not that a bloodline be erased.
Survivors
And the texts themselves show it was not a literal extermination. Canaanites keep turning up later in the very same story, after they have supposedly been wiped out (Judges 1:27-36). You cannot annihilate a people and then keep running into them a few chapters on. The total-destruction reading does not even fit the book it comes from.
The language
That is partly because phrases like destroy everything that breathes were standard war rhetoric across the ancient world. Scholars like Paul Copan point out that kings of the day routinely boasted of totally annihilating enemies who plainly lived on. It was the hyperbole of the battlefield, the ancient version of we crushed them, not a literal head count.
The theory you'll hear
But the text literally says leave nothing alive that breathes. It does, and that is exactly the sweeping war language the ancient world traded in, the way a coach today says we destroyed them with nobody dead. The proof is in the text itself: the people supposedly left with nothing breathing are alive and active later in the same story. The wording is fierce. The reality was expulsion, and the defeat of armies that stood and fought.
✓
Confidence: the command was to dispossess and drive out, not to exterminate a race. The survivors in the text, and the war rhetoric of the age, both rule out the literal-genocide reading.
Second / Why judgment?
Step 2 of 4
A horrific culture, centuries of patience

It was judgment on a monstrous culture, not a land grab.

Even as a bounded judgment, why this at all? The answer the Bible gives is that this fell on entrenched, horrific evil, and only after extraordinary patience.

The wickedness
These were not peaceful villagers. Canaanite religion practised child sacrifice, burning their own children to death in worship, alongside temple prostitution and bestiality. This is not only the Bible's claim. Ancient documents from the region, studied by scholars like Clay Jones, describe practices so grim the Bible does not even spell them out. It is judged, as Craig frames it, much as the world of Noah's flood was judged.
400 years
And God did not act on a hair trigger. Genesis says he held back for around four hundred years, because, in the text's own words, the wickedness was not yet complete (Genesis 15:16). That is not bloodlust; it is four centuries of room to change course, the very same long patience he would later show, and eventually run out of, with Israel itself.
No favourites
Which is the other half of it. Israel got no free pass. When Israel later sank into the same evils, God swept them into exile at the hands of Assyria and Babylon. As Knechtle puts it, do not imagine the Israelites were God's pets. The standard was identical for everyone. This is judgment on conduct, not favouritism for a race.
The theory you'll hear
That's just the winners smearing the people they wiped out. It would be, if the Bible were the only source. But the depravity turns up in the Canaanites' own surviving literature, dug up by archaeologists, not written by Israel. And the giveaway that this is about behaviour, not bloodline, is that the very same God turned the very same judgment on his own chosen people the moment they did the same things.
✓
Confidence: this was judgment on a genuinely monstrous culture, corroborated from outside the Bible, after four centuries of patience, and applied even-handedly to Israel itself.
Third / The children
Step 3 of 4
The part that stays hard

The hardest part is the children. It deserves honesty.

Even granting all of that, the genuinely hard piece remains: children would have died. No serious answer waves that away, so here are the two things Christianity actually says, slowly.

The hard part
This is where even careful defenders stop and concede the difficulty. Among the adults, the corrupt could flee and the innocent did not have to die. The children are the real weight of the objection, and pretending otherwise would be dishonest. Hold the difficulty here; do not let anyone, on either side, rush you past it.
Whose life
The first thing Christianity says is about who God is. If he is the author and giver of every life, he is not a peer murdering his equal; he is the one from whom all life comes and to whom it returns. When a human takes life, it is theft of what was never theirs. The giver of life is simply not in that same position, however uncomfortable that is to sit with.
Not the end
The second thing is that, on the Christian view, death is not annihilation. Children who die before they could be morally accountable are received by God, not erased. That does not make their death small, but it changes what it means. Under atheism a dead child is simply gone. On the Christian account, that child is safe, and the story is not over for them.
The theory you'll hear
Nothing could ever justify a God who lets children die. And yet children die in every flood, famine and disease, on anyone's worldview; the agony of innocent death is not unique to this story, it is the whole problem of suffering. What is unique to Christianity is the claim that those children are not lost, that the God who allows the death is also the one who gathers them in, and who later steps into innocent suffering himself.
✓
Confidence: the children are the genuinely hard part, and it is not dodged. The Christian answer is that the giver of life holds rights we do not, and that for those children death was not the end.
Fourth / Stepping back
Step 4 of 4
A one-off, and the outrage itself

It was a one-off, and the outrage proves a point.

Finally, two things that change how the whole question sits: one about the event, and one about the person asking it.

Not a blueprint
This was a unique, bounded moment, a specific judgment on a specific culture at one point in history. It is reported, not commanded for all time. Nowhere does it license anyone today to take up a sword in God's name, and the New Testament pointedly does the opposite. Anyone who uses these texts to justify violence now is abusing them, and Christians should say so loudly.
Your standard
And notice what the objection quietly assumes. To be genuinely outraged that this was evil, you have to believe there is a real, objective right and wrong, binding even on God. But that universal moral law is exactly what points back to God in the first place. The depth of the outrage you feel is borrowing the very thing the objection is trying to argue away.
The cross
Last, the same God who judges does not stay safely above the suffering. The Bible's whole storyline runs to a cross, where God takes the judgment onto himself rather than dealing it out. Fyodor Dostoevsky wrestled bitterly with exactly these Old Testament passages, and what finally answered him was not a neat argument but the sight of God crucified.
The theory you'll hear
This is just damage control for an embarrassing text. It is the opposite of a cover-up. Christianity does not hide these chapters; it prints them and hands them to you to wrestle with. What it asks is that you read them as what they are, a bounded judgment on a monstrous culture, not a charter for cruelty, and that you notice where the same God ends up: not on the throne of a war camp, but on a cross.
✓
Confidence: the conquest was a one-time judgment, never a standing command, and the moral outrage it stirs quietly assumes the very God it questions. The story ends not in conquest, but at the cross.
