A fair question worth facing

Isn't God egotistical to demand our worship?

It sounds like a vain ruler fishing for applause. Picture any human who insisted everyone praise him, and you would rightly call him insecure. The objection only holds if God is one being among many. The whole shape of it changes once you ask what worship actually is and who it is for.

First / The definition
Step 1 of 4
What humility actually is

Humility is seeing yourself truly.

We treat humility as thinking little of yourself. It is not. It is an honest read of who you really are.

The real test
A humble person is not the one who underrates themselves. It is the one who sees themselves accurately, without inflation and without false modesty. Even Paul could say his conscience was clear yet that this did not make him innocent (1 Corinthians 4:4).
The one exception
If you are the greatest and most worthy being there is, then an honest self-assessment will say so. For a human that is delusion. For God it is simply accurate. He cannot point to a higher standard than himself, because there is not one.
Not boasting
This is not bragging in the human sense. Paul refused to boast in himself and boasted only in the cross (Galatians 6:14). Truthful speech, even glad speech about something real, is not the same as conceit (2 Corinthians 7:14).
The theory you will hear
Calling yourself the greatest is the very definition of arrogance. For anyone who is not, yes. Arrogance is a gap between the claim and the truth. Where there is no gap, where the being really is supreme, the claim is not pride. It is honesty about reality.
✓
Pride is overrating yourself. A being who is genuinely supreme is not overrating anything by saying so.
Second / The purpose
Step 2 of 4
Who the command serves

Worship is for you, not for him.

A vain king demands praise to fill an emptiness in himself. The command to worship runs the other way.

Made for it
We are made in his image and built with eternity set in our hearts (Ecclesiastes 3:11). Worship is not a tax he levies. It is the activity we were designed for, the thing that finally fits.
Lining up
To worship God is to see reality the right way up and respond to it honestly. To refuse is to live out of step with the way things actually are, which damages the worshipper, not the one declined.
The broken swap
When people turn from the spring of living water, they dig their own cracked cisterns that hold nothing (Jeremiah 2:13). We will worship something. Aiming it at what is worthy is what keeps us whole.
The theory you will hear
A loving God would not need us bowing to him at all. Correct, and he does not. The command is not for his benefit but for ours, the way a good parent insists a child eat well. It directs us toward the one good large enough to satisfy us, rather than the cisterns that leave us empty.
✓
He commands worship the way health is prescribed, not the way a tyrant extracts tribute. The benefit lands on us.
Third / The need
Step 3 of 4
Why this is not insecurity

A God who needs nothing.

Ego is hunger. The insecure crave applause because they lack something. God lacks nothing at all.

Self-sufficient
Everything that exists comes from him, through him and returns to him (Romans 11:34-36). The whole earth already belongs to him (Psalm 50:1). He gains nothing from praise that he did not already possess.
Joy overflowing
C. S. Lewis noticed that we spontaneously praise whatever we enjoy, and that the praise is not flattery added on. It is enjoyment reaching its full height. Praise does not feed God. It completes our delight in him.
Stooping low
Far from craving status, the high and holy God chooses to dwell with the lowly and crushed (Isaiah 57:15), stooping even to look on the heavens and the earth (Psalm 113:5-6).
The theory you will hear
Wanting praise at all is proof of insecurity. Only when you need it. Think of a great piece of music. Withholding praise from it does not punish the music, it shortchanges you. Calling something glorious that truly is glorious is not flattery. It is the natural overflow of having seen it clearly.
✓
A being who needs nothing cannot be feeding an ego. Our praise adds nothing to him and completes everything in us.
Fourth / The evidence
Step 4 of 4
The opposite of vanity

The one who stooped to serve.

If you still suspect ego, look at what this God actually did when he came among us.

He emptied himself
Though he was in the very form of God, he did not cling to it. He emptied himself, took the form of a servant and humbled himself to death on a cross (Philippians 2:6-8). That is the record of the being accused of vanity.
Came to serve
He said plainly that he came not to be served but to serve, and to give his life as a ransom for many (Mark 10:45). He took the place of the one who waits at the table, not the one who reclines (Luke 22:27).
Gentle and lowly
The one who knows the Father fully invites the weary to come to him, describing himself as gentle and humble in heart (Matthew 11:27-29). An egomaniac does not wash feet and die for his accusers.
The theory you will hear
That is just clever branding for the same power grab. A power grab does not end at a public execution between criminals. The cross gains him no applause and no advantage. It is the costliest possible disproof of ego, a supreme being laying down his life for people who could give him nothing in return.
✓
The God charged with arrogance is the one who knelt to serve and died for his critics. Vanity does not behave like that.
Putting it together
The bottom line
Four steps, taken together

Fitting, not flattering.

1 · The definition
Humility is an honest self-assessment. For the one truly supreme being, saying so is accuracy, not pride.
›
2 · The purpose
The command to worship is for us, lining us up with reality. It heals the worshipper rather than serving God.
›
3 · The need
He needs nothing. Praise adds nothing to him. As Lewis saw, it simply completes our own enjoyment of him.
›
4 · The evidence
He emptied himself, served, and died on a cross. The being accused of vanity behaves like its opposite.
›

So is God egotistical to seek worship? Only if he is not really worthy of it. Worship is the fitting response to what is supremely good, the way awe is the fitting response to something magnificent. He commands it for our good, not to fill a need he does not have, and then he proves the point by stooping to serve and to die. Praising what is truly excellent does not flatter him. It completes our joy (Romans 11:34-36).
