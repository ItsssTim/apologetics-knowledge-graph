Sources you can actually examine

Is the New Testament historically reliable?

The worry is fair. These are old documents about miracles, written by believers, and we are asked to take a lot on their word. So treat them the way you would treat any other ancient witness. Ask who wrote them, ask when, ask whether the story holds together under pressure, and ask whether anyone could have checked it. The answers are better than most people expect.

First / The sources
Step 1 of 5
Who is actually behind the page

Eyewitnesses, and a scribe to hold the pen.

Start with the most basic question. Where did this material come from, and does a secretary doing the writing change the answer?

First hand
The writers claim to report what they saw and touched, or careful research from those who did (1 John 1:1) (Luke 1:1-4). John says Jesus promised the Spirit would remind them of what he had taught (John 14:25-26).
A scribe helped
Dictating to a secretary was simply normal in the ancient world. Paul names his scribe Tertius, who openly says he wrote the letter down (Romans 16:22), much as Cicero describes dictating his own correspondence.
Signed by hand
The named author still controlled the content and authenticated it personally. Paul takes the pen for his closing greeting in his own large hand (Galatians 6:11) (1 Corinthians 16:21).
The theory you will hear
If a secretary did the writing, Paul did not really write his letters. A scribe taking dictation is not a ghostwriter inventing the content. Legal papyri of the period were drawn up by scribes yet remained the binding word of the named party. Paul authored, authenticated, and signed. The hand on the page does not own the letter.
✓
The chain runs to people who were there, and a scribe holding the pen never breaks it.
Second / The standard
Step 2 of 5
The test a courtroom would use

Treat them the way you treat a witness.

A long line of senior lawyers and judges have found the Gospel testimony solid. The reason is the standard they apply to evidence every day.

Admissible
Under the ancient documents rule, old records of known origin are received as evidence. The Gospels offer firsthand testimony or careful research (1 John 1:1) (Luke 1:1-4), and would be admitted in a common law court.
Innocent first
Documents, like defendants, are taken as honest until shown otherwise. Critics have tried for two centuries and have not impeached the Gospels. The burden sits with the accuser, and the accusation has not landed.
Cross examined
Hostile witnesses who knew the events were still alive. The historian A. N. Sherwin-White noted the reporting rate for the Jesus of the Gospels is better than for Tiberius Caesar, whose career rests on four sources.
The theory you will hear
Believers wrote it, so of course it backs their man. Then sift it as a court would, where bias is assumed and tested, not waved away. Enemies with the means, the motive and the opportunity to refute the story did not. The Harvard evidence scholar Simon Greenleaf judged that testimony sifted this way reaches, for all practical purposes, the certainty of demonstration.
✓
Judged by the rules used to weigh real testimony, the Gospels are admissible and were never successfully impeached.
Third / The interlock
Step 3 of 5
Honesty visible from the inside

The accounts quietly answer each other.

Sometimes one writer leaves a small gap and another fills it without trying to. The more offhand the fit, the harder it is to fake.

A palace leak
Matthew records Herod speaking privately to his own servants (Matthew 14:1-2). How could a fisherman know that? Luke, elsewhere, mentions that a woman from Herod's household followed Jesus (Luke 8:3).
An odd silence
Luke says Peter, James and John told no one what they saw on the mountain, and never explains why (Luke 9:36). Mark supplies the missing order, that Jesus told them to keep it quiet until after he rose (Mark 9:9).
Pilate's question
John has Pilate abruptly ask Jesus about being a king, with no lead-in. Luke gives the charge that prompts it (Luke 23:1-4), and John later adds the reply that his kingdom is not of this world (John 18:36).
The theory you will hear
These are just the same stories copied between books. Shared wording would prove copying and nothing more. This is the opposite. One account raises a question it never notices, and a separate account answers it in passing. Fiction does not interlock this way, because invented tales are not anchored in one shared reality. When many such links crisscross the narratives, they build a strong cumulative case.
✓
Writers filling each other's gaps by accident are reporting something real, not rehearsing a script.
Fourth / The text
Step 4 of 5
Less time, more copies

Written early, and preserved better than the classics.

Two practical questions decide whether a text survives intact. How close to the events was it written, and how well was it copied down to us?

Early
The first three Gospels come before the fall of Jerusalem in AD 70, and John not long after. That leaves too little time for legend to crowd out memory while eyewitnesses could still object.
Well attested
The text survives in thousands of Greek copies and thousands more in Latin, where prized classical works survive in only a handful. Famous ancient histories sit on far fewer copies, written far longer after the events they record.
It checks out
The cultural detail fits the ground. A Jerusalem pool once doubted by scholars (John 5:2) was confirmed by excavation. The writers knew they were not telling everything, only what mattered (John 21:25) (2 Timothy 3:16-17).
The theory you will hear
The text has been copied so often it cannot be the original. The opposite follows. With thousands of manuscripts to compare, copying errors stand out and get corrected, rather than hiding. We can trust the New Testament far more than the classical works the same scholars cite without a second thought, because the evidence for its wording is richer by a wide margin.
✓
Close to the events and abundantly copied, the New Testament is the best attested document of the ancient world.
Fifth / The forgery charge
Step 5 of 5
The last objection, faced squarely

Were some letters written under a false name?

A serious charge deserves a serious hearing. The claim is that certain letters only pretend to be by Paul. Two findings undo it.

The old claim
Nineteenth century scholars argued the Pastoral letters did not sound like Paul in vocabulary and style, and later extended the doubt to Second Thessalonians and Ephesians. It rested on counting words.
Better testing
Sharper analysis, such as Anthony Kenny in 1986, found no subset of the letters that is genuinely non-Pauline. A writer who uses secretaries and varies his style across years will not read like a single fixed sample.
The verdict
The ancients had blunt words for a fake, a lie, a counterfeit, an illegitimate writing, and they denounced forgers as thieves. When the church found a forgery, such as Third Corinthians, it threw the forgery out.
The theory you will hear
A devoted disciple wrote in Paul's name to honour him, and everyone understood. The ancients did not work that way. Arrian recorded the teaching of Epictetus but signed it as his own. A man who published his father's notebooks put his father's real name on them. No one penned fresh letters under a borrowed name as a compliment. And a writing built on a lie about its own author is a strange place to look for truth. Paul, for his part, signed to prove it was his (2 Thessalonians 3:17).
✓
There is no compelling case that any New Testament document is a forgery, and the ancients themselves would have been the first to say so.

So is the New Testament historically reliable? Examine it the way you would examine any ancient witness and it stands up well. The sources go back to people who were there, the testimony survives cross examination, the accounts confirm each other from the inside, the text reaches us early and intact, and the forgery charge does not hold. You are not asked to believe blindly. You are asked to read the witnesses Luke set out to record in order (Luke 1:1-4), and weigh them.
