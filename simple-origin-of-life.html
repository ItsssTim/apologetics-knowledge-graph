The origin of life

Could life have arisen by chance?

Even if the universe was set up for life, you still have to get life started, and the usual story is that, given a warm pond and enough time, chemistry simply stumbled into the first living thing. It is a confident tale. It also runs into a wall that has only grown higher the more we learn about what a living cell actually is.

First / It's code, not chemicals
Step 1 of 4
What actually has to be explained

It's not chemicals. It's code.

The first surprise is that the famous warm pond was never the hard part. The real problem is of a completely different kind.

Not just chemicals
Getting some basic building blocks, amino acids and the like, to form is relatively easy, and experiments have managed it. But that is like making ink and paper. It is nowhere near making a sentence. The leap from a soup of chemicals to a living cell is not a leap in quantity. It is a leap into a different category altogether.
A living code
Because at the heart of every living cell sits something nobody expected: a digital code. DNA stores instructions in a four-letter chemical alphabet, which the cell reads, copies and translates to build the machinery of life. The simplest cell that can reproduce itself is a miniature information-processing factory of staggering, tightly integrated complexity.
The real question
So the real origin-of-life question was never just where the chemicals came from. It is where the information came from, the coded instructions that turn lifeless molecules into a living, self-copying machine. And information is a very particular kind of thing, with a very particular kind of source.
The theory you'll hear
Life started as simple chemicals in a warm pond, and complexity came later. The warm-pond picture is a relic. The simplest cell that can reproduce is already breathtakingly complex, running on coded information. Coaxing a few amino acids to form was never the hard part. The hard part is the origin of the coded instructions that organise them into a living machine, and no pond writes those on its own.
✓
Confidence: the origin of life is not mainly a problem of chemistry but of information. At the centre of even the simplest cell is a digital code, and the real question is not where the molecules came from but where the coded instructions did.
Second / Chance can't write it
Step 2 of 4
Couldn't chance manage it?

Chance can't write the code.

The instinctive reply is that, given enough time, even wildly unlikely things will eventually happen. It is a comforting thought. The numbers are merciless with it.

The monkeys
The popular version is the monkeys at typewriters: hammer away long enough and you will eventually get the works of Shakespeare. It feels plausible until you actually try it. In real experiments and calculations, random typing never produces even a single line. It generates an ocean of gibberish and stalls there, because meaningful sequences are unimaginably rare among meaningless ones.
The numbers
And the numbers for life are far worse than for a line of Shakespeare. The odds of assembling even one functional protein by chance, let alone the hundreds a minimal cell needs all working together, are so vanishingly small that the whole age of the universe and every atom in it fall short by an astronomical margin. There has simply not been enough time, or enough matter, by orders and orders of magnitude.
Not enough time
This is the quiet flaw in time plus chance will do it. Time is not a creative force; it is just duration, and the resources of the actual universe are finite. For specified, functional information, the probabilistic well runs dry long before you arrive anywhere. Chance is not a small part of the answer here. It is a non-starter.
The theory you'll hear
Given enough time, even very unlikely things happen; the monkeys eventually type Shakespeare. Only in a universe with limitless time and matter, and ours has neither. Run the actual numbers for assembling even one functional protein by chance and the odds are so steep that the entire age and material of the cosmos falls short by a colossal margin. The monkeys never finish the first line. Time is not a magic wand for specified information.
✓
Confidence: the odds against chance assembling the functional information in even the simplest cell are so vast that the whole universe, in age and matter, comes nowhere near enough. Time plus chance is not a creative force; for coded information it is a non-starter.
Third / Information needs a mind
Step 3 of 4
Where information comes from

Information points to a mind.

If chance is ruled out, the question becomes simple and pointed: what is the one thing we actually know of that produces this kind of information?

What makes info
Think of every other case where we can trace functional information to its source: a book, a piece of software, a recipe, a message scratched into a wall. In every single one, the source is a mind. We have never once observed unguided matter, left to itself, write a code or compose an instruction. Information of this kind has exactly one known cause: intelligence.
A real language
And the information in DNA is not a loose metaphor. It is a genuine code, with an alphabet, with rules for stringing the letters together, and with a translation system in the cell that reads it and builds accordingly. Scientists do not just compare it to language for poetry's sake; they use the actual mathematics of information to study it, because that is what it is.
The inference
So the reasonable conclusion, as Stephen Meyer argues, is an inference to the best explanation. We know what writes code: minds do. We have never seen anything else manage it. Faced with the densest information storage known to science, sitting inside every living thing, the explanation that actually fits the evidence is that it too came from a mind.
The theory you'll hear
Calling it design is just god-of-the-gaps, plugging God into what we can't yet explain. No; it is the opposite of a gap argument. It is a positive inference from what we do know: in every other case where we can trace the source of functional information, it comes from a mind, never from unguided matter. Inferring a mind here is the same reasoning that lets an archaeologist tell a carved tool from a plain rock.
✓
Confidence: in every case where we can trace functional information to its source, it comes from a mind, never from unguided matter. DNA is a real code, and the best explanation for it is the same as for every other code we know of: an intelligence behind it.
Fourth / A positive inference
Step 4 of 4
Two things this is not

It's a positive inference, not a gap.

Two objections come fast here, and both miss what is actually being claimed. Clearing them up is the difference between a strong argument and a caricature of it.

Not a gap
First, this is not we cannot explain it, therefore God. That would be a gap argument, and a weak one. The claim is the reverse: we positively know what produces coded information, namely intelligence, and we positively know what does not, namely chance and physical law on their own. The design inference rests on what we have learned, not on what we have yet to learn.
Like an archaeologist
It is the everyday logic of detecting a mind at work. When an archaeologist finds a flint shaped into an arrowhead, she does not invoke a gap; she infers a toolmaker, because she knows the difference between what nature does and what minds do. Reading design in the cell is the very same reasoning, applied to the most sophisticated information system ever found.
Before evolution
Second, this is not a problem that evolution solves. Natural selection can only act on things that already reproduce, and reproduction already requires the coded information in question. So evolution cannot have produced the first information; it presupposes it. The origin of life is a separate, and harder, question than the evolution of life, and it is the one this is about.
The theory you'll hear
Evolution explains how all this complexity gradually built up. It would, if there were already life to evolve, but that is precisely the thing in question. Natural selection cannot even begin until you already have a self-copying cell rich in information, so it cannot be what produced that information in the first place. The origin of life is a different and prior question from the evolution of life, and it is the harder one.
✓
Confidence: the design inference is positive, not a gap, the same reasoning that distinguishes a tool from a rock. And it concerns the origin of life, not its later evolution, since natural selection cannot begin until the coded information already exists.

So could life have arisen by chance? On the evidence, no, and the reason is not a gap in our knowledge but something we have positively learned. Life runs on a code, codes carry information, and in all of human experience information has one known source: a mind. Chance has nowhere near the time or the resources to write it, and evolution cannot be the answer, because it cannot start until the information is already there. The deeper we have looked into the living cell, the less it resembles a lucky accident, and the more it reads like what it is: a message. And behind every message there is a mind.
