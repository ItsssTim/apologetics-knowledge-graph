The question

If Jesus did not rise bodily, what naturalistic theory best explains the data — and does any of them actually work?

This is the defensive half of the resurrection apologetic. Q-3.3.1 through Q-3.3.4 build the positive case — empty tomb, post-mortem appearances, the origin of the disciples’ belief. Q-3.3.5 is the demolition derby: it lines up every rival a sceptic can offer — legend, hallucination, conspiracy / stolen body, swoon, wrong tomb, the unknown twin, and the visionary reading — and tests each against the same body of facts.

The strategic shape is an eliminative inference to the best explanation. The apologist rarely tries to prove the resurrection directly; he argues that once the data is granted, every naturalistic rival fails on explanatory scope, plausibility, or both. McLatchie asserts the candidates are mutually exhaustive — legend, lie, honest mistake, or fact — so eliminating three leaves the fourth. The recurring capstone: hostile scholars, having conceded the alternatives “are a joke,” reach for an unknown identical twin — which functions as a reductio of naturalism itself.

The convergent argument — eliminative IBE

Every expert deploys a version of the same eliminative structure. The cleanest articulation is McLatchie’s four-way exhaustive disjunction: the data has exactly four candidate explanations, three fail, and the fourth stands by elimination.

The disjunction

The data has exactly four candidate explanations: later legend, the disciples lied, the disciples were honestly mistaken, or Jesus really rose.

Observed variants. McLatchie: “these four categories of explanation are mutually exhaustive.” The Knechtles fold swoon and theft into the “legend” and “lie” branches. Craig runs a three-facts → IBE-by-criteria version — weighing each hypothesis by explanatory scope, explanatory power, plausibility, and degree of ad hoc-ness.

Eliminate 1

Legend / myth-development fails: the creed is too early, attestation too dense, and women-as-witnesses is exactly what a legend would suppress.

Supports. The 1 Corinthians 15 creed dates within months of the crucifixion (Huff, via O’Collins). Luke travelled with Paul and had access to living witnesses (McLatchie). The character of Jesus is not scrambled across the four gospels — which the skeptic’s own telephone-game mechanism predicts it should be (Gilson). The gospels read as history, not mythology (Knechtle, C.S. Lewis).

Eliminate 2

Conspiracy / lie fails: multi-party conspiracies break under threat of death, and liars make poor martyrs.

Supports. Conspirators would not have chosen women as their witnesses, nor proclaimed in Jerusalem where producing the corpse would have ended it (McLatchie, citing Saurin). “They died for what they claimed to have seen” (Knechtle). The 9/11 parity actually inverts: those men died for what they thought true, not for a known fabrication.

Eliminate 3

Honest mistake / hallucination fails: the appearances were poly-modal, to groups, over forty days — and could not produce an empty tomb or convert enemies.

Supports. Hallucinations are individual and brief; “one can hardly hallucinate an empty tomb” (McLatchie). A Jewish hallucination would yield assumption to heaven, not resurrection (Craig’s signature Jewish-frame move). The conversions of the hostile James and the persecuting Paul cannot be grief-visions. “There is no such thing as a mass hallucination” (Huff).

Conclusion

Therefore Jesus rose — the resurrection has the greatest explanatory scope, power, and plausibility, and a non-negligible prior given the messianic context.

Capstone. McLatchie and Huff add the Bayesian-prior move (natural theology + messianic trajectory raise the prior); Craig adds religio-historical context as the parity-breaker (“not a bald anomaly”); the Knechtles add the explosive pre-Constantinian growth of the church. The case is abductive, not deductive — its force is comparative.

How each expert deploys it

William Lane Craig 9 transcripts
The four-facts demolition + the Jewish-frame rebuttal
Signature moves
  • Four-facts base (Joseph’s tomb, empty tomb found by women, multiple appearances, origin of belief) → IBE by criteria: scope, power, plausibility, ad hoc-ness. Conclusion via N.T. Wright.
  • The Jewish-frame rebuttal of hallucination — a hallucinated Jesus would appear “in Abraham’s bosom,” yielding belief in assumption, not resurrection. His single most distinctive contribution.
  • The James / Paul conversion problem — you cannot write off the appearance to a hostile brother and a persecuting Pharisee as grief-hallucinations.
  • Swoon = “the theological equivalent of the Flat Earth theory” — Quintilian on the spear-thrust; the JAMA hypovolaemic-shock article.
  • Stolen body / family-theft dismantled by motive, opportunity, time, undisturbed grave-clothes, and conspiracy-decay; “utterly anachronistic.”
  • The unknown-twin theory as a self-undermining reductio, paired with the Pinchas Lapide hostile-witness anchor.
Methodological mismatch

Against Ehrman’s “any naturalistic story beats a miracle on prior probability,” Craig answers with an explanatory-power critique rather than on Ehrman’s own Bayesian terms. Against Law’s UFO/Venus parity and Sinnott-Armstrong’s ice-cream parody he is forced into 30-second or no replies by moderators.

Jonathan McLatchie 8 transcripts
The four-hypothesis dialectic + the ALIVE table
Signature moves
  • The explicit four-way mutually-exhaustive disjunction (myth / lie / honest-mistake / resurrection), run as a comparative table — the structural backbone of the whole node.
  • The ALIVE acrostic: Appearances, Low status of women, Immediate proclamation in Jerusalem, Voluntary suffering, Empty tomb — the resurrection scores on every row.
  • Against conspiracy: the immediate Jerusalem proclamation (Saurin) — preaching at Pentecost “while the eyes of their enemies were yet sparkling with rage.”
  • Against legend: Luke as Paul’s companion with access to “that treasure trove of living witnesses.”
  • The Bayesian-prior turn (vs Blais): concede the framework, contest the priors — the atheist sets P(theism) too low.
  • Critiques Dale Allison’s objective-vision reading as blurring Ascension into Resurrection (with Lydia McGrew).
Cliffe & Stuart Knechtle 12 transcripts
Street-level cumulative case + cost-asymmetry
Signature moves
  • The genre argument: the gospels read as historical narrative, not myth — marked by gratuitous and embarrassing details (153 fish, the cushion, Alexander and Rufus as checkable “footnotes”).
  • The cost-asymmetry / pre-Constantinian growth argument (Stuart) — converts lost reputation, property, family, life; the Dan Brown “Constantine invented it” thesis is empirically false.
  • The Jewish-paradigm-shift argument (Kuhn) — Jews had more hoops to jump through; an individual, mid-history, crucified Messiah was “a joke.”
  • The 9/11 parity inversion (Cliffe) — died for what they were convinced was true, not for a known lie.
  • The Luke 24 anti-hallucination data — the disciples’ first reaction is “we’re delusional”; Jesus answers by eating and being touched.
  • Enemy attestation — the Sanhedrin’s stolen-body rumour concedes there was something to explain.
Known weaknesses

Stuart does not rebut Tom Jump’s rate-of-growth parity (Falun Gong, Mormonism) — he pivots. The pagan-parallel factual errors of “The Inquisitor” (Krishna crucified, virgin-born, trinitarian — all false) go largely uncorrected. Newdow’s George-Washington structural analogy is never engaged on its own terms.

Wes Huff 3 transcripts
The 1 Corinthians 15 creed + the mass-hallucination line
Distinctive moves
  • The pre-Pauline 1 Corinthians 15 creed — death, burial, raised on the third day, appearances to Peter, the Twelve, James, and 500 — dates “within months” (O’Collins).
  • Paul’s verification clause (“some of whom are still alive today”) places the claim in falsifiable space: go talk to them.
  • The knock-down line: “there is no such thing as a mass hallucination” — 500 people cannot share identical content over forty days.
  • Miracles incompatible with honest mistake — against the Howard-Snyder “merely mistaken” objection: “how is he doing those miracles if he was mistaken?”
Caveat / slip

Huff engages only the crude “mass hallucination” caricature, not the sophisticated bereavement-vision case. He also calls Gerald O’Collins “a German scholar”; O’Collins SJ is Australian.

John Lennox 4 transcripts
Hume was wrong — science cannot forbid a resurrection
Distinctive moves
  • Science describes regularities; it cannot forbid an exception. Knowing dead bodies don’t normally rise is precisely what lets us recognise a miracle.
  • The C.S. Lewis hotel-drawer analogy — the missing £50 means “the laws of England have been broken, not the laws of arithmetic.”
  • Christians don’t claim natural processes raised Jesus — God “fed a new event into” the regularities he built. Hume undone by Antony Flew.
  • Exposes atheist unfalsifiability — Atkins admits even seeing the resurrection “before my very eyes” he would call hallucination.
  • Refuses generic dismissal of parity claims (Mormon plates, Mohammed’s moon, Pythagoras): investigate each, weight by stakes.
Lee Strobel 1 transcript
Live-archaeology update on the “no burial” objection
Distinctive move
  • Answers the wrong-tomb / no-burial objection (Crossan, Ehrman): that Romans left crucifixion victims for dogs and birds, so the tomb was “empty” because Jesus was never in it.
  • Rebuts with the Roman Digesta, which permits release of execution victims for burial.
  • Cites the growing archaeological record of buried crucifixion victims — the Yehohanan heel-bone-and-spike find and a recent UK find. “That kind of shoots that down.”
Date-check the data

Strobel dates Yehohanan to “the early ’90s”; the Giv’at ha-Mivtar find was 1968. The “within the last year” second find is almost certainly Fenstanton, Cambridgeshire (2021–22). Treat the argument as stable; the data points are time-stamped.

The naturalistic theories — and why each fails

Theory 1

Legend / myth-development

The story grew over time — Mark has no appearances; later gospels add earthquakes and risen saints; the whole thing parallels pagan dying-and-rising gods.

The Inquisitor (pagan parallels), Alex O’Connor (Cleopas / Proculus), Peter Atkins (“80 years later”)

Strongest response

The character of Jesus is not scrambled

The 1 Cor 15 creed is too early; women-as-witnesses is what a legend would suppress. Gilson’s reductio: the skeptic’s own telephone-game mechanism predicts a scrambled text — yet Jesus and the secondary cast are remarkably consistent across all four. The gospels read as history, not myth.

Gilson not-scrambled reductioHuff / McLatchie early creedKnechtle genre criterion

Theory 2

Pagan parallels — dying-and-rising gods

Krishna, Osiris, Dionysus, Horus were “crucified and resurrected” centuries earlier; even Justin Martyr conceded the parallels. The resurrection is borrowed myth.

The Inquisitor, O’Connor (via Richard Carrier)

Strongest response

Genre distinction + radical content difference

Knechtle: the Vedas and Egyptian texts claim myth; the gospels claim history. Osiris — chopped into 14 pieces, reassembled to rule the underworld — is “radically different.” Evidentialist method: “no evidence Elvis rose; a truckload that Jesus did.”

Knechtle genre + Elvis parityFactual parallels uncorrected

Theory 3

Hallucination (individual)

Grief, alienation, and apocalyptic expectation produced visions the disciples interpreted as the risen Jesus — just as Whitley Strieber insisted his alien abduction “seemed absolutely real.”

Keith Parsons (Encyclopedia of Psychology)

Strongest response

Jewish frame yields “assumption,” not “resurrection”

Craig’s five-fold reply: hallucinations can’t explain physicality, the number / variety / groups, or the empty tomb; the disciples had no disposition; and a hallucinated Jesus would be seen glorified in heaven — yielding belief in assumption, a different category from resurrection.

Craig Jewish-frame + five-fold rebuttal

Theory 4

Mass / collective hallucination

The “500 at once” appearance (1 Cor 15) was a grief-induced collective vision — a whole community seeing what it longed to see.

Modern critical scholarship (via Huff)

Strongest response

There is no such psychological phenomenon

Huff: “you can’t get five hundred people to lose it the exact same thing at the exact same time,” over forty days. The appearances were poly-modal — sight, touch, conversation, shared meals — not a single hysterical episode (McLatchie).

Huff no-mass-hallucinationMcLatchie poly-modal appearances

Theory 5

Bereavement vision

A third to a half of the bereaved experience a waking hallucination of the lost loved one — the sophisticated, evidence-backed version of the hallucination theory.

Parsons; Dale Allison (objective-vision form)

Partial response — lightly engaged

“They don’t conclude the person is risen”

Craig: the bereaved don’t conclude their loved one rose bodily from the dead; and Paul and James weren’t grieving followers. But the strong, scholarly bereavement-vision case is asserted against more than argued against on the grief-experience data.

Craig partial replyStrong form under-engaged

Theory 6

Conspiracy / the disciples lied

Early Christianity was a successful cult, “like the Branch Davidians in a larger form” — people invent stories to gain followers, write them down, and others believe them.

Mike Newdow, Jim Majors, David Smalley

Strongest response

Liars make poor martyrs

Multi-party conspiracies break down under threat of death; conspirators would not pick women as witnesses or proclaim in Jerusalem where the corpse could be produced (McLatchie, Saurin). The disciples “died for what they claimed to have seen” (Knechtle).

McLatchie Jerusalem proclamationKnechtle died-for-what-they-saw

Theory 7

Stolen body / family-theft

Family members raided the tomb at night; Roman soldiers killed them; all three bodies decomposed in a common grave; people found the tomb empty and inferred resurrection.

Bart Ehrman (family-theft scenario), the deist tradition

Strongest response

No motive, opportunity, or time — and anachronistic

Craig: the family didn’t believe in Jesus (no motive); only Joseph and the women knew the location (no opportunity); Friday-to-Sunday is no time; undisturbed grave-clothes disprove robbery; conspiracies surface. And no first-century Jew had the category of an individual mid-history resurrection. Enemy attestation of the Sanhedrin rumour (Stuart).

Craig motive / opportunity / timeStuart enemy attestation

Theory 8

Swoon / apparent death

Jesus survived the cross, revived in the cool tomb, and walked out — or, in Shabir Ally’s Islamic version, was taken down alive and raised from the tomb by God.

Shabir Ally; the historic swoon theory

Strongest response

“The theological equivalent of the Flat Earth theory”

Craig: Roman executioners ensured death by the spear-thrust (Quintilian), and JAMA judged Jesus died of hypovolaemic shock. Soldiers pierced rather than broke his legs (Schreiner, John). Ally’s revised version contradicts Surah 4:157 and makes Allah a deceiver.

Craig Quintilian + JAMA + Quran reductioSchreiner verified death

Theory 9

Wrong tomb / no burial at all

Romans did not bury crucifixion victims — they were thrown to dogs or left for birds. So the “empty tomb” is question-begging: Jesus was never in a tomb to begin with.

John Dominic Crossan, Bart Ehrman

Strongest response

Roman law and archaeology both allow burial

Strobel: the Roman Digesta “specifically does allow for bodies of execution victims to be turned over for burial,” and the archaeological record of buried crucifixion victims is growing (Yehohanan; a recent UK find). Joseph of Arimathea’s tomb was a known, checkable location (Stuart).

Strobel Digesta + archaeologyStuart known tomb location

Theory 10

The unknown identical twin

Jesus had a secret twin — separated at birth, or the Judas-Thomas of Syriac tradition — who appeared after the crucifixion and was mistaken for the risen Jesus.

UC-Irvine professor (Craig), a Berkeley professor (Stuart), Ehrman (Acts of Thomas)

Strongest response

A reductio of naturalism itself

Universally treated as self-undermining: a never-seen identical twin, unknown even to his own mother, appearing only at the convenient moment, who still doesn’t explain the empty tomb. That credentialled scholars reach for it “shows to what desperate lengths skepticism must go” (Craig).

Craig desperation reductioMcLatchie forensic absurdity

Theory 11

“Any naturalistic story beats a miracle” (prior probability)

A miracle is by definition the least probable explanation, so any naturalistic scenario — however unlikely — is more probable. Baffling reports crop up anyway (the 1967 Wake County UFO that was really Venus).

Bart Ehrman, Stephen Law, Brian Blais, “Nathan”

Partial response + methodological mismatch

Contest the priors, not the framework

McLatchie meets it head-on: concede Bayes, but natural theology and the messianic context mean the prior is “not at all low.” Craig adds religio-historical context as the parity-breaker. But Craig and Knechtle largely answer this probability objection with an explanatory-power argument — the seam where the strongest sceptics slip away.

McLatchie contest-the-priorsProbability vs power mismatch

Canonical quotes

There are four candidate hypotheses which purport to explain the relevant data, and I would maintain that these four categories of explanation are mutually exhaustive… number one, that the apostles themselves didn’t claim Jesus arose from the dead and it’s a later developing myth or legendary claim; second, that they did claim Jesus rose but they lied about it; thirdly, they did claim it but they were honestly mistaken about it; and fourthly, Jesus really did rise from the dead. Jonathan McLatchie · the four-way exhaustive disjunction
Hallucinations, as projections of the mind, don’t contain anything that’s not already in the mind… so given their Jewish expectations, if they were to hallucinate Jesus, they would see visions of Jesus in glory in Abraham’s bosom — and at most that would have led to the belief that Jesus had been assumed into heaven, which is a totally different category in Jewish thinking from resurrection from the dead. William Lane Craig · the Jewish-frame rebuttal of hallucination
