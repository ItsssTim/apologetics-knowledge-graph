The question

Is a human being composed of two parts (body + soul) or three (body + soul + spirit)?

Granting that there is some immaterial constituent in man — the prior question, settled at Q-13.2.2 — the follow-on dispute is how many such constituents there are. Trichotomists lean on texts that name “soul” (psyche) and “spirit” (pneuma) side by side, pre-eminently Hebrews 4:12, “dividing soul and spirit,” and hold the spirit to be a distinct third part. Dichotomists hold that body and soul exhaust the person, and that “spirit” is another name for the one immaterial soul.

The stakes are modest and intramural: this is a dispute within the dualist camp, not between dualists and materialists. Its practical edge is pneumatological — whether regeneration and the indwelling Spirit operate on a separate “spirit-part,” or on the one soul in its God-ward orientation.

The convergent case for dichotomy

Not a deductive syllogism but a small cumulative case: one expert’s reframing-plus-argument converging with another’s independent affirmation of body–soul unity. Three lines of reasoning push toward the two-part view.

Stream A · The reframe

  • The part-count is “somewhat academic”
  • The real question: monism vs any immaterial constituent
  • Once dualism is granted, dichotomy is the default
  • Carried by Craig

Stream B · Proliferation

  • No consistent ontological soul/spirit distinction
  • Press the language and “heart,” “mind,” “inner man” follow
  • That over-generates entities — a reductio
  • Carried by Craig

Stream C · Body–soul unity

  • The person is body and soul together
  • Death is the abnormal separation of the two
  • The structure named is two-fold, not three
  • Carried by Schreiner
Resolution

“Spirit” is a functional, not ontological, designation — the soul in its God-ward relation, not a third thing of which we are composed.

The positive proposal. The soul insofar as it functions in relation to God is called the spirit; the same soul in its everyday rational functions is called the soul. The God-ward dimension is real and experiential, but it is a different function of one immaterial part. Craig’s conclusion: a basic dualism-interactionism of soul and body is adequate both philosophically and biblically.

How each expert treats it

William Lane Craig 1 transcript
The dominant voice — “spirit” as function, not substance
The move, in four beats
  • Subordinate the question. Ranks the dispute below monism-vs-dualism: it is “somewhat academic” once an immaterial constituent is granted.
  • Deny a consistent ontological distinction. No “strong and consistent distinction in Scripture or philosophy” between soul and spirit.
  • Proliferation-of-entities argument. Press soul/spirit to deliver two substances and you must reify “heart,” “mind,” and “the inner man” too — multiplying parts without warrant.
  • Functional proposal. The soul in its God-ward relation is the “spirit”; in its everyday rational functions, the “soul.” One part, two functions.
Why the functional reading

It lets Craig affirm the experiential reality of the God-ward “spiritual” life — “we relate to God ... in a way that mere animals don’t” — without conceding pious trichotomists any extra ontological furniture.

Patrick Schreiner 1 transcript · via Wes Huff
Body–soul unity and the anti-Platonic frame
What he contributes
  • Does not address trichotomy at all — supplies the premise the dichotomist relies on: the person is a body-and-soul unity, named in two parts.
  • Anti-Platonic, incarnational. “This physical stuff which is very anti-platonic is good and God came to redeem it.”
  • Death as abnormal rupture. Death separates soul from body; resurrection reunites them — “that’s not the way it’s supposed to be.”
  • Every term he reaches for is two-fold (body / soul), never three.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

Scripture distinguishes soul and spirit — so man is body + soul + spirit

Hebrews 4:12 speaks of the word “dividing soul and spirit”; 1 Thess 5:23 of “your whole spirit and soul and body.” The spirit is the distinct God-conscious third part.

Trichotomist tradition (Watchman Nee; classic dispensational anthropology)

Strongest response

The distinction lacks ontological force

Press soul/spirit language for two substances and you must do the same for “heart,” “mind,” and “inner man” — proliferating entities. Better read “spirit” as the soul in its God-ward function. (Craig engages the Heb 4:12 prooftext directly in the Part 9 Q&A — see gaps.)

Craig proliferation argument + functional reading

Objection 2

A functional reading flattens the spiritual life

If “spirit” is just a synonym for the soul, the God-conscious dimension that trichotomy was protecting is lost — reductive piety.

Pious trichotomist worry

Strongest response

The God-ward dimension is fully affirmed

Craig: “we do have a spiritual dimension ... by which we know God in a way that mere animals don’t.” It is a genuine function of the immaterial part — just not a separate substance. Nothing experiential is denied.

Craig function-not-thing distinction

Objection 3

An over-spiritualised anthropology — the body as a prison the self escapes

If the real person is the immaterial part, the body is inessential, and salvation is the soul’s release from matter (Gnostic / Platonic).

Gnosticism; popular dualist piety

Strongest response

Anti-Platonic incarnationalism

Schreiner: the body is good, God “came to redeem it,” and the resurrection reunites body and soul because the separation at death “is not the way it’s supposed to be.” The incarnation itself is the witness that matter matters to God.

Schreiner incarnational frame
