Is faith evidence-based trust, or believing without evidence?

The “faith is believing without evidence” caricature, rebutted — faith is the ordinary word for evidenced trust, and science itself rests on it. Five experts treat it in 26 substantive extractions.

The question

When a Christian says they have “faith,” do they mean evidence-based trust — or believing something for which there is no evidence?

This is a definitional dispute before it is anything else. The New Atheist polemic — Dawkins’s “believing where there’s no evidence,” Mark Twain’s “believing what you know ain’t true,” Coyne’s “belief without evidence sufficient to convince any reasonable person” — treats the answer as semantically settled: faith is the thing you reach for when reason runs out. If that definition holds, every theistic argument is dead on arrival.

The apologist’s reply is not to defend believing-without-evidence but to reject the definition as a caricature. “Faith” (Latin fides, Greek pistis) is an ordinary word for trust, and trust is extended on evidence of reliability. The load-bearing move is then a parity claim: science itself rests on a foundational faith — the rational intelligibility of the universe — so “science vs faith” smuggles in the conclusion at issue. The real question is relocated: not do you have faith? (everyone does) but what is the evidence base for the faith you already hold?

The convergent argument — a definition-correction, then premises

Target

The redefinition: the opponent treats faith as belief without — or against — evidence.

Variants in the corpus. Dawkins: “believing where there’s no evidence.” Twain: “believing what you know ain’t true.” Coyne: “belief without evidence sufficient to convince any reasonable person.” Dillahunty: “believing something without sufficient evidence.” Aron Ra: “complete trust that is not based on evidence.” The whole apologetic turns on refusing this definition rather than satisfying it.

Premise 1

“Faith” is an ordinary word, not a religious technical term.

Etymology. It descends from Latin fides (fidelity, trust) and Greek pistis (assurance). Lennox: “we get fidelity, trust” from it. Knechtle adds Latin religio — “binding together.” The word does ordinary work long before it does religious work.

Premise 2

Trust is proportioned to evidence; trusting without evidence is not faith but gullibility.

The carve-out. Knechtle: “if there’s no evidence of reliability and you believe something, you’re a gullible fool.” Lennox: “if you have no grounds, then it’s blind faith” — a real but distinct category the Bible itself condemns. Genuine faith demands evidence first.

Premise 3

We already extend evidence-based faith constantly — to a spouse, a pharmacist, an oncoming driver, a bank manager.

The everyday parity. Knechtle’s catalogue: the pharmacist’s bottle, the green light, the marriage. Lennox: the bank manager who wants evidence before the loan; the wife of 47 years — “I couldn’t prove mathematically that she loves me, but I’d stake my life on it.”

Premise 4

Science itself rests on a prior faith — that the universe is rationally intelligible.

The science parity (the heaviest move). Lennox deploys Einstein as hostile witness (“I cannot imagine a scientist without that faith… faith in the rational intelligibility of the universe”) and Polkinghorne (“physics is powerless to explain its faith”). So “science vs faith” concedes the very ground in dispute.

Premise 5

Christianity self-describes as evidence-based — the New Testament presents itself as evidence.

Textual anchor. Lennox on John 20:30-31: the signs (sēmeia) “are written that you might believe… here’s the evidence on which faith is based. If Christianity wasn’t evidence-based, you wouldn’t have the New Testament, because it claims to be evidence.”

Conclusion

Everyone lives by faith; the question is not whether faith is present but whether it is well-evidenced — and the redefinition exists to hide that symmetry.

Relocation of the burden. Knechtle: “we all have to live by faith… the question for the thinking person is who or what do I trust, and what is the evidence?” The case is abductive and cumulative, not deductive: each premise is contestable, and the atheist contests exactly the move from everyday trust to religious trust (P3 → P5).

How each expert deploys it

John Lennox 16 transcripts
The dominant voice — conceptual hygiene before anything else
Signature set-piece
  • Names the redefinition and its authors. Faith “has been redefined… believing where there’s no evidence” — Dawkins primarily, Mark Twain as folk ancestor, “even got into Webster’s dictionary.”
  • Etymological correction + the load-bearing follow-up: “the next question is, what is the basis of your trust? And if you’ve no basis, then you’re credulous.”
  • Everyday analogies: the bank manager and the loan; the wife of 47 years; the football team. Trust in persons is “broader and more cumulative” than trust in facts.
  • The science-is-faith-based parity — Einstein, Polkinghorne, Wigner’s “unreasonable effectiveness of mathematics”: “I’m often asked to give lectures on science and faith. I never will, because that is an atheistic formulation.”
  • Internal-critique reductios: the Singer “faith you were brought up in” demolition (“cyberspace went electric”); the Dawkins-on-his-wife anecdote (“Leave my wife out of it”).
  • NT self-description: John 20:30-31 in nearly every deployment.
Distinctive refinement

“Proof” vs “evidence”: “You only get proof in the strict sense in my own field of mathematics. In every other field, including science, we speak of evidence, of pointers, of being convinced beyond reasonable doubt.”

Distinctive refinement

The two-wing framework: “an eagle needs two wings to fly” — the objective wing (fine-tuning, possibility of science, resurrection) and the subjective wing (transformation). Either alone is “sterile intellectualism” or “absolute subjectivism.”

Cliffe Knechtle 7 transcripts
The street-level deployment — lived unavoidability
Signature delivery
  • The formula, repeated verbatim: “faith is evidence of reliability plus commitment.”
  • The unavoidability catalogue: the earth’s rotation, the pharmacist’s bottle, the green light, marriage — “all of us have faith.”
  • The folk-caricature, named and disowned: “a little boy once said to me, faith is believing what you know to be a lie” — rejected from inside Christianity: “before I believe, I demand evidence.”
  • The agnosticism-is-incoherent move: doubt is not a vacuum but a switched object of faith — “what is the option you find more attractive, and what’s the evidence?”
  • The symmetry close: “you cannot prove there is no God — that’s a faith position.”
Register note

No etymology, no hostile-witness physicists — Knechtle does lived unavoidability. The relevant question, restated: “what is most reasonable, what is most plausible in light of the evidence — not what is provable.”

William Lane Craig 1 transcript
The precision definition
Distinctive move
  • Supplies the cleanest analytic statement in the corpus: faith is not an epistemology at all.
  • “Faith is not a way of knowing something. Faith is trusting in something that you have good reason to believe is true.”
  • This dissolves Coyne’s “faith is a vice in science, a virtue in religion”: once faith is reclassified from a route to belief into a posture toward what is already believed, “there’s no conflict between faith and science.”
  • The most defensible answer to Dillahunty — it concedes faith does no evidential work; it is downstream of the evidence, not a substitute for it.
Jonathan McLatchie 1 transcript
The evidentialist dissenter — parity turned inward
Distinctive move
  • Runs the parity argument defensively, against a fellow-Christian methodology — rejecting Reformed epistemology as a warrant.
  • “I don’t have any internal witness of the Holy Spirit that I would consider to be veridical.”
  • The discrimination problem: how to distinguish Plantinga/Craig’s appeal “from the burning in the bosom you might hear from a Mormon?”
  • Unified-track epistemology: “my confidence is based exclusively on the public evidence… the way I arrive at my conclusion is the same way I try to persuade others.”
Fault-line within the camp

McLatchie exposes a disagreement inside the apologetic camp: Lennox’s two-wing model and Reformed epistemology both admit a subjective warrant that McLatchie’s evidentialism refuses.

Wes Huff 1 transcript
The relevance frame
Distinctive move
  • Treats faith obliquely — the reliability that warrants trust is shown by survival under assault.
  • The anvil-and-hammer image: “the Bible is the anvil that has worn out many hammers — through academic, philosophical, intellectual scrutiny.”
  • Resurrection converts reliability into authority: “people who rise from the dead have more credibility than people who don’t.”
  • C.S. Lewis closer: Christianity “if false, is of no importance; if true, of infinite importance; the only thing it cannot be is moderately important.”

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

Faith = belief without sufficient evidence; with good reason you don’t need faith

“Faith is the excuse people give for believing something when they don’t have a good reason. If you have a good reason, you give the reason — not an appeal to faith.”

Matt Dillahunty; Jerry Coyne (Faith vs Fact)

Strongest response

Reject the definition; faith is non-epistemic trust

Craig: faith is not a way of knowing but trust in what you already have good reason to believe — so it does no evidential work and competes with nothing. Lennox: that is blind faith, a real but distinct category the Bible itself condemns.

Craig faith reclassifiedLennox blind-faith carve-out

Objection 2

“If there were evidence, you wouldn’t call it faith”

“We only need to use the word faith when there isn’t any evidence. If there were evidence for it, you’d just say evidence.”

Richard Dawkins (debate, embedded clips)

Strongest response

Re-label the concession

This conflates faith with belief-without-evidence. Faith in a spouse, in relativity, in any reliable witness is evidenced trust. When Dawkins grants that the little signs of a wife’s love are “perfectly good evidence,” Lennox re-labels: “Yes — that is faith.”

Lennox relativity vs flying-spaghetti-monster

Objection 3

Equivocation: you slide from “faith in a spouse” to “faith in God”

The dictionary gives “two completely unrelated contexts” — one is bare trust on evidence, the other is “complete trust that is not based on evidence.” The analogy trades on the first to license the second.

Aron Ra; PZ Myers (“a very peculiar definition of evidence”)

Partial — not closed

Asserted to be the same posture

Knechtle and Lennox insist the two are one posture — trust on evidence of reliability — differing only in object. But the atheist’s real point is that the checkability of the evidence differs, and that is not directly rebutted.

Knechtle / Lennox same-posture claimGap on checkability

Objection 4

What you call faith is just induction

“I don’t know why you’re calling induction faith. Inductive trust based on past reliability isn’t faith.” Materialists have novel testable predictions, not faith at all.

Tom Jump

Strongest response

Induction itself rests on an unprovable commitment

Stuart and Cliffe Knechtle: that the future resembles the past, and that one’s senses track reality, are themselves unprovable commitments — faith positions. “You trust your perception of reality… that is faith.”

Stuart Knechtle trust-in-perceptionCliffe Knechtle symmetric-commitment

Objection 5

Hitchens’ razor

“What can be asserted without evidence can be dismissed without evidence” — so most God-claims can simply be glossed past.

Christopher Hitchens (via Dillahunty)

Strongest response

Concede the razor; turn it on the asserter

Lennox: “atheists have no faith” is itself asserted — while Dawkins writes 400 pages defending what he believes, and Hitchens’s “our beliefs are not a belief” is “just nonsense.” The corpus argues theism meets the bar rather than evading it.

Lennox 400-page-book reductio

Objection 6

Religion stops inquiry; science presses on

“Religion teaches us to be satisfied with not understanding… ‘God did it,’ we don’t need to work on it.” Faith is a facile pseudo-explanation that prevents further work.

Richard Dawkins

Strongest response

Faith is upstream of inquiry, not a stop to it

Lennox: doing science presupposes faith in rational intelligibility — so the science/faith opposition is false. And the scholar’s “cardinal sin” is pronouncing outside your field without engaging the best opposing evidence, which the New Atheists do on theology.

Lennox intelligibility + “cardinal sin”

Objection 7

Fideist counter (from the other side): belief should rest on faith, not evidence

“I believe in design because I believe in God; I do not believe in God because I believe in design” (Newman). Conversion is a gift, not a syllogism — Saul met Christ, not the ontological argument.

Michael Ruse (fideist atheist)

Strongest response

The Damascus road was evidence

Lennox: Paul was convinced by “an appearance of the risen Christ” — evidence of a kind. Then he turns the tables: “I’m beginning to worry that you’re not an evidence-based atheist,” exposing fideism as the genuinely unfalsifiable stance.

Lennox resurrection-as-evidence + reversal

Objection 8

Jesus rewards faith arbitrarily

“Your faith has healed you” looks like rewarding fideistic, muster-it-up belief — as if walking confidently across a rickety bridge keeps it from collapsing.

Alex O’Connor

Strongest response

Peter on the water vs the rickety bridge

Lennox: trust was an essential ingredient but Jesus was the source of power. Peter was invited onto water Jesus already stood on; there is no general promise to catch someone testing God. He denounces prosperity-gospel “enough faith cures cancer” as un-biblical and pastorally damaging.

Lennox invitation vs testing-God distinction

Persistent gaps in the corpus

Gap 1

The equivocation charge is never actually rebutted

Dillahunty and Aron Ra claim that publicly-checkable, defeasible trust and a commitment that persists beyond the public evidence are genuinely different things — and that the analogies license the second from the first.

The central gap — flagged in the raw source

Gap — only one engagement

Knechtle restates rather than refutes

His repeated answer is to re-assert his own definition (“he restates his own definition rather than refuting Dillahunty’s”). Craig’s reclassification of faith as non-epistemic trust is the only head-on engagement — and it appears in a single extraction.

Gap 2

The “tentative working belief” distinction goes unanswered

“I don’t hold it as a matter of faith; I hold it as something I have to constantly question.” This marks a real difference between commitment and provisional acceptance under revision.

PZ Myers

Gap — not engaged

The symmetry claim may simply be false

Knechtle does not engage it. “We both commit under uncertainty” collapses if the atheist’s posture is revisable in a way confessional faith is not — precisely the distinction PZ Myers presses.

Gap 3

The impasse is purely lexical — no shared adjudication criterion

Across the Knechtle / Aron Ra / T-Jump exchanges both sides assert a definition of “faith” and talk past each other; the moderator has to redirect.

A recurring stand-off pattern

Gap — no neutral test

The apologist only appears to win by reframing

The corpus never supplies a neutral test by which a listener could decide whose definition is right — so the debate terminates in a definitional stand-off rather than a resolution.

Gap 4

Contradictory confident religious experiences are not discriminated

Lennox’s relational-certainty model (knowing God as you know a beloved spouse) needs criteria to distinguish veridical Christian assurance from equally confident Muslim mystic certainty or Mormon “burning in the bosom.”

Implicit; raised internally by McLatchie

Gap — objection present, answer absent

The corpus contains the objection but not the reply

Lennox grants Buddhism “much good” and pivots to gift-vs-merit rather than evaluating rival epistemic claims. McLatchie raises exactly this discrimination problem from inside the camp — against Reformed epistemology — and no one resolves it.

Gap 5

Divine hiddenness is hand-waved

The “why doesn’t God show up on demand / why no healed amputees” challenge sits uneasily with the corpus’s own evidentialism (“before I believe, I demand evidence”).

Matt Dillahunty

Gap — evasive on its own premises

“He’s God, not a plaything”

The retreat to “God has reasons I don’t understand” is flagged by Dillahunty as evasive. If more evidence would help, its withholding is exactly what is in dispute (cross-refs Q-1.7.1 divine hiddenness).
