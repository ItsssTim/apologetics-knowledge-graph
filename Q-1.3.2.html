The question

Granting that objective moral values exist, can they be grounded in anything less than God?

Q-1.3.1 asks whether objective moral values exist at all. This node concedes — or argues — that they do, and presses the grounding question: what kind of reality could make moral claims true independently of human opinion?

Its strategic appeal is that it engages a commitment almost no one can disown. As Craig puts it, “you can shut your eyes to cosmology, but every day you get up and answer how you live the question of whether other people have intrinsic moral worth.” Where the Kalam needs a listener who cares about thermodynamics, this argument runs on a moral intuition the interlocutor already has and cannot consistently abandon — which is why Craig calls it far more effective than his beloved cosmological argument.

The convergent argument

Premise 1

If God does not exist, objective moral values and duties do not exist.

The load-bearing move. Craig insists on the ontology / epistemology distinction: the claim is not that atheists cannot know or live morality, but that atheism supplies no foundation. Huff (after Zagzebski): morality is a rational enterprise that, like maths and logic, cannot be grounded in contingent human minds. Knechtle (folk): “inanimate matter does not distinguish between good and evil — you have to have a who.”

Premise 2

Objective moral values and duties do exist.

Supports. Apprehended in moral experience — “no more reason to deny than the objective reality of the physical world” (Craig). Anchored in clear cases (torturing a child, racism, the Holocaust, female genital mutilation) and a long bench of hostile witnesses who concede the premise: Ruse, Antony, Cave, Mackie.

Conclusion

Therefore God exists — a transcendent, personal mind whose holy character is the locus and paradigm of moral value.

Grounding. Craig grounds duties in God’s commands, which necessarily reflect his holy and loving nature — the Euthyphro “third horn.” Knechtle and Perry ground value in the imago Dei; Huff and McLatchie argue the source must be a necessary, rational, sentient being. A caveat all the theists concede: the argument reaches only a mind / a transcendent moral order, not the Christian God — “even if it’s a spaghetti monster.” The road to Christianity runs through the resurrection.

How each expert deploys it

William Lane Craig 10 transcripts
The architect — the two-contention case
Signature delivery
  • Contention 1: if God exists, values are grounded in his nature (Anselm: “the Locus and Paradigm of moral value”); duties are constituted by his commands, “far from arbitrary” because they reflect his holy and loving nature.
  • Contention 2: if not, three sub-moves — the value problem (humans no worthier than “insects, rats or hyenas”); the is/ought gap (Fodor); ought-implies-can (Harris denies free will).
  • The ontology / epistemology distinction — against Hitchens, Atkins, Sinnott-Armstrong, who keep answering “atheists can be good.”
  • Hostile-witness stacking: P1 conceded by Ruse and Mackie; P2 conceded by atheist moral realist Louise Antony and Peter Cave.
  • The “argument from evil FOR God” reversal — the atheist’s own “evil exists” premise proves objective values, hence God.
  • Endnote-mining against Harris’s footnote that flourishing-peaks and goodness-peaks are possibly non-identical.
Scope concession

“What I’m offering Dr Harris tonight is not a new set of moral values… rather, a sound foundation for the objective moral values and duties that we both hold dear.” The argument is conditional — it grounds morality, it does not by itself reach Christianity.

Cliffe & Stuart Knechtle 11 transcripts
Street-level — “the law above the law”
Signature moves
  • “You need a who.” “You cannot show me a foot of justice. You cannot give me a pound of love… They are intangible” — matter cannot ground value.
  • The MLK “law above the law” anchor — King’s Birmingham letter only coheres if there is “some type of Mind” above Alabama’s law.
  • Imago Dei grounds the wrongness of racism; Baldwin is weaponised first to pre-empt the “pompous enlightened white guy” frame.
  • The “live it out” challenge (vs PZ Myers): “when Stalin slaughtered millions, he was not a hypocrite — for in atheism, morality is relative.”
  • Reaches a clean Euthyphro answer: “an eternal value that flows from the character of God.”
  • Stuart: the Tom-Holland genealogical move (modern morals parasitic on Christianity, Gal 3:28); the obligation-based / sensible-knave argument vs Smalley.
John Lennox 4 transcripts
The Dawkins internal critique
Signature moves
  • Rarely a free-standing syllogism — deployed as internal critique within the problem of evil: the objector invokes a category his worldview has abolished.
  • Dawkins against Dawkins: “no design, no purpose, no evil and no good… DNA just is and we dance to its music” — so “the problem of evil vanishes intellectually.”
  • The curious-inconsistency diagnosis: Dawkins keeps moralising against God because he is “a moral being made in the image of God.”
  • Hostile-witness stack: Mackie, Charles Taylor, Solzhenitsyn (“we have forgotten God”).
  • The “rebel against your genes” self-contradiction; Hume’s is/ought; the Dostoevsky/Nietzsche prediction.
Preston Perry 5 transcripts
The imago-Dei reductio by questions
Signature moves
  • “Where do you get your moral standard from?” asked five or six times, refusing every topic-pivot, until the standard is located in the self.
  • The self-grounding collapse: a standard from oneself cannot adjudicate against a killer’s standard from himself — “I don’t have one.”
  • Intuition as evidence: “when you said murder is wrong, that’s evidence that you were created in the image of God.”
  • The day/night analogy: you can’t name evil without a standard of good.
  • “We live like atheists but hold to the Christian worldview” — the parasitism thesis in one line. Buries all technical structure under conversational English.
Wes Huff 1 transcript
The Zagzebski rational-source argument
Distinctive moves
  • Morality is a rational enterprise; moral facts are discovered, not created — like maths and logic.
  • Moral realism is true: “when I die, murder doesn’t stop being morally wrong.”
  • Humans are flawed and contingent, so cannot be the source; therefore a necessary rational source is required, and to be rational it must be sentient — “therefore the moral lawgiver is God.”
  • Epistemology / ontology: evolution explains how we came to know, not why facts exist. The Scrivener “pants” analogy; Ruse as hostile witness.
Jonathan McLatchie 1 transcript
The intrinsic-value reductio
Distinctive moves
  • Distinguishes pragmatic goodness (utility) from moral goodness (intrinsic virtue).
  • Objectivity test: “if Hitler had won… and eliminated everyone who thought the Holocaust immoral,” it would still be wrong — better assumed in a theistic universe.
  • The stroke-victim reductio: if value is grounded in cognitive capacity, a stroke victim loses moral worth — a bullet no humanist will bite.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

“Atheists can be moral, so you don’t need God”

The most-repeated reply. Hitchens: “name me a moral action I couldn’t take because I don’t believe in the supernatural.”

Hitchens, Atkins, Smalley, PZ Myers, Newdow, Dawkins

Strongest response

Ontology, not epistemology

The claim is not that atheists can’t be or know good, but that atheism gives no foundation. “The question is one of foundation and logical grounding” (Huff). Stuart Knechtle concedes atheists can be good people, then presses the is/ought gap.

Craig ontology/epistemology, repeatedHuff the “pants” analogyS. Knechtle good-but-ungrounded

Objection 2

The Euthyphro dilemma

Is something good because God commands it (then goodness is arbitrary), or does God command it because it is good (then goodness is independent of God)?

Baggini, Steinhart; the standard atheist reply

Partial response — usually only when raised

The “third horn”: God’s nature

Goodness is neither divine fiat nor independent — it flows from God’s holy and loving nature; commands reflect that nature. But across most popular deployments the dilemma is neither raised nor pre-empted, leaving the flank open.

Craig canonical third hornKnechtle “eternal value that flows from the character of God”Unaddressed when not raised

Objection 3

Morality is grounded in evolution + social conditioning

Reciprocal altruism in kin groups plus a “shifting moral Zeitgeist” account for human morality without God (Dawkins); bonobos and mama bears show pre-moral behaviour (Smalley).

Dawkins, Smalley, Atkins, Harris

Strongest response

Explains epistemology, not ontology

Evolution explains how we came to believe, not why the facts are true (Huff). Herd morality has utility but no binding force — the rapist is merely “the moral equivalent of Lady Gaga, out of step with the herd” (Craig). You can’t derive an ought from an is.

Huff epistemology/ontologyCraig Lady Gaga reductioLennox Hume’s is/ought

Objection 4

Harris — good = flourishing of conscious creatures

Science can ground a universal morality by identifying the good with the well-being of conscious creatures; no God required.

Sam Harris, The Moral Landscape

Strongest response

Redefinition, and universality ≠ objectivity

Solving the value problem by redefining terms is “nothing but word play.” “The Nazis could have won and established a universal morality” — the issue is objectivity, not universality. Harris’s own footnote admits flourishing-peaks and goodness-peaks are possibly non-identical, which on identity theory destroys the claim.

Craig endnote-mining + universality/objectivity

Objection 5

Godless normative realism / atheistic Platonism

Objective moral facts exist as brute, non-natural, supervening features of reality — or as necessary truths like the laws of logic. No “who” is required. “Even if God is dead, objective morality abides.”

Erik Wielenberg, Eric Steinhart

Partial response

Concrete object beats causally-isolated abstracts

Theism grounds value in a concrete object (God), not “extravagant” abstract objects; abstract values generate no duties; a “form” cannot be loved or ground a relational moral world. But Wielenberg’s symmetry rebuttal (mind-body causation = abstract-object causation) is met only partly.

Craig concrete/abstract disanalogyKnechtle “can’t love a form”Symmetry only half-answered

Objection 6

Compassion / “do no harm” is a sufficient ground

Moral motivation is grounded in Humean sympathy and fellow-feeling, psychologically sufficient even without metaphysical grounding (Baggini); rape is wrong because it causes suffering (Sinnott-Armstrong).

Baggini, Newdow, Sinnott-Armstrong

Largely unanswered

“Suffering just is” — but the middle position stands

“If there is no God, what’s wrong with suffering? Suffering just is” (Knechtle), and on what basis condemn FGM-cultures who believe it benefits the community? Yet Baggini’s “middle position” — not arbitrary, not fully objective — gets no direct engagement; Knechtle reverts to the imago-Dei script.

Knechtle FGM test caseHumean middle position unmet

Objection 7

“Name a moral act a believer can do that an unbeliever can’t”

If religion adds nothing to moral capacity, the moral argument is idle.

Christopher Hitchens

Strongest response

Wrong question — but answered anyway

The question is ontological grounding, not behavioural capacity. But examples are given: tithing, worship, “love the Lord your God with all your heart” — obligatory on theism, not on atheism.

Craig tithing / greatest commandment

Objection 8

Society / the gang / the majority defines morality

Morality is the bar a society sets; protest just raises the bar. The death penalty is legal though some call it murder — societies legitimately differ.

Richardson, Perry’s interlocutors

Strongest response

Societies are demonstrably wrong

Apartheid, the Dred Scott 3/5 ratio, slavery-endorsing 18th-century society — if the majority defines right, none of these can be called wrong, which no one will accept. The majority doesn’t determine truth.

Knechtle apartheid / Dred ScottPerry the slavery test case

Objection 9

“God’s morality is just God’s opinion”

What we read of God’s and Jesus’s morality is their opinion — no more objective than mine. And if goodness is grounded in God’s character, why isn’t that character itself an arbitrary brute fact?

Randolph Richardson, Eric Steinhart

Gap — the deepest unanswered pressure

No expert closes the loop

The reply is that goodness flows from God’s necessary, eternal character, not arbitrary preference. But no one explains why grounding goodness in a necessary person is non-arbitrary in a way grounding it in a necessary principle is not. Steinhart: “I would have thought the moral law is the character of God.” Knechtle pivots to “your idol is yourself.”

Aggregation gap — brute-fact rejoinder

Objection 10

Tu quoque — religion causes atrocities

Apartheid was a Dutch Reformed creation; Nazism, the Crusades, the Inquisition. With God “all things are thinkable.” Hitler “was definitely a Christian.”

Hitchens, PZ Myers

Strongest response

Concede, then redirect to the 20th century

Grant Christian atrocities — but the bloodiest century was the atheist 20th, which Nietzsche “called perfectly.” Hitler despised Christianity. A Christian who murders is a hypocrite; an atheist who murders, on his own worldview, is not.

C. Knechtle Nietzsche’s predictionS. Knechtle Hitler rejected Christianity

Objection 11

Moral value is grounded in cognitive capacity

Humans matter because of their rational / cognitive capacities — a naturalistic account of human worth that needs no God.

Implicit humanist position

Strongest response

The stroke-victim reductio

If value tracks cognitive capacity, then someone who has a stroke and loses cognitive capability “somehow loses part of their intrinsic moral value” — a conclusion no one will accept, so cognition cannot ground worth.

McLatchie canonical reductio
