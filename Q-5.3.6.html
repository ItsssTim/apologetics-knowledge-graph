Are life’s molecular machines irreducibly complex?

Irreducible complexity moved from the flagellum to the foundations — DNA replication and bacterial cell division, the systems on which natural selection itself depends. Stephen Meyer dominates; Jonathan McLatchie supplies the sharpest argument.

The question

Are the molecular machines at the foundation of life best explained by a designing intelligence rather than by mutation and natural selection?

This is the specific, biological end of the design argument. Where Q-1.2.3 argues abstractly that digital information traces to a mind, this node puts named molecular systems on the table — the DNA-replication apparatus, the bacterial cell-division machinery, the flagellar motor — and asks whether the neo-Darwinian mechanism can build them by a series of functionally-selectable intermediates.

Two features make the node unusual. Its dominant voice is a single expert: Stephen Meyer supplies fifteen of the twenty extractions, and his preferred signal is not Behe’s irreducible complexity but his own information argument. And the strongest case made under this heading is not the flagellum but self-replication itself — McLatchie’s observation that natural selection presupposes differential survival, which presupposes replication, so selection cannot explain the origin of replication without circularity.

The convergent argument — inference to best explanation

Premise 1

Certain molecular systems exhibit a feature — irreducible complexity, or large amounts of specified digital information — that, in our uniform experience, has only one known cause.

Which feature is the signal? McLatchie: multi-component machines where removing any one part breaks the whole. Meyer: “information always arises from an intelligent source.” Lennox: a candidate conservation law for information, analogous to the conservation of energy.

Premise 2

The neo-Darwinian mechanism of mutation plus selection cannot produce that feature.

Three failure modes. For irreducibly complex machines, no series of functionally-selectable intermediates exists. For self-replication, selection presupposes the very thing to be explained. For information, mutations degrade rather than generate it. McLatchie: “you can’t invoke natural selection to explain the existence of self-replication without assuming the existence of the very thing you’re trying to explain.”

Premise 3

Intelligence is the only known cause of the feature in question — foresight, code, circuitry, irreducible complexity.

Observed phrasings. McLatchie: “only a conscious intelligent being can visualize a complex end goal and then bring all everything together that’s needed to actualize that end goal.” Meyer: “we know of only one known cause of circuits and that is engineering.”

Conclusion

Therefore intelligent design is the best explanation of these systems — an inference governed by the same uniform-experience canons as ordinary historical science.

Abductive, not deductive. The force is comparative (better than the Darwinian alternative) and leans heavily on hostile-witness concessions from mainstream biologists — Müller, Tokuriki, Crick, Gates, and Darwin himself. Meyer: “a better explanation is actually intelligent design… consistent with everything we know about the cause and effect structure of the world.”

Signature five-beat capsule

Named arguments & analogies

  • Bill Gates hostile witness — “DNA is like a software program but much more complex than any we’ve ever devised… software always comes from programmers.”

On the flagellum specifically

Meyer reframes Behe’s IC as a testable temporal-ordering prediction: design predicts flagellar genes are older than the Type-III secretion system; co-option predicts the reverse. He claims the genomic evidence shows the T3SS is “a devolutionary byproduct,” confirming Behe over Miller. The corpus asserts but thinly sources this reversal.

Jonathan McLatchie 1 transcript
The self-replication anchor — the only on-topic case
Signature moves
  • The DNA-replication machine — enumerates initiation proteins, helicase, polymerase, primase, single-strand binding proteins, topoisomerase, sliding clamp, clamp loader, ligase, the Okazaki apparatus; remove any one and you get not a half-replicator but a broken one.
  • The circularity reductio (his strongest move) — cell division is cleaner than the flagellum because it is self-replication; selection presupposes differential survival, which presupposes replication, so selection cannot ground replication’s origin without circularity.
  • Foresight — only a conscious being can visualise a complex end goal and assemble everything needed to actualise it.
  • T3SS reversal — the secretion system is “an evolutionary degradation product of the bacterial flagellum”; pressure to swim precedes pressure to inject poisons.
Weak move

Dismisses the scientific consensus as “an uninformed consensus” — most biologists “don’t know much about the scientific challenges to evolution.” Rhetorically convenient but evidentially thin; the corpus offers no principled account of when a consensus should be discounted.

John Lennox 1 transcript
The strategic redirect — declines Behe’s hill
Signature moves
  • Concedes the jury is out on Behe’s strong irreducible-complexity claim, because it “involves proving a negative, and proving a negative is very difficult in science.”
  • One level down — invokes Peter Medawar’s suggested law of conservation of information: natural processes are “brilliant at transmitting” and optimising information but cannot create it.
  • One level up — the major evidence for God comes “in the bits we do” understand; “the very fact that we can do science points towards God.”
  • Category-mistake defuse — you cannot deduce atheism from biology; Darwin’s American champion Asa Gray was a committed Christian.
Robert Stovold critic · 1 transcript
The strongest critique of irreducible complexity
His case against IC
  • Behe’s mousetrap isn’t truly irreducible — remove the base, it still traps mice.
  • IC wrongly assumes evolution proceeds only by sequential addition, ignoring loss-of-parts from over-engineered systems, duplication, change of function, and gradual modification making non-essential parts essential.
  • These mechanisms “have been observed in the laboratory” and were “predicted by evolutionary theory” in the 1930s.
  • ID attacks “a straw man weakened version of evolutionary theory.”
Peter Atkins critic · 1 transcript
The partial concession
What he grants
  • Concedes “intelligent design does raise questions that we materialist scientists have an obligation to answer.”
  • But notes non-design biologists were “already speculating” about junk-DNA function — so the ENCODE result is not uniquely an ID prediction.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

Behe’s flagellum was explained by Miller’s T3SS co-option

The Type-III secretion system is a functional precursor of the flagellum — so the machine is not irreducibly complex after all.

Granado; Ken Miller, Finding Darwin’s God

Strongest response

The T3SS is a degradation product, not a precursor

Recent scholarship indicates the T3SS is derivative of the flagellum — selective pressure to swim precedes pressure to inject poisons; flagellar genes are older. Genomic ordering confirms design’s temporal prediction over co-option.

McLatchie swim-before-poisonMeyer T3SS on plasmids / islands of pathogenicity

Objection 2

Naturalistic mechanisms already explain new information

Exon shuffling, gene fusion and de novo origin produce new genes — the 2003 paper cited in Dover refuted ID on exactly this point.

Matzke, Elsberry & Gishlick (2004); Kitzmiller v. Dover

Strongest response

They presuppose the information — question-begging

All the cited mechanisms presuppose existing protein folds; “de novo origin” merely relabels the mystery. The paper “begs the important questions, it doesn’t solve them” — like cutting and pasting chapters from books that already exist.

Meyer Turek interview

Objection 3

Antibiotic resistance proves mutation builds novelty

Bacteria mutate to become resistant — demonstrable, observed, beneficial mutation. Extrapolate it and you get macroevolution.

Caller “John from Kentucky”; standard reply

Strongest response

Resistance runs “downhill informationally”

Resistance damages mission-critical systems (cell wall, protein synthesis); remove the antibiotic and resistant strains lose. You cannot extrapolate from a mechanism that degrades systems to the origination of new ones — it’s going the wrong direction.

Meyer downhill framingGrassé hostile witness (via Anderson)

Objection 4

“Evolution has been proved in the lab”

31,000 generations of E. coli produced a citrate-metabolising strain; fruit-fly experiments show change. The mechanism works.

Triumphalist headlines

Strongest response

Survival, not arrival

These are microevolutionary tweaks drawing on pre-existing genetic information. “Natural selection explains the survival but not the arrival” — it does not account for the origin of whole new body plans.

Meyer Socrates in the City

Objection 5

Cells destroy good components — that’s not engineered

When a gene circuit is switched off, the cell rips out a perfectly good switch and throws it away. That flies in the face of the idea these systems look designed.

Arthur Hunt (Biola Q&A)

Strongest response

Hunt’s own words are engineering terms

“You used a couple of interesting words — one was control, an engineering concept; the second is circuitry.” A circuit operating differently than we’d design it doesn’t undermine an IBE keyed on specified information. We know of only one cause of circuits: engineering.

Meyer vocabulary judo

Objection 6

New body plans came from rewiring existing networks

You don’t need new information — novelty arises by re-wiring existing developmental gene regulatory networks.

Charles Marshall, Science

Strongest response

Presupposes what it must explain

Marshall assumes (a) that dGRNs can be altered — falsified by Davidson’s work, where any mutation to core regulatory elements shuts down development — and (b) multiple unexplained prior sources of information. Question-begging.

Meyer EPS 2019

Objection 7

ID is unfalsifiable — a science-stopper

Intelligent design makes no testable predictions; it just labels gaps “designed” and halts inquiry.

Peter Atkins; standard demarcation charge

Strongest response

The junk-DNA prediction, confirmed by ENCODE

ID-ers predicted in the 1990s that non-coding “junk” DNA would prove functional, against the Darwinian consensus. ENCODE (2007 / 2012) confirmed it — “a result that has confirmed a specific prediction of the theory of intelligent design.” Signature in the Cell lists ten predictions.

Meyer Hewitt & Atkins debate

Objection 8

You’re cherry-picking which phenomena are designed

You attribute some biology to design and some to evolution as it suits you — that’s arbitrary.

Peter Ward

Strongest response

A principled line: origination vs degradation

The distinction is between the origination of new information, machines and body plans (designed) and the degradation or recombination of existing information (microevolution). Resistance mutations confer advantage by destroying functional information.

Meyer vs Ward

Objection 9

The mousetrap isn’t really irreducibly complex

Remove the base of Behe’s mousetrap and it still traps mice. IC also wrongly assumes evolution can only add parts sequentially, ignoring loss-of-parts, duplication, change of function and gradual modification — all observed in the lab.

Robert Stovold (vs Lennox)

Gap — unanswered

Lennox concedes and changes the subject

Lennox grants Behe’s strong form is hard to prove and redirects to information theory; he never engages Stovold’s specific counter-mechanisms. The single best critique of IC in the corpus receives no rebuttal from the Christian side.

Objection 10

“DNA information” is a metaphor

Biological “information” is Shannon/syntactic, not the semantic kind we attribute to minds. The whole argument equivocates between two senses of the word.

Shannon-vs-semantic critique (see Q-1.8.5)

Gap — never engaged

The load-bearing premise goes undefended

Meyer’s entire edifice rests on DNA information being the same kind as a paragraph or software. Across all fifteen Meyer transcripts the equivocation objection is never raised or answered.

Objection 11

The search is not random — cumulative selection

The Wistar and Axe probabilities treat mutation as a blind random search. But selection cumulatively preserves favourable variants, and neutral networks make functional sequence space far more connected than the bike-lock model assumes.

Cumulative-selection reply; Tokuriki / Tawfik neutral networks

Gap — not engaged where it matters

Cited selectively, not answered

Meyer cites Tokuriki for his side (fold instability) but never engages the neutral-network literature against him, nor the cumulative-selection rebuttal to combinatorial-inflation arguments.

Aggregation gap

Objection 12

ENCODE’s “function” claim is contested

Graur and others argue ENCODE conflated biochemical activity with selected function — so the “80% functional” figure does not vindicate the ID junk-DNA prediction.

Graur et al. (2013)

Gap — never engaged

The best demarcation move rests on a contested premise

The junk-DNA predictive-success argument is Meyer’s strongest answer to the “unfalsifiable” charge, but it depends on ENCODE’s function estimates, which Graur disputes. Meyer does not address the critique in any transcript here.
