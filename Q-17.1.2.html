The question

Of the three positions — libertarian free will, compatibilism, hard determinism — which is defensible, and which can ground moral responsibility?

Where the paired node Q-17.1.1 asks the prior question of whether humans have free will at all, this node is about the taxonomy and the contest between the options. It presupposes that some sense of “freedom” is on the table and asks which account survives scrutiny.

The Christian apologists in this corpus are uniformly libertarian, and fight on two fronts: compatibilism is a verbal rescue that weakens “freedom” until it can no longer carry responsibility, and hard determinism is self-defeating and unliveable. The content that distinguishes this node from Q-17.1.1 is the engagement with John Martin Fischer’s semicompatibilism, the ledger-vs-accountability distinction, and the Libet neuroscience challenge with its dualist-interactionist rebuttal.

The convergent case — three evidence streams, not one syllogism

The corpus does not converge on a single deductive argument. It converges on a libertarian / anti-determinist strategy run across three independent streams. Each is contested separately; together they make both compatibilism and hard determinism expensive to hold.

Stream A · The trichotomy itself

  • “Determined or random” is a false dichotomy (Knechtle)
  • Reasons-responsive agency is a third option
  • The agent acts for reasons — so neither determined nor random
  • Libertarian / compatibilist / hard-determinist menu

Stream B · Compatibilism is too thin

  • Fischer’s reasons-responsiveness
  • The “ledger” view of responsibility
  • Verbally compatible with determinism only by weakening the concept
  • Fails to capture “liability to punishment or worthiness of praise”

Stream C · Determinism is unliveable

  • Ought-implies-can: “puppets have no obligations” (WLC)
  • Krauss: “act as if you have free will”
  • The Slovenia “become farmers” anecdote
  • The determinist keeps moralising (Coyne / Harris)

Dominant deployment. Craig (6 transcripts) carries the philosophical weight on all three streams — the only voice who engages compatibilism by name (Fischer, the ledger view) and the only one who rebuts the Libet experiments directly. Lennox supplies the reductio that radical determinism “destroys all meaning” and grounds libertarian freedom in Genesis. Knechtle supplies the folk-level false-dichotomy move, debating live against a compatibilist (Tom Jump) and a probabilistic determinist (Aron Ra). The convergence is on the conclusion — libertarian agency grounded in an immaterial soul — far more than on any shared argument.

How each expert deploys it

William Lane Craig 6 transcripts
The philosophical heavyweight — Fischer, the ledger, and Libet
Four moving parts
  • The mechanism — why materialism precludes freedom: mental states “just float along” on prior brain states with no causal efficacy of their own. If the mental is epiphenomenal, the will can do nothing.
  • Fischer’s semicompatibilism — gives “a tentative yes” that reasons-responsiveness could obtain under full physical determination, which is precisely why it fails as libertarian freedom.
  • The redefinition critique — a “ledger” view of responsibility is a verbal rescue; the question is the adequacy of the weakened concept, and it cannot capture “liability to punishment or worthiness of moral praise.”
  • Unliveability — the Slovenia “become farmers” anecdote + the ought-implies-can endnote-mining of Harris (“puppets do not have moral responsibilities”).
Positive view & the Libet rebuttal
  • Libertarianism grounded in substance dualism: the soul is “a free moral agent that can make decisions for reasons rather than just random acts of chance.” Even an atheist, he says, would be “better to be a dualist.”
  • Libet Response 1 (the veto / “free won’t”) — subjects retained the ability to veto the initiated action; Libet himself judged the results “fully compatible with the existence of free will.”
  • Libet Response 2 (the time lag is predicted) — on the Eccles model the soul uses the brain as an instrument “just as a pianist uses a piano”; finite-velocity neural signals mean awareness must lag the decision. Backed by Meixner; guarded against the “unconscious decision” misreading.
Signature move

The reframe: the most famous neuroscientific weapon against free will is converted into confirmation of the dualist model — the Libet data are “exactly what we ought to expect if dualism interactionism is true.”

John Lennox 1 transcript
The reductio + the Genesis grounding
Distinctive moves
  • The meaning reductio — if everything were determined “our conversation would be completely meaningless… every word would have been pre-programmed.” Radical determinism “defeats itself because it destroys all meaning.”
  • Freedom as precondition — God “gifted [us] the capacity to say yes or no,” which “creates the possibility of the most important things in life: love, morality.”
  • Genesis grounding — “the highest element in life is that capacity to choose,” dramatised in the single prohibition of Eden.
  • Even God respects it — Jesus’s lament over Jerusalem (“you wouldn’t”) evidences “a real Power of Choice” God does not override.
  • Positions himself as “neither an atheist determinist nor a Christian determinist” — libertarian on both fronts.
Tactical hallmark

Hostile witness: leans on the neuroscientist Peter Tse’s own quantum-indeterminism case — “a confirmation from a scientist that does not share my worldview” — plus Polkinghorne’s critique of radical determinism.

Cliffe Knechtle 1 transcript
The false-dichotomy move at folk level
Signature deployment
  • The false dichotomy — against “determined or random,” ordering pizza shows a third category: “I consult my past experience, I consult my taste buds… so I use my reason, but that’s not determinism.”
  • Responsibility presupposes freedom — “you hold others responsible… that’s why some of your relationships blow up in animosity and others blossom with love.”
  • The soul inference — “the only way there can be a soul and a real self is if there is more to reality than matter and energy.”
  • His opponents usefully model the non-libertarian options: Tom Jump (explicit compatibilism + the determined-or-random dilemma + fMRI prediction data); Aron Ra (probabilistic-spectrum view — “we have a percentage”).
Hostile-side tension

Aron Ra runs a “percentage” view of freedom yet elsewhere says he feels fully determined (“I would have made every single same choice”) — an internal instability on the atheist side that the libertarian case exploits.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

“Every choice is either determined or random”

Either a choice is determined by prior reasons — in which case it is not free — or it is determined by nothing, in which case it is random. Libertarian free will is incoherent.

Tom Jump (Knechtle debate); the classic luck/randomness dilemma

Strongest response

False dichotomy — agency is the third option

Knechtle: reasons-guided deliberation is neither blind compulsion nor a dice-roll. Craig: the soul is “a free moral agent that can make decisions for reasons rather than just random acts of chance.”

Knechtle pizza-shop false dichotomyCraig agent-causation (see gaps — named, not developed)

Objection 2

Compatibilism lets us keep choice without libertarian free will

Determinism is true, but we are still free, because what matters is how we are determined — by internal desire (free) versus external coercion (unfree).

Tom Jump; Walter Sinnott-Armstrong

Strongest response

A redefinition that cannot carry responsibility

Craig: you can always weaken the concept to fit determinism, but then the weakened concept must be assessed for adequacy — and it fails to capture moral responsibility as “liability to punishment or worthiness of moral praise.”

Craig adequacy-of-the-concept critique

Objection 3

Fischer — “reasons-responsiveness is enough for freedom”

On semicompatibilism, an agent is free if suitably reasons-responsive: if you are persuaded by reasons, that counts as a free decision, even if physical causes produced your assent.

John Martin Fischer (raised by a questioner)

Strongest response

“Tentative yes” — which is exactly the problem

Craig concedes reasons-responsiveness could obtain under determinism — and that is precisely why it is inadequate as libertarian freedom: it is compatible with the agent being “entirely determined and caused by purely physical states.”

Craig Defenders Q&A concession-as-critique

Objection 4

The “ledger” view evades accountability objections

Treat moral responsibility as a record of good and bad marks rather than accountability, and it survives under determinism without requiring the ability to do otherwise.

Accountability-vs-ledger literature (raised by a questioner)

Strongest response

A tally is not responsibility

Craig: a moral-ledger record “just isn’t adequate to what we would talk about when we mean moral responsibility” — responsibility has to be more than a kind of ledger.

Craig same redefinition critique

Objection 5

Libet proves the brain decides before “you” do

A readiness signal appears in the brain ~515ms before the finger moves, but conscious awareness of the decision only ~200ms before. The brain “decides” before consciousness does — proof of determinism or materialism.

Benjamin Libet’s readiness-potential result, cited by determinists

Strongest response

Two-pronged — veto, then turn the datum

(1) Libet’s own veto finding: conscious control survives the readiness potential, and Libet judged it compatible with free will. (2) The signal-before-awareness lag is predicted by dualism-interactionism — finite-velocity neural signals (Eccles’ pianist/piano; Meixner) — so the datum confirms rather than refutes the soul.

Craig veto + the “exactly what we should expect” reframe

Objection 6

“Then the Libet decision is unconscious / not really free”

If awareness lags the decision, the decision was made unconsciously — which is no better for libertarian agency than determinism.

Anticipated misreading of Response 2

Strongest response

The decision is conscious; only its registration lags

Craig pre-empts this explicitly: the decision “is conscious but it just takes a little while” to register. Meixner: an informed decision needs only prior consciousness of the relevant facts, not simultaneous consciousness of the deciding.

Craig / Meixner the “not unconscious” guardrail

Objection 7

Free will is a mystery independent of materialism

Agent-causation can’t escape the randomness worry, and theism doesn’t dissolve it either — so libertarian free will is mysterious for reasons that have nothing to do with whether materialism is true.

Peter van Inwagen (raised by a questioner)

Strongest response

Concede, then reframe via reasons-responsive agency

Craig grants this is probably right about van Inwagen’s reasoning, then reframes: the worry dissolves if the immaterial substance is treated as “a free moral agent that can make decisions for reasons rather than just random acts of chance.”

Craig “that’s a good point” + agent reframe

Objection 8

Determinism undermines responsibility — yet its defenders keep moralising

Harris and Coyne deny moral responsibility (“a social construct, not an objective reality”) while filling their work with moral indignation at jihadists, oppressors, and the religious.

WLC’s internal critique of Harris and Coyne

Strongest response

Ought-implies-can: puppets have no obligations

Craig lets the lived moralism rebut the metaphysics: “puppets do not have moral responsibilities, machines have no moral obligations.” Harris’s own endnotes concede the point, and he never responds to it across three speeches.

Craig endnote-mining internal critique

Objection 9

Behaviour modification still works on determinism

Determinism doesn’t preclude punishment-as-deterrent or rehabilitation — you can still “reprogram the robots,” so the practical stakes of denying free will are low.

Coyne / Harris (via WLC commentary)

Strongest response

Reprogramming is engineering, not morality

Kevin Harris (with WLC): if it is all reprogramming, there is no morality left — and a Hitler or Stalin in charge of the reprogramming has done nothing “wrong” on that view.

Kevin Harris / Craig reductio on behaviour modification

Objection 10

Radical determinism is simply forced on us by physics

Whatever our intuitions, a closed physical universe leaves no room for an undetermined will. Determinism is the default that science requires.

Anticipated (physicalist default)

Strongest response

Self-defeating — and the physics is contested

Lennox borrows the neuroscientist Peter Tse’s quantum-indeterminism case and Polkinghorne’s critique: “radical determinism… defeats itself because it destroys all meaning,” including the determinist’s own argument.

Lennox Tse + Polkinghorne, reductio

Objection 11

The stronger neuroscience — Soon et al. / Schurger

The 2008 Soon fMRI studies claim up to ~7–10 seconds of predictive lead time on a choice; Schurger reinterprets the readiness potential as stochastic accumulation toward a threshold. Both go well beyond Libet’s original ~550ms paradigm.

Soon et al. (2008); Aaron Schurger

Gap — not engaged

Rebuttal calibrated only to the classic result

Craig answers only Libet’s ~550ms paradigm. The dualist time-lag reply scales in principle, but a multi-second predictive window puts real pressure on the “finite neural velocity” explanation (~550ms is plausibly signal-propagation; several seconds is not). Sound against the experiment he names; weaker against the literature he does not.

Persistent gaps in the corpus

Gap 1

Agent-causation named but never developed

The luck/randomness dilemma is the single most important pressure point on libertarianism.

Gesture, not account

Craig identifies the solution — the soul acts for reasons, so its choices are neither determined nor random — but never gives the positive account of agent-causation that would actually rebut “either determined or random.” Left as a gesture.

Aggregation gap

Gap 2

Compatibilism never engaged on its strongest merits

The redefinition critique assumes libertarian freedom is required for responsibility.

Best replies untouched

No engagement with Frankfurt-style cases (detaching responsibility from the ability to do otherwise) or the Strawsonian reactive-attitudes account. Against Sinnott-Armstrong, WLC simply assumes a soul is needed for libertarian agency without defending the move.

Aggregation gap

Gap 3

Causal status of the readiness potential unadjudicated

Is the readiness potential the cause of the decision, or a correlated build-up?

Sidestepped

Craig leans on the veto finding rather than contesting the causal status of the readiness potential directly — an available move he does not make.

Aggregation gap

Gap 4

Determinist instability exploited, not theorised

Aron Ra holds a “percentage” view yet feels fully determined; Coyne/Harris moralise while denying responsibility.

Rhetorical gift, not analysis

The corpus treats these tensions as debate-winning gifts but never develops why determinism characteristically produces this instability between stated metaphysics and lived practice.

Aggregation gap

Gap 5

Soon / Schurger successor literature

See Objection 11 — the empirical front is fought only on Libet’s original ground.

The most consequential gap

The whole node’s neuroscience rebuttal is anchored to a single 1980s result. The aggregator should flag any deployment of the Libet reply as needing a supplement for the stronger fMRI and stochastic-accumulation successors (tracked at Q-5.6.8).
