Can a Christian lose salvation?

The sharp edge of the perseverance debate — staged as a genuine Calvinist–Arminian dispute. Three experts; and a verdict that is neither, resting on the distinction between what a believer can do and what a believer will do.

The question

Can a person who has been genuinely born again forfeit salvation by falling away — or is the regenerate believer eternally secure?

This is the central dispute of the doctrine of perseverance, and the corpus treats it as a genuine intramural Christian disagreement, not a believer-versus-skeptic exchange. A confident answer settles whether the warning passages of Hebrews 6 and 10 describe a real danger to real believers or a hypothetical aimed at nominal ones.

The three voices do not line up neatly. McLatchie and the Knechtles lean toward eternal security; Craig — the dominant voice, with three lecture transcripts — argues the opposite, and argues it as a Molinist, producing a position that is neither standard Calvinism nor standard Arminianism. The whole debate turns on one distinction Craig presses relentlessly: the difference between what a believer can do and what a believer will do.

The competing positions — a cumulative dialectic, not a single syllogism

This is not one argument with agreed premises but a three-cornered exchange. The experts converge on the outcome (the elect will in fact persevere) while dividing sharply on the modality (whether a true believer genuinely could fall). The pivot is Craig’s modal-vs-de-facto distinction: eternal security says the believer cannot fall; Arminianism says he can, and some do; Craig says he can but, providentially, will not.

Eternal security · Reformed-leaning

  • Carried by McLatchie; Stuart Knechtle in part
  • The regenerate cannot finally fall
  • Golden chain: John 6, Romans 8, Philippians 1
  • Perseverance is a condition God Himself fulfils
  • Apostate either was never sincere or returns

Conditional perseverance · Arminian

  • Expounded by Craig (Part 19), in its own voice
  • A born-again believer can lose salvation
  • Apostasy = renouncing Christ, taken at face value
  • Entry by faith; exit by repudiating faith
  • Romans 11 grafted branches genuinely broken off

Molinist synthesis · Craig’s verdict

  • Apostasy is genuinely possible (modal)
  • God orders warnings + grace so the elect persevere
  • The elect will freely persevere (de-facto)
  • “Can fall” ≠ “will fall”
  • Guarantee located in middle knowledge, not irresistible grace

How each expert frames it

William Lane Craig 3 transcripts
The architect of the Molinist verdict
Signature moves
  • Modal vs de-facto distinction. Grants the Arminian that a regenerate believer genuinely can apostatise — but on his Molinist account God so arranges warnings and gifts of grace that the elect will in fact freely persevere. A real possibility that providentially never actualises.
  • Arminian case in its own voice (Part 19). The entry/exit symmetry is load-bearing: “just as individuals become members of the group by placing their faith in Christ, so they can fall away… by renouncing Christ.” Developed via Romans 11 and Robert Shank’s Elect in the Son.
  • “Molinism disguised as Calvinism.” The Calvinist who treats the warnings as a means of grace has smuggled in counterfactuals of creaturely freedom — if the warnings work by motivating free perseverance, that just is middle knowledge wearing a Calvinist label.
  • Schreiner as hostile witness. Enlists Reformed scholar Thomas Schreiner — who holds the warnings are real means of preservation — to concede that the warnings address real believers and do real work. The opponent’s admission is the evidence.
  • Apostasy is renunciation, not stumbling (Part 10). The present-tense verbs of 1 John 3:6–9 exclude a settled lifestyle of sin, not every fall; 1 John 1:8 rules out perfectionism. Fixes apostasy as deliberate departure, not carnal sin.
The verdict in one line

Neither “eternally secure” (Calvinist) nor “genuinely losable, and lost by some” (Arminian): a believer can fall, but the elect will not, because God’s middle knowledge guarantees a free perseverance. The de-facto guarantee does the Calvinist’s work without irresistible grace.

Pastoral landing (Part 21)

Defuses the contentious doctrine into three view-neutral duties for “Calvinist, Arminian, or just plain confused”: self-examination, gathering for encouragement (“I tremble, frankly, when I hear young Christians say I don’t like church”), and never writing off a backslider — “only God knows his heart.”

Jonathan McLatchie 1 transcript
Eternal-security cumulative case — with a conditionality twist
Distinctive moves
  • The golden-chain texts. John 6:39, 44 (“I will raise him up on the last day”); Romans 8:29–30 (unbroken from foreknowledge to glorification); Philippians 1:6 (God completes the work He begins).
  • The intercession argument. If salvation can be lost, the Son interceding as mediator has His prayer rejected by the Father — which McLatchie finds “theologically problematic.”
  • The Acts 27 shipwreck analogy — his signature. Paul is told by an angel that all aboard will be spared, yet tells the sailors they “must remain with the ship in order to be saved.” Salvation is “already guaranteed by God” and yet “contingent and conditional” on perseverance.
  • Explicitly flags the issue as intra-Christian: “this might be an issue on which panelists disagree.”
Convergence worth noting

McLatchie’s “conditional yet guaranteed” sits remarkably close to Craig’s de-facto claim — both say the elect will persevere through a real condition. The live difference is purely modal: McLatchie’s framing implies the believer cannot finally fall; Craig insists he genuinely can.

Stuart Knechtle 1 transcript
Folk-level — the sincerity criterion
Signature deployment
  • Hold John 10 and Matthew 7 together. “No one can pluck them out of my hand — so total security in the faith,” set against “I never knew you. Depart from me… even though you did all these good works.”
  • The sincerity criterion. “Were you ever sincere? And do you remain sincere? And if you fall away, do you come back sincerely?” — re-framing once-saved-always-saved as a question about the genuineness of faith.
  • The cheap-grace warning. Lady Gaga’s “born this way” as an emblem of grace without truth — “that’s chief [cheap] grace… and that’s what we see in our culture today.”
  • Truth-and-grace closer. “Jesus came to meet you where you’re at, but he’s not going to leave you where you’re at.”

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

The Hebrews 6/10 warnings teach that real believers can lose salvation

Scripture repeatedly makes salvation conditional on perseverance and warns of falling away. If those warnings address genuine believers, then loss of salvation must be a real possibility.

Arminian exegesis; warning-passage literature

Strongest response

Conditional — but the condition is guaranteed

McLatchie concedes scripture teaches conditional perseverance, then resolves it via the Acts 27 analogy: conditional yet guaranteed. Craig agrees the warnings are real and address believers — that is his modal claim — but the de-facto guarantee means the elect heed them. Full treatment at Q-9.4.3.

McLatchie Acts 27 conditional-yet-guaranteedCraig warnings real, but heeded

Objection 2

John 10:28–29 / Romans 8 secure the individual believer absolutely

“No one is able to snatch them out of my hand” reads most naturally as a promise to the individual believer, not merely to a group — an unconditional guarantee against ever falling.

Calvinist / eternal-security exegesis

Resolved by concession, not rebuttal

Re-routed through corporate election

Craig (Part 19) absorbs the security texts into a corporate reading — the group is unsnatchable; the individual is secure “insofar as he remains grafted in.” He does not engage the natural individual reading of “my hand” on its own terms. Stuart holds John 10 alongside Matthew 7 via sincerity rather than choosing.

Craig corporate-election re-routingHypothetical authorial concession

Objection 3

If salvation can be lost, the Son’s intercession is rejected by the Father

The Son intercedes as our mediator. If a believer for whom He prays is nonetheless lost, the Father has rejected the Son’s prayer — a theologically intolerable result.

Used by the eternal-security side (McLatchie)

A genuine cost of the Arminian view

Blunted, not directly rebutted

Treated as a real theological cost of holding that believers can fall. Craig does not rebut it head-on, but his Molinism arguably defuses it: the elect, whom the Son intercedes for, do in fact persevere, so the intercession is never finally frustrated.

McLatchie intercession argumentCraig de-facto guarantee answers it

Objection 4

The apostate “never had it to begin with”

When an ostensibly authentic Christian falls away, the Calvinist says he was only ever a nominal believer — unregenerate all along — so no genuine salvation was lost.

Calvinism (the Part 18 foil)

Strongest response

Take apostasy at face value

Craig (Part 19): the Arminian simply rejects the redescription. Romans 11’s grafted branches are genuinely broken off; Shank reads apostasy as the loss of a real possession. The direct rebuttal of the “nominal Christian” move is developed in the assessment.

Craig Romans 11 + Shank

Objection 5

The warnings are a Calvinist means of grace, not proof believers can fall

A Calvinist can affirm the warnings are real and addressed to believers — they are simply the instrument God uses to keep the elect from falling. So the warnings do not establish that anyone genuinely could be lost.

Thomas Schreiner, Reformed exegesis

Strongest response (internal critique)

“Molinism disguised as Calvinism”

Craig: treating warnings as the instrument that causes free perseverance presupposes counterfactuals of creaturely freedom — God using His knowledge of how believers would respond. That just is Molinism. Schreiner is enlisted as a hostile witness conceding the warnings address real believers.

Craig internal critique + Schreiner hostile witness

Objection 6

Doesn’t the new birth then demand sinless perfection? (1 John 3:9)

If “no one born of God commits sin,” then either Christians never sin (false) or the doctrine collapses into perfectionism — and any believer who sins has fallen away.

Internal puzzle within 1 John

Strongest response

Verb aspect — lifestyle, not every fall

Craig (Part 10): the present-tense Greek verbs exclude a settled lifestyle of sin, not isolated falls; 1 John 1:8 rules out perfectionism. The regenerate stumble, confess (1:9), and are cleansed. Apostasy is deliberate renunciation, not carnal sin — which dissolves the carnal-Christian tension at Q-10.4.2.

Craig habitual-present reading

Objection 7

A fruitless profession still counts — “I prayed the prayer”

Someone who made a decision for Christ as a child is saved for life regardless of subsequent fruit; grace is unconditional, so the changed-life expectation is legalism.

Free-grace / cheap-grace

Strongest response

No fruit, no right to assurance

Craig: the altar-call responder who “lived like the devil ever since” has “no right” to assurance — a true new birth ought to work itself out in a changed life. Stuart: the sincerity test (“were you ever sincere?”) and the Lady Gaga “chief grace” warning.

Craig fruit criterionStuart Knechtle sincerity test

Objection 8

Conditional security destroys assurance

If a genuine believer can fall away, no one can ever be sure of final salvation — the doctrine corrodes the very assurance the gospel is meant to give.

Pastoral / Calvinist worry

Reframed pastorally, not closed

Assurance as a practice, not a proof

Craig (Part 21) redirects to self-examination, assembly, and refusing to write anyone off — “only God knows his heart.” He argues the reverse (that the Calvinist reading undermines assurance) in Part 20, which this corpus does not capture. The worry is reframed rather than dissolved.

Persistent gaps in the corpus

Gap 1

The individual-security texts are conceded, not rebutted

Craig’s handling of John 10:28–29 and the individualistic reading of Romans 8 works by re-routing them through corporate election — the group is unsnatchable, the individual only conditionally so.

The strongest eternal-security prooftext, left unengaged on its own terms

Gap — the most consequential

Resolved by hypothetical authorial concession

The corpus grants the corporate frame rather than defeating the natural individual reading of “no one is able to snatch them out of my hand.” In Part 19 the security texts are simply “absorbed into the corporate reading” — a concession move, not a rebuttal.

Aggregation gap

Gap 2

The habitual-present reading of 1 John 3 is contested

Craig’s whole definition of apostasy-as-renunciation (and the exclusion of carnal sin) rests on the verb-aspect argument, presented as the plain meaning of 1 John 3:6–9.

Debated exegesis — some read the passage perfectionally or eschatologically

Gap — one reading among several

A standard solution, but not the only one

The habitual-present is a respectable but disputed reading; Craig flags no controversy. Acceptable for a lay class, but the load-bearing definition of apostasy here inherits whatever uncertainty attaches to the aspectual argument.

Aggregation gap

Gap 3

An epistemic tension inside Craig’s own position

Part 20 confidently identifies the category of the genuine apostate; Part 21 insists “we can’t know if that person is apostate or not; only God knows his heart.”

Internal to Craig’s Defenders lectures

Gap — real category, no operational test

Undetectable in practice

The apostate category is metaphysically real but, by Craig’s own admission, practically undetectable — leaving the doctrine without any test for who has actually crossed the line of no return. The two lectures sit in unresolved tension.

Aggregation gap

Gap 4

The modal claim is asserted, not staged against the intercession argument

McLatchie’s intercession objection is a real cost of holding that believers can fall. Craig’s Molinism plausibly answers it, but the two experts never engage each other directly.

A rebuttal the corpus implies but never performs

Gap — the rebuttal is left implicit

The two voices pass without meeting

A de-facto guarantee (the elect persevere) would answer the intercession worry, but no transcript stages Craig’s modal view explicitly against McLatchie’s objection. The strongest possible synthesis is available but never assembled in the source material.

Canonical quotes

Just as individuals become members of the group by placing their faith in Christ, so they can fall away from that group by renouncing Christ. By ceasing to have faith in Christ and repudiating Christ, they are then no longer members of this elect group, and therefore forfeit their salvation. William Lane Craig · expounding the Arminian case, Defenders Part 19
Once a person has been regenerated and declared righteous by God, that person can never fall away, and he is therefore eternally secure. If you do see someone who… falls away from the faith, the Calvinist will say that that person was really only a nominal Christian. William Lane Craig · stating the Calvinist foil he rejects, Part 19
No one who is born of God just goes on repeatedly committing sin, living a life of sin… that doesn’t mean that he will never fall, but when he does fall he can bring that to God in confession and be forgiven and cleansed. William Lane Craig · apostasy is lifestyle-renunciation, not every fall, Part 10
Paul is told by an angel that no one will perish, that everyone will be spared from the shipwreck, and yet Paul tells the sailors on that boat that you must remain with the ship in order to be saved. So their salvation is already guaranteed by God; nonetheless their salvation is contingent and conditional upon them staying with the boat. Jonathan McLatchie · the Acts 27 conditional-yet-guaranteed analogy
If we can lose our salvation then the Son’s prayers are not answered, because the Son as our mediator is making intercessory prayers before the Father — and you would have to say the Son is interceding on our behalf but the Father is rejecting the Son’s prayer request, which I think is theologically problematic. Jonathan McLatchie · the intercession argument
