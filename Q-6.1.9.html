Is atheism psychologically motivated?

The diagnostic counter-move to “I just follow the evidence” — and the corpus’s most double-edged argument, strongest when it owns its own symmetry and weakest when it slides into ad hominem.

The question

When a person rejects God, is that the conclusion of dispassionate inquiry — or is it driven by motive: the wish for moral autonomy, the will not to be accountable, the desire that God not exist?

This is the apologetic answer to the atheist self-image of evidential neutrality. It is diagnostic, not evidential: it does not try to prove God exists, it tries to show that the atheist’s professed neutrality is itself a fiction and that motivated reasoning is operating beneath the stated reasons.

The move is double-edged by design, and that is its central peril. If unbelief can be psychologised (“you’re running from accountability”), so can belief (“you’re afraid of death”). The strongest deployments here — Jones, Craig — concede the symmetry openly and turn it into the point. The weakest — Knechtle, before a sophisticated interlocutor — deploy it as near-ad-hominem and get caught.

The case structure — three converging streams, not a syllogism

This is not a deductive argument but a cumulative diagnostic case, assembled from three independent kinds of evidence, each contributed by a different expert. Crucially the corpus presents it as a defeater for the atheist’s neutrality claim, never as a proof of theism.

Distinctive moves
  • Whatever Coyne and Harris use to indict religion — confirmation bias, repressing disconfirming evidence — applies with equal force to atheism.
  • Asymmetry exposed: atheists redirect Stalin and Mao away from atheism — “exactly the same thing” Coyne and Harris forbid religious people from doing.
  • The “outlandish belief” charge: atheists accept that the universe “just popped into being uncaused out of nothing, which is worse than magic,” to avoid theism.

Lee Strobel2 transcripts
Autobiographical case study — wish-fulfilment in reverse
Distinctive moves
  • The causal chain: atheism → “no accountability” → “the most logical way… would be as a hedonist” → a “self-destructive” life → “a lot of rage.”
  • Folds in the Feuerbachian projection thesis — offered hostilely against his former self (people “made up” heaven “because they’re afraid of death”).
  • “I tend to be logical, I tend to be rational” positions hedonism as the consistent atheist outworking.
  • Conversion as reverse-evidence: investigated against the grain of self-interest; character change is ongoing confirmation.
  • Self-protective disclaimer, twice: “I’m not saying all atheists are like this… I’m just saying for me.”
Cliffe Knechtle1 transcript
Criminal-and-police diagnosis — and a cautionary tale
Signature deployment
  • Criminal-and-police analogy: atheists “do not find god for the same reason that a criminal doesn’t find the police — they’re running away.”
  • Anecdotal confession as evidence: “Harvard, Stanford, junior college” friends who admit moral autonomy is the motive.
  • Self-inclusion as a disarming device: “I understand that I want to run away from God.”
Tactical liability

Against Baggini this is the most fraught moment of the exchange. Baggini calls it an ad hominem, flags the symmetry, and denies the empirical claim (“that’s not my experience at all”). Knechtle retreats to “I did not say you, sir” and never engages the methodological critique.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

This is just an ad hominem — motive doesn’t bear on truth

Diagnosing why someone disbelieves says nothing about whether their disbelief is correct. The argument commits the genetic fallacy.

Julian Baggini (vs Knechtle)

Partially handled

Owned by Jones / Craig, fumbled by Knechtle

Jones and Craig reframe the symmetry as evidential (the refusal to admit bias is the tell). But Knechtle himself only retreats to “I didn’t mean you” and never answers the methodological charge. The deeper limit — that the move can only neutralise neutrality, never prove theism — is never marked.

Jones / Craig reframe symmetryKnechtle does not engage

Objection 2

You can’t psychologise unbelief without psychologising belief too

If atheists are “running from accountability,” Christians are “running from mortality.” The diagnosis cuts both ways and so cancels out.

Baggini; implicit throughout

Strongest response

Yes — exactly, and that is the point

Jones: “as a Christian I accept that is true of me too… maybe I’m subconsciously suppressing the truth of atheism.” The bias is universal; what is revealing is the atheist’s refusal to concede it. Symmetry conceded as a feature, not a bug.

Jones owns the symmetryCraig goose-and-gander

Objection 3

“I just follow the evidence — you can’t force belief”

The honest atheist insists belief is involuntary: show me sufficient evidence and I’ll believe; I simply haven’t been shown it.

Generic atheist (Aron Ra named as a case)

Strongest response

Empirically false — for everyone

Jones: motivated-reasoning research and Hume’s “reason is a slave to the passions” show this self-image is false of all humans. Hostile-witness use of atheist-friendly psychology: the claim is either ignorance of the research or self-deception.

Jones psychology + Hume

Objection 4

Not all atheists live like that or are like that

Plenty of atheists are moral, fulfilled, and untroubled by guilt. The hedonism-and-rage picture is a caricature.

Implicit; anticipated by the speakers

Strongest response

Hedonism is the consistent outworking, not a description

Strobel pre-empts explicitly (“I’m not saying all atheists think this way… I tend to be logical”), framing hedonism as where the premises lead if followed through, not as a universal sociological claim.

Strobel consistency framing

Objection 5

Atheism didn’t cause Stalin or Mao — those were political pathologies

The atheism of the great twentieth-century killers was incidental; the real drivers were ideology, power, and circumstance.

Standard atheist redirection

Strongest response

That is the very move you forbid believers

Craig: this is “exactly the same thing” Coyne and Harris attack religious people for — redirecting causation away from the worldview. The double standard is the tell: atheists grant themselves the deflection they deny others.

Craig parity / internal critique

Objection 6

The confession is anecdotal — “your atheist friends” prove nothing

A handful of self-reporting friends, and one man’s autobiography, are not data. Other atheists report the opposite.

Baggini: “that’s not my experience at all”

Gap — largely unanswered

Met only by counter-anecdote

Knechtle offers no method beyond personal report; Baggini’s counter-anecdote meets it on equal footing. Only Jones escapes this by appealing to research rather than acquaintance — but he cites the literature generally, naming no study.
