The question

Is the rise of modern science best explained as a fruit of Christian theology, or did science emerge in spite of Christianity — or merely alongside it by accident?

This is a historical question with apologetic teeth. If the “warfare” narrative is right, Christianity is the enemy science had to defeat, and faith survives only as a relic the laboratory has outgrown. If the opposite is true — that the doctrine of a rational Creator was the conceptual precondition for the scientific revolution — then the New Atheist framing inverts the actual record, and the discipline supposed to have buried God turns out to be one of his cultural offspring.

The argument is abductive — an inference to the best explanation of a striking fact: modern science erupted once, in one civilisation (Christian Western Europe, 16th–17th centuries), among believers, and did not erupt in technologically superior civilisations — notably China — that lacked the unifying concept of a single rational Creator.

The convergent argument — inference to best explanation

Evidence

The pioneers of modern science — Galileo, Kepler, Newton, Boyle, Maxwell, Faraday — were overwhelmingly believers, and said their faith motivated the work.

Observed phrasings. Lennox: belief in God “was the motor that drove it.” Meyer: “deeply religious men who believed that what they were doing was actually studying nature for the glory of God.” Stark census (via Lennox): of 52 founders, 60% devout believers, 38% conventional, one sceptic (Halley).

Best explanation

Belief in a rational Creator generated the expectation that nature is law-governed and intelligible — the precondition of doing science at all.

The lawgiver premise. Whitehead / C.S. Lewis: “men became scientific because they expected law in nature and they expected law in nature because they believed in a lawgiver.” Meyer’s two pillars: order (laws because a lawgiver) and intelligibility (we can read nature because we bear the image of its rational Author).

Mechanisms

Specific doctrines supplied the method: creation is not divine (so study, don’t worship), creation is contingent (so go and look, don’t deduce a priori), and humans are commissioned to name and explore.

Doctrinal drivers. Lennox: contingency drove Kepler to “just go and look” and break with Aristotle; the Genesis mandate to name the animals makes “taxonomy the fundamental intellectual discipline.” Barron: a desacralised world plus universal intelligibility. Meyer: contingent creation produced the empirical method “rather than just doing armchair philosophizing as many of the Greeks did.”

Negative control

The “everyone was religious then” reply is defeated by China: an equally sophisticated, equally religious civilisation that produced technology but not abstract science — because it lacked the single-Creator concept.

The control case. Joseph Needham, the Marxist sinologist, spent his life trying to explain on Marxist principles why abstract science never arose in China and concluded “the Chinese lack the unifying concept of a single Creator.” This isolates which theology matters, defeating the “religion as such” reply.

Conclusion

Therefore the best explanation of the rise of modern science is the Judeo-Christian doctrine of creation. The “science vs faith” framing is a historical inversion.

Scope and exposed flank. Lennox: “arguably it was the Judeo-Christian tradition that gave me my subject.” Meyer: “we’re now returning to the perspective that gave us science in the first place.” The case is cumulative, not demonstrative — its weak point is the inference from correlation (believers founded science) to causation (their belief was the cause), the objection Atkins and Krauss press.

Michael Ruse 1 transcript (atheist convergence)
The atheist who concedes the genealogy — then severs it
Distinctive moves
  • Full concession — “modern science owes basically its being to Christianity… to Anglican Christianity.” The rarest item in the corpus.
  • The root-metaphor shift (via Mary Hesse) — the governing metaphor moved from organism to machine as clocks proliferated.
  • “God became a retired engineer” — under the machine metaphor God turned explanatorily idle within practising science, even for believers like Newton and Boyle. The strongest internal challenge: grant the origin, sever the continuing dependence.
Why it matters

Ruse grants the whole historical thesis Lennox and Meyer defend, then opens the door to Dawkins’s “intellectually justified atheist.” This is the objection the corpus answers least well — see gaps.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

“Everyone was religious then”

It is no powerful argument that science originated with religious men when everyone in that period was religious. The founders’ faith is background noise, not a cause.

Generic; raised to Lennox at Yale

Strongest response

The China control isolates the variable

An equally religious, technologically superior civilisation that lacked the single-Creator concept produced technology but not abstract science. The relevant variable is which theology, not religion as such.

Lennox Needham negative control

Objection 2

Correlation, not causation

Belief in God may have motivated them, but that does not make God real — nor even show the belief was the cause rather than a cultural accompaniment.

Peter Atkins, Lawrence Krauss

Concession + reframe

It is cumulative evidence, not a proof

Lennox concedes the narrow point — “it doesn’t show it’s true… but it doesn’t show it’s false either” — and reframes the history as part of an inference to best explanation of explanatory power, not a demonstration.

Lennox explicit IBE framing

Objection 3

“The church was the only game in town”

The Christian church was the National Science Foundation of the age; if you weren’t associated with it you weren’t educated. Of course science arose from the only seat of money and power.

Lawrence Krauss

Strongest response

The deeper premise survives the monopoly

Again the Needham counter-instance; and the premise that “shot science forward” — the rational intelligibility of the universe — remains Christian-grounded regardless of who held institutional power.

Lennox intelligibility premise

Objection 4

“Thanks — now get out of the way”

Even granting Christian origins, science doesn’t need Christianity now. The metaphor shifted from organism to machine and “God became a retired engineer” — explanatorily idle within practising science.

Krauss; Ruse (Hesse machine-metaphor); Dawkins (“emancipation”)

Only partially answered

Mechanism vs agency — but the historiography stands

Lennox’s mechanism-vs-agency distinction answers the logical point (a sophisticated mechanism is evidence of, not a substitute for, a designer). But no expert engages Hesse’s root-metaphor history of how scientific practice shed God as an input.

Lennox mechanism vs agencyHesse historiography not met

Objection 5

The Galileo affair proves suppression

The church silenced Galileo and stood in the way of scientific progress — the paradigm case of religion versus science.

Standard conflict-thesis exhibit

Strongest response

The conflict reading is “mythological”

Galileo was a believer start to finish; first attacked by Aristotelian philosophers, not the church; his own PR blunder (the Pope’s view in “Simplicio’s” mouth) escalated it. Professional historians — Brooke, Russell — reject it as a paradigm of conflict.

Lennox Q-5.7.3 treatment

Objection 6

“The warfare narrative is just the obvious history”

Science and Christianity have always been at war; that is simply how the story runs.

New Atheist popular framing

Strongest response

A datable 19th-century invention

The warfare model is a Draper–White revisionist construction — “one book was called the history of the warfare between Christianity and science” — manufactured in the late 1800s and now sustained by Dawkins, Krauss, Hawking.

Meyer historiographical reframe

Objection 7

Newton kept his religion out of his science

Newton was a very religious man, but he took great pains to keep his ideas about intelligent design out of his scientific work.

US Civil Rights Commission opposite number

Strongest response

Refuted from the General Scholium

The Principia’s own General Scholium makes a design inference from planetary stability: “this most beautiful system… could only proceed from the council and dominion of an intelligent and powerful being.” The design reasoning is in the heart of the science.

Meyer Newton against the secular-Newton trope

Objection 8

Why not Islam? Why so late?

Islam is also a strict monotheism with a single rational Creator and a rich medieval science. If that concept is the cause, why no scientific revolution there — and why did Christianity take 1,500 years to produce one?

Comparative-civilisations objection (latent in the corpus)

Gap — not engaged

The specificity claim is left exposed

Lennox gestures at “Judeo-Christian tradition” but never engages the Islamic case; Barron excludes Islamic and Chinese contexts without argument. Neither the Islamic counter-case nor the medieval-gap / late-onset problem is addressed anywhere.

Aggregation gap

Persistent gaps in the corpus

Where the case is honestly weak:

1. The Islamic counter-case. A monotheism with a single rational Creator and a rich medieval science that did not birth the revolution — unengaged. This most directly threatens the specificity of the causal claim.

2. The delayed-onset / medieval-gap problem. If Christian theology is the cause, why a 1,500-year lag? The late-medieval precursors (Grosseteste, Buridan, Oresme, the Mertonians) go unmentioned.

3. Ruse’s “retired engineer.” An atheist grants the genealogy in full, then severs the continuing dependence via the organism-to-machine metaphor shift. Lennox answers the metaphysics but not Hesse’s historiography of practice.

4. Correlation-to-causation, unresolved. Lennox openly concedes the founders’ belief “doesn’t make God real”; the corpus never converts the correlation into demonstrated causation.

5. Newton’s heterodoxy. The load-bearing exemplar was an anti-Trinitarian (Arian-leaning). Critics can argue his “Christianity” was not orthodox — not addressed by any expert.

6. The continuity question. A Christian worldview being a sufficient incubator does not show the content of the theories required Christian premises. The corpus does not separate “Christianity made the scientists” from “Christianity made the science.”
