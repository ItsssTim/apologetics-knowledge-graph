The question

Can one and the same Jesus be at once truly God and truly man without contradiction — and if the union is genuinely mysterious, is that mystery a reason to reject it?

This is the central metaphysical claim of orthodox Christology: the hypostatic union — one person subsisting in two complete natures, divine and human. If Jesus is only God, the crucifixion, the human mediator and the real temptation collapse; if only man, the divinity claim, the atonement and the resurrection apologetic collapse.

The node draws two pressures. From Islam and unitarian objectors the charge is incoherence — God cannot die, cannot be ignorant, cannot have a God over him. From the honest believer the pressure is existential — how can a finite mind hold a doctrine it cannot fully explain, and how can a man who cannot sin be genuinely tempted? The corpus answers the first with a definitional distinction and the second by reframing mystery as the cost of the doctrine, not its disqualification.

Scope. This node defends the deity and full humanity of Christ. The christological heresies + the Chalcedonian definition are built as Q-8.1.2; Craig’s constructive Neo-Apollinarian model is Q-8.1.4. Both are cross-referenced rather than duplicated.

The argument structure — a cumulative biblical case, not a syllogism

Craig builds this the way the Defenders class does: an abductive cumulative case in which Scripture independently affirms both natures, and the apparent contradictions dissolve once each predicate is mapped to the right nature. The surprising half is the humanity stream — deliberately front-loaded, the finite limitations that make believers “squirm” converted into a doctrinal requirement secured by 1 John 4.

Stream A · The deity data

  • “In the form of God” (Phil 2:6)
  • Receives worship reserved for Yahweh — latreuo / pelach (Daniel 7)
  • Raises himself (John 2:19)
  • Eternal Father–Son intimacy
  • The Creator God in human flesh

Stream B · The humanity data

  • Physical birth (Luke 2:7)
  • Real temptation (Matt 4:1; Heb 4:15)
  • Growth in wisdom (Luke 2:52)
  • Hunger, fatigue, thirst (John 4:6; Mark 4:38)
  • Ignorance of the hour (Mark 13:32)
  • Torture & death (Luke 23)
  • Humanity essential (1 John 4:1–3)

Stream C · The coherence move

  • Two natures, not one nature
  • Predicates attach to a nature
  • Death = separation, not cessation
  • Kenosis = privilege, not attributes
  • Addition, not subtraction
  • Mystery owned, not dodged

How each expert deploys it

William Lane Craig 4 transcripts
The biblical-data architect
Distinctive moves
  • Two-natures data, humanity front-loaded: “the New Testament affirms both the deity and the humanity”; a read-aloud catalogue of the finite limitations — birth, temptation, growth, hunger, ignorance (Mark 13:32), death, moral perfecting (Heb 5).
  • Humanity is essential, not optional: 1 John 4:1–3 — deny the incarnation-in-the-flesh and “you are a heretic.” The anti-docetic anchor that closes the “deity only” gap.
  • Addition, not subtraction: the incarnation adds a human nature; it does not subtract divine attributes (rejects subtraction-kenosis — cross-ref Q-8.1.4).
  • The genuine-temptation constraint: converts a lay question into “one of the constraints on an acceptable model” — agent/object distinction; allure felt “in his human nature but not in his divine nature.”
  • Chalcedonian rule as instrument: rules out Lutheran consubstantiation — communicating divine ubiquity to the human nature “confounds the natures.”
Why he leads now

Craig’s four-part Doctrine-of-Christ unit makes him the fullest treatment of the data. He is the one expert who develops the full humanity at length — and the one who names the hard constraints (genuine temptation) a model must meet, deferring the model itself to Q-8.1.4.

Jonathan McLatchie 5 transcripts
The Chalcedonian anchor — against Islam
Signature moves
  • Parity argument: against the “chromosome” objection, locates a parallel two-natures concept inside Sunni Islam — the eternality of the Quran (eternal in tablets, instantiated in physical form over 23 years). The Sheikh never answers it.
  • Death is not cessation of existence: death is separation of soul from body. “Natures don’t die; human persons die.” The whole God-man died — the only way to say he died for our sins.
  • Kenosis guardrail: he laid aside the privilege of the divine attributes, not the attributes — the latter “would be heresy” (same anti-subtraction instinct as Craig).
  • Mediation (Hebrews sermon): “had to be fully man… had to be fully God” (Isaiah 59:16).
  • OT divine-human Messiah: Daniel 7 Son of Man receives latreuo / pelach; Psalm 110 shows him divine (v.5) and human (v.7).
Central clarification

“He is a man but not only a man.” Offered as the one-line answer to every Muslim “either/or” objection — each rests on reducing Christ to a single nature.

Cliffe Knechtle 2 transcripts
The mystery-owned framing
Signature moves
  • Voluntary self-limitation: “the eternal God stepping into space and time,” who “limited himself” while incarnate, then ascended “back outside of time” to see “all of time at a glance.”
  • B-theory of eternity without naming it — “God is eternal in that line” (cross-ref Q-7.3.2); the miracles are his evidence the limitation was voluntary.
  • Mystery owned, not dodged: concedes “beyond my ability to precisely explain” inside a substantive claim, not as an escape from one.
  • Universalising the mystery: “you don’t totally understand yourself… you don’t totally understand mom and dad” — the demand for total comprehension is one the objector applies to nothing else.
Distinctive angle

Knechtle supplies the corpus’s only existential treatment: not “is it coherent?” (McLatchie) or “what does Scripture say?” (Craig) but “how can you believe what you can’t fully explain?”

Preston Perry 2 transcripts
The self-resurrection wedge — vs JWs
Signature moves
  • Self-resurrection as proof of deity: resurrection requires having died; Scripture says Jesus raised himself; “how do you resurrect yourself?” A creature cannot.
  • Meets the JW reductio (“for three days there was no God”) by distinguishing Jesus dying in the flesh from the Trinity persisting in three persons — the same human/divine-nature split, at street level.
  • Functional (not ontological) submission; eternal Father–Son intimacy (“always… for all of eternity”).
  • Openly names the impasse — “that’s where we hit an impass” — as a deliberate rhetorical close.
Formulation caveat

Perry’s summary “one nature, two distinct persons” inverts the Chalcedonian formula (one person, two natures). Substance orthodox; wording loose. His clip defends deity only — now offset corpus-wide by Craig’s humanity catalogue.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

Chromosome argument — “either chimpanzee or human, never both”

Humans and chimps differ by chromosome count; the chromosomes make the difference. You cannot be two species at once, so Jesus cannot be God and man both.

Sheikh Inamullah Mumtaz

Strongest response

Two natures, not two species

The objection conflates two natures with one. God and man are not two species requiring chromosomal hybridity but distinct ontological categories — a divine nature added to a human nature requires no genetic fusion. Parried further by the Quran-eternality parity.

McLatchie two-natures clarification + parity

Objection 2

“God cannot be killed”

The eternal God cannot die; therefore the one who died on the cross cannot have been God.

Mansoor Ahmed, Paul Williams, Jehovah’s Witnesses

Strongest response

Death is separation, not cessation

In the Abrahamic tradition death is the separation of soul from body, not cessation of existence. “Natures don’t die; human persons die.” The divine nature did not die; the human body did; the whole God-man died as a person — the only way to say he died for our sins.

McLatchie death-concept critiqueSusic divine nature not killedPerry street-level variant

Objection 3

If the divine nature didn’t die, who ran the universe for three days?

If the divine nature persisted while the body lay dead, then either it was unconscious (impossible for God) or the death was not real.

Anticipated Muslim objection

Strongest response

Fully conscious throughout

The divine nature never died or lost consciousness: “the divine nature did not die; Christ was fully conscious” during the three days. Death does not entail cessation of existence, so there is no governance gap.

McLatchie consciousness through death

Objection 4

Mark 13:32 — “the Son does not know the hour”

Jesus says only the Father knows the day of his return, not even the Son. An ignorant being cannot be the omniscient God.

Sheikh Mumtaz; treated as humanity-data by Craig

Strongest response

Real ignorance is real humanity — with two readings of the tension

Craig presents the verse as straightforward humanity-data, not embarrassment. The deity tension resolves either way: (a) waking conscious knowledge veiled while subconscious / divine knowledge remained exhaustive; or (b) Augustine — “to know” can mean “to reveal,” so Jesus does not reveal the hour.

Craig frames it as dataMcLatchie subliminal-knowledgeSusic Augustine’s lexical reading

Objection 5

Christ has a God over him (1 Cor 11:3; Revelation)

“The head of Christ is God”; even the glorified Jesus speaks of “my God.” A being with a God over him is not God.

Paul Williams, Jehovah’s Witnesses

Strongest response

Functional subordination + flesh-relation

Functional subordination is compatible with ontological equality. Once the Word became flesh (John 1:14), the Father is naturally his God in the sense of flesh-relation (Jer 32:27, “God of all flesh”). The glorified Christ retains his humanity (Rev 22:16) — he never stops being a man, while also being fully God.

McLatchie flesh-relation + Rev 22:16Perry Trinitarian framing

Objection 6

Phil 2 — “he emptied himself,” so was not God on earth

If Christ emptied himself of divinity at the incarnation, he was not God while on earth.

Paul Williams

Strongest response

Privilege laid aside, not attributes — addition, not subtraction

Kenosis means he laid aside the privilege of the divine attributes (e.g. omnipresent geographic deployment), not the attributes themselves. To say he laid aside the attributes “would be heresy.” The incarnation adds a human nature; it does not subtract divine ones.

McLatchie kenosis guardrailCraig addition-not-subtraction

Objection 7

“If Jesus is God and died, then God was dead — for three days there was no God”

The JW reductio: who crucified Jesus, and who resurrected him? If Jehovah God died, then for three days there was no God.

Jehovah’s Witnesses

Strongest response

Died in the flesh; raised himself

Jesus died in the flesh while the Trinity persisted in three persons; he then raised himself — which a created being could not do. The self-resurrection forces the dilemma: either he died and rose, or he never died and the gospel collapses, or he is God.

Perry self-resurrection dilemma

Objection 8

Son of Man is a lesser power subject to the Ancient of Days

In Daniel 7 the Son of Man is distinct from and subject to the Ancient of Days — the “two-powers heresy” the rabbis condemned (Segal, Chilton). So he is exalted but not the Almighty.

Shabir Ally (citing Alan Segal, Bruce Chilton)

Strongest response

He receives worship reserved for Yahweh

The Daniel 7 Son of Man receives latreu&omacron; / pelach — worship reserved for Yahweh — rides the clouds (a Yahwistic act), and Daniel 7:27 applies “Most High” to him. He is divine, not merely exalted.

McLatchie Aramaic worship-verbs + Dan 7:27

Objection 9

Lutheran consubstantiation confounds the natures

Real presence in the Supper requires Christ’s body to be ubiquitous — so the communicatio idiomatum communicates divine ubiquity, invisibility and immateriality to his human nature.

Lutheran sacramentology

Strongest response

Confounding the natures is the Chalcedonian error

Communicating divine properties to the human nature “confounds the natures” — the very error Chalcedon prohibits. So consubstantiation fails on Christological grounds. (Does not touch Catholic transubstantiation, which uses substance-accident metaphysics.)

Craig Chalcedonian rule as instrument

Objection 10

Was Jesus genuinely tempted, or did temptation “bounce off like bullets off Superman”?

Scripture says he was “tempted in every way as we are” (Heb 4:15), yet “God cannot be tempted with evil” (James 1:13). If he is impeccable — no possible world in which he sins — the temptations seem to be empty showpieces, and he cannot sympathise as our high priest.

Class member, via Heb 4:15 / James 1:13 / Anselm

Partly answered — named as a constraint

Allure in the human nature; resolution deferred

Craig grants the entailment (impeccability is real), adopts the agent/object distinction (Satan is the agent; James 1:13 denies only that God can be the internally-moved object), and locates the felt allure “in his human nature but not in his divine nature.” But he leaves it open — how a lure can be genuine given no possible world of yielding is the deepest unresolved problem here (and the hinge to the Q-8.1.4 model).

Craig constraint named, only partially resolvedResolution → Q-8.1.4

Objection 11

How can one person bear genuinely incompatible properties?

Passible and impassible, omniscient and limited, mortal and immortal — granting two natures, how does a single person coherently possess contradictory predicates at once?

Modern analytic Christology (Morris, Flint, qua-predication debates)

Gap — largely unaddressed

Asserted, never defended here

Every expert asserts the predicate-to-nature mapping, which blocks the surface contradiction, but none engages the harder compositional-vs-instrumentalist / qua-predication question in this corpus. McLatchie “uses Chalcedonian language straight.” Craig’s constructive answer is deferred to Q-8.1.4 / Q-8.3.3.

Aggregation gap

Objection 12

Is the incarnation above reason or against reason?

The “mystery” move (“you don’t totally understand yourself”) is rhetorically strong, but does not distinguish legitimate mystery (truths above reason) from illegitimate appeal to mystery (truths against reason). Which is the incarnation?

Sharp philosophical interlocutor

Gap — line never drawn

Mystery asserted, not bounded

Knechtle universalises mystery powerfully but never draws the above-reason / against-reason line. A sharp interlocutor could press exactly here, and the corpus offers no demarcation.
