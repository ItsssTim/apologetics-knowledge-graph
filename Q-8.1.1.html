The question

Can one and the same Jesus be at once truly God and truly man without contradiction — and if the union is genuinely mysterious, is that mystery a reason to reject it?

This is the central metaphysical claim of orthodox Christology: the hypostatic union, codified at Chalcedon (451) — one person subsisting in two complete natures, divine and human, “without confusion, without change, without division, without separation.” If Jesus is only God, the crucifixion and the human mediator collapse; if only man, the divinity claim, the atonement and the resurrection apologetic collapse.

The node draws two pressures. From Islam and unitarian objectors the charge is incoherence — God cannot die, cannot be ignorant, cannot have a God over him. From the honest believer the pressure is epistemic — how can a finite mind hold a doctrine it cannot fully explain? The corpus answers the first with a definitional distinction and the second by reframing mystery as the cost of the doctrine, not its disqualification.

The convergent argument — one person, two natures

Premise 1

Christ is one person possessing two complete, distinct natures — fully God and fully man.

Observed variants. McLatchie (Chalcedonian): the same nature was not both God and man simultaneously — two natures in one person. Perry (imprecise): “one nature, two distinct persons” — a Trinitarian formula mis-applied to Christology; substance orthodox, formulation slips.

Premise 2

Predicates that seem contradictory attach to different natures, not to one nature.

The dissolving move. Death, ignorance, weakness, hunger and having-a-God attach to the human nature; eternality, creative power and worship-worthiness attach to the divine nature. McLatchie's one-liner: “He is a man but not only a man.”

Premise 3

The union is soteriologically necessary, not idle speculation.

The “had to be” support. McLatchie (Hebrews): a mediator “had to be fully man… and yet he also had to be fully God.” Perry: only God can raise himself, so the self-resurrection is itself evidence of deity.

Conclusion

Therefore the two-natures union is internally coherent; the “either/or” objections beg the question by presupposing a single nature.

Residual mystery. The epistemic remainder (Knechtle) is conceded openly and reframed as the universal condition of finite minds — “you don't totally understand yourself” — not a Christian-specific liability.

How each expert deploys it

Jonathan McLatchie 5 transcripts
The Chalcedonian anchor — dominant voice
Signature moves
  • Parity argument: against the “chromosome” objection, locates a structurally parallel two-natures concept inside Sunni Islam — the eternality of the Quran (eternal in tablets in paradise, instantiated in physical form to Muhammad over 23 years). The Sheikh never answers it.
  • Death is not cessation of existence: death is the separation of soul from body. “Natures don't die; human persons die.” The divine nature did not die; the whole God-man died — which is the only way to say the God-man died for our sins.
  • Kenosis guardrail: he laid aside the privilege of the divine attributes, not the attributes — the latter “would be heresy.”
  • Mediation (Hebrews sermon): grounds Chalcedon in soteriology — “had to be fully man… had to be fully God” (Isaiah 59:16).
  • Two solutions to Mark 13:32 ignorance — subliminal divine knowledge (McLatchie) and Augustine's “to know” = “to reveal” (Susic).
Central clarification

“He is a man but not only a man.” Offered as the one-line answer to every Muslim “either/or” objection — each rests on reducing Christ to a single nature.

Cliffe Knechtle 2 transcripts
The mystery-owned framing
Signature moves
  • Voluntary self-limitation: Jesus is “the eternal God stepping into space and time,” who “limited himself” to human capability while incarnate, then ascended “back outside of time” to see “all of time at a glance, past, present, future.”
  • B-theory of eternity without naming it — “God is eternal in that line” (cross-ref Q-7.3.2); miracles are his evidence the limitation was voluntary.
  • Mystery owned, not dodged: concedes “beyond my ability to precisely explain” inside a substantive claim, not as an escape from one.
  • Universalising the mystery: “you don't totally understand yourself… you don't totally understand mom and dad” — the demand for total comprehension is one the objector applies to nothing else.
Distinctive angle

Knechtle supplies the corpus's only existential (not metaphysical) treatment: not “is it coherent?” but “how can you believe what you can't fully explain?”

Preston Perry 2 transcripts
The self-resurrection wedge — vs JWs
Signature moves
  • Self-resurrection as proof of deity: resurrection requires having died; Scripture says Jesus raised himself; “how do you resurrect yourself?” A creature cannot.
  • Meets the JW reductio (“for three days there was no God”) by distinguishing Jesus dying in the flesh from the Trinity persisting in three persons — the same human/divine-nature split, at street level.
  • Functional (not ontological) submission; eternal Father–Son intimacy (“always… for all of eternity”).
  • Openly names the impasse — “that's where we hit an impass” — as a deliberate rhetorical close.
Formulation caveat

Perry's summary “one nature, two distinct persons” inverts the Chalcedonian formula (one person, two natures). Substance orthodox; wording loose. His clip defends deity only — the real humanity is never developed.

William Lane Craig 1 transcript
The Chalcedonian rule as instrument
Distinctive move
  • Uses the Chalcedonian watchword as a test: “you must not divide the person or confound the natures… one person in two natures.”
  • Rules out Lutheran consubstantiation: communicating divine ubiquity, invisibility and immateriality to Christ's human nature “confounds the natures and so is unacceptable christologically.”
  • The only extraction that deploys the two-natures rule as a discriminating instrument rather than a thesis to defend — showing the doctrine doing live work in a sacramental dispute.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

Chromosome argument — “either chimpanzee or human, never both”

Humans carry 46 chromosomes, chimps 48; the chromosomes make the difference. You cannot be two species at once, so Jesus cannot be God and man both.

Sheikh Inamullah Mumtaz

Strongest response

Two natures, not two species

The objection conflates two natures with one. God and man are not two species requiring chromosomal hybridity but distinct ontological categories — a divine nature added to a human nature requires no genetic fusion. Parried further by the Quran-eternality parity.

McLatchie two-natures clarification + parity

Objection 2

“God cannot be killed”

The eternal God cannot die; therefore the one who died on the cross cannot have been God.

Mansoor Ahmed, Paul Williams, Jehovah's Witnesses

Strongest response

Death is separation, not cessation

In the Abrahamic tradition death is the separation of soul from body, not cessation of existence. “Natures don't die; human persons die.” The divine nature did not die; the human body did; the whole God-man died as a person — the only way to say he died for our sins.

McLatchie death-concept critiqueSusic divine nature not killedPerry street-level variant

Objection 3

If the divine nature didn't die, who ran the universe for three days?

If the divine nature persisted while the body lay dead, then either it was unconscious (impossible for God) or the death was not real.

Anticipated Muslim objection

Strongest response

Fully conscious throughout

The divine nature never died or lost consciousness: “the divine nature did not die; Christ was fully conscious” during the three days. Death does not entail cessation of existence, so there is no governance gap.

McLatchie consciousness through death

Objection 4

Mark 13:32 — “the Son does not know the hour”

Jesus says only the Father knows the day of his return, not even the Son. An ignorant being cannot be the omniscient God.

Sheikh Mumtaz, Muslim apologetic

Strongest response

Two compatible solutions

(a) In his waking conscious knowledge certain truths were veiled while his subconscious / divine knowledge remained exhaustive; or (b) following Augustine, “to know” can mean “to reveal” — Jesus does not reveal the hour. Either suffices.

McLatchie subliminal-knowledgeSusic Augustine’s lexical reading

Objection 5

Christ has a God over him (1 Cor 11:3; Revelation)

“The head of Christ is God”; even the glorified Jesus speaks of “my God.” A being with a God over him is not God.

Paul Williams, Jehovah's Witnesses

Strongest response

Functional subordination + flesh-relation

Functional subordination is compatible with ontological equality. Once the Word became flesh (John 1:14), the Father is naturally his God in the sense of flesh-relation (Jer 32:27, “God of all flesh”). The glorified Christ retains his humanity (Rev 22:16) — he never stops being a man, while also being fully God.

McLatchie flesh-relation + Rev 22:16Perry Trinitarian framing

Objection 6

Phil 2 — “he emptied himself,” so was not God on earth

If Christ emptied himself of divinity at the incarnation, he was not God while on earth.

Paul Williams

Strongest response

Privilege laid aside, not attributes

Kenosis means he laid aside the privilege of the divine attributes (e.g. omnipresent geographic deployment), not the attributes themselves. To say he laid aside the attributes “would be heresy.”

McLatchie kenosis guardrail

Objection 7

“If Jesus is God and died, then God was dead — for three days there was no God”

The JW reductio: who crucified Jesus, and who resurrected him? If Jehovah God died, then for three days there was no God.

Jehovah's Witnesses

Strongest response

Died in the flesh; raised himself

Jesus died in the flesh while the Trinity persisted in three persons; he then raised himself — which a created being could not do. The self-resurrection forces the dilemma: either he died and rose, or he never died and the gospel collapses, or he is God.

Perry self-resurrection dilemma

Objection 8

Son of Man is a lesser power subject to the Ancient of Days

In Daniel 7 the Son of Man is distinct from and subject to the Ancient of Days — the “two-powers heresy” the rabbis condemned (Segal, Chilton). So he is exalted but not the Almighty.

Shabir Ally (citing Alan Segal, Bruce Chilton)

Strongest response

He receives worship reserved for Yahweh

The Daniel 7 Son of Man receives latreu&omacron; / pelach — worship reserved for Yahweh — rides the clouds (a Yahwistic act), and Daniel 7:27 applies “Most High” to him. He is divine, not merely exalted.

McLatchie Aramaic worship-verbs + Dan 7:27

Objection 9

Lutheran consubstantiation confounds the natures

Real presence in the Supper requires Christ's body to be ubiquitous — so the communicatio idiomatum communicates divine ubiquity, invisibility and immateriality to his human nature.

Lutheran sacramentology

Strongest response

Confounding the natures is the Chalcedonian error

Communicating divine properties to the human nature “confounds the natures” — the very error Chalcedon prohibits. So consubstantiation fails on Christological grounds. (Does not touch Catholic transubstantiation, which uses substance-accident metaphysics.)

Craig Chalcedonian rule as instrument

Objection 10

How can one person bear genuinely incompatible properties?

Passible and impassible, omniscient and limited, mortal and immortal — granting two natures, how does a single person coherently possess contradictory predicates at once?

Modern analytic Christology (Morris, Flint, qua-predication debates)

Gap — largely unaddressed

Asserted, never defended

Every expert asserts the predicate-to-nature mapping, which blocks the surface contradiction, but none engages the harder compositional-vs-instrumentalist / qua-predication question. McLatchie “uses Chalcedonian language straight” and does not engage it.

Aggregation gap

Objection 11

Did Christ have a complete human mind and soul? (Apollinarianism)

If the divine Logos replaced or stood in for the human mind, the “humanity” is incomplete — Apollinarianism. A node titled “fully God and fully man” must defend the real, complete humanity.

Patristic / systematic-theology objection

Gap — largely unaddressed

Humanity defended nowhere at depth

No expert defends a complete human mind/soul against Apollinarianism. Perry's clip defends deity only and never develops the real humanity at all — striking for this node.

Aggregation gap

Objection 12

Is the incarnation above reason or against reason?

The “mystery” move (“you don't totally understand yourself”) is rhetorically strong, but does not distinguish legitimate mystery (truths above reason) from illegitimate appeal to mystery (truths against reason). Which is the incarnation?

Sharp philosophical interlocutor

Gap — line never drawn

Mystery asserted, not bounded

Knechtle universalises mystery powerfully but never draws the above-reason / against-reason line. A sharp interlocutor could press exactly here, and the corpus offers no demarcation.
