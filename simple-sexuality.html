The fairness question

If God is love, why is homosexuality a sin?

It is one of the sharpest tensions people feel: 1 John says God is love, yet an old verse calls this an abomination. Before the real answer, two things have to be faced honestly, that loaded word, and the fact that the church has often handled this worse than almost anything else.

First / That word
Step 1 of 4
What the word abomination really means

That word doesn't mean what it sounds like.

So much of the sting is in the word abomination. It lands like God finds you personally revolting. That is a mistranslation of the tone, not just the term.

The word
The Hebrew behind it, to'evah, is broad: it means taboo or detestable, the language of an ancient holiness code (Leviticus 18:22). The very same label falls on things like dishonest scales and certain foods. It is not a special word reserved to say that you, personally, are uniquely disgusting to God. Read that way, it is being misheard.
Deeper case
And the serious Christian view does not even stand on that one shock-verse from a holiness code. It rests on a positive vision of what marriage is built for, which is step two. Leading with abomination is exactly how this argument gets turned into a caricature, by its defenders and its critics alike.
Owning it
And the most honest thing first: the church has often made this the headline sin, singled it out above all others, and treated gay people with a contempt you will not find anywhere in Jesus. By some accounts it is now the single biggest reason people leave the faith. That cruelty is a real wrong, and pretending it away would be its own dishonesty.
The theory you'll hear
Calling it an abomination is just old hatred with a Bible verse stapled on. In practice it has sometimes been exactly that, and that deserves to be named and refused. But the considered Christian view is not the placard at the protest. It is quieter and more careful, and as the next steps show, it asks every bit as much of straight people as of anyone else.
✓
Confidence: the shock-word is a poor place to start, and the church's cruelty here is real. The actual teaching is more careful, and far more even-handed, than the caricature.
Second / Whose rule is it?
Step 2 of 4
One ethic, not a rule for some

The standard falls on everyone, not just some.

The assumption underneath is that Christianity has a rule aimed at gay people. It does not. It has one vision of sex that asks a great deal of nearly everybody.

One standard
The Christian view is that sex belongs inside marriage, understood as a lifelong covenant between a man and a woman. That single standard asks exactly as much of an unmarried straight person, a divorced person, anyone single for life. A celibate gay believer and a celibate straight believer are in precisely the same position. It is not a rule for them, it is one vision for everyone.
On the list
And it is never singled out the way the church has singled it out. The New Testament lists same-sex activity right alongside greed, gossip, pride, drunkenness and straight immorality, the respectable sins most people commit without a second thought (1 Corinthians 6:9-11). As one apologist puts it, you do not go to hell for being gay any more than you go to heaven for being straight. That was never the dividing line.
Want vs act
It is worth being precise about what is and isn't condemned. The historic view is about what a person does, never the mere experience of attraction, which almost nobody chooses. God does not condemn the pull itself, nor the person who carries it. And wherever it came from, no one is judged for a desire they did not pick, only for what we all must weigh: what we do with the desires we actually have.
The theory you'll hear
God made me this way, so condemning it makes him a cruel hypocrite. Two things defuse the trap. First, the careful view does not say God designed anyone for same-sex union; in a world bent from its pattern, everyone's desires are pulled out of true in some direction, not gay people alone. Second, and more to the point, God condemns no one for an attraction they never chose. An unchosen pull is not a command to act, and all of us steward desires rather than simply obey them. Made this way and then damned for it was never the claim.
✓
Confidence: it is one demanding sexual ethic for everybody, not a rule for gay people. The same line is drawn for the single, the divorced and the straight.
Third / Why would love say no?
Step 3 of 4
Love is not the same as yes

Love isn't the same as saying yes to everything.

Underneath is the real engine of the question: if God loves us, why deny anyone something they want so deeply? Two answers, and they fall on all of us.

The design
The Christian no rests on a bigger yes. Marriage, on this view, is not only companionship; it is built to picture something, the faithful covenant love between Christ and his people, which is why its shape is written into the man-and-woman pattern from the start (Genesis 2:24). The claim is not that same-sex affection is uniquely wicked. It is that this particular thing was designed for a particular purpose.
Love is honest
And love is not the same as approval. The people who love you most are sometimes the ones who tell you the hard thing. Knechtle uses parenting: caring whether someone is truly flourishing is not less love, it can be more, and he is quick to add that some of his own closest friends are gay. A God who simply said yes to everything we wanted would not be more loving. He would just be more absent.
More than this
And the deepest claim is that you are not your sexuality. The culture says your romantic and sexual desires are the truest, deepest thing about you. Christianity says, to gay and straight alike, that they are not, that your real identity and worth sit in being made and loved by God, far underneath any attraction. That is meant as a relief, not an insult.
The theory you'll hear
Telling a gay person they must be celibate for life is simply cruel. This is the hardest form of the question, and it deserves honesty rather than a slick reply. It is a genuinely heavy calling, and Christianity does not pretend it is light. But it asks something costly of many people, the lifelong single, the unhappily married, and it insists the deepest human need was never sex but love and belonging, which it says are open to everyone in friendship, family and God. Hard, yes. Crueller than the alternative on offer? That is the real question.
✓
Confidence: the no flows from a design and from a love honest enough to tell the truth, not from disgust. And it lands on everyone's desires, because none of us is only what we want.
Fourth / Is God still love?
Step 4 of 4
The same door for everyone

God is love, and the door is the same for all.

Back to where the question began: 1 John, God is love. That is true, and it turns out not to be in tension with any of this, once you see what the love is actually holding out.

Honest love
A love that only ever affirmed would be flattery, not love. The God of 1 John loves people enough to tell them the truth and then to do something about it, at his own cost on a cross. That is a deeper thing than approval, and it is held out to every single person without exception.
No tiers
There is no special tier of sinner here. Everyone, gay and straight, falls short of the design, the straight world spectacularly so. Nobody walks up to God as the acceptable one looking down on the rest. Everyone comes on identical terms: not as the person who got it right, but as someone loved and forgiven who did not.
Welcome
Which means a gay person is made in God's image, loved by God, and welcomed on exactly the terms everyone else is. Whatever the church has too often communicated, the message itself was never stay away. It is come, like everyone else, and discover that you were always worth far more than your desires told you.
The theory you'll hear
So gay people are sinners headed for hell. No, and that was the first thing to fall. You do not go to hell for being gay any more than for being straight; the question is the one every person faces, what you do with God himself. Gay people are not a category of the damned. They are people, loved, and invited in on the same footing as the rest of us, all of whom needed grace and none of whom earned it.
✓
Confidence: God is love, and real love is honest, not merely permissive. The same grace, the same welcome and the same worth are held out to everyone, with no one a second-class case.
Putting it together
The bottom line

So how does a God of love call it sin?

So the tension eases once you stop reading it as a God repulsed by certain people. The actual claim is stranger and gentler: one demanding vision of sex that humbles everyone, a love too honest to merely flatter us, and a door held open to gay and straight on identical terms. You were never being told you are an abomination. You were being told, with all of us, that you are loved, and that you are far more than what you want.
