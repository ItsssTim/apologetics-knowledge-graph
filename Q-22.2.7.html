The origin of Satan & the fall of the angels

The doctrine everyone assumes the Bible teaches and almost no one can locate in it. William Lane Craig builds a cumulative case from clues — and refuses the three proof-texts the tradition leans on hardest. One expert, seven substantive treatments; doctrinal exposition, not debate.

The question

Does Scripture teach that Satan and the demons were originally good angels who rebelled by free will and fell — and if so, on what textual basis, given that no passage explicitly narrates it?

The origin of Satan is the doctrine everyone assumes the Bible teaches and almost no one can locate in it. There is no narrative of an angelic fall anywhere in Scripture — not even in Genesis 3, where the serpent simply appears, already opposed to God, with no account of where it or evil came from. Craig’s method is therefore explicitly a cumulative case from clues: “the best that we can do is to try to piece together various clues and intimations in Scripture.”

What makes this node distinctive is its two-sided structure. The first half is demolition — Craig refuses the two passages the popular tradition leans on hardest (Isaiah 14’s “Day Star” and Ezekiel 28’s “anointed cherub in Eden”) and the apocalyptic favourite (Revelation 12:4’s “third of the stars”), reading all three as eisegesis. The second half is construction — a numbered chain of genuine intimations (Job 1:6 → Luke 10:18 → 2 Peter 2:4 / Jude 6 → 1 John 3:8 → 1 Timothy 3:6) that converge on the traditional doctrine as “very plausible.”

Doctrinal-exposition note

Honest density: Medium · single expert

This is the gap-fill anchor for the §22 Angelology branch, and the whole node rests on one source: a single Craig Sunday-school lecture (Doctrine of Creation, Part 22, Defenders class, 2018-12-13) to a sympathetic lay audience, with named questioners — Bruce, Dennis, Ben — interacting live. It is systematic-theology exposition, not adversarial apologetics: there is no atheist interlocutor and no second expert. The page presents what the traditional doctrine is, how Craig builds the scriptural case for it, and which proof-texts he refuses to use, and flags the live problems — the first-sin theodicy, the contested “no pagan myths” premise, the idiosyncratic “limited atonement” redefinition — in the objections and gaps sections rather than manufacturing a clash the source does not contain.

The shape of the case — clear away, then build up

The lesson has a definite architecture, and the aggregation preserves it: Craig first removes the bad evidence, then assembles the good evidence, then draws a deliberately tentative conclusion. The argument is a cumulative case, not a proof — its force is the convergence of weak individual clues, and Craig front-loads that fact, which both licenses a modest conclusion and inoculates him against the very charge (over-reading) he levels at the Isaiah / Ezekiel readers.

Constraint

Whatever Satan is, he is part of the created order — no dualism, no eternal rival to God.

Colossians 1:15-16. Satan is a created being, not a co-eternal opposite. But no text explains his origin, so the method must be inference from clues. This rules out a metaphysical answer and forces a cumulative-case method from the outset.

Demolition

The three popular proof-texts are eisegesis — even though the doctrine itself “may well be correct.”

Read in context. Isaiah 14 is about the king of Babylon and Ezekiel 28 the king of Tyre, in hyperbolic language; the myth-critical variant is “nephelokokkygia,” finding shapes in the clouds; Revelation 12:4 is a vision of the future, not a primeval fall. Craig strips away all three while granting the doctrine may be true.

Construction

A numbered chain of genuine intimations — none decisive alone — whose combined weight makes the doctrine plausible.

The converging clues. Job 1:6; Luke 10:18; 2 Peter 2:4 + Jude 6; 1 John 3:8; 1 Timothy 3:6; 1 Timothy 5:21. Craig’s framing of each is candidly weak (“it’s unclear,” “minimally I think at least it shows”) — the force is the convergence, not any single verse.

Conclusion

The traditional understanding of Satan and the demons is “very plausible”: originally-good angels rebelled by free will and fell; some are the demons; the earth now lies under their sway.

An inference to the best explanation, held confidently-but-tentatively — an inference from “hints,” not a deduction. Craig’s wry release-valve on the bleak conclusion: “the earth now lies under the power and sway of these demonic angels — great news, isn’t it.”

What Craig refuses to use — the rejected proof-texts

This is the half of the lesson that distinguishes Craig from popular treatments, and it is his signature “the doctrine may be true, but not from this verse” move applied three times over. He affirms the angelic fall while sawing off the very branches the tradition sits on.

Isaiah 14 & Ezekiel 28 earthly kings, not Lucifer
Internal critique — the “read in context” criterion
The refutation
  • Isaiah 14 is framed at v.4 as “a taunt against the king of Babylon”; vv.16-17 describe a conqueror who “shook kingdoms” and “did not let his prisoners go home” — “clearly talking about an earthly king.”
  • Ezekiel 28 is framed at v.11 as “a lamentation over the king of Tyre”; the indictment is for dishonest trade — a charge that “wouldn’t obviously be applicable to Satan.”
  • The verdict: “the interpretation is not being read out of the text, it’s being read into the text.” The superhuman-sounding lines are non-literal of a human king — but non-literal is hyperbole, not a non-human referent.
Nephelokokkygia the demythologised-myth hypothesis
Reductio — the hypothesis fails its own evidential test
The refutation
  • The critical claim: the prophets borrowed and demythologised pre-existing pagan angelic-fall myths, then applied them to the kings.
  • The defeater: the hypothesis is “ultimately untestable since we know of no such pagan myths in the ancient Near East.”
  • The named vice: positing undocumented pre-biblical myths to decode a text is nephelokokkygia (Aristophanes’ “Cloudcuckooland”), a Greek coinage Craig learned “for the first time this week” from an unnamed OT scholar — glossed as “finding shapes in the clouds.” The same anti-parallelomania reflex he applies to Genesis-and-ANE-myth debates.
Revelation 12:4 a future vision, not a primeval fall
Internal critique — the book’s stated purpose controls
The refutation
  • The reading rejected: that “his tail swept down a third of the stars” teaches a pre-creation fall of a third of the angels.
  • The control: Revelation is, by John’s own stated purpose (Rev 1:1, 1:19), a vision of “what is going to take place” — so the Michael-vs-dragon warfare and the dragon’s defeat are future events, not a primeval past.
  • Held tentatively: “I’m very reluctant to take this symbolic narrative … and project it into the past rather than into the future … but if you could convince me I’m perfectly open.”
Method consistency

The same “don’t import a primeval fall into a text about something else” criterion is applied to Isaiah 14, Ezekiel 28 and Revelation 12 — making this transcript a tight demonstration of one hermeneutical principle across multiple texts.

The constructive case — the converging clues

Having cleared the bad evidence, Craig assembles the genuine intimations into a numbered cumulative chain. The strands grid below reads as one converging case; no single clue is meant to carry the weight.

Job 1:6

  • Satan appears among the angelic “sons of God” in the heavenly throne room.
  • “Some sort of an angelic being who has now set himself against God.”

Luke 10:18

  • “I saw Satan fall like lightning from heaven.”
  • At minimum “the idea clearly expressed of a satanic fall from heaven” — even if the precise referent is uncertain.

2 Peter 2:4 + Jude 6

  • Angels “who sinned” cast into nether gloom / chains until judgment.
  • “Clear” evidence of an angelic fall — possibly the Genesis 6 “sons of God.”

1 John 3:8

  • “The devil has sinned from the beginning.”
  • Sin on Satan’s part that “would obviously result in a sort of fall.”

1 Timothy 3:6

  • The proud convert risks “the condemnation of the devil.”
  • Suggests Satan’s sin was pride / arrogance, raising his heel against his Creator.

1 Timothy 5:21

  • The phrase “elect angels” implies a contrasting class.
  • A body of non-elect, fallen angels under God’s condemnation.

The “how” — sealing of the angelic will

Craig raises his own hardest follow-up — “how could an angel of God fall away, and is it possible that more angels might fall away in the future?” — and answers with an epistemic-distance theodicy he explicitly downgrades.

Stage 1

Epistemic distance enables the original choice.

To be free to rebel, the angels had to be created “at a sort of epistemic distance, at arm’s length so to speak” from God — otherwise the overwhelming vision of God’s glory would have left no room for free defection. The “epistemic distance” motif is John Hick’s soul-making language, uncredited here.

Stage 2

The choice seals the will.

Having made their choice, “the wills of the elect angels are then sealed … by being given a fuller vision of God’s greatness and glory so irresistible that further sin is impossible.”

Conclusion

There is no danger of a further angelic fall — their free choice having been made, their will is now sealed.

Marked speculative. Craig downgrades the whole fragment himself: “on this matter we can only offer plausible speculations.” The keystone is left implicit — he never directly states why the sealing-vision could not precede the choice (the answer his own structure implies: an irresistible vision would have removed the very freedom the choice required).

Can the fallen angels be saved? — Origen, “limited atonement,” Anselm

Asked whether the demons could now repent, Craig gives a connected multi-claim answer, all negative.

Origen

Apokatastasis rejected — the church condemned it; Matthew 25:41 affirms eternal fire.

Origen held that “in the end even Satan himself will be saved and everything will be restored to its original good condition.” The church condemned this as heresy, and Matthew 25:41 affirms the eternal fire “prepared for the devil and his angels” — “notice the eternality of the punishment.”

Scope

The atonement is “limited” — to the human species.

“Christ’s atoning death was offered on behalf of mankind, not on behalf of angels, and so in that very peculiar sense the doctrine of the limited atonement is true: it is limited to the human race and does not include angels.”

Anselm

The elect replace the fallen angels.

Medieval theologians like Anselm held “the number of [the elect] was chosen by God to precisely replace the number of the angels who had fallen away” — a Cur Deus Homo theme.

Theodicy rider

It is not unloving of God to withhold atonement from beings who would never take it.

“God may know that Satan and the demons, having become evil, would never freely choose to repent and believe even if provision were made for them — so why make it.” Craig pre-empts the pastoral objection (“lest anyone think it unloving”) before it is voiced.

Two honesty flags

Two points in the source are load-bearing enough to surface on their own. Both are places where Craig’s claim is either contestable on the evidence or liable to be misread.

Honesty flag 1

The “no pagan ANE myths” premise is contestable

Craig asserts flatly that “we know of no such pagan myths in the ancient Near East,” making the demythologised-myth hypothesis untestable. But the mainstream critical case for Isaiah 14 specifically appeals to attested Ugaritic material — the Helel / Shahar (“Day Star, son of Dawn”) motif and Athtar, who ascends to Baal’s throne and fails.

Critical / Ancient Near Eastern scholarship

Gap — premise stated too confidently

Craig does not engage the Ugaritic evidence

He does not rebut the Helel / Shahar / Athtar body of evidence, so the “untestable / unattested” premise is stated more confidently than the scholarship straightforwardly allows. This is the one place his factual premise is genuinely vulnerable.

Aggregation flag

Honesty flag 2

“Limited atonement” here is NOT the Reformed / TULIP sense

Craig calls the atonement “limited” in a deliberately surprising way — meaning its scope is the human species (humans, not angels). The phrase is a Reformed-laden surprise-hook designed to catch attention.

Terminological — risk of conflation with §9.3

Flag — possible terminological trap

Species-scope, not definite atonement

This is not what the Calvinist “L” of TULIP means (scope = the elect within humanity / definite atonement). Craig himself flags it as “peculiar,” but the aggregator must not conflate it with §9.3 election or Q-8.2 extent-of-the-atonement. It is a terminological surprise, not a Reformed commitment.

Objections surfaced — and the corpus’s honest reach

Because the source is a single sympathetic lecture, the objections are of two kinds: the few raised live by the class (and answered), and the substantive problems the doctrine raises that this lone source does not resolve. Craig’s candour about his own weak clues is itself the chief internal objection-handling.

Objection 1

Isaiah / Ezekiel could be deliberate “parodies” of Satan’s fall layered onto the kings

Bruce appeals to Daniel’s “Watchers” and territorial conflicts with Michael to suggest earthly leaders “are playing out Satan’s plans,” so God could parody Satan’s fall through them.

Self-raised by a questioner (Bruce)

Strongest response

Reapply the contextual test

“I just don’t see anything in the context that would suggest that these are parodies of Satan’s fall being applied to human beings — why not instead just take it to be hyperbolic language that is applied to these kings.” Nothing in the context signals a parody, so hyperbole about the actual kings is the simpler reading.

Craig “read in context” criterion

Objection 2

Revelation 12 shifts in time — vv.7-8 could be “panoramic,” reaching into the past

A questioner (Ben) notes that Revelation 12 moves back and forth between past, present and future, so the Michael-vs-dragon material could be panoramic — explaining how Satan got his position as “prince of the air.”

Self-raised by a questioner (Ben)

Strongest response

Concede the possibility, require a positive reason

“Yes, you could interpret that … I guess I would have to be convinced that there’s some good reason to take it that way.” Craig grants the possibility but says he would need positive reason to override the book’s stated future orientation; he has not taken it that way.

Craig defeasible-conviction posture

Objection 3

Whence the very first sin in a perfectly good creature?

How can a perfectly created will, with no prior defect, choose evil at all? This is the classic Augustine / Anselm charge that the first defection is incoherent — the deepest live problem the doctrine raises.

Augustine, Anselm, the philosophical tradition

Gap — partly answered, marked speculative

Epistemic distance, but the dilemma is not navigated

Craig raises it but answers only with “plausible speculations.” The epistemic-distance gesture faces a dilemma he does not address: either the pre-choice state had enough knowledge to be culpable (so the “distance” did no work), or it did not (so the fall was non-culpable). The home for this is §2 / Q-2.1.

Aggregation gap

Objection 4

Dual-fulfilment / typology — why not a both/and?

The tradition reads many passages (Psalm 110, Isaiah 7:14) as genuinely about a human subject and intentionally signifying a higher referent. Why is that typological both/and excluded for Isaiah 14 / Ezekiel 28? And what of the Eden / “anointed cherub” language that strains a purely human king of Tyre?

The traditional / typological reading

Gap — not engaged

Treated as an exclusive either/or

Craig treats “about the king” and “about Satan” as mutually exclusive but does not say why typology is excluded here when it is accepted elsewhere. (Bruce gropes toward this with “parody”; Craig answers the parody framing, not the typology framing.) The Eden / cherub lexical strain is left unexplained on his hyperbole reading.

Aggregation gap

Objection 5

The atonement’s human exclusivity is asserted, not warranted

Craig states “Christ’s atoning death was offered on behalf of mankind, not on behalf of angels” as a flat premise. What grounds the exclusivity — why are angels excluded?

Doctrinal — scope of the atonement

Gap — no scriptural warrant given

The obvious text is never cited

Craig offers no scriptural ground for excluding angels — the obvious text, Hebrews 2:16 (“he does not take hold of angels”), is cited nowhere. The claim is doctrinally traditional but here simply asserted.

Aggregation gap

Objection 6

Why are fallen angels sealed in evil while fallen humans stay redeemable?

The “would never freely repent” rider presupposes that the demons’ wills are sealed in evil — the mirror of the elect angels’ sealing in good. But why this asymmetry between sealed-fallen-angels and still-redeemable humans?

Internal — the sealing logic

Gap — asymmetry ungrounded

The sealing was argued only for the elect

Craig gave the sealing-of-the-will argument only for the elect angels and never explains why fallen angels are fixed in evil while fallen humans remain redeemable. The asymmetry is presupposed, not grounded.

Aggregation gap

Objection 7

Revelation 12:5 narrates a past event — undercutting “all future”

Revelation 12 demonstrably includes a past event — the Messiah’s birth / ascension (v.5) — before moving forward. That softens the flat “this is all future” premise on which Craig rests his rejection of a primeval-fall reading.

Mainstream recapitulation reading of Revelation 12

Gap — tension unaddressed

The first-advent reference is not engaged

Craig’s “all future” reading sits in tension with v.5’s evident reference to Christ’s first advent, which he does not address. The chapter’s mixed time-frame is the very thing the questioners were pressing.
