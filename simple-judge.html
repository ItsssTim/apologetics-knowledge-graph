The most quoted line in the room

Who are you to judge?

It is the verse everyone knows, often the only one. Say that something is wrong and back it comes: did not Jesus tell you not to judge? The worry underneath is real and worth honouring. Nobody likes a smug person looking down their nose. But the line is doing more work than it can carry, and a careful look shows what it actually forbids.

First / The boomerang
Step 1 of 4
A rule that cannot keep itself

Telling me not to judge is a judgment.

Start with the move itself, before any Bible verse. The person says you have no right to call anything wrong. But notice what they just did.

The claim
No one should judge anyone. Calling a thing right or wrong is arrogant, intolerant, off limits to you.
The catch
That sentence is a judgment. It rates your behaviour as wrong and corrects you for it. To forbid all moral assessment, the speaker has to make one, so the rule breaks itself the moment it is spoken.
What follows
Making moral assessments is not optional or rare. We do it every time we say cruelty is bad or courage is good. The question was never whether to assess, only how.
The theory you will hear
That is just a clever word trick. It is not a trick, it is the heart of the matter. Everyone draws moral lines, the sceptic included, the second they object to racism or cruelty. So the real disagreement is never about whether we judge. It is about which judgments are fair and humble and which are not.
✓
A ban on all judging refutes itself, because the ban is a judgment. Moral assessment is something we all do and cannot avoid.
Second / The context
Step 2 of 4
The line has a next sentence

Read the next breath, not just the slogan.

Do not judge, so that you will not be judged (Matthew 7:1). People stop there. Jesus does not. The very next lines tell you what he is targeting.

The target
He pictures a man with a plank in his own eye fussing over the speck in someone else's (Matthew 7:5). The fault he names is hypocrisy, the harsh critic who is blind to himself.
The fix
His remedy is not to stop helping. First take the plank out of your own eye, then you will see clearly to remove the speck. The goal is clear sight that helps, not silence.
The balance
A few breaths on he tells the same crowd not to throw what is holy to dogs or pearls to pigs (Matthew 7:6). That instruction is impossible without first making a discerning judgment about people and things.
The theory you will hear
You are reading a loophole into a plain command. The opposite is true. Pulling four words out of a paragraph and ignoring the rest is the misreading. The plank, the speck and the warning about pearls all sit in the same short speech. Taken whole, it condemns the proud fault-finder and assumes you will still assess wisely.
✓
In context the warning is against hypocritical, self-blind condemnation. The same passage tells you to keep your eyes open and discern.
Third / The teacher
Step 3 of 4
He practised what he is supposed to forbid

The same teacher judged, plainly.

If Jesus banned all moral evaluation, he never told himself or his followers. He did the reverse.

His own words
Stop judging by outward appearances, he said, and judge with right judgment (John 7:24). He does not cancel judging. He corrects bad judging and commands the good kind.
His instructions
He told followers to size up a response and move on from those who would not receive them, even shaking the dust off their feet (Matthew 10:14), which his messengers later did (Acts 13:51). That is a clear assessment acted on.
The church too
A community is told to act on a member's open sexual immorality rather than ignore it (1 Corinthians 5:5), and to restore someone caught in sin gently (Galatians 6:1). Both require an honest moral call.
The theory you will hear
Then the Bible just contradicts itself on judging. It would, if judge meant the same thing each time. It does not. Sometimes it means condemn with contempt, the thing forbidden. Sometimes it means assess wisely and act, the thing commanded. Two senses of one word, not a contradiction.
✓
The very teacher quoted against judging told people to judge rightly and to act on what they discern. The command targets a kind of judging, not the act itself.
Fourth / The spirit
Step 4 of 4
The dividing line is the heart

It was always about the spirit.

So what is actually forbidden? Not the assessment, but the posture behind it. Two people can say the same words from opposite hearts.

The wrong way
Proud condemnation that looks down, writes a person off, and quietly assumes you would never fall yourself. That is the plank-eyed critic, and that is what Jesus shuts down.
The right way
Humble, self-aware assessment that names a wrong while knowing you are capable of the same. The old line catches it well: there but for grace go I. Same honesty about the wrong, no contempt.
A simple test
Treat others as you would want to be treated (Matthew 7:12). Ask whether you are weighing this the way you would want yours weighed: fairly, kindly, hoping for the person's good.
The theory you will hear
That still lets you call my choices wrong. Yes, but it changes everything about how. The point is not to win or to feel superior. It is to be honest about right and wrong while staying humble about yourself, hoping for the other person rather than sneering at them. That is a world away from who do you think you are.
✓
What is forbidden is the proud, contemptuous spirit. What is asked is honest assessment held with humility and a wish for the other person's good.
Putting it together
The bottom line
Four steps, taken together

Not whether you judge, but how.

Telling someone not to judge is itself a judgment. We all make moral calls and cannot avoid it.

So who are you to judge? The honest answer is that everyone does, the sceptic included, and the verse never said otherwise. What Jesus shut down was the smug critic with a plank in his own eye, not the ordinary work of telling right from wrong. The question to carry is gentler than the slogan: am I weighing this the way I would want mine weighed, fairly, humbly, hoping for your good (Matthew 7:12).
