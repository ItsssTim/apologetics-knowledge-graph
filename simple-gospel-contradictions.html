Four accounts, told four ways

Don't the Gospels contradict each other?

There are four accounts of Jesus, and they do not line up perfectly. Different details, different emphases, different angles. Surely that means contradictions, and contradictions mean you cannot trust any of it. Ask a detective or a historian, though, and they will tell you that is exactly what truthful, independent witnesses look like.

First / Different angles
Step 1 of 3
What actually counts as a contradiction

Different angles are not contradictions.

Most of the so-called contradictions are not contradictions at all. They are the same event seen from different seats.

The car crash
Four people watch the same crash from four corners. One notices the colour of the car, one the sound, one the pedestrian, one the skid. Their accounts differ, yet none is false. They are filling in each other's blind spots. A real contradiction is one account saying a thing happened and another saying that same thing did not. Most alleged Gospel contradictions are nothing like that.
One angel or two
Take a classic example: one account mentions an angel at the tomb, another mentions two. But one does not deny the other. If there were two, there was certainly one. At least one is not contradicted by two; it is completed by it. Much of the supposed conflict is this kind of partial telling.
Chosen on purpose
The Gospels openly capitalise on different angles, each writer shaping his account for his own readers and his own emphasis. They never claim to be four identical transcripts. John even says there was far more that could have been written and was deliberately left out (John 21:25). Selecting different details is what every honest storyteller does.
The theory you'll hear
The details do not match, so the whole thing is unreliable. Difference in peripheral detail is not the same as contradiction. Two reports conflict only if one affirms exactly what the other denies, and most of the famous examples are simply different witnesses noticing different things. Minor divergence is the texture of real testimony, not a crack in it.
✓
Confidence: a contradiction is one account affirming what another denies. Most Gospel differences are not that; they are different witnesses filling in different details of the same event, the way any four honest accounts of one moment will.
Second / The red flag
Step 2 of 3
Why the differences are evidence

Perfect agreement would be the red flag.

Here is the twist. If the four accounts matched word for word, that would be the suspicious thing, not the reassuring one.

Ask any court
Detectives and judges know the pattern well. When several witnesses give word-for-word identical testimony, the conclusion is not that they are honest. It is that they got together and agreed on a story. Flawless agreement is the fingerprint of collusion, not of truth.
They did not collude
The Gospel writers do the opposite. They diverge in order and detail, each going his own way. As Wes Huff points out, if they all told it identically you could accuse them of colluding, and they plainly did not. The very differences that look like a weakness are the proof they were not reading from one shared script.
Independent witnesses
So the messiness is doing real work. It marks these out as independent voices reporting the same events, not conspirators rehearsing a line. The variation is not the problem to explain away. It is part of the evidence that you are hearing from several people who actually saw something.
The theory you'll hear
If they were edited later to agree, the differences would be gone. Exactly, and that is the point: the differences are still there. Nobody smoothed them out. If a later hand had harmonised the four into one tidy story, we would have one tidy story. Instead we have four that keep their own angles, which is precisely what independent, un-coordinated testimony looks like.
✓
Confidence: identical accounts signal collusion; courts treat word-for-word agreement as a warning, not a comfort. The Gospels diverge because they did not coordinate. The differences are evidence of independence, not of fiction.
Third / United where it counts
Step 3 of 3
United on the core, honest about the awkward

Where it counts, they agree, even when it hurts.

For all their differences in detail, the four converge completely where it matters, and they keep details no inventor would ever choose.

One core
On the heart of it the four are united: Jesus was crucified, was buried, the tomb was found empty, and he was encountered alive. That shared core is exactly what the earliest summary of the faith already records (1 Corinthians 15:3-7). The disagreements are at the edges; the centre holds.
The embarrassing detail
And they preserve things a fabricator would have deleted. All four make women the first witnesses of the empty tomb, in a culture where a woman's testimony was given little weight. If you were inventing a story to convince people, those are the last witnesses you would choose. They are there because that is how it happened.
Liars tidy up
Invented stories smooth away anything unhelpful. These accounts leave in the disciples cowering, doubting, misunderstanding, running away. You do not write your own founders as fools and cowards unless you are bound to tell it as it was. The awkward details are a mark of honesty, not of myth.
The theory you'll hear
You are just harmonising the problems away to protect the Bible. The test is simple and fair: can both accounts be true at once? If yes, there is no contradiction, only difference. We are not forcing the pieces; complementary detail genuinely fits, the core is unanimous, and the embarrassing parts no liar would invent are left standing. That is following the evidence, not dodging it.
✓
Confidence: on the central claims the four fully agree, matching the earliest creed, and they keep the embarrassing details, like women as first witnesses, that no inventor would choose. United where it counts, honest where it stings.

So do the Gospels contradict each other? On the small details they differ, exactly as four honest, independent witnesses to one event always will. On the core they stand united, and they keep the awkward details a forger would have quietly deleted. Flawless agreement would be the thing to fear, the mark of a rehearsed story. What we actually have looks like the opposite: four people who saw the same thing and each told it in their own voice. That is not the signature of fiction. It is the signature of testimony.
