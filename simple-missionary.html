A charge worth taking seriously

Wasn't missionary work just colonialism?

The picture is familiar. Westerners arrive, the empire follows, and a whole way of life is erased in the name of God. Some of that really happened, and it should be named without flinching. But the popular story flattens a far more surprising record, one where missionaries were often the loudest critics of empire, not its servants.

First / The honest part
Step 1 of 4
Start by agreeing

Yes, real harm was done. Name it.

A defence that pretends mission was spotless is not worth hearing. The honest answer begins by conceding what is true.

It happened
Some who carried the Christian name did treat the cross as a flag of conquest. Local customs were despised, churches leaned on colonial power, and the line between sharing faith and imposing culture was crossed.
No excuses
None of that should be softened. People made in God's image were wronged by those who claimed to serve him, and that wrong is not erased by good done elsewhere.
The real question
But conceding the abuses is not the same as accepting the verdict. Was empire the rule of mission, or the betrayal of it? That is what the record has to settle.
The theory you will hear
If even some missionaries served empire, the whole project is tainted. By that test every movement is tainted, since every movement has its hypocrites. The fair question is not whether abuses occurred, it is whether they expressed the faith or contradicted it.
✓
Conceding the wrongs honestly is what earns you the right to be heard on the rest. Start there, not with defensiveness.
Second / The category error
Step 2 of 4
Two things being confused

The gospel belongs to no single culture.

The whole accusation rests on a quiet assumption: that to spread Christianity is to spread the West. History says otherwise.

Born in the East
The faith began in the Middle East and spread first through Africa, Asia and the Near East, long before it reached northern Europe. From the start it was sent to all nations (Matthew 28:19), never the property of Western civilisation to export.
Every tongue
Unlike an empire, the Christian message claims to fit every people without making them stop being that people. Its own vision of the end is a crowd drawn from every nation, tribe and tongue (Revelation 7:9), so it travels into a culture rather than replacing it with another one.
The mix-up
Confuse the gospel with Western habits and of course mission looks like colonisation. Separate the two, as the faith always has when it says there is no Jew nor Greek in Christ (Galatians 3:28), and the charge loses its footing.
The theory you will hear
In practice missionaries still brought Western clothes, schools and manners. Some did, and confusing the two was a genuine failure. But that is a failure to live up to the message, not a feature of it. A faith that began in Roman-occupied Judea was never Western to begin with.
✓
Spreading a faith that belongs to no one culture is not the same as exporting one culture. Hold those two apart and the objection thins out.
Third / The record
Step 3 of 4
What missionaries actually did

They fought for the people empire used.

If mission were merely a tool of empire, you would not expect to find missionaries so often standing against it. Yet that is what the record keeps showing.

Against settlers
Again and again missionaries defended indigenous peoples against the very colonists and traders they are now blamed for serving, protesting seizures of land, forced labour and the cruelty of the slave routes.
Languages saved
By turning unwritten languages into written ones and translating Scripture into them, missionaries gave hundreds of peoples their first books and dictionaries. That preserved tongues that might otherwise have vanished.
The vulnerable
They built schools and clinics open to all, taught reading widely rather than to a chosen few, and campaigned against practices that fell hardest on women, children and the poor.
The theory you will hear
Writing down a language was just a tool for converting people more efficiently. Motives can be mixed, but the result stands. A people with its language in writing, its own literate teachers and a record of its stories is harder to erase, not easier. Preservation is the opposite of erasure.
✓
A movement that defended the conquered and wrote down their languages is doing the reverse of cultural imperialism. The blanket charge does not fit the evidence.
Fourth / The aftermath
Step 4 of 4
Look downstream

Then look at what came after.

If mission were chiefly an arm of oppression, the places it reached should be worse off. The measurable pattern runs the other way.

Later freedom
When researchers map where independent mission stations once stood, those regions tend to show stronger later democracy, broader voting and a freer press. The link is strong enough that it is hard to wave away as accident.
Mass literacy
Mission schooling spread reading far beyond elites, often reaching girls first and widening literacy across whole societies. Much of the modern map of education in the developing world traces back to it.
The honest test
None of this proves every missionary was good or every method wise. It does mean the simple equation of mission with colonial damage cannot survive contact with what the regions went on to become.
The theory you will hear
Correlation is not cause, so the data proves nothing. Fair caution, and no one should claim a single cause for a continent. But a consistent link between early mission and later democracy and literacy is the opposite of what the colonialism story predicts. At minimum it sinks the easy version of the charge.
✓
When the long-run data ties missions to more democracy and more education, the caricature of mission as pure plunder simply stops holding up.

So was missionary work just colonialism? Where it was, that betrayal deserves to be owned without excuse. But the wider record shows a movement that more often opposed empire, defended indigenous peoples, gave them their languages in writing and left behind more freedom and more reading than it found. Honest mission honours the people it reaches while still calling them to Christ. That is not conquest. It is the opposite of it.
