Where does the soul come from?

The question

When a new human comes into existence, where does its soul come from — specially created, propagated from the parents, pre-existent, or emergent from the body?

Granting substance dualism (that a human is body plus an immaterial soul — the prior question of Q-13.2.2), a further question follows at once: how does any individual soul originate?

This is not idle metaphysics. It drives straight into the ensoulment-timing question behind the abortion debate (Q-15.3.1): if God creates each soul at conception, the conceptus is already a person; if the soul emerges only when the brain reaches a threshold of complexity, personhood arrives later.

The four views — a survey with reasoned elimination

This is not a deductive syllogism but a taxonomy with reasoned elimination — an inference to the best explanation across four options, each weighed for biblical fit and philosophical plausibility, each pinned to a patristic or contemporary representative. Craig ranks them; he does not prove one.

View 1 · Pre-existence

  • Origen (Platonic)
  • Souls exist with God before their bodies and the world
  • God then incarnates them in particular bodies
  • Verdict: ruled out — biblically excluded
  • The view that earned Origen’s condemnation

View 2 · Creationism

  • Clement of Alexandria
  • God creates each soul ex nihilo
  • Ensouled “at the moment of conception”
  • Verdict: attractive, with a misgiving
  • IVF mass-fertilisation seems to “force God’s hand”

View 3 · Traducianism

  • Tertullian
  • The soul is propagated by the parents’ souls
  • No miraculous creative act of God needed
  • Verdict: least plausible
  • Gametes are not human beings and have no souls

View 4 · Emergentism

  • William Hasker (contemporary)
  • A real new mental substance, not an epiphenomenon
  • Emerges when the brain reaches sufficient complexity
  • Verdict: attractive, but under-explained
  • Risks collapsing into non-reductive materialism

How Craig treats it

William Lane Craig 1 transcript
The judicious four-view survey — Doctrine of Man, Part 12 (2020)
View 1 · Pre-existence (Origen) — ruled out
  • Souls exist with God before both body and world; God then “incarnates them in particular bodies.”
  • The survey’s only flat elimination, and it is biblical, not philosophical: “scripture never contemplates” pre-existent souls.
  • The view that “brought Origen’s condemnation as a heretic” — a §19 church-history hook. Asserted from silence, not exegeted from a text.
View 2 · Creationism (Clement) — attractive, with a misgiving
  • God creates each soul ex nihilo; “it would seem natural to say that this would occur at the moment of conception” — the move that ties the node to abortion.
  • A reductio of discomfort: IVF mass-fertilisation seems to “force God’s hand,” leaving him “trapped by the medical proliferation of these cells.”
  • Entertains a repair (God only ensouls the embryo he knows will mature) but flags it as possibly “a little bit ad hoc” — a “sense of discomfort,” not “a decisive objection.”
View 3 · Traducianism (Tertullian) — least plausible
  • As the body is the product of sperm + egg, so the soul is “produced by the souls of the parents” with no special creative act.
  • Another reductio: it is “inconceivable how all of the millions and millions of sperm could be carrying the soul of the father” — the gametes “are not human beings, they don’t have souls.”
  • Entertains the “soul-ish properties / remote cause” repair but still judges the view “even more implausible” — his strongest negative short of the biblical exclusion of pre-existence.
View 4 · Emergentism (Hasker) — attractive, but under-explained
  • Carefully distinguished from non-reductive physicalism (where mind is a mere epiphenomenon and there is no real soul).
  • Hasker “pushes emergentism further”: at a threshold of brain complexity “the soul will naturally emerge” as “a new mental substance.” Craig stresses this is “a dualistic view.”
  • The chief worry: it may “lapse back into some kind of non-reductive materialism rather than a real dualism.” We need to hear how biological complexity produces an immaterial substance.
The hedged verdict

No firm commitment. Pre-existence is eliminated biblically, traducianism downgraded philosophically; creationism or emergentism are the two “most attractive” — but he declines to choose between them. “I have no strong opinions … but retain an open mind.” Philosophy of mind “is not my area of specialization.” The humility is itself tactical: it models honesty for a lay class and pre-empts overclaiming.

Un-noticed tension

Emergentism makes the soul dependent on and produced by the brain’s complexity — which sits awkwardly with the substance dualism and survival of bodily death Craig defends in Parts 8–11. A soul that emerges from the brain raises an obvious question about whether it can survive the brain’s death. Craig commends emergentism without addressing the compatibility.

Objections handled — all raised internally

With one expert and no adversary, every objection here is one Craig raises against the views himself, then answers or registers as undecided. This is an internal-critique survey, not an exchange.

Objection 1

Souls pre-date their bodies (Origen / Plato)

Souls exist with God before the body and the world; God then sends them into bodies at creation.

Strongest response

Craig argument from scriptural silence

Objection 2

Creationism makes God’s soul-creation hostage to fertility doctors

If God creates a soul for every conceptus, then IVF mass-fertilisation and embryo cell-splitting “force God’s hand” — he becomes “trapped” into ensouling every lab-produced embryo.

Craig’s own reductio of discomfort

Response — a misgiving, not a defeater

Entertains a repair, but only registers discomfort

Perhaps God ensouls only the embryo “he knows will actually grow up and become a more mature human being.” Craig flags the repair as possibly “a little bit ad hoc” and is explicit that this is a “sense of discomfort,” not “a decisive objection.”

Craig charitable steelman + hedge

Objection 3

Traducianism can’t work — gametes have no souls to transmit

If the soul is propagated from the parents, how could “millions and millions of sperm” each carry the father’s soul? The sperm and egg “are not human beings, they don’t have souls.”

Craig’s own reductio of discomfort

Response — rejected as least plausible

Repair entertained, view still downgraded

The traducianist might say gametes bear “soul-ish properties” that combine into a new soul, the parents being a “remote cause.” Craig still finds the view “even more implausible” — his strongest negative verdict short of the biblical exclusion of pre-existence.

Craig gamete-soul mechanics

Objection 4

Emergentism collapses into non-reductive physicalism

If the soul merely “emerges” from brain complexity, the position may be materialism in disguise — an epiphenomenon, not a real immaterial substance.

Craig’s chief worry about Hasker

Response — a demand, not a refutation

Hasker means real dualism — but owes a mechanism

Hasker intends a genuine new mental substance, not an epiphenomenon. The worry is “lapsing back into… non-reductive materialism rather than a real dualism.” Craig calls for an account of “exactly how” biological complexity yields an immaterial substance before the view can be trusted.

Gap 2

The strongest theological case for traducianism is never engaged

The historic Reformed argument (Shedd) is that traducianism best explains the transmission of original sin — how a soul created fresh and ex nihilo could be born guilty. This is the classic objection to creationism and for traducianism.

W. G. T. Shedd, Augustinian tradition

Gap — a notable omission

Evaluated only on gamete mechanics

Craig downgrades traducianism purely on the gamete-soul problem and never touches its standard dogmatic rationale — a striking omission given the §13.3 original-sin material elsewhere in the run. Flag for Q-13.3.2.

Gap 3

The emergentism-vs-survival tension is left un-noticed

Emergentism makes the soul produced by the brain’s complexity. But Craig (Parts 8–11) defends a soul that consciously survives bodily death. Can a soul that emerges from the brain outlast the brain’s destruction?

Tension with his own intermediate-state dualism
