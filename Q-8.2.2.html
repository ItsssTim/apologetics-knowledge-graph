Why does the atonement require Christ to be both God and man?

Only a God-man can accomplish the atonement: deity gives the sacrifice infinite worth, true humanity lets him represent and substitute for us. Four experts treat it in eight extractions — but the deity-and-humanity dyad is carried in full by William Lane Craig alone.

The question

Why could only a God-man accomplish the atonement — why is neither a mere man nor a mere divine fiat enough?

The classical answer runs on two converging necessities. Christ’s deity gives his sacrifice infinite worth and efficacy: no mere mortal could offer an atonement sufficient for the sins of all mankind. Christ’s true humanity lets him represent and substitute for the human race — on the patristic principle that what is not assumed is not healed, a Saviour who did not genuinely take our nature could not redeem it.

Deny the first and the atonement shrinks to a mere moral example; deny the second and you get a Gnostic Christ who never truly suffered or died. The cross therefore requires vere deus, vere homo — truly God and truly man. The same logic answers the two popular objections: “why does God demand blood when he could just forgive?” and the “cosmic child abuse” caricature — because the Son is God, the cross is God absorbing his own penalty, not a Father punishing an innocent third party.

The convergent case — two necessities, not a syllogism

Unlike the Kalam (a deductive syllogism), this is an abductive cumulative case with two load-bearing legs. Each leg blocks a different heresy; together they fix the Chalcedonian formula as the only Christology that lets the atonement work. One caveat: the full dyad is mounted by Craig alone — the other three experts carry the value, justice and “why-blood” materials but not the “not assumed / not healed” humanity argument.

  • Infinite worth of the victim

Leg 1 · Deity

Only a divine person can offer an atonement of sufficient worth for all mankind.

Craig. “No mere mortal could have offered sufficient atonement for the sins of mankind.” Surrender Christ’s divinity and you are driven to moral-influence theories — Christ as mere exemplar — which “cannot do justice to the biblical motifs.” Huff, McLatchie and the Knechtles supply the value-and-justice dimension: the gravity of sin warrants a sacrifice of this magnitude.

Leg 2 · Humanity

Unless he truly took our human nature, he did not redeem it.

Craig, on the anti-Apollinarian principle. “If he hasn’t truly taken our human nature then he hasn’t redeemed human nature and his redemption and atonement is in vain.” A Christ who only seems human (Gnostic / docetic) cannot suffer or die, so cannot atone. Carried by Craig alone in the corpus.

Convergence

Therefore the atonement requires Christ to possess all the essential properties of deity and of humanity — vere deus, vere homo — neither of which entails sin or mortality.

The hinge. Sin and mortality are universal but not essential to humanity (else pre-Fall Adam and the glorified blessed would be non-human — “preposterous”). So Christ is sinless and still truly human; his power to forgive sin belongs to his divine nature, not a defect in his human one.

William Lane Craig 2 transcripts
The architect of the deity-and-humanity dyad
Signature argument
  • Deity is essential to efficacy — no mere mortal can offer sufficient atonement.
  • Deny deity → moral-influence theory — his diagnostic genealogy of post-Enlightenment Christology.

The “fully” vs “truly” exchange

A dissenting student insists Christ was “not 100% human” because he “had the ability to forgive sin — what human could do that?” Craig’s reply: (a) drop “100% / fully” for truly — the creeds never affirmed a percentage; (b) the moon-walking analogy — before 1968 it was universal that no one had walked on the moon, yet that is not essential to being human; (c) forgiving sin is a power of his divine nature. “He has two natures.”

Wes Huff 3 transcripts
Costly forgiveness + the value of the victim
Distinctive moves
  • Justice-mercy dilemma — a perfectly just judge condemns all; a perfectly merciful judge releases all; at the cross “the wrath of God was fully satisfied on Jesus,” so both meet.
  • Forgiveness is always costly — the BMW with no insurance; the slanderer. Cost-free forgiveness is incoherent.
  • Mercy vs grace — “not getting what you do deserve” vs “getting what you don’t deserve.”
  • com-passion etymology — God is not aloof; he suffers with us.
  • Value = what someone will pay — “what did God pay for you?” (the evangelistic close).
Implicitly answers the abuse caricature

Huff uses the deity point to defuse “cosmic child abuse” without naming it: it is the second person of the Trinity who steps into humanity and pays the price, and “the father hates sin because it murdered his son.” The cross is self-sacrifice by God, not the punishment of a third party.

Cliffe & Stuart Knechtle 2 transcripts
Judge-and-justifier; the gravity of sin
Distinctive moves
  • Cliffe — God is both judge and justifier: he himself absorbs the penalty he justly imposes; he “becomes Man in Christ” and dies to forgive.
  • The Jean Simon DeLatel father-for-son-at-the-guillotine story as substitution (presented as historical; provenance uncertain).
  • Stuart — the “why blood?” objection is a modern distortion: “brand new thinking” that “came out of Hume”; “we are like fish in water.”
  • Cross-cultural parity — every pre-modern civilisation practised blood sacrifice; the modern West is the outlier.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

“Cosmic child abuse” — the Father punishes an innocent third party

Penal substitution has God the Father venting wrath on an innocent victim — his own Son — for the sins of others. That is not justice but abuse.

Steve Chalke / progressive anti-PSA; implied by Muslim interlocutors

Strongest response

The deity of Christ dissolves it

The Son is God, so the cross is God absorbing his own penalty — not a third party. Knechtle’s judge-and-justifier duality makes the point cleanly; Huff: the second person of the Trinity steps into humanity and pays, and “the father hates sin because it murdered his son.”

Objection 2

Why couldn’t God just forgive without a death or blood?

An omnipotent God could “Thanos sin away” — remove sin by fiat, or make humans without a sinful nature. The whole crucifixion mechanism is unnecessary.

Skylar Fiction; the Muslim “Bismillah” model

Strongest response

Cost-free forgiveness is incoherent; the gravity of sin warrants the cross

Forgiveness is always costly — the forgiver absorbs the cost (Huff: the BMW, the slander). The Quranic “he just forgives because he can” model fails on the same logic. And the magnitude of sin warrants a sacrifice of this magnitude (Stuart Knechtle).

Huff costly-forgivenessStuart Knechtle gravity of sin

Objection 3

“Fully human” lays a sin nature on Christ

To call Christ “fully” or “100% human” is to attribute to him the sin nature he did not have. Better to say he was less than 100% human.

Dissenting Defenders student

Strongest response

Say truly, not “fully”; sin is universal, not essential

Drop “100% / fully” for vere homo — the creeds never affirmed a percentage. Sin is a universal but not essential property: the moon-walking analogy shows a universal property (pre-1968) that is plainly not essential to being human.

Craig universal-vs-essential + moon-walking

Objection 4

A being who can forgive sin and cannot sin is super-human, so not truly human

Christ “had the ability to forgive sin — what human could do that?” A being with divine powers and no capacity for sin is categorically above humanity.

Same dissenting student (dignity-of-person intuition)

Strongest response

Two natures — the power to forgive belongs to deity

Christ’s power to forgive sin is a property of his divine nature, not a feature of his human one. Lacking merely-human limitations no more disproves his humanity than being omniscient would. “He has two natures.”

Craig two-natures distinction

Objection 5

Punishing one person for another’s sin is unjust (Ezekiel 18)

No just court punishes A for B’s crime. Substitutionary atonement is therefore immoral, and a death cannot in any case do the moral work — you still have to do good works.

Ahmed Sraa (vs Huff); implicit in Shabir Ally

Partial response

Penal substitution is the only account where no sin is overlooked

Sin is dealt with to the just degree — borne either by the sinner or by the sinless substitute (McLatchie). Justice and mercy meet at the cross (Huff). But Huff does not directly rebut Ahmed’s restatement in that debate, and the transfer mechanism is asserted rather than defended.

Objection 8

If Jesus died for the sins of mankind, why do we still suffer?

If the atonement was accomplished, suffering should have ended. Its continuation tells against the claim that anything was achieved.

Objection 9

Limited atonement — did Christ really die for all?

If some persons fall outside the scheme — e.g. rational, morally sensitive humans not descended from Adam — then Christ did not die for everyone.
