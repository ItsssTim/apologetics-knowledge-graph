The question

How should the fulfilment of Old Testament messianic prophecy be evaluated — by a “100%-precise-prediction-must-come-true” standard, or by probability?

This is a methodological Q-node, not a content one. It does not ask which prophecies Jesus fulfilled — that work is spread across Q-3.6.4, Q-3.6.6, Q-3.6.10, Q-3.6.11 and Q-3.6.14. It asks how the argument from prophecy should be constructed and defended.

The answer that dominates the corpus is Jonathan McLatchie’s: discard the certainty criterion, evaluate each fulfilment by its likelihood ratio, and multiply many small independent ratios into a massive cumulative case. He brands it his “Messianic Convergence,” and it is his signature contribution to contemporary apologetics.

This is an expert deep-dive, not a broad cross-expert node. Five of the six substantive treatments are McLatchie’s; John Lennox supplies one adjacent, popular-register treatment against Christopher Hitchens but does not deploy the Bayesian apparatus. The page is therefore built around McLatchie’s full treatment, with Lennox as the un-rigorous foil the method is designed to replace.

The convergent argument — probabilistic, not “precise prediction”

McLatchie reframes the prophecy debate as a question about likelihood ratios rather than certainty. His EAZY 2021 opening statement — his most complete deployment — runs as a five-step probabilistic argument to a signature cumulative figure.

Premise 1

The strength of evidence for a proposition is the ratio P(E|H) / P(E|¬H).

Why it matters. P(E|H) need not be high — it only needs to be higher than P(E|¬H). This dissolves the “the prophecy isn’t a 100% certain prediction” objection at the root: a fulfilment that is merely more expected on Christianity than on its falsehood already counts as evidence.

Premise 2

Small independent likelihood ratios multiply exponentially.

McLatchie: “if the initial ratio were 2 to 1, ten pieces of independent evidence would have a cumulative power of more than a thousand to one.” No single piece needs great weight to build a massive cumulative case. (This step quietly assumes independence — see the gaps below.)

Premise 3

Interpretive uncertainty does not destroy evidential value.

Worked example. A 1-in-1,000 event, combined with only 10% confidence that your reading of the prophetic text is correct, still yields a likelihood ratio of about 100. Low confidence in the interpretation is survivable if the event is improbable enough on the background information.

Premise 4

Six deliberately conservative fulfilments multiply to a Bayes factor of 10 billion.

The six streams. Passover-timed death (×10), Psalm 22 crucifixion (×100), Bethlehem birth (×1,000), global spread of Christianity (×100), Jewish ethnicity (×10), Daniel 9 / temple destruction (×10). Every factor is justified as undercounted; the listener is invited to substitute their own numbers.

Premise 5

Even a prior of one-in-100-million is swamped.

The payoff. A prior probability that low for “God orchestrating history” is overcome by the 10-billion likelihood ratio, yielding a posterior of approximately 0.99.

Conclusion

Cumulative messianic prophecy rationally compels assent — and reflexively lifts the prior on the resurrection.

“It works both ways.” Prophecy convergence raises the prior probability of the resurrection, and the resurrection in turn raises the prior on Jesus being the Messiah and therefore on his fulfilment of prophecy.

The convergent evidence streams

The six fulfilments are chosen to be outside Jesus’s control and outside human design — the class McLatchie says carries genuine weight. He triages every candidate fulfilment into human design, divine design, or coincidence, and argues only from the cases least explicable as design or chance.

Stream A · Outside Jesus’s control

  • Passover-timed death — “Christ our Passover lamb” (1 Cor 5)  ×10
  • Firstfruits-Sunday resurrection — Lev 23; “firstfruits of those fallen asleep” (1 Cor 15)
  • Global Gentile inclusion — Isa 42, Isa 49, Dan 7, Zech 9  ×100
  • Jewish ethnicity of the Messiah  ×10

Stream B · Improbable on background

  • Bethlehem birth — Micah 5:2  ×1,000
  • Psalm 22 crucifixion details  ×100
  • Daniel 9 / destruction of the temple  ×10
  • Lydia McGrew: a Palestinian Jewish male teacher being crucified ≈ 1/1,000

Stream C · Reflexive lift

  • Prophecy convergence raises the prior on the resurrection
  • The resurrection raises the prior on Jesus being the Messiah
  • … which raises the prior on his fulfilment of prophecy
  • Cumulative Bayes factor across the six: 10 billion

The Passover / firstfruits convergence is the most-developed single example: crucifixion at Passover, resurrection on the Sunday matching the Feast of Firstfruits (on the Sadducean reckoning). McLatchie assigns this convergence a conservative Bayes factor of 50 in his published work — granting Paulogia’s “Jerusalem was fullest then” point by under-weighting rather than dismissing it.

How the experts deploy it

Jonathan McLatchie 5 transcripts
The architect of “Messianic Convergence”
Signature moves
  • Quantified base factors, transparent assumptions. Every fulfilment gets a number; every number is justified as deliberately undercounted; the listener may substitute their own. The 10-billion cumulative factor is his headline debate line.
  • Human design / divine design / coincidence triage. He argues only from fulfilments confident-to-have-happened and unlikely to be human design or chance — pre-empting retro-fitting by selecting only facts not in dispute (Josephus on the Passover death).
  • The fulfilled-vs-failed asymmetry. A surprising fulfilment is strong confirming evidence; an apparent failure with alternative readings available is only weak disconfirming evidence.
  • Symmetrical parity against bespoke criteria. The demand for the literal name “Jesus” in the Tanakh is turned on its target via a Muhammad-by-name parity.
  • Scriptural grounding for evidentialism. Deut 18 prophet-test, Isa 41 “set forth your case,” Matt 11 “tell John what you see,” Acts 17:30–31, Rom 1:20 — read against presuppositionalist readings of the same texts.
Audience tuning

McLatchie explicitly distinguishes how he argues with atheists (the human / divine / coincidence triage) from how he would argue with Orthodox Jews. The framework is not one-size-fits-all.

The popular-math tutors
Distinctive contributions
  • Lydia’s ratio framing. The Bayes factor “doesn’t have to be high in some absolute sense… it’s a ratio… they do not have to sum to one.” Even 0.01 / 0.001 is “a very top-heavy ratio.”
  • Tim’s Earl Grey tea analogy. A cabin with a fresh cup of tea steeping: the probability of fresh tea given non-habitation is far smaller than given habitation — so the tea tells you the cabin is inhabited. An equation-free intuition pump for the whole method.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

A prophecy must be a 100%-certain, explicitly-asserted future prediction to count

“It needs to be asserted as a prophecy — if someone doesn’t actually claim this is an event that will happen in the future, then it’s just a bunch of vague-sounding prose.”

Jonathan “Jump” (Shabir Ally’s second), critical interlocutors

Strongest response

Evidence is a ratio, not a certainty

P(E|H) need only exceed P(E|¬H). The strongest cases are explicit predictions (P≈1 on Christianity), but retrospective and typological fulfilments still carry evidential value — they are recognised in hindsight yet remain improbable on the falsehood of the hypothesis.

McLatchie likelihood-ratio reframe

Objection 2

You’re cherry-picking — count the failed prophecies as misses

“If you don’t include the failed prophecies then you’re not accurately assessing the data — you’re cherry-picking.”

Jonathan “Jump”

Strongest response

Epistemic quantitative asymmetry

A surprising fulfilment is strong confirming evidence; an apparent failure, where alternative interpretations are available, is only weak disconfirming evidence. A worked example puts the confirming Bayes factor far above the disconfirming weight of any apparent miss, so honest accounting still favours the hypothesis.

McLatchie fulfilled-vs-failed asymmetry

Objection 3

A single failed prophecy from an all-knowing being debunks the whole book

“If there is a single failed prophecy in a Bible written by an all-powerful perfect being, [it’s] debunked — that’s like a 100% failure.”

Jonathan “Jump”

Strongest response

Apparent failure ≠ confirmed failure

Scriptural interpretation is under-determined and probabilistic. Alternative readings of any apparent miss reduce its disconfirming weight below the confirming weight of the specific, improbable fulfilments. The “100% failure” framing conflates two very different things.

McLatchie under-determination reply

Objection 4

Bayesian epistemology isn’t evidence — it just restates your beliefs

“Bayesian epistemology is like any form of argumentation — it’s not evidence of anything, it’s just a formulation of your already-held beliefs.” (The math-washing charge.)

Jonathan “Jump”; implicit in Lydia’s “PTSD” hedge

Strongest response

No more “self-evidence” than modus tollens

Bayes is a framework that structures inference, exactly as modus tollens or modus ponens structure a deductive syllogism. Garbage-in-garbage-out depends on whether the inputs are warranted — which is a question about the inputs, not about Bayes.

McLatchie framework-not-evidence reply

Objection 5

Of course Jesus died at Passover — Jerusalem was fullest then

“On the weekend that Jerusalem is most full of people… the fact that he died during Passover week seems highly unremarkable.”

Paulogia

Strongest response

Granted — and accommodated by a conservative factor

McLatchie concedes the point is fair and answers it by weighting: he assigns the Passover convergence a deliberately conservative Bayes factor of 50 rather than something larger. The objection lowers the number; it does not remove the evidence.

McLatchie conservative-weighting reply

Objection 6

The Gospels were written to match the prophecies (retro-fitting)

“The legend that… grew to match the prophecies” — and Hitchens: “it’s all reverse engineering… the word in Hebrew Alma actually just means marriageable young woman.”

Paulogia; Christopher Hitchens

Strongest response

Argue only from facts not in dispute

McLatchie restricts the case to events unlikely to be retro-fitted — that Jesus really died at Passover is established by Josephus and not in dispute. Lennox, by contrast, asserts prophecy grounds the faith in history but never engages the reverse-engineering charge directly.

McLatchie facts-not-in-disputeLennox history-anchor (weaker)

Objection 7

Demand a bespoke criterion — the literal name “Jesus” in the Tanakh

“If I could find the word Jesus in the Tanakh somewhere… that’s referring to the deity figure, then I’ll be okay.”

TenakTalk (Greg, William)

Strongest response

Symmetrical parity — the Muhammad criterion

The criterion is arbitrary and has no textual basis; God already bears many names and titles. By the same logic a Jew would have to accept Muhammad unless the Tanakh names and excludes him. The proper method is inference to the best explanation over the weight of evidence (Vladimir Susic reinforces).

McLatchie Muhammad-by-name parity

Objection 8

Swinburne’s over-reach: we should expect God to send a redeemer

Swinburne tries to argue “it’s likely that God would do something like… send Jesus to redeem us” — shouldering a heavier burden than the argument needs.

Richard Swinburne (cited critically)

Strongest response

You never need an absolutely high P(E|H)

Lydia McGrew: all you must show is that the evidence is much higher given the hypothesis than given its negation. The same point answers the “why would the risen Jesus bother appearing to his sceptical brother James?” worry — P(James converts | resurrection) need not be near one, only far above P(James converts | no resurrection).

Lydia McGrew ratio-not-absolute reply

Objection 9

The contemporary Jewish authorities — closer to events — judged Jesus a fraud

“The Jewish religious authorities of the time… were the people who were the religious authority in the area… they thought he was a sorcerer and a fraud and an impostor” — and they were “much closer than the authors of any of the gospels.”

Christopher Hitchens

Gap — largely unaddressed

Lennox changes the subject

Lennox replies that prophecy roots Christianity in real history but does not rebut the eyewitness-authority point. McLatchie’s apparatus could absorb it (rejection by authorities is itself weighable evidence), but no transcript actually does so.

Aggregation gap

Objection 10

What distinguishes a legitimate typological resonance from an illegitimate one?

Any past pattern of God’s action — even mass-slaughter of enemies — could be retro-labelled a “typological prediction” and counted as confirmatory if later instantiated. So what stops the method confirming anything?

Jonathan “Jump” (structural worry)

Gap — largely unaddressed

Conceded, never resolved

McLatchie concedes “it could be some evidence depending on particulars of the case” but never specifies what separates a legitimate typology (Passover) from an illegitimate one (Roman-empire mass-murder). Alongside the non-independence of the multiplied ratios, this is the deepest unrepaired hole in the methodology.

Aggregation gap

Persistent gaps in the corpus

Five places where the methodology is exposed and the corpus offers no adequate repair. (Objections 9 and 10 above are the two that surface in live debate; the remaining three are aggregator-identified.)

1 · The non-independence problem. The six likelihood ratios are multiplied as if independent, but death-by-crucifixion and the Psalm 22 details are not statistically independent given Jesus’s Jewishness and first-century Roman context. The exponential-multiplication step (Premise 2) depends on an independence that is never defended.

2 · The “any OT pattern could be a typological prediction” worry. Conceded by McLatchie, never resolved — the method lacks a principled boundary separating legitimate typology (Passover) from illegitimate (Roman-empire mass-murder).

3 · The firstfruits-calculation assumption. The Passover / firstfruits Sunday alignment relies on the Sadducean reckoning; the Pharisees disagreed and Galilean practice may have differed, so the Sunday correlation is less load-bearing than the argument needs. Unaddressed.

4 · The crucifixion-date harmonisation. The Friday vs Wednesday / Thursday debate within Christian harmonisation bears directly on the firstfruits correlation but is never engaged.

5 · The eyewitness-authority point. Hitchens’s charge that the contemporary Jewish authorities — closer to events than the evangelists — judged Jesus a fraud is left essentially unanswered (Objection 9).
