Does Hume’s argument rule out miracles?

The most-cited philosophical defeater for miracle-belief — and the gate every apologist must clear before the resurrection can be argued as history. Four experts dismantle it; John Lennox dominates across seven transcripts.

The question

Does David Hume’s argument rationally rule miracles out in advance — so that no testimony could ever make a miracle the best explanation of the evidence?

Hume’s Enquiry §X is the most cited objection to miracles in the modern corpus. Two arguments hide under one name. The metaphysical version: a miracle is “a violation of the laws of nature,” and since “firm and unalterable experience” has established those laws, the proof against any miracle is “as entire as any argument from experience can be imagined.” The epistemic version: testimony to a miracle is always less probable than the falsehood of the testimony, so a rational person never has grounds to accept it.

The apologetic task is not to prove a particular miracle. It is the prior, abductive job of clearing the ground — showing Hume’s argument does not make miracle-belief irrational a priori, so that whether the resurrection occurred can be reopened as a live historical inference. Every expert here treats Hume as a gatekeeper to be dismantled before the positive case is even reached.

The convergent argument — inference to the best explanation

Datum

There exists substantial testimony to miracles — the gospel resurrection accounts, and (per Keener) hundreds of millions of contemporary claimants — plus the survival and transformation those events allegedly caused.

Observed. Lennox grounds his faith on the resurrection “as a matter of history,” with 500+ witnesses “at different times of day or night.” McLatchie: “hundreds of millions of people today claim to have experienced miracles.” This is the data both explanations must account for.

Blocker

Hume’s explanation: uniform experience establishes inviolable natural law, and makes testimony-error always more probable than a real miracle — so the data is always better explained by error or fraud.

Deployed by interlocutors. Atkins: “always more reason to disbelieve the report than to believe what is reported.” Krauss: “we have never ever witnessed the laws of nature being suspended.” Paulogia: a sincere mistake leaves “the exact same kind of testimony — indistinguishable.”

Defeater

The blocker fails four ways: it begs the question (“uniform experience” presupposes all positive testimony false); it is self-refuting (Hume rejected causation and induction, so cannot ground “laws of nature”); it misdescribes miracle (a new event fed into the system, not a violation of it); and it predates the probability calculus.

Division of labour. Huff and McLatchie press circularity; Lennox presses Hume-contra-Hume and the hotel-drawer reframe; Craig presses the mathematical error — “Hume simply didn’t understand the probability calculus.”

Best explanation

With Hume’s gate removed, openness to miracle is the rational stance. Knowledge of nature’s regularities is precisely what makes a miracle recognisable — and whether a given miracle occurred becomes a live evidential question.

The positive inference. Lennox: “you cannot recognise a miracle unless you know the normal laws.” McLatchie: the intrinsically low prior is “more than adequately overcome by the positive evidence in this case.” The case is abductive — no premise forces the conclusion, but once the defeater falls, theism out-explains a naturalism that can only stipulate its “uniform experience.”

How each expert deploys it

John Lennox 7 transcripts
The dominant voice — reframe, reductio, hostile witness
Signature moves
  • The hotel-drawer parable (his signature set-piece, credited to C.S. Lewis). Laws of nature are descriptions, not prescriptions that hold God prisoner. Put $100 in the drawer two nights, find $50 the third morning — you blame “the laws of Alabama,” not arithmetic; the arithmetic is what lets you detect the theft. The drawer is an open system; an agent can act in it without breaking any law.
  • Hume contra Hume — “the worst analysis of Miracles I’ve ever read.” Hume rejected cause-and-effect and the uniformity of nature, so cannot appeal to “firm and unalterable experience.” The same skepticism “works backwards in time as well as forwards.”
  • The question-begging charge — to know experience is “absolutely uniform,” Hume “would need total access to every event of the history of the universe.” He “simply assumes what he wants to prove.”
  • Joseph and Mary refute Hume’s secondary argument: Joseph “knew exactly where babies come from” and wanted a divorce — ancients knew the norm. Knowing the law is the precondition of recognising the exception, not an obstacle to it.
Hostile-witness power-move

Antony Flew — “the world’s leading expert on dealing with Hume’s philosophy… the Richard Dawkins of his age” — told Lennox personally late in life: “Hume’s wrong, and I would have to write all my books again.” Hume’s own leading expositor recanting is Lennox’s strongest card.

Jonathan McLatchie 2 transcripts
The Bayesian / circularity specialist
Distinctive moves
  • Circularity via Keener — “current research shows human experience is far from uniform; hundreds of millions claim to have experienced miracles.” Hume can preserve “uniformity” only by assuming all that testimony false — “the very point he’s trying to establish.”
  • Authenticating-signs predict rarity — if miracles function as signs (John’s signs; 2 Cor 11–12; Deut 18), they must “recognisably deviate from the way nature normally behaves” or be “robbed of their evidential value.” The rarity Hume counts against miracles is exactly what theism predicts.
  • Frequentism is the wrong tool — rejects Paulogia’s “millions err daily, few miracles occur” base-rate move because the low base rate is built into the hypothesis.
  • The Maxim is mathematically false — sufficient testimony “in terms of quantity and quality” can overcome any finite prior; the low prior is “more than adequately overcome by the positive evidence.”
  • Disciplined disclaimer: “I’m not vouching for the veracity of all those miracle claims” — the attack is purely structural.
Opponent’s concession

Paulogia frankly admits “I’m not sure for me there is an amount of testimony that could be” — openly conceding his miracle-prior is unfalsifiable. McLatchie does not press this as hard as he could.

Wes Huff 1 transcript
The begging-the-question diagnosis
Distinctive moves
  • The clean circle — “Hume defined miracles as violations of universal laws, and since he believed universal laws were universal, he was effectively defining miracles as something that couldn’t happen, then concluding on that basis they couldn’t happen.” To sustain “completely universal laws” Hume “had to ignore” standing miracle testimony.
  • Etymological reframe (Huff signature) — a miracle is “an extraordinary act of God”: Latin extra (out of) + ordo (arrangement), “outside the normal course of events.” God sustains nature consistently but “is by no means limited” from acting extraordinarily.
  • Reproducibility limit — science cannot disprove miracles “because science depends on reproducibility and miracles by definition are unreproducible.”
  • Professional humility — “I’m not alone here” — offloading further reading to Ian Hutchinson (MIT, Can Scientists Believe in Miracles?).
William Lane Craig 1 transcript
The probability-calculus rebuttal
Distinctive moves
  • Hume predates Bayes — “Hume simply didn’t understand the probability calculus, and a contemporary scientist should not appeal to David Hume.” The resurrection’s intrinsic improbability is offset because the probability of the evidence given no resurrection is itself very low.
  • Hostile witness on Hume scholarship — even Hume specialist Peter Millican concedes the Maxim fails.
  • Deployed against Atkins, who had made Hume’s “always more reason to disbelieve the report” his central evidential principle — recasting the whole objection as a mathematical error rather than a profound insight.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

Miracle = violation of inviolable natural law

A miracle is “a violation of the laws of nature”; uniform experience has established those laws, and they have “never ever” been observed to be suspended.

Hume; Atkins (“suspension of natural law… never observed”); Krauss

Strongest response

Reframe the law — a new event, not a violation

Laws of nature describe regularities; they do not cause or constrain. God feeds a new event into an open system, breaking nothing. Christians never claimed Jesus rose “by natural processes in the grave” — an agent “outside the system” acted.

Lennox hotel-drawer / “laws of Alabama”Huff extra + ordo positive account

Objection 2

“Uniform human experience” forecloses miracles

Human experience uniformly testifies against miracles, so a miracle always lies outside the realm of experience and can never be probable.

Hume; Krauss (“never witnessed the laws of nature being suspended”)

Strongest response

Circularity — “uniform” pre-judges the testimony

“Uniform” can be asserted only by assuming all positive testimony false in advance — the very conclusion at issue. Keener documents hundreds of millions of claimants; to know experience is uniform Hume would need “total access to every event of the history of the universe.”

McLatchie Keener dataHuff begs the questionLennox the access objection

Objection 3

Always more reason to disbelieve the report (the Maxim)

It is always more probable that the testimony is false than that the miracle occurred; sincere mistake leaves testimony “indistinguishable” from a true report.

Hume; Atkins; Paulogia

Strongest response

A probability-calculus failure

Hume predates Bayes; even Hume scholar Millican concedes the Maxim fails. Sufficient testimony “in terms of quantity and quality” can overcome any finite prior — the universal-impossibility claim is “mathematically false.”

Craig Millican + probability calculusMcLatchie the maths

Objection 4

Frequentist base rates — millions err daily, miracles are vanishingly rare

On any given day millions are mistaken and millions lie, but there can only be a few miracles, if any — so testimony can never become more likely than mundane error.

Paulogia

Strongest response

Rarity is predicted, not counter-evidence

If miracles are authenticating signs they must deviate recognisably from nature — otherwise they lose their evidential point. The frequentist approach is “inappropriate” because the low base rate is built into the hypothesis being tested.

McLatchie authenticating-signs hypothesis

Objection 5

Hume said “the last word” on miracles

Hume ruled miracles out in the name of science and “put the final nail in God’s coffin” — the issue is settled.

Hitchens

Strongest response

Hume contra Hume + hostile witness

Hume rejected causation and induction, so cannot ground the “laws of nature” his argument needs — and Antony Flew, his leading expositor, recanted: “Hume’s wrong, and I would have to write all my books again.”

Lennox self-refutation + Flew

Objection 6

Ancients believed in miracles because they were pre-scientific

People in the past accepted miracle reports only because they didn’t know the laws of nature; modern knowledge dissolves the credulity.

Hume’s secondary argument

Strongest response

The texts presuppose the norm

Joseph “knew exactly where babies come from” and moved to divorce Mary because he knew the norm; the man born blind (John 9) knew it too. Knowing the law is the precondition of recognising the exception.

Lennox Joseph and Mary; John 9

Objection 7

Even a direct eyewitness sighting would be dismissed as hallucination

“Even if I was standing at the foot of a cross and saw the resurrection before my very eyes, I would put it down to hallucination — it’s an extension of the David Hume argument.”

Atkins

Strongest response

Self-exposing unfalsifiability

By conceding that nothing could ever convince him, Atkins reveals his own position as faith-based and immune to evidence. Lennox flips the “what evidence would convince you?” demand back onto the skeptic.

Lennox unfalsifiability reversal

Objection 8

Religious belief is document-based, not evidence-based

Belief in the resurrection rests on “your reading of a document” — no more evidential than believing in The Great Gatsby.

Krauss (“Do you believe in The Great Gatsby?”)

Strongest response

A document is evidence

“Your science is based on the reading of nature. Of course it’s evidence.” The category “document” does not strip testimony of evidential force — though here both speakers retreat to “another program” rather than press the resurrection-as-history axis.

Lennox parry, not pursued

Objection 9

The gospels are too late and are propaganda

The gospels were written “80 years later than the event” and are “political propaganda sheets” invented to make their point — unreliable as historical testimony.

Atkins

Response — partial; a factual error left standing

Historiographical quality, but the date uncorrected

Lennox cites Luke as a Roman-historian-grade source (Sherwin-White) and the 500+ witnesses against mass-hallucination. But Atkins’s “80 years” figure is mainstream-false (Mark c. 65–70 AD, ~35 years on) and Lennox never corrects it.

Lennox Sherwin-White + 500 witnessesDating error uncorrected

Objections not adequately addressed

Gap 1

The Bayesian / Earman formalisation of Hume

The strongest modern version reframes Hume in probability theory: testimony-error is always more probable than a miracle. Does the corpus meet it on its own terms?

Brian Blais; John Earman (Hume’s Abject Failure)

Gap — largely unmet

Only two experts enter the probability frame

Huff explicitly does not engage Earman or the Bayesian literature; Lennox addresses only the metaphysical “violation” formulation, never the epistemic point. McLatchie concedes he does not resolve Blais’s reframing before the talk moves to prayer studies.

Gap 2

Cross-religious miracle parity

Hume’s secondary argument: rival religions all report miracles, and these mutually cancel — so testimony in any one faith is undercut by the rest.

Hume’s secondary argument; standard skeptical move

Gap — touched, never developed

Gestured at, not engaged

Lennox waves at it (“you’d be crazy to believe every claim to miracle”) but no expert engages the parity argument. Huff’s own section flags it as unaddressed.

Gap 3

Discriminating genuine from false testimony

If miracle claims abound, how does one tell veridical testimony from fraud, error, or rival-religion claims — the very discriminating work Hume’s argument was built to do?

Implicit in the structural rebuttals

Gap — the positive epistemology owed

Structural win, positive account missing

Huff asserts “there are claims all the time” but never says how to sort them. McLatchie disclaims the burden (“I’m not vouching for the veracity”) — winning the circularity point but leaving the discrimination criteria unbuilt.

Aggregation gap

Gap 4

How a non-physical agent inputs an event into matter

The whole reframe rests on God “feeding a new event” into a physical system. What is the mechanism — or even the metaphysics — by which a non-physical agent acts on matter?

Implicit; owed by the “agent outside the system” move

Gap — analogy carries the weight alone

No positive account supplied

The hotel-drawer parable establishes that no law is broken, but no expert offers a positive account of how an agent outside the system effects an input within it. The analogy is left to do all the work unaided.

Aggregation gap

Gap 5

Hume’s epistemic argument on its own terms

Even granting the metaphysical reframe, Hume’s deeper move is about the credibility of testimony, not the nature of law. Is it answered?

Hume’s primary epistemic argument

Gap — the dominant voice sidesteps it

Lennox answers the wrong half

Lennox’s seven transcripts brilliantly dismantle the “violation of law” formulation but address the testimony-probability formulation only glancingly. The epistemic argument is met (partially) only by Craig and McLatchie, in single transcripts each.
