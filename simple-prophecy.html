A pattern that is hard to explain away

Did the Old Testament predict Jesus?

Christians say Jesus fulfilled prophecy. The sceptic hears one of three things: the predictions are too vague to count, or they were written after the fact, or believers cherry-picked the hits. The portrait drawn centuries early turns out to be more specific, and far easier to date, than most people expect.

First / The test
Step 1 of 4
A test anyone can run

Read the description first. Then guess who.

The evangelist D. James Kennedy tried this on a man who took the Bible to be a merely human book.

The setup
Kennedy read him a string of statements and asked only whether, as a fair and educated man, he thought they described someone real. He did not say where the statements came from.
The verdict
The man heard of a figure forsaken and mocked, hands and feet pierced, clothing gambled away, silent before his accusers, buried among the rich. He said plainly that this was the life, death and resurrection of Jesus, then asked if there was more.
The reveal
Kennedy told him these were not reports written after Jesus. They were Hebrew prophecies, written centuries before he was born. The sceptic had identified Jesus from his own enemies' Scriptures.
The theory you will hear
The Gospel writers just wrote Jesus to match the prophecies. Possible for a detail or two. But the predictions were fixed in writing long before, and the Greek translation read across the ancient world was made well over a century before Jesus. You cannot back-date a document that already sat in libraries.
✓
When someone hears the prophecies cold and names Jesus, the portrait is doing the work, not Christian spin.
Second / The portrait
Step 2 of 4
Not vague, strangely exact

The portrait is oddly specific.

Vague predictions are cheap. A horoscope works by staying loose. These do the opposite.

The crucifixion
Psalm 22 describes a man forsaken, mocked with wagging heads, hands and feet pierced, bones out of joint, his clothing divided by the casting of lots (Psalm 22:1) (Psalm 22:16) (Psalm 22:18). It reads like an eyewitness at a Roman execution, written before Rome ruled anything.
The servant
Isaiah 53 foretells one silent before his accusers, assigned a grave with the wicked yet with a rich man in his death, numbered with criminals, bearing the sins of many (Isaiah 53:7) (Isaiah 53:9) (Isaiah 53:12).
The fine print
Thirty pieces of silver thrown to the potter (Zechariah 11:12-13), not one bone broken (Psalm 34:20), gall and vinegar to drink (Psalm 69:21), born in Bethlehem yet from of old (Micah 5:2).
The theory you will hear
You could read those back onto almost any death. Try it. The cluster is tight: a specific manner of execution, a specific betrayal price, a specific burial, a specific birthplace. Very few lives could absorb all of it at once. One did, in detail.
✓
This is not one fuzzy line that could fit many people. It is a detailed composite that fits one.
Third / The dating
Step 3 of 4
The dating problem for the sceptic

They were written first. Provably.

The whole objection rests on the prophecies coming late. They do not.

In Greek by 150 BC
The Hebrew Scriptures were translated into Greek at Alexandria, the version called the Septuagint, more than a century and a half before Jesus. The predictions sat in libraries across the ancient world before he drew a breath.
A death before the method
Psalm 22 describes death by the piercing of hands and feet centuries before crucifixion was a known form of execution (Psalm 22:16). You do not describe a punishment that has not been invented unless you are seeing past your own age.
A closing window
Daniel set the Messiah's death before a coming destruction (Daniel 9:26), and Jacob's blessing said the sceptre would not leave Judah until he came (Genesis 49:10). Both run out in the first century. The window shut in AD 70.
The theory you will hear
These texts were written late, after the events. Even on the latest sceptical dating, they are centuries before Jesus, and the Greek translation puts it beyond doubt. Late-dating cannot reach back past a book already copied and shelved across the Mediterranean.
✓
The prophecies predate Jesus by manuscript, not just by tradition. The fulfilment could not have been arranged backwards.
Fourth / The odds
Step 4 of 4
When coincidence runs out

The odds collapse fast.

One match is a coincidence. The question is what happens when they stack.

Just eight
The mathematician Peter Stoner estimated the chance of one man fulfilling just eight of these predictions by accident at one in ten to the seventeenth power. That is a one followed by seventeen zeros.
What that means
Cover the state of Texas in silver dollars two feet deep, mark a single coin, blindfold a man and send him to pick one. His chance of the marked coin on the first grab is those odds. And Jesus answered far more than eight.
The whole point
Jesus pointed to these same Scriptures and said they were about him (Luke 24:44). The prophetic thread running through the whole Hebrew Bible converges on one person.
The theory you will hear
You can make probability say anything. True, and the exact number is an illustration, not a proof on its own. But you do not need the precise figure. A handful of independent, specific, pre-written predictions all landing on one life is already a pattern that chance struggles to carry.
✓
One vague prophecy is a coincidence. A dense cluster of specific ones, written first and met in a single life, is a signature.

So did the Old Testament predict Jesus? It drew his portrait in advance, in ink that can be dated, with a detail no horoscope would risk. The honest reply to a sceptic is not louder insistence. It is the invitation Kennedy gave: read these for yourself, then tell me who they describe. The Scriptures Jesus said were about him have been pointing the same direction the whole time (Luke 24:44).
