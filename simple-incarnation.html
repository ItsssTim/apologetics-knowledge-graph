A claim that sounds impossible

How can Jesus be both God and man?

Christians say Jesus is fully God and fully man at once. The sceptic hears a square circle. God is all powerful and all knowing, a man is neither, so claiming both of one person looks like saying he is and is not the same thing. The charge feels airtight until you slow down and ask what would actually make it a contradiction.

First / The rule
Step 1 of 5
What a contradiction really needs

A contradiction has strict conditions.

Before you call something impossible, it helps to know what impossible actually requires.

The rule
A real contradiction says a thing both is and is not the same way, in the same respect, at the same time. Standing and sitting at once is impossible. Standing now and sitting later is not. The respect and the timing matter.
The charge
The objection assumes the church claims Jesus is God and not God in the very same sense. If that were the claim, it would collapse. No careful believer has ever said it.
The actual claim
Historic teaching says Jesus is one person with two complete natures, divine and human. The two are not the same kind of thing, so they do not cancel. Different respects, held in one person, is exactly the case the rule allows.
The theory you will hear
Fully God and fully man is just a square circle with nicer words. A square circle fails because square and round describe the same feature, shape, in opposite ways. God and man describe two different natures, not one feature pulling two directions. So the comparison does not hold. The work is to show the two natures do not collide, and that is the rest of this card.
✓
It would only be a contradiction if Jesus were God and not God in the same sense at the same time. That is not what is claimed.
Second / The distinction
Step 2 of 5
Essential is not the same as common

Most of what we share is not what makes us human.

The objection leans on a hidden assumption: that everything humans have in common is part of being human. The philosopher Thomas Morris showed why that breaks.

Essential
An essential property is one that, if you removed it, would stop the thing being what it is. Take rationality away and you no longer have a human person. That kind of property is non negotiable.
Common
A common property is one most members happen to share without it being required. It is common for a human to have two hands, but losing a hand does not make someone less than human. Common is not the same as essential.
The move
Limits like not being all knowing or all present may be common to humans rather than essential to humanity. If so, a human being could lack those limits and still be fully and truly human.
The theory you will hear
Being limited just is part of being human. Drop the limits and it is not a real human any more. That sounds obvious, yet the two hands case shows our instinct misfires. We routinely confuse what humans usually have with what humanness requires. Once you separate the two, an unlimited human is no longer a square circle. It is an unusual member of the kind, not a denial of it.
✓
Lacking a divine attribute may be merely common to humans, not essential to being human. That single distinction defuses the charge.
Third / The phrasing
Step 3 of 5
Fully human, not merely human

Fully human and merely human are different claims.

Two small words carry the whole answer. Miss them and the objection looks unbeatable. Catch them and it dissolves.

Merely human
To be merely human is to be human and nothing more, which means carrying all the usual limits and no divine nature alongside. We are each merely human. That is the ordinary case.
Fully human
To be fully human is to hold every property essential to humanity, a real body, a real mind, a real will. Jesus has all of these. He is human in the complete and proper sense, not a costume or a sketch.
The summary
The philosopher Ronald Nash put it cleanly: Jesus has every property essential to deity and every property essential to humanity, and none of those is limiting. So he is fully human without being merely human.
The theory you will hear
If he is not merely human then he is not really human, just God in a human suit. Not so. Fully human means he lacks nothing humanity needs. He grew, learned, hungered, wept and died, all genuinely. He is more than merely human, never less than fully human. Adding a divine nature does not subtract a single thing from the human one.
✓
Orthodox Christians claim Jesus is fully human without being merely human. Once that is clear, the alleged contradiction disappears.
Fourth / The direction
Step 4 of 5
He added, he did not subtract

The incarnation is a gain, not a loss.

A popular fix imagines God shrinking down to fit into a man. The church has always said the opposite happened.

The wrong picture
Kenotic teaching says the divine Son set aside his divine powers to become a man, emptying himself of deity to make room for humanity. It reads the emptying of Philippians as God giving up being God.
The problem
A God who can stop being God was never God to begin with. If deity can be switched off, it was never essential, and the one who switched it off is not the God of the Bible. That cure is worse than the disease.
The better reading
The emptying was the Son taking the form of a servant, adding a human nature and laying down his glory and rights, not his deity (Philippians 2:7). He veiled what he had. He did not delete it.
The theory you will hear
Scripture says he emptied himself, so he must have given up being God. The text says he emptied himself by taking on something, the nature of a servant, not by shedding deity. Emptying here is downward movement and self giving, the infinite stooping to serve. Read that way, deity is never at risk. The incarnation is addition, God taking up manhood, not subtraction of his Godhood.
✓
Becoming man did not cost him his deity. He added a full human nature to an undiminished divine one.
Fifth / The pictures
Step 5 of 5
Pictures help, then they break

Every analogy helps a little, then runs out.

People reach for comparisons to make the union feel familiar. They are useful as far as they go, and it matters to know how far that is.

Useful pictures
A person who is both a parent and a child, water that is solid and liquid at the right edge, a king who walks among his people as one of them. Each shows two true things held together without conflict.
Where they fail
None is exact. The same water is rarely ice and liquid in the same place, and a parent and child are still one nature, not two. Press any picture hard and it bends into a different error, so hold them loosely.
The honest stance
The union of God and man in one person is unique. Nothing else in creation is quite like it, so we should expect no analogy to land perfectly. A picture can open the door. The reality stands on its own.
The theory you will hear
If you cannot give me a clean example, the idea is probably incoherent. Having no perfect parallel is not the same as being impossible. Plenty of real things are one of a kind and resist tidy comparison. We do not reject them for lacking a twin. The argument never needed an analogy to work. The distinctions in the earlier steps carry it. The pictures are a kindness, not the proof.
✓
A missing perfect analogy is exactly what you would expect for something genuinely unique. It is not evidence of a contradiction.
Putting it together
The bottom line
Five steps, taken together

Two natures, one person, no collision.

1 · The rule
It is only a contradiction if Jesus is God and not God in the same sense at once. Two different natures do not cancel.
›
2 · The distinction
Two hands are common to humans, not essential. So a divine attribute being absent may be common to us, not required for humanness.
›
3 · The phrasing
Jesus is fully human without being merely human. He lacks nothing humanity needs and holds a divine nature besides.
›
4 · The direction
The emptying was addition, not subtraction. He took a servant's form (Philippians 2:7), veiling his glory, never deleting his deity.
›
5 · The pictures
Analogies help then break, because the union is genuinely unique. No perfect parallel is needed for the answer to hold.
›

So how can Jesus be both God and man? Not by being God and not God in the same breath, which no one claims, but by holding two complete natures in one person. Once you see that being limited is common to us rather than essential, that fully human is not merely human, and that the incarnation added manhood without subtracting deity, the square circle turns out to be nothing of the kind. It is the deepest news in the world, that God came near without ceasing to be God.
