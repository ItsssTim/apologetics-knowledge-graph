The Canaanite conquest & OT violence: was it just?

The sharpest of the “difficult Old Testament” challenges — a God who commands conquest and regulates slavery. Three experts answer it through five contested evidence streams.

The question

When the Old Testament has God command the conquest of Canaan — and regulate slavery rather than abolish it — is that a moral monstrosity, or can it be defended as just?

This is the challenge most likely to be raised as a knock-down: a God who orders the killing of “men, women, children and babes in arms” (1 Samuel 15:3) and writes slave law (Exodus 21, Leviticus 25) looks less like the ground of objective morality and more like Putin with a holy book.

The apologetic case is multi-stream and internally contested. There is no single syllogism. The experts do not even agree on which streams to deploy: the strongest scholarly move — the Copan/Flannagan hyperbolic-rhetoric reading — is carried fully only by Wes Huff, while Craig declines it and the Knechtles assert it without grounding.

The convergent case — five evidence streams

This is an abductive, cumulative case, not a deductive chain. Five distinct streams run across the corpus; their force is meant to be joint, and an interlocutor must answer all of them. The weakest joint: streams A and D do not by themselves resolve the command to kill children.

Stream A · Divine prerogative

  • God as author and giver of life has the right to take it
  • The creature cannot fully audit the Creator
  • Craig (strongest), Huff, both Knechtles

Stream B · Hyperbolic rhetoric

  • “Utterly destroy” is conventional ANE war-language
  • Proven: the groups reappear (Simon the Canaanite)
  • Huff (Copan-grounded), Michael Jones, Stuart (asserted)

Stream C · Moral context

  • Child sacrifice, temple-prostitution slave-raids
  • Judgement after 400 years of forbearance
  • All experts

Stream D · Drive-out, not kill

  • The command was divestment of land
  • Flight produced survival — “no one needed to die”
  • Craig (signature), Cliffe

Stream E · Instruction ≠ endorsement

  • OT regulates a fallen institution it never affirms
  • Gen 1–2 ideal; Gen 3 fall; NT trajectory dissolves it
  • Cliffe (canonical), Huff, Stuart

Joint force · The weak seam

  • Taken together, the streams remove the genocide-and-chattel charge
  • But the experts who lean on prerogative do not carry hyperbole
  • The 1 Sam 15:3 child-killing command stays exposed

How each expert deploys it

Wes Huff2 transcripts
The layered-context model — the dominant voice
Signature method
  • Stacks four contexts: canonical, historical, linguistic, narrative-purpose — run together, never one alone.
  • Character of God: anchors on Exodus 34:6, “the most quoted verse of the Bible in the Bible.” Signature illustration: Jonah’s complaint is precisely God’s mercy.
  • 400-year delay: Genesis 15:16 — God waited for “the sin of the Amorites” to reach fullness.
  • Internal critique: the atheist complains God does nothing about evil, then complains when the conquest shows him doing something.
  • Hyperbolic ANE rhetoric (Copan-grounded) — the move that distinguishes him. Names Is God a Moral Monster? and Did God Really Command Genocide?; proof is internal: the Canaanites reappear, down to “Simon the Canaanite.”
  • Signature analogies: the Star Wars Death Star and the Maple Leafs “got slaughtered.”
  • On slavery (vs Carson): Leviticus 25 imposes the death penalty on man-stealing; the Decalogue is founded on liberation from Egypt; OT law differs from the tiered Code of Hammurabi.
Hostile witness

Deploys Tom Holland’s Dominion (“not a Christian”), Rodney Stark and John Dixon: every abolition movement in history has been Christian — so the moral framework Carson uses to judge the Bible is itself a Christian inheritance.

Cliffe & Stuart Knechtle7 transcripts
Moral-intuition pumps + instruction-not-endorsement
Signature deployment
  • The “400 years of forbearance” refrain — the canonical Knechtle move, in every transcript.
  • Stuart’s intuition-pumps: the ISIS-camp analogy; the McDonald’s mass-shooter (“is that murder? No — that’s a form of justice”); “your locks prove you want justice.”
  • Stuart’s hyperbole assertion: “there’s never genocide cuz these groups always pop back up” — the right conclusion, without Copan’s scholarship.
  • Cliffe’s OT/NT distinction: Israel had a war mandate; “Jesus came with a towel, not a sword.” Plus parity-within-the-OT: God judged Israel too, via Assyria and Babylon.
  • Cliffe on slavery (Cornell, canonical): Roman indenture ≠ American chattel; “slaves obey your masters” is “not Spartacus”; Galatians 3:28 + Philemon lay “the foundation for abolition,” as Wilberforce later did.
  • Matthew 19 hermeneutic: divorce permitted “for hardness of heart,” never the ideal — applied to slavery, polygamy, bad government.
  • Stuart’s Frederick Douglass case: lived Black-Christian experience is itself evidence the Bible is not pro-slavery; Hagar (Gen 16:13) is “the first to name God.”
Deflection risk

Stuart’s “white suburban guy” framing and Cliffe’s “my African friend agrees / only the West pushes this” are sociological dismissals, not answers. Stuart’s infant-salvation move (“those babies will probably be in heaven”) generates a reductio he half-notices and does not resolve.

William Lane Craig3 transcripts
Drive-out telos + giver-of-life prerogative
Distinctive moves
  • Divine prerogative (vs Parsons): “God as the author and Giver of Life has the right to take human life as he wills” — he concedes he himself has no such right.
  • Drive-out, not genocide (signature): the telos was land-divestment; “if they had simply fled… no one would have died.”
  • The jihad contrast (transferable): jihad is conversion-by-force (evangelistic telos); the conquest “was not an attempt to convert them to Judaism” — “completely different from Jihadism.”
  • Defers twice to “reasonablefaith.org Question of the Week #16” rather than give “a sound-bite answer.”
Strategic narrowing

Against Hitchens, Craig recasts the Canaanite question as “an issue about biblical inerrancy and inspiration… not one that is on the floor tonight” — punting rather than defending on the merits. He never invokes the Copan hyperbole reading anywhere in this corpus, despite co-authoring with Flannagan elsewhere.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

“God commanded genocide” (1 Samuel 15:3)

Samuel orders Saul to “utterly destroy” the Amalekites — men, women, children, babes in arms, herds and flocks. This is a command to commit genocide.

Skylar Fiction, Hitchens, Parsons, Dillahunty

Strongest response

Hyperbole + drive-out telos + delayed, non-racial judgement

ANE war-language is conventional hyperbole (Copan); the groups reappear, so no literal extermination occurred. The command was divestment of land — flight meant survival. And judgement fell on Israel too (Assyria, Babylon), so it was not racial.

Huff Copan hyperboleCraig drive-out telosCliffe / Stuart 400 years, non-racial

Objection 2

“A God who slaughters babes in arms is a monster”

Whatever the body count, the command to kill children is the sharp edge. “It would be more consistent that we called it the word of a demon than the word of God” (Paine).

Keith Parsons (Paine as hostile witness), Skylar Fiction

Gap — weakly answered

Prerogative asserted; infant-salvation backfires

Craig asserts divine prerogative without engaging the moral intuition; Stuart offers infant salvation (2 Sam 12), which generates its own reductio — if killing children sends them to heaven, why is it ever wrong? No expert defends the imperative on the hyperbole reading.

Objection 3

“This is just Putin / a terrorist with divine cover”

Yahweh “is more similar to Putin than” a loving father; Samson, name swapped, is “the world’s first suicide bomber.”

Skylar Fiction, Dr Josh Bowen

Strongest response

The Nietzsche counter-charge

Strip the divine judge and Nietzsche is right — violence is simply natural. The atheist owes “an intellectual defense against the naturalness of violence and suffering.” Moral objectivity requires the Creator who is “allowed to judge in ways we don’t fully understand.”

Stuart Nietzsche reductioCliffe moral-objectivity grounding

Objection 4

“Defending it requires special pleading”

To make OT violence coherent the believer must first assume God had morally sufficient reasons — a lens applied in advance that would equally vindicate Marduk, Asher, or Chemosh.

Dr Josh Bowen

Gap — largely unanswered

Stuart changes the subject

The strongest single objection in the corpus. No expert rebuts it on its own terms; Stuart counter-reframes to the Nietzsche question rather than addressing the structural charge of circularity.

Aggregation gap

Objection 5

“The Bible legislates slavery, so it condones it”

“Condone” just means permit. The Torah permits owning people, subjecting them to inheritance, beating them — and never prohibits the institution.

Matt Dillahunty

Strongest response

Instruction / regulation ≠ endorsement

Same logic as Matthew 19 on divorce — permitted “for hardness of heart,” never the ideal. Genesis 1–2 is the garden, Genesis 3 the fall. Leviticus 25 imposes death for man-stealing; the Decalogue is founded on liberation from Egypt.

Cliffe Matthew 19 accommodationHuff man-stealing death penalty

Objection 6

Leviticus 25:44–46 is explicit foreign chattel slavery

Foreign slaves are bought, owned as property (ahuzzah), bequeathed to children as inheritance, and held in perpetuity (leolam). This is chattel slavery, full stop.

Dr Josh Bowen (consensus scholarship)

Gap — not exegeted

Cliffe answers with trajectory, not text

The two passages on which the scholarly case rests (Lev 25:44–46; Exod 21:20–21) are conceded the field. Cliffe answers with NT trajectory and general principle but never engages the text Bowen, Dillahunty and Hayes press.

Aggregation gap

Objection 7

“No verse you cite even contains the word slavery”

If God can forbid shellfish and mixed fabrics, he could have said “thou shalt not own people as property.” The argument from silence is decisive given the Torah’s legislative density.

Matt Dillahunty

Gap — not met head-on

Trajectory addresses the NT, not the OT silence

Cliffe and Huff point to Galatians 3:28, Philemon, the imago Dei and abolition history (Holland, Noll, Wilberforce, Douglass). But the absence of an OT prohibition — Dillahunty’s actual point — is never directly answered.

Aggregation gap

Objection 8

“Slaves obey your masters” (Eph 6:5 / Col 3:22) endorses slavery

The New Testament continues to condone slavery: “honour thy slave master as you would honour God.”

Billy Carson, skeptical readers

Strongest response

“Not Spartacus” + Philemon reframe

Paul forecloses armed revolt while simultaneously holding Galatians 3:28; Philemon reframes owner and slave as brothers; Wilberforce did the same within the system. Huff: Paul works against the socioeconomic order he addresses.

Cliffe “not Spartacus” + WilberforceHuff works against the order

Objection 9

“How is the conquest different from jihad?”

A holy war commanded by God to seize land looks indistinguishable from Islamic conquest.

Q&A questioner (Craig session)

Strongest response

Difference in telos

Jihad is conversion-by-force, “a means of spreading the religion of Islam by bringing non-Muslim countries into submission.” The conquest “was not an attempt to convert them to Judaism” — it was land-divestment and judgement. Completely different telos.

Craig telos contrast

Objection 10

Deuteronomy 23:15–16 protects runaway slaves (used pro-Bible)

Apologists cite the runaway-slave protection as evidence the OT is humane toward slaves.

Knechtle (the claim); Bowen (the rebuttal)

Gap — rebuttal unanswered

Refers only to foreigners fleeing into Israel

Bowen: the passage concerns foreign slaves fleeing into Israel, not Israelite slaves leaving Israelite masters — “consensus scholarship.” Knechtle does not reply, leaving the pro-Bible reading undefended.
