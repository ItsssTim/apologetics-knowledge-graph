Adam and Eve — historical or literary?

The hermeneutical hinge of the science-and-faith branch: fix the genre of Genesis 1–11 first, and most collisions with the scientific record resolve before the data is even reached.

The question

Is Adam a literal historical couple, a literary figure, or both — and does the genre verdict dissolve the conflict between Genesis 1–11 and science?

Almost every apparent collision between Genesis and modern science — the age of the earth, common descent, a single first human pair, a global flood — turns not on the data but on a prior decision about what kind of text Genesis 1–11 is. Get the genre wrong and you manufacture a conflict the text never intended; get it right and the conflict often evaporates without surrendering any historical content.

The corpus converges on one move — identify the genre first, reconcile second — then splits on which verdict is correct. The dominant proposal, William Lane Craig's “mytho-history,” is built to refuse the binary the popular debate assumes: that Genesis must be either literal history or pure symbol. The literal-vs-literary Adam is its sharpest test case.

The convergent move — genre is prior to reconciliation

All four experts run the same two-stage structure, and the disagreement is entirely at stage two. The case is abductive, not deductive — each reading is argued as the best explanation of the textual data, and the cumulative effect is that no single literalist reading can claim to be the obvious one.

Stream A · Genre signals

  • Talking serpent (Gen 3)
  • Trees of life & of knowledge
  • God walking in the Garden
  • “Four corners of the earth” (idiom)
  • Sun standing still (Josh 10)

Stream B · Grammar / structure

  • Toledoth formula (Gen 2:4)
  • Genesis 1:1 in a distinct past tense
  • Election grammar of the image verse (Heiser)
  • ~11 toledoth sequels across Genesis
  • Day-sequence brackets the “days”

Stream C · Reconciliation payoff

  • Age of earth: no conflict (Lennox)
  • Common descent: compatible (Jones)
  • Historical Adam: preserved (Craig)
  • Single first pair: optional (Jones)
  • “Overreading” avoided (all four)

How each expert deploys it

William Lane Craig 1 transcript · dominant
Mytho-history as the resolving genre
Signature framing
  • Classifies Genesis 1–11 as “mytho-history” — a term from Assyriologist Thorkild Jacobsen for a figurative narrative telling of an actual historical event.
  • The narrative is saturated with genre signals — talking serpent, the magic trees, an anthropomorphic God in the Garden — flagging a non-literal register by design.
  • Yet four biblical factors (continuity, genealogies, Pauline usage, the Adam/Christ parallel) anchor a real historical Adam behind the figurative dress (developed at Q-13.4.1).
  • The label dissolves the false binary: literalist and mythicist make the same mistake — assuming figure and reference are mutually exclusive. Calls for “a more sensitive genre analysis.”
Aggregation signal

Craig self-corrects his own course design: “If I had it to do over again, I would move much of the material dealing with the creation of man from the Doctrine of Creation to this locus.” The full genre argument — with the discriminator criterion (Q-5.4.11) — lives in those Doctrine of Creation lessons and is only summarised here.

Michael Jones 1 transcript
Toledoth sequel + the non-first elected Adam
Distinctive moves
  • No single “straightforward” reading exists — against Ehrman's “you're wiggling out” charge: “what is the straightforward reading?” Many plain readings are themselves consistent with evolution.
  • The toledoth formula (Gen 2:4) marks Genesis 2 as a sequel — “it's like a chapter marker,” recurring ~11 times across Genesis.
  • So the image-bearing humanity of Genesis 1 can be pre-Adamic; on Heiser's grammar they were “elected to be the image of God.”
  • Adam is therefore the first “priest of creation,” not the first man — “similar to how Israel was elected” from among the nations.
  • “Structuralism”: the laws of nature are fine-tuned to channel evolution toward human-like life, so evolution and design are not rivals.
John Lennox 1 transcript
Genesis 1:1 outside the day-sequence
Distinctive moves
  • “The beginning is not part of the sequence of days” — Genesis 1:1 is in “a different past tense from the sequence of days.”
  • Brackets the days deliberately: “what I'm about to tell you has got nothing to do with what you believe about the days” — a refusal to defend contested ground he does not need.
  • So the standard cosmological age is no problem; insisting otherwise is “overreading” — his contribution to the corpus's vocabulary.
  • Defuses the young-earth genealogy objection: the literary structure puts Genesis 1:1 outside the day-sequence, breaking the chain that supposedly pins the age short.
Cliffe Knechtle 1 transcript
No scientific language anywhere in the Bible
Signature deployment
  • Phenomenological language is not a scientific claim — ABC News says “sunrise” without endorsing an antiquated solar system: “it's a figure of speech.”
  • “Four corners of the earth” is idiom, not cosmography (Joshua 10 sun-standing-still treated the same way).
  • The Bible's genres are historical, didactic, poetic, apocalyptic — never scientific: “there is absolutely no scientific language anywhere in the Bible.”
  • The bluntest, broadest frame in the set — and the most exposed (see gaps): demanding scientific answers of a non-scientific text is a category mistake.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

“You're wiggling out of the plain text — that's dishonest”

The Christian who reconciles Genesis with evolution is being dishonest with the straightforward interpretation of Genesis 1.

Bart Ehrman

Strongest response

There is no single “straightforward” reading

The toledoth / election reading is itself the plain ancient reading, held by Walton, Collins and Heiser — not a modern escape hatch. The objection presupposes a single plain reading the text does not supply.

Jones internal critique

Objection 2

Genesis 1 and Genesis 2 contradict each other

Two incompatible creation accounts sit side by side — different order, different emphasis — so the text cannot be straight history.

Critical scholarship, popular skeptic

Strongest response

Genesis 2 is a sequel, not a rival account

Genesis 2:4's toledoth formula (“these are the generations of”) marks Genesis 2 as what comes after — “it's like a chapter marker” — not a contradictory second creation.

Jones toledoth structure

Objection 3

The genealogies fix the earth's age at a few thousand years

Adding up the genealogies from Adam pins creation at roughly 6,000 years ago — flatly contradicting the 13.8-billion-year cosmological age.

Young-earth creationism

Strongest response

Genesis 1:1 sits outside the day-sequence

“In the beginning” is in a different past tense from the days; it refers to events before the sequence, of unstated duration. The genealogical chain never reaches back to the “beginning,” so the age is simply not stated.

Lennox day-sequence grammar

Objection 4

Either Genesis is literal history or it is myth — you can't have both

Figurative language and historical reference are mutually exclusive. If the serpent talks, the whole thing is fiction; if it's history, the figures must be literal.

Both literalists and mythicists

Strongest response

A false binary — mytho-history is a third thing

A “historical drama” is a figurative telling of a real event; the symbolic language and the historical referent coexist by design. Literalist and mythicist share the very premise mytho-history denies.

Craig genre reframe

Objection 5

The talking serpent and the magic trees are obviously fiction

A talking snake, trees that confer life and moral knowledge, and a deity strolling in the cool of the day are plainly mythological — this can't be reporting real events.

Atheist, mythicist

Strongest response

Precisely — they are genre signals

The fantastic elements flag the figurative register of mytho-history; they are not embarrassments. The four biblical factors anchor a real Adam behind the figurative dress.

Craig figure-and-fact coexistence

Objection 6

Joshua 10 / “four corners” commit the Bible to a false cosmology

The sun standing still and the “four corners of the earth” show the Bible holds an antiquated, false picture of the physical world.

Atheist, skeptic

Strongest response

Phenomenological figure of speech

Modern weather reports say “sunrise” without endorsing geocentrism. The Bible's genres are historical, didactic, poetic, apocalyptic — it contains no scientific language anywhere, so it commits to no cosmology.

Knechtle category-mistake reframe

Objection 7

Evolution flatly contradicts the design of Genesis

An undirected, contingent evolutionary process is incompatible with the purposeful creation Genesis depicts.

Bart Ehrman, naturalist

Strongest response

“Structuralism” — evolution channelled toward humanity

On this view the laws of nature are fine-tuned to force evolution “down a specific road” toward human-like life, so re-running it would yield humanity again. Evolution and design are not rivals; theistic evolution sits comfortably with the cultural reading.

Jones structuralism + theistic evolution

Objection 8

Genesis requires a single first human pair, contradicting population genetics

Genetics indicates the human line never passed through two individuals; it descends from a population of thousands. A literal Adam and Eve as sole progenitors is ruled out.

Population genetics, critical scholarship

Answered — but the corpus disagrees with itself

Pre-Adamic election (Jones) vs literal first pair (Craig)

Jones: on the toledoth reading the Genesis 1 population is pre-Adamic and image-bearing by election, so Adam need not be the first human. But Craig keeps a literal first pair under figurative dress. The two reconciliations are doctrinally different and the corpus never adjudicates between them.

Jones election from a populationUnresolved Craig–Jones split

Gap 1

The discriminator problem — which elements are figure, which are claim?

If Genesis 1–11 is figurative telling of a real event, how do you tell which elements are figurative dress and which are historical claims? Without a criterion, “mytho-history” risks being a licence to declare inconvenient bits symbolic.

The deepest gap in the set

Gap — explicitly deferred

Craig concedes it is treated elsewhere

The “fantastic-to-the-ancient-author” criterion (Q-5.4.11) lives in his Doctrine of Creation lessons and is not supplied here. This lesson asserts the genre without giving the test for applying it.

Aggregation gap

Gap 2

“No science anywhere in the Bible” is overbroad

Knechtle's sweeping claim invites the rejoinder that Genesis 1–11 at least makes historical-existential claims — a real Adam, a real Fall — on which salvation history depends.

Sympathetic critic

Gap — distinction not drawn

“No scientific language” ≠ “no empirical claims”

Knechtle does not separate “the Bible uses no scientific register” from “the Bible makes no claims that intersect the empirical world.” The hard cases live in exactly that gap.

Aggregation gap

Gap 3

Concordism is dismissed but never engaged

Day-age and other concordist readings — which do try to map Genesis onto the scientific sequence — are treated as obviously mistaken (“overreading”), but no expert argues against them directly.

Day-age / concordist readers

Gap — assumed defeated

The literary readings are taken to have won

Literary-framework and mytho-history are assumed correct rather than defended against concordism on its own terms. (Concordism-as-fault is its own node, Q-5.4.6.)

Aggregation gap

Gap 4

The toledoth-as-sequel reading is asserted, not defended

Jones claims the formula “always” opens a sequel. But some scholars read Genesis 2:4a as a summary heading closing Genesis 1, not opening a new section — which would undercut the pre-Adamic move.

Competing Hebrew exegesis

Gap — counter-reading unaddressed

Live exegetical dispute left untouched

The whole pre-Adamic / election manoeuvre rests on the sequel reading of Genesis 2:4. The summary-heading alternative is never engaged.
