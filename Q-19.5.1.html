The question

What were the major heresies the church faced, how are they identified, and what orthodox doctrine did each one deny?

A heresy, in the corpus’s technical sense, is not merely an unpopular opinion. It is a teaching that arose within the Christian movement, claimed the name of Christ, and yet contradicted the apostolic faith on a load-bearing point — the person of Christ, the means of salvation, or the doctrine of God. The standard apologetic move is therefore diagnostic: name the heresy, locate it in time, and identify the precise orthodox claim it inverted.

This page is a survey, not an exhaustive treatment. Wes Huff supplies the Gnostic stream — Gnosticism, its sibling docetism, and the “lost gospels” they produced. William Lane Craig supplies the Pelagian stream — the medieval quarrel over original sin and grace. Two heresies named in the title, Arianism and the christological errors behind Chalcedon, are treated elsewhere (Q-7.1.3, Q-8.1.2) and cross-linked below.

The diagnostic — heresy as denial

The two experts never share a stage, and Gnosticism and Pelagianism are unrelated errors centuries apart. What is shared is a method: each heresy is identified by the orthodoxy it denies, and — where possible — pinned to a date that exposes it as a later innovation rather than the original faith. Three streams, one fixed point.

Stream A · Gnosticism

  • Denies: salvation is external (Christ’s finished work)
  • Asserts: salvation is internal — secret gnosis
  • “You have the spark of divinity in you”
  • A 2nd-century movement that “appropriated Jesus”
  • Never agreed with itself — no unified system

Stream B · Docetism

  • Denies: Christ was truly, physically human
  • From Greek dokein — he only seemed to have a body
  • Dualist root: physical = bad, spiritual = good
  • Gospel of Peter: 90-foot Jesus, talking cross
  • Reverses the modern problem: the offence was his humanity

Stream C · Pelagian grace

  • Pelagius: denies the Fall corrupted the will
  • Semi-Pelagian: grace given only on free petition
  • Denies: God takes the initiative in grace
  • Augustine: we bear Adam’s actual culpability
  • The medieval default the Reformation set out to reverse

How each expert treats it

Wes Huff 6 transcripts
Gnosticism, docetism & the “lost gospels” — the dominant voice
The Gnostic case (multi-pronged)
  • Soteriological inversion. Biblical salvation is “done for us external to us”; Gnostic salvation is “inside of you” — you unlock your own latent divinity through secret gnosis.
  • Dating filter. “A second century idea” arriving “close to 100 years after the time of Jesus” — so anything Gnostic “fails the criteria of apostolicity.”
  • Docetism (Greek dokein, “to seem”): the dualist sibling that denies Christ’s physical body. Signature reversal — in the ancient world the hard sell was Jesus’s humanity, not his divinity.
  • Onomastic tell. The names fit “4th Century Egypt,” not first-century Galilee and Judea — internal data betrays the provenance.
  • Canon-list argument. “No ancient list of Gospels includes Gnostic texts” — the church’s contemporaneous verdict, not a retroactive purge.
  • Internal incoherence. “The Gnostics didn’t agree with themselves”; the texts are “purposefully confusing” because opacity is the test of initiation.
Signature move

Internal critique by recitation. Reading Thomas 114 (“make her male…”) or the Gospel of Peter’s 90-foot Jesus aloud is the argument — the text refutes its own claim to the apostolic Jesus. Paired with the criterion of embarrassment (women as first resurrection witnesses).

William Lane Craig 1 transcript
The Pelagian / Augustinian taxonomy on grace
Three historic positions, two heterodox
  • Augustinianism (orthodox benchmark): the Fall corrupted human nature; we bear Adam’s culpability; grace is unmerited and God takes the initiative.
  • Pelagianism (the heresy, from the prior lecture): the will is unimpaired; humans can merit salvation by free effort.
  • Semi-Pelagianism (the focus): a mediating error that “attempts to come closer to… Augustinian… Original Sin than Pelagius himself did.”
  • Concedes the Fall weakened the will, “but… the basic tendency to good still lies within man.”
  • Grace is necessary but responsive — “given only in response to your free will.”
  • The decisive denial: God does not take the initiative; he has not “predestined some to one end and some to the other.”
Historical claim

By the Middle Ages semi-Pelagianism was the church’s widespread default, and “original sin” had eroded to mean merely an inherited inclination to sin — Augustine’s stronger claim that we bear Adam’s culpability “had been largely eclipsed.” Recovering it became a project of the Reformation.

Signature move

Sympathetic inhabitation before classification. Craig voices the semi-Pelagian’s prayer in the first person (“I need your help… give me the grace to live a holy life”) before naming the position heterodox — showing why so pious-seeming a view became so widespread.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

The Gospel of Peter’s resurrection scene records what really happened

If an early gospel describes the risen Jesus emerging from the tomb, why not take it as a genuine eyewitness account?

Popular / sensationalist

Strongest response

It is a docetic narrative, not history

A 90-foot Jesus whose “head is in the clouds,” flanked by angels, with a talking, prophesying cross, betrays a denial of Christ’s physical body. More plausibly it is a later apologetic “fixing” the embarrassing female-witness problem by substituting Jewish and Roman officials.

Huff docetism + criterion of embarrassment

Objection 2

The Gnostic gospels are a suppressed, equally-early Christianity

The “lost gospels” were hidden by the institutional church to bury a rival, equally-ancient form of the faith.

Popular (“Da Vinci Code” register)

Strongest response

They are late, and openly on display

Manuscript dating and internal name-data place them in 2nd–4th-century Egypt; they post-date the apostles whose names they borrow. Standing before the Gospel of Thomas in Cairo’s Coptic Museum, Huff notes there is nothing “suppressed” about a text on public display.

Huff dating + provenance + on-location point

Objection 3

The Gnostic gospels were arbitrarily excluded by power politics

The four-fold canon was a political choice by the winning party; the excluded gospels are no less authentic.

Popular / Pagels-adjacent

Strongest response

Excluded by a contemporaneous verdict

They were ruled out because they teach “a made-up Jesus” incompatible with the apostolic faith, and because no ancient list of authoritative gospels ever included them. The judgment was made at the time, not imposed retroactively.

Huff canon-list argument

Objection 4

Gnostic divine-spark talk is just an alternative Christology

Why privilege the canonical account? Gnostic texts present their own legitimate picture of a divine Jesus.

Comparative-religion register

Strongest response

Inverted soteriology, and self-contradicting

The soteriology is the inverse of Christianity’s (inner divinity vs Christ’s external work), and “the Gnostics didn’t agree with themselves” — one Gnostic text contradicts another — so there is no coherent rival orthodoxy to defer to.

Huff internal-incoherence + Hinduism analogy

Objection 5

Surely the ancient hard sell was Jesus’s divinity, as it is now

Modern skeptics resist Jesus’s deity, so the early controversy must have been over whether he was God.

Modern intuition

Strongest response

The reverse — his humanity was the offence

“No problem with Jesus being God, but if he’s God he can’t be man.” Docetism, not Arianism, was the live pressure in this stream; the Incarnation — a truly physical Christ — was the offensive claim.

Huff reversal of the modern problem

Objection 6

If the Fall only weakened the will, isn’t responsive grace the humble view?

Asking God for help and receiving grace in return sounds like the most biblical, least presumptuous account of salvation.

The semi-Pelagian’s self-understanding

Strongest response

It reverses the order of grace

Craig grants it feels pious — he voices the prayer sympathetically — but the position has God waiting on the sinner’s free petition. Augustine (and the apostolic teaching) have God taking the initiative; that is the decisive difference.

Craig order-of-grace diagnosis

Objection 7

“Original sin” just means we have an inherited tendency to sin

The doctrine of original sin is simply the observation that humans are inclined toward wrongdoing — nothing stronger.

The medieval default Craig is diagnosing

Strongest response

That is the eroded semi-Pelagian meaning

Craig: the “inherited inclination” reading is precisely the watered-down sense that displaced Augustine’s stronger claim — that we actually bear the culpability of Adam’s sin — which “had been largely eclipsed” by the Middle Ages.

Craig historical-erosion point

Objection 8

Gnosticism was a legitimate early Christianity later suppressed

The scholarly (Pagels-style) case: Gnostic Christianity was one valid early variant, not a heresy — the “winners” simply wrote the losers out.

Elaine Pagels, academic Gnosticism scholarship

Popular trope rebutted; academic case untouched
