Sharing your faith

How do I share the gospel with a Hindu friend?

Hinduism is vast and welcoming, so welcoming that it will happily make room for Jesus as one more god. So the gentle work is not adding Jesus to the shelf; it is showing why he will not fit there. Here is how.

First / Understand them
Step 1 of 4
Where they're coming from

It's less one religion than a whole world of them.

Hinduism is not a single creed but an enormous, ancient family of beliefs. So step one is simply to find out what your particular friend actually holds.

Many or one
Hinduism has many gods, who are often understood as faces of one ultimate reality. For many Hindus that reality is impersonal, more a force than a Father. So when you say God, they may hear something quite unlike the personal God you mean. Ask, and listen, before assuming you are even talking about the same thing.
Karma
At the centre sit karma and rebirth: your deeds in this life shape the next, and the soul cycles through life after life. The goal is release from that endless wheel. It is a deeply moral vision, you really do reap what you sow, but it is also a heavy one, because the debt of karma is never quite paid off.
Very open
And Hindus are typically warm and inclusive. They will often gladly accept that Jesus was holy, even divine, and simply add him to the many they already honour. That openness is genuinely lovely, but it is also the main challenge: they can say yes to Jesus while changing nothing, because they have filed him as one more option among many.
The mistake to avoid
Disrespecting their devotion or their images of God. Mocking idols or treating their worship as primitive slams the door and wounds someone who is often sincerely seeking God. Honour the real devotion and the real longing for the divine that is there. You are not introducing them to spirituality; you are introducing them to a Person.
✓
Remember: Hinduism is a whole world of belief, usually warm and open. The challenge is not hostility; it is that they will happily add Jesus to the shelf rather than follow him alone.
Second / Where to begin
Step 2 of 4
The debt of karma, the longing for a person

Start with the debt of karma and the longing for release.

Two deep things in Hindu belief are open doorways: the weight of a karmic debt no one can fully pay, and the heart's reach for a God who is personal.

The debt
Begin with karma, which they already feel keenly: every wrong has to be paid for, life after life. Gently ask the honest question it raises, how could anyone ever finish paying? The wheel turns on and on precisely because the debt is never settled. That ache, a moral debt you can never clear, is exactly where the gospel speaks.
A person
Many Hindus already pour out deep devotion to a personal deity, and that instinct, the longing to be loved by a God who knows your name, is a profound bridge. Behind the impersonal force, the heart keeps reaching for a personal God. Christianity says that God is real, is personal, and has come looking for you in particular.
One life
And gently raise the wheel itself. Christianity offers not endless rebirth but one life, and then a hope: a debt finally paid, a self finally restored, off the wheel for good. For someone quietly weary at the thought of cycling through life after life, that is a startling and genuinely welcome alternative.
The mistake to avoid
Letting them quietly absorb Jesus as one more avatar. This is the single most common outcome, and it changes nothing. Lovingly and gently, the conversation has to reach the point where Jesus cannot simply be added. He claimed to be not one path among many, but the one way that God himself opened (John 14:6). That uniqueness is the hinge of the whole thing.
✓
Remember: build on the unpayable debt of karma and the longing for a personal God, then gently press the one thing that matters, Jesus cannot just be added to the others.
Third / Point to Jesus
Step 3 of 4
A personal God, the debt paid

A personal God, the debt paid, and you kept whole.

The gospel answers the very longings Hinduism stirs, but in ways it cannot itself offer: a personal God, grace instead of karma, and a self that is saved rather than dissolved.

Grace not karma
This is the great relief. Karma says you must pay your own moral debt, life after life, and never quite manage it. The gospel says the debt has already been paid for you, in full, by God himself on the cross. You do not climb off the wheel by endless effort; you are lifted off it by grace (Ephesians 2:8-9). For someone carrying lifetimes of debt, that is overwhelming news.
Known, loved
And the God of the gospel is not an impersonal force to be absorbed into, but a Person who knows you by name and loves you as you are. The devotion many Hindus already pour out finds its true home here, in a God who is personal all the way down, and who loved you first, before you ever reached for him.
You restored
And you are not an illusion to be dissolved back into the One. Christianity says your self is real and precious, and God's plan is not to erase you but to raise you, healed and whole, in a restored world. Not absorption, but resurrection. You get to be you, at last as you were always meant to be.
The mistake to avoid
Presenting Jesus as simply the best of the gods. Best-of-the-gods still leaves him on the shelf with all the others. The claim is stronger and stranger: the one true God, personal and loving, come in person to do for you what no amount of karma ever could. He is not the finest option on the shelf; he is the one who takes the shelf apart.
✓
Remember: offer what Hinduism longs for but cannot give, a personal God who loves you, a debt paid by grace not worked off by karma, and a self restored rather than dissolved.
Fourth / The posture
Step 4 of 4
Warm, respectful, lovingly clear

Warm, respectful, and lovingly clear.

Hindus are often the warmest people you will ever share with. Match the warmth, and hold the one hard point with love rather than force.

Honour them
Meet warmth with warmth. Hospitality, friendship and genuine respect for their devotion open the way; contempt closes it on the spot. Many Hindus are sincerely seeking the divine, and you are not there to sneer at that search, but to point it gently toward the One it was always quietly reaching for.
Lovingly clear
The hard part is also the loving part: gently, without a trace of arrogance, helping them see that Jesus will not fit as one more god. That clarity can feel almost impolite in such an open culture, so it has to come wrapped in real affection, more an invitation than a correction. But it cannot be skipped, or nothing actually changes.
It's God's job
And rest in this: you cannot argue anyone off the wheel. Your part is to love, to live a life that shows a personal God at work, to point gently to Jesus, and to pray. The Spirit reaches hearts in ways you never could. Be patient; this is a road that is usually walked slowly, over a long friendship.
The mistake to avoid
Mistaking their friendly yes for real faith. A Hindu may warmly agree that Jesus is wonderful and mean only that he is one more wonderful god. Do not mistake politeness for surrender. Keep gently, lovingly returning to the one question that matters: not is Jesus good, but is Jesus Lord, alone.
✓
Remember: match their warmth, honour their devotion, hold the uniqueness of Jesus with love rather than force, and leave the changing of hearts to God.
