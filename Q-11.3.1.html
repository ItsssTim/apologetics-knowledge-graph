Baptism: ordinance or sacrament?

A means by which God imparts grace, or the sign of a grace already given? William Lane Craig steel-mans the sacramentalist case in its strongest form, then dismantles it across the book of Acts.

The question

Is baptism a sacrament — an actual channel by which God imparts saving grace — or an ordinance, the outward sign of a grace already received by faith?

Before anyone argues over the mode of baptism (immersion vs sprinkling) or its subjects (infants vs believers), a prior question silently decides the rest: does the water itself do something, or does it signify something already done? Craig makes this the organising hinge of the whole unit — “if we're not clear on that distinction the rest of the class won't make any sense.”

The stakes are soteriological. If baptism is a means of grace, it lies on the critical path to salvation and one is regenerated in the act. If it is an ordinance, regeneration is the inward work of the Spirit received by faith, and water baptism is the outward, once-for-all sign of an inner reality. The same New Testament texts are claimed by both sides; adjudication turns on whether Scripture lets water baptism and Spirit baptism come apart.

The cumulative case — evidence streams, not a syllogism

This is not a deductive proof but a cumulative, inductive case. WLC builds the sacramentalist position by accumulating texts under two pillars, then answers it with two counter-arguments — the second a survey of the whole book of Acts. The fork that decides everything: do water baptism and Spirit baptism coincide? The same scholar, Beasley-Murray (a Baptist), is enlisted on both sides.

Sacramentalist · Pillar 1

  • Baptism ↔ justification (Rom 6)
  • Buried & raised with Christ (Col 2:11–14)
  • “Washed…justified” (1 Cor 6:11)
  • Passive voice → God is the agent
  • “This is what actually happens”

Sacramentalist · Pillar 2

  • Baptism ↔ Spirit baptism (Acts 2:38)
  • One Spirit, one body (1 Cor 12:13)
  • Baptised into Christ (Gal 3:27–28)
  • Washing of regeneration (Titus 3:5)
  • Capstone: Beasley-Murray concedes “a symbol with power”

Ordinance reply · WLC endorses

  • Same gifts attach to faith as to baptism (Beasley-Murray's own concession)
  • Conversion texts omit water (2 Thess 2; Gal 3:1–5)
  • Paul relativises baptism (1 Cor 1:13–17)
  • Across Acts, water & Spirit baptism never coincide
  • Baptism is “a means of faith,” not grace
The fork

The sacramentalist is committed to water baptism and Spirit baptism being co-incident — that simultaneity is what makes baptism a means of grace.

Test against Acts. Cornelius and Paul receive the Spirit before water baptism (Acts 9, 10); the Samaritans receive it after (Acts 8); the Spirit-less Ephesian disciples are re-baptised while the “fervent in Spirit” Apollos is not (Acts 18–19).

Conclusion

Across the book of Acts, Spirit baptism never coincides with water baptism — so baptism is not the instrument of regeneration but the climax and sign of a faith already present.

WLC's settled verdict. “Baptism is not a means of grace; it is a means of faith. It is an expression of a person's faith in Christ.” He explicitly rejects baptismal regeneration.

Signature method

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

These are intramural disputes — sacramentalist vs ordinance readings of the same texts — not unbeliever attacks. All are adjudicated by Craig except where noted.

Objection 1

1 Cor 6:11 (“you were washed”) is a baptismal-regeneration verse

The “washing” language plus the phrase “in the name of the Lord Jesus Christ” marks this as baptismal — so justification happens in the act of baptism.

Sacramentalist case (Defenders Part 3)

Strongest response

“Washed” is spiritual, not the formula baptismal

“The washing here is not talking about the physical washing you have in water; it is talking about spiritual cleansing.” And “in the name of the Lord Jesus” is not a baptismal formula (cf. Eph 5:20, 2 Thess 3:6), so it cannot prove a baptismal reference.

Craig Defenders Part 4

Objection 2

Romans 6:3ff proves baptism unites us to Christ's death and resurrection

In baptism we are buried and raised with Christ and so made members of his body — “this is not just some sort of symbolic expression, this is what actually happens.”

Sacramentalist case

Strongest response

A metaphorical, symbolic description

“This isn't a literal burial with Christ, but metaphorically speaking we are buried with him in the waters of baptism, so this serves a symbolic function” — the outward sign of an already-present spiritual reality.

Craig Defenders Part 4

Objection 3

The passive voice shows God acts through the water rite

“You were buried with him” uses the passive voice — God is the active subject who buries you in baptism. So these are literally acts of God performed in the rite.

Sacramentalist case (Defenders Part 3)

Strongest response

Divine agency, yes — water as instrument, no

The passive voice shows God is the agent; it does not show the water rite is the instrument rather than faith. The blessings are equally ascribed to faith, so the water need not be the channel.

Craig Defenders Parts 4–5

Objection 4

Acts 2:38 / Titus 3:5 / John 3:5 tie baptism to forgiveness and regeneration

Scripture ascribes forgiveness, the gift of the Spirit, the washing of regeneration, and new birth to baptism — “no gift or power in the New Testament is not ascribed to baptism.”

Beasley-Murray catalogue (Part 3)

Strongest response

Internal critique — the same gifts attach to faith

Beasley-Murray himself concedes “precisely the same gifts of grace are associated with faith as with baptism.” Conversion and baptism follow so closely they are described as one process, so the blessings can be ascribed to either half. Baptism need not be the cause.

Craig Defenders Part 4, turning the sacramentalist's own scholar

Objection 5

Water and Spirit baptism are co-incident

“It is in water baptism that one is baptized in the Holy Spirit. These happen at the same time, and that's why water baptism is a sacrament, or a means of grace.”

Sacramentalist commitment (Part 5)

Strongest response

The Acts survey: they never coincide

Cornelius's household and Paul received the Spirit before water baptism; the Samaritans received it after. “Spirit baptism never coincides with water baptism. Never!” So the rite is not the instrument of regeneration.

Craig Defenders Part 5, inductive climax

Objection 6

Pre-Pentecost baptisms (John's, Jesus') are a special case

No baptism conveyed the Spirit before Pentecost, so the fact that John's and Jesus' baptisms did not convey it proves nothing about whether the rite later does.

Sacramentalist rejoinder

Strongest response

The Great Commission continues the same baptism

“It is a continuation of the baptism that Jesus was carrying out during his ministry…it is an external sign of repentance and faith.” The continuity tells against treating Spirit-conveyance as intrinsic to the rite.

Craig Defenders Part 5

Objection 7

Pentecost (Acts 2) and Cornelius (Acts 10) are unique, non-repeatable

The disciples were already baptised before Pentecost, and Cornelius marks the first Gentile reception — both exceptional cases that cannot set a pattern.

Sacramentalist rejoinder

Strongest response

Even granting uniqueness, the point stands

“But even if that is true, the point remains that Spirit baptism didn't take place in conjunction with water baptism for these disciples.” The non-coincidence is what the argument needs.

Craig Defenders Part 5

Objection 8

“Fervent in spirit” (Acts 18:25) means merely zealous, not Spirit-filled

Apollos being “fervent in spirit” is just a disposition of zeal (cf. Rom 12:11 “be aglow with the spirit”), not a reference to the Holy Spirit — so he is no counter-example.

Sacramentalist rejoinder

Strongest response

Both verses do refer to the Holy Spirit

“Acts 18:25 is talking about a person who is filled with the Holy Spirit, and that was the case with Apollos” — which is why the regenerate Apollos needed no re-baptism, while the Spirit-less Ephesian disciples did.

Craig Defenders Part 5

Objection 9

1 Peter 3:21 — “baptism now saves you”

Peter says plainly that baptism “now saves you” — the most direct statement of baptismal efficacy in the New Testament, and the sacramentalist capstone.

Sacramentalist capstone

Strongest response

The verse glosses itself

Baptism saves “not as a removal of dirt from the body, but as an appeal to God for a clear conscience.” It saves as an expression of the believer's faith — a calling out to God — not as a mechanical means of grace.

Craig Defenders Part 5

Objection 10

Re-baptism could just be a “re-commitment” of one's life to Christ

A Baptist pastor pressed WLC: re-baptising believers in the Jordan need not deny their first baptism — it could simply mark a renewed dedication.

Pastor John Herring (Defenders Part 8)

Strongest response

Baptism is a once-for-all initiation

On reflection it “became all the more clear…that such an action would be completely inappropriate.” Baptism is the climax of initiation; to repeat it “is in effect to invalidate the earlier baptism.”

Craig Defenders Part 8, pastoral ruling

Objection 11

Reformed paedobaptism: infant baptism is valid initiation

Infant baptism is grounded in the covenant-circumcision link of the Old Testament — the child is brought into the covenant community, so the baptism is valid and a later “believer's baptism” is the invalid repetition.

Reformed tradition (Knechtle steel-mans)

Strongest response

Obedience an infant cannot render

Knechtle: “Jesus calls us to be baptized in obedience to him. I wasn't obeying him when I was baptized as an infant.” The theology is understood but rejected. (WLC's Part 7 verdict, assumed in Part 8, agrees.)

Persistent gaps in the corpus

Gap 1

Acts 8 (the Samaritans) — “difficult for any view”

WLC's own concession: the passage is “so strange…that it is difficult for any view of baptism to understand,” yet he leans on it for the non-coincidence point anyway.

WLC's honest admission, Defenders Part 5

Gap — conceded soft spot

The confirmation reading goes unanswered

He does not engage the standard sacramentalist/Catholic reading that Acts 8 distinguishes baptism from a separate confirmation / laying-on-of-hands rite — so the delay is about confirmation, not about whether baptism regenerates. The non-coincidence survives; what Acts 8 positively teaches is left open by his own admission.

Most notable gap on the page

Gap 2

The Acts cases may themselves be exceptional

The free-church reading: the “Spirit-before-water” episodes are redemptive-historical firsts (a narrative of transitional events), not a normative pattern.

Alternative free-church exegesis

Gap — not engaged

Cuts the generalisation the other way

If the non-coincidences are transitional one-offs, the emphatic “NEVER coincides” generalisation is blunted in the opposite direction. WLC does not address this counter-move.

Aggregation gap

Gap 3

The prepositional force of Acts 2:38 is never exegeted

“Be baptized…for the forgiveness of sins” — the crux of the classic sacramentalist argument turns on whether the Greek eis is causal or resultative.

Classic baptismal-regeneration argument

Gap — not parsed

Appears only as a catalogue proof text

Acts 2:38 surfaces only inside the Beasley-Murray list; WLC never parses the preposition that the strongest sacramentalist case depends on.

Gap 4

Beasley-Murray's concession is conditioned on believer's baptism

The Baptist scholar's “sacramental” reading presupposes baptism by faith — it does not by itself license infant baptismal regeneration ex opere operato.

Distinction relevant to §11.3.2

Gap — not flagged

The Catholic/Lutheran use obscures it

WLC does not note that the same hostile-witness case, when used to defend infant baptismal regeneration, smuggles in a step Beasley-Murray never granted. A distinction that matters when the sacramentalist case migrates to the infant-baptism debate.

Mode of baptism is raised but not adjudicated
