Why does God allow children to suffer?

The sharpest edge of the problem of evil — and the rare Q-node where atheist interlocutors repeatedly out-argue the defenders, who answer honestly with “I don’t have a solution.”

The question

If God is good and all-powerful, why does he permit the suffering of children — and does the free-will defence actually reach this case?

The standard free-will defence explains moral evil as the price of genuine freedom. But the dying child raises two extensions it does not obviously cover: natural evil — a brainstem glioma is not anyone’s free choice — and proportion: even granting that love requires freedom, why this volume and intensity of suffering, and why is paediatric cancer “on the menu” at all?

The corpus is honest about its limits here. Two of four experts explicitly say “I don’t know.” The Christian case does not win as a deductive refutation; it wins, if at all, as a cumulative existential case — that atheism leaves the suffering child with nothing, while Christianity offers a suffering God, a resurrection, and final justice.

The case — multi-strand, not syllogistic

The defenders run five converging streams. The strongest deployments (Lennox, Craig) explicitly concede the intellectual puzzle is unsolved, then pivot to the existential and cruciform strands. The cumulative claim is not that any one strand answers the dying child, but that atheism removes the questioner without touching the suffering.

Strand A · Free-will defence

  • Love requires freedom — “robots can’t love”
  • God partially limited his power
  • Chaos entered at Genesis 3
  • Omnipotence excludes contradictions
  • No principled stopping point for intervention

Strand B · Cruciform theodicy

  • “What is God doing on a cross?”
  • God entered the suffering, not distant from it
  • Christ bore suffering we cannot conceive
  • The crucifixion looked purposeless in AD 30

Strand C · Eschatological hope

  • Resurrection — death is not the end
  • Children who die are in heaven (2 Sam 12)
  • Final justice — no one gets away with it
  • Moral conscience is not an illusion

Strand D · Sovereignty / greater good

  • Not a purposeless evil
  • God is outside time; sees the outcome
  • A non-sovereign God is the scarier option
  • Molinist optimal-salvation purpose (Craig)

Strand E · Internal critique / humility

  • Atheism erases “evil” as a category
  • Plantinga’s “no-seems” defence
  • Atheism has nothing to say to the dying child
  • “On your worldview, why are you upset?”

Strand F · The honest concession

  • “I don’t have a solution” (Lennox)
  • “I can’t make up an answer” (Knechtle)
  • The megaphone framing fails for paediatric cancer
  • God is ultimately responsible — no sophistry around it

How each expert deploys it

John Lennox2 transcripts
The dominant voice — concession + cruciform pivot
Distinctive moves
  • Faces a palliative-care interviewer’s three-case escalation — brainstem glioma, borderline-personality anguish, dementia — and concedes the C.S. Lewis “pain is God’s megaphone” framing fails for them.
  • Experienced vs observed: “cancer looks very different to an oncologist than it looks to the young mother who’s just been told she’s 6 months to live.”
  • Atheism doesn’t solve it, only removes the questioner — and leaves the majority of humanity “without justice.”
  • Atheism erases the category of evil (Dawkins, River Out of Eden): “DNA just is and we dance to its music.”
  • Free-will defence compressed: “robots can’t love” + the parental-risk analogy (holding his newborn daughter who could grow up to reject him).
  • Coventry Cathedral — beauty and devastation as the mixed datum to explain.
  • Cruciform pivot + resurrection + a moral-universe claim that names the abuser concretely.
Major concession

“When it comes to tsunami or these horrific diseases you can’t blame a person, so you blame God — and that’s true, absolutely true. There’s no philosophical sophistry that gets you around that. I don’t have a solution to it.” One of the cleanest moments in the corpus of an apologist abandoning a beloved framing.

William Lane Craig2 transcripts
The analytic case — three-tier PoE dismantling
Distinctive moves
  • Logical PoE — universally abandoned. Adding “God has morally sufficient reasons,” if even possibly true, proves compatibility.
  • Evidential PoE — requires probability judgments “we are simply not in a position to make.” Molinist optimal-salvation: God’s purpose may require a world “suffused with natural and moral suffering.”
  • Emotional PoE — answered by the cross: “Christ bore a suffering of which we can form no conception.”
  • Structural intervention against Hitchens’s Fritzl case: separate is theism coherent from is theism true. Hitchens concedes coherence.
  • Carries the Dennison/Wilson panel wing: the where-does-intervention-stop free-will defence; the falsifiability reversal (produce Christ’s corpse); eschatological justice grounds moral condemnation.
Notable concession (Hitchens)

“If I make the relevant assumptions, yes, then it’s internally fairly consistent… if I wasn’t a believer I would not feel God owed me an explanation.” A striking grant from one of the New Atheists.

Cliffe Knechtle5 transcripts (with Stuart)
Folk free-will defence + children-in-heaven pastoral
Signature deployment
  • Genesis 1 good / Genesis 3 rebellion: “God did not create evil… when God stepped back, chaos, destruction and death entered.”
  • No love without freedom: “if it ain’t free it ain’t love.”
  • Omnipotence excludes contradictions: “God cannot make square circles.”
  • The deathbed dilemma (signature): the atheist has only “tough luck, kid… fate, destiny, chance”; the Christian has “a suffering God who died on a cross.”
  • Children-in-heaven pastoral resolution: David’s “I look forward to seeing that child one day” (2 Sam 12) + Jesus drawing children to himself.
  • Authority-reframe + relativism reductio (“on your worldview, why are you so upset?”).
Known weakness

Against Smalley, Knechtle never answers the “options-menu” point (God set the menu; cancer-at-three needn’t have been on it) or the omnipotence-requires-no-tool argument — he deflects to an evolutionary-indignation tu quoque, which Smalley repeatedly calls “sidestepping.”

Preston Perry1 transcript
Sovereignty / IBE, pastorally framed
Distinctive move
  • Treats this as an emotional problem first — dignity before theology, with a deconvert molested as a child.
  • God outside time knows the outcome we cannot; a non-sovereign God is the scarier option.
  • The crucifixion as the test case (his lead move, before any argument): purposeless-looking in AD 30, in fact the mechanism of redemption.
  • Testimony / IBE confirmation: his wife Jackie, molested at five, whose book Gay Girl, Good God has set “thousands” free.
  • Distinguishes the perpetrator’s free will from God’s purposeful permission — “I don’t believe anything is a purposeless evil” — without claiming God was pleased.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

Wouldn’t a world without free will be better — no evil?

If God removed free will, there would be no moral evil and no suffering caused by it. So why not?

Hypothetical interlocutor; Dennison’s own steelman

Strongest response

No love without freedom; no stopping point for intervention

Genuine love must be free. And once God intervenes to block every misuse of freedom, there is no principled line: “at what point once God starts intervening does God stop?” Freedom itself would be destroyed.

Knechtle “if it ain’t free it ain’t love”Dennison / Craig panel where-do-we-draw-the-line

Objection 2

“Pain is a megaphone” trivialises the worst cases

How is paediatric brainstem cancer, or borderline-personality anguish, or dementia that destroys the very faculty that could seek God, a “megaphone to rouse a deaf world”?

Palliative-care interviewer (Veritas / Harvard)

Response — concede & pivot

The framing fails here; pivot to cruciform + resurrection

Lennox concedes the Lewis framing cannot honestly be deployed against these cases — “I agree with your verdict, it’s awful” — and reroutes to the cross, the resurrection, and final compensation rather than a solution.

Lennox steelman-then-concede

Objection 3

God created everything; cancer in children exists; therefore God created it

“If it exists, God created it. Cancer in children exists. Therefore God created cancer in children.” Isaiah 45:7 — “I create evil.”

David Smalley

Strongest response

Isaiah 45:7 is judgment language; evil is privation

The Hebrew “evil” there is destruction-judgment, not metaphysical evil — “ripped right out of context.” Evil is the absence of goodness; God creates things perfectly good, and free will introduces brokenness.

Knechtle privation + context

Objection 4

Even granting free will, God set the menu

“He took some stuff off the table — we can’t fly, we can’t jump over buildings — but you know what we can do? Get cancer when we’re three. Your good God put that on the list.”

David Smalley

Gap — largely unanswered

Knechtle deflects to evolutionary tu quoque

The single most damaging gap. God could have created a world where paediatric cancer was not metaphysically available. No defender engages this on its own terms; Knechtle pivots to “why does it bug you from an evolutionary perspective?” which Smalley calls “sidestepping.”

Aggregation gap

Objection 5

“God needs reasons to permit evil” contradicts omnipotence

“Saying God has a prerequisite to allow evil in order to accomplish something else is to say he is not all-powerful — like needing a tool to change my oil because I’m not all-powerful.”

David Smalley

Gap — unanswered

Verbal concession without reconciliation

Knechtle says “I agree with you actually, but again you’re totally missing it” without ever articulating what the agreement amounts to or how omnipotence survives the tool-objection.

Aggregation gap

Objection 6

A watching deity who lets a child be raped for 24 years is absent or complicit

The Fritzl case — a daughter imprisoned and raped for 24 years, her prayers unanswered. To say she’ll “get a better deal in another life” is “wickedly immoral.”

Christopher Hitchens

Strongest response

God grieves; eschatology grounds the condemnation; coherence ≠ truth

God will not abolish freedom wholesale (“where do we draw the line?”); he grieves (“God weeps”); and only an eschatological framework grounds the very claim that this is wrong. Craig splits coherence from truth — Hitchens concedes coherence.

Dennison free-will + griefWilson eschatologyCraig coherence / truth

Objection 7

Atheists face death calmly; atheism need not entail despair

David Hume died cheerfully without belief in an afterlife; the Christian Boswell went expecting to see his resolve shatter and was himself shaken. Atheism is tough but not nihilistic.

Julian Baggini

Partial response — arguably loses the exchange

Despair-claim is contestable

Knechtle insists “the only logical response is despair… case closed” for the dying baby. But Baggini absorbs the hammer and reframes atheism as “tough but not inevitably miserable,” out-narrating Knechtle’s Hume case. The corpus has no rejoinder.

Knechtle despair-conclusionBaggini re-frames

Objection 8

The logical problem of evil disproves God

An all-good, all-powerful God and the existence of evil are logically incompatible — the ancient Epicurean dilemma.

Standard / Epicurus

Strongest response

No demonstrable contradiction; possibility suffices

No philosopher has identified the hidden premises that yield an explicit contradiction. Adding “God has morally sufficient reasons,” if even possibly true, proves compatibility. The logical PoE is universally abandoned.

Craig Reasonable Faith compression

Objection 9

The amount of suffering makes God improbable

Even if logically possible, the sheer quantity and severity of innocent suffering make God’s existence evidentially improbable.

Smalley; generic evidential argument

Strongest response

We cannot make those probability judgments

Such ambitious probability judgments are beyond human reach. A Molinist optimal-salvation purpose could require a suffering-rich world; Plantinga’s “no-seems” — we cannot say there are no good reasons we don’t see.

Craig Molinist purposeKnechtle Plantinga no-seems

Objection 10

Was it God’s plan for me to be molested?

“Why did he plan for me to get molested? Why did he plan for me not to have a good relationship with my mother?”

Deconvert interlocutor (Perry)

Strongest response

Free will of the perpetrator vs purposeful permission

Perry distinguishes the perpetrator’s free act from God’s sovereign, purposeful permission, refusing the binary “God planned it as evil / God isn’t real.” “I don’t believe anything is a purposeless evil” — while insisting God was grieved, not pleased.

Perry permissive-vs-causative + dignity-first

Objection 11

“I had to heal myself; God wasn’t there”

“I had to bring peace on myself, I had to learn to meditate… I did that alone. I don’t know where he was when all that was taking place.”

Deconvert interlocutor (Perry)

Partial response

Concede the relief; argue God is still its source

Perry concedes the relief is real and does not try to discredit it, arguing God can still be the actual source of healing, and that pain often masks unresolved anger at God. He never names a specific greater-good for her suffering.

Perry stays generalno specific greater-good

Objection 12

God commanded the killing of children (1 Samuel 15, Canaanites)

An all-powerful God had non-violent options — he could simply “make all the Germans drop dead” rather than command Israelites to slaughter children violently. Choosing war contradicts “God is love.”

Skylar Fiction, Dr. Josh Bowen

Partial response — method left unanswered

Extended warning, children-to-heaven, the cross

Stuart cites a century of prior warning, judgment against child-sacrifice and bestiality, children swept in going to heaven, and the tension dissolving at the cross (Dostoevsky as hostile witness). But the specific “why violent war over miraculous removal” method-challenge is never disputed.

Stuart / Cliffe Knechtle grace-and-judgmentmethod unanswered

Objection 13

Christianity itself has caused massive suffering

Christians — in Northern Ireland and elsewhere — have killed in Christ’s name. Doesn’t that count against the faith?

Oxford colleagues / general

Strongest response

Grant it fully — and indict the violence

Lennox grants the charge without qualification: “I’m ashamed… that the name of Jesus has ever been associated with an AK-47 or a bomb” — Jesus told his disciples not to use violence. The Peter-cutting-off-Malchus’s-ear image: “I’ve a big job to put the ears back on.”

Lennox concede + internal critique
