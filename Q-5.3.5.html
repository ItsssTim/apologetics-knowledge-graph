Is theistic evolution a coherent and defensible position?

An in-house Christian dispute fought in the open — not whether God exists, but what “evolution” means and how much God must do directly. Four experts, five substantive treatments, three incompatible verdicts.

The question

Can a Christian consistently affirm both classical theism and evolutionary biology — and if so, what exactly is being affirmed when someone says “theistic evolution”?

This is an intramural Christian dispute, not a believer-versus-skeptic debate. Every voice in the corpus is a theist. The contested ground is not whether God exists but what “evolution” means and how much God must do directly for an account to remain orthodox.

The argument turns on a definitional fork: “evolution” can mean (1) change over time, (2) common descent, or (3) the neo-Darwinian mutation-selection mechanism as the sufficient, unguided cause of biological complexity. Whether theistic evolution is coherent depends on which sense is in play — and most of the corpus’s heat comes from experts attaching the word to different senses.

The convergent argument — a shared diagnosis, three verdicts

Premise 1

“Evolution” equivocates across at least three senses; the alleged conflict with theism arises only when it is wedded to sense (3) — the unguided neo-Darwinian mechanism.

Universal. Lennox: “evolution means many things… microevolution… that’s not controversial. What is maybe more controversial is whether all of life and its variation can be produced by natural selection and mutation.” Meyer parses the same three meanings; Murray & Churchill and Jones each insist the word be defined before the question is answered.

Premise 2

The Genesis “contradiction” rests on a contested literalist reading; read in its own ancient and literary context, the text does not mandate a young-earth, fixed-species, or 24-hour-day picture.

Craig and Jones (via Knechtle). Craig: the day-three vegetation rules out a literal week — the text is not “a film being run on fast-forward.” Jones: the toledoth of Genesis 2:4 makes Genesis 2 a sequel, leaving room for pre-Adamic image-bearing populations from whom Adam and Eve are elected.

The Fork

Given a directed process, is theistic evolution coherent — or does it dissolve into intelligent design, or fail because the mechanism it baptises is being abandoned?

This is where the experts split. Meyer: a directed process simply is ID, so TE wedded to an undirected mechanism is incoherent — “not even God can direct an undirected process.” Murray & Churchill: TE has only three core commitments and is fully compatible with providence, miracle and natural theology. Lennox: if God directs, fine — but mainstream biology is itself abandoning the neo-Darwinian mechanism.

Verdicts

No single conclusion — the shared diagnosis fans out into three incompatible Christian verdicts.

Meyer: theistic evolution is “backing a losing horse.” Murray & Churchill: ID theorists already are theistic evolutionists; the camps should merge “shoulder to shoulder.” Craig & Lennox: the Christian’s reservations should be scientific, not theological — “it’s the naturalist who’s in the box.” The disagreement is genuine and lives entirely on the Christian side of the table.

Stephen Meyer 1 transcript
The dilemma-and-disenchantment critic
The cleanest dilemma in the corpus
  • Horn 1 — incoherence — “not even God can direct an unguided undirected process… as soon as God is directing it it’s no longer undirected.”
  • Horn 2 — collapse into ID — “if God is directing the evolutionary process to a propitious outcome that is a form of intelligent design”; the only dispute left is “continuous or more episodic.”

Rhetorical tilt

Meyer’s “backing a loser” framing is pointed for the Christian audience: it positions theistic evolutionists as out of step with both ID and cutting-edge biology, “an odd time… to baptize Darwin” just as the establishment grows “disenchanted with the Darwinian mechanism.”

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

Theistic evolution is incoherent — God cannot direct an undirected process

If “evolution” means the unguided neo-Darwinian mechanism, then attaching “theistic” to it is a contradiction in terms: a process cannot be both undirected and divinely guided.

Meyer’s own framing, raised against unguided-TE

Strongest response

Concede it — then the coherent versions just are ID

Meyer sharpens the dilemma: a directed process is intelligent design, so the only surviving question is “continuous or more episodic.” Murray & Churchill sidestep by defining TE around evolution as the best explanation, not the exclusive cause.

Meyer the directed/undirected dilemmaMurray & Churchill best ≠ exclusive

Objection 2

TE makes God’s activity invisible — “what sort of God disguises his activity?”

If God’s work can be fully described in terms of chance processes and natural selection, then he has so concealed himself that his agency is indistinguishable from no agency at all.

Stated by Lennox as the standard objection

Strongest response

The self-winding watch

Lennox: chance movements can be the product of design — “the chance movements of my arm wind it up; it’s a very intelligently designed watch. It’s harder to make one of those than… a wind-up spring.”

Lennox chance as designed mechanism

Objection 3

God’s “direction” would look random to us (the dice-roll case)

Couldn’t God foreknow and intend an outcome that is, to us, indistinguishable from chance — “it’s random, but God knew it would go this way” (Snake Eyes)?

Audience to Meyer

Strongest response

A hidden hand is just design under another name

Meyer: a randomness “that actually has a hidden hand behind it” is intelligent design renamed. And the Darwinian claim that the appearance of design is an illusion logically commits one to a process that “was not mindful… unguided and undirected.” You cannot have it both ways.

Meyer hidden-hand reductio

Objection 4

Genesis 1 contradicts evolution; harmonising it is dishonest

“You are wiggling out of what Genesis 1 says — you’re being dishonest with the straightforward interpretation of Genesis 1.”

Bart Ehrman (as foil)

Strongest response

There is no single “straightforward” reading

Jones: the toledoth sequel structure and Hebrew election language are the ancient plain reading (Walton, Collins, Heiser) — not an evasion. Craig adds the day-three-vegetation internal indicator against a literal 24-hour week.

Michael Jones toledoth + electionCraig day-three vegetation

Objection 5

“Don’t you have to believe in six-day creation to be a Christian?”

A young inquirer believed he had to accept young-earth creationism before he could give his life to Christ — and was “prevented” from doing so by that supposed requirement.

A young inquirer (Craig’s anecdote)

Strongest response

YEC is not a Christian requirement

Craig: explicit denial. When told he need not believe it, the young man “threw his hands in the air and said hallelujah.” YEC functions as an evangelistic stumbling block, not a doctrinal gate.

Craig YEC as evangelistic liability

Objection 6

How much miraculous activity can TE tolerate? (the boundary problem)

If MTE permits miracles, where is the line? Either it is so permissive it absorbs progressive creationism, or so restrictive it collapses back into deism.

Craig, pressing Murray & Churchill

Response — reframed, not resolved

“Some, but not too much” — vagueness as a feature

Churchill: many small miracles integrated into evolutionary processes count as TE; a few large de novo miracles that supersede them do not. TE “should [not] be thought of as having sharp boundaries.” Craig leaves it flagged as a Dodge-that-isn’t.

Churchill integrated vs superseding miracles

Objection 7

TE baptises a mechanism science itself is abandoning

The neo-Darwinian mutation-selection mechanism has “very limited creative power” and the establishment is growing disenchanted with it — so harmonising faith to it is a losing strategy.

Meyer and Lennox (pressed against TE)

Unanswered — the sufficiency question is bracketed

No TE-side defence of the mechanism

This is the critics’ attack, and the corpus offers no rebuttal. The EPS panel explicitly brackets the sufficiency question (“there’s no way we could possibly address that”). Murray & Churchill’s third plank presupposes exactly what Meyer denies.

Meyer / Lennox press the claimSufficiency never adjudicated

Objection 8

TE lets the naturalist frame the Christian as closed-minded

Religion is cast as the dogmatic party clinging to creation while science follows evidence freely.

Implicit cultural objection

Strongest response

It’s the naturalist who’s in the box

Craig (crediting Plantinga): “if naturalism is true then evolution has to be true — there isn’t any other source.” The naturalist is forced to be an evolutionist; the Christian alone “can follow the evidence where it leads.”

Craig the box reversal

Objection 9

Where does the soul enter the lineage?

If humans are not reducible to the merely biological, and common descent is granted, at what point in the hominid lineage does the non-physical soul — and the historical Adam — appear?

Raised implicitly by Barron’s anthropology proviso

Gap — raised and dropped

Non-reductionism asserted, integration deferred

Barron insists there is “something in us… not reducible to the merely biological or physical”; Craig affirms openness to common descent. But neither addresses how the non-reductionist anthropology sits beside the evolutionary lineage. The transition is gestured at and abandoned.

Barron soul-not-reducibleSoul-in-the-lineage unresolved
