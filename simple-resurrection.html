First E / Execution
Step 1 of 4
Was he actually dead?

You can't rise from the dead if you were never dead.

Everything else hangs on this. When Lee Strobel put the question to a medical doctor and asked what crucifixion actually does to a body, the answer left almost no room to wonder whether Jesus survived it.

Flogged
It started before the cross. Roman flogging used a flagrum, a short whip braided with jagged bone and balls of lead, swung again and again across the back. It did not bruise, it tore. Skin and the muscle beneath it were ripped away in strips until the back was an open wound, sometimes deep enough to expose the spine. The physician Strobel interviewed explained that victims routinely slid into hypovolemic shock from the blood loss alone, and that a good number of men died right there at the post, before a cross was ever in the picture.
The cross
Crucifixion itself kills slowly, by suffocation. Hanging by the arms pins the chest, so to breathe out at all you have to push your whole weight up on the iron nail through your feet. You spend hours dragging yourself up for every breath until you simply cannot anymore. And this was run by professional execution squads who did it constantly and were held personally answerable for the result. A Roman soldier who let a condemned man come down alive could be put to death in his place, so they did not leave the job half finished.
The spear
To hurry a death along, soldiers would shatter the victim's legs with an iron bar, so he could no longer push up to breathe and suffocated within minutes. They did this to the two men crucified beside Jesus. When they came to Jesus they saw he was already dead and left his legs alone. To be certain Jesus was gone, a soldier instead drove a spear up into his side, and out came a flow of blood followed by a clear watery fluid, (John 19:34) a sign the blood had already begun settling and separating after the heart stopped. The spear did not kill him. It confirmed a death that had already happened.
The theory you'll hear
The swoon theory: the idea that Jesus never actually died, he only fainted, and the cool air of the tomb later revived him. The problem is the state he would have been in. A man whose back was flayed open, who hung nailed up for hours, was run through with a spear, given no food, water or medical care, and then sealed behind a boulder, does not quietly come round, shove the stone aside, slip past a guard and walk away. Even the sceptic David Strauss admitted that a half-dead man crawling out of a tomb would have struck his followers as someone in desperate need of a doctor, not as the risen Lord who had beaten death.
✓
Confidence: whatever you make of the rest, the death itself is about as certain as anything in ancient history. He really did die.
Second E / Early testimony
Step 2 of 4
Is it just a legend that grew over time?

The story didn't drift in centuries later.

The natural suspicion is that an ordinary man was slowly turned into a miracle long after he died, the way legends usually form. The timeline makes that nearly impossible.

The creed
In 1 Corinthians 15, Paul writes out a short, rhythmic summary: that Christ died for our sins, was buried, was raised on the third day, and then appeared to Peter, to the twelve disciples, and to a crowd of more than five hundred people at once. (1 Corinthians 15:3-8) Scholars across the board, believing and sceptical, recognise it as an early creed that Paul was handed and passed on, not a line he composed himself.
The date
Because Paul received that creed before he ever wrote the letter, historians trace it back to within a few years of the crucifixion, and many date its core to within months of it. That is nowhere near enough time for a legend to form. Legends need generations and distance from the event. This had almost neither.
Go and ask
Paul also points out that most of those five hundred witnesses were still alive as he was writing. That is effectively a challenge to any doubter: the people who saw this are walking around right now, go and ask them yourselves. You only write something like that when the witnesses are real and within reach.
The theory you'll hear
The legend theory: that the resurrection was a tall tale that snowballed across the decades into something miraculous. But a story cannot snowball while the original eyewitnesses are still alive, still reachable, and the central claim is already locked into a fixed creed within a few years of the event. The legend explanation arrives far too early to be a legend.
✓
Confidence: the claim that he rose was in circulation almost immediately, not invented by a later generation.
Third E / Empty tomb
Step 3 of 4
Was the grave really empty?

Even his opponents admitted the body was gone.

You do not actually have to take the Gospels on faith for this one. The situation itself does most of the arguing.

In Jerusalem
The disciples began openly preaching the resurrection in Jerusalem, the very city where Jesus had just been buried, only weeks after it happened. If the body was still in the tomb, the authorities had the simplest possible way to shut them down: walk to the grave, carry out the corpse, and the movement is finished on day one. They never did, which is hard to explain if the body was still there to be produced.
The women
Every account has women finding the empty tomb first. In first-century Jewish culture a woman's testimony carried little weight and was not even admissible in a court. If you were inventing a story to win people over, women are the last witnesses you would choose to build it on. They are in the account for one likely reason: that is simply how it happened.
The cover story
Look at how the Jewish authorities responded. They did not say the tomb still held a body. They said the disciples had come by night and stolen it. But to accuse someone of stealing the body is to admit the body was missing. Both sides agreed on the basic fact. They only argued about where it had gone.
The theory you'll hear
The stolen-body theory: that the disciples themselves took the corpse and staged the whole thing. But those same men then spent the rest of their lives being beaten, jailed and executed for insisting they had seen Jesus alive. People will sometimes die for a lie they have been talked into believing. Almost nobody dies for a lie they personally cooked up and know for a fact is false.
✓
Confidence: his own followers and the authorities who wanted him forgotten agreed the tomb was empty. The only real question left is what happened to the body.
Fourth E / Eyewitnesses
Step 4 of 4
Did people actually see him alive?

It wasn't one person, seeing him once.

This is the part that is genuinely hard to wave away, because of how many people were involved and what it turned them into.

Many
The early creed in 1 Corinthians 15 lists the sightings in waves: Jesus appeared to Peter, then to the twelve disciples together, then to a crowd of more than five hundred at one time, then to his brother James, and last of all to Paul. Different people, different places, sometimes one on one and sometimes in large groups, spread across several weeks. That is a great many separate sightings to dismiss all at once.
Transformed
These were people who had scattered and gone into hiding the moment Jesus was arrested. Within weeks that same frightened group was standing up in public declaring he was alive, and most of them were eventually tortured and killed for refusing to take it back. People will die for something they sincerely believe is true. They do not die for something they know they made up, and these men were in a position to know exactly which it was.
Even sceptics
Two of the witnesses started out against him. James, Jesus' own brother, did not believe in him during his lifetime, yet he became a leader of the Jerusalem church and was later killed for it. Paul was actively arresting and imprisoning Christians until, by his own testimony, he met the risen Jesus and changed sides entirely. People do not usually convert to the very movement they are trying to destroy.
The theory you'll hear
The hallucination theory: that grief and longing made the disciples imagine him. Two things sink it. First, a hallucination is a private event inside one person's own mind, much like a dream. Two people cannot share the same one, and a crowd of five hundred all having the identical vision is not something that occurs. Second, and this is the decisive part, a hallucination only ever happens in the head. It does nothing to a body. So even if every disciple had somehow imagined him, the corpse would still have been lying in the tomb, and the authorities could have ended the whole thing in an afternoon by carrying it out for everyone to see. There was no body to carry out.
✓
Confidence: too many witnesses, too many settings, too deep a change in them, including former enemies. Something real stood behind it.
Putting it together
The bottom line
Four steps, one conclusion

So, can you be confident?

1 · Execution
He really died. The flogging, the cross and the spear leave no realistic room for doubt.
›
2 · Early testimony
The claim he rose was circulating within a few years, recited as a creed, not a legend that grew up later.
›
3 · Empty tomb
The grave was empty, and even the authorities conceded it by claiming the body had been stolen.
›
4 · Eyewitnesses
Many people, including former opponents like James and Paul, were convinced they saw him alive and died saying so.
›

Take any one piece on its own and a clever sceptic might find a workaround. Line all four up at once, the death, the early creed, the empty tomb and the eyewitnesses, and the explanation that accounts for everything at the same time is the one the first witnesses gave. He rose.
