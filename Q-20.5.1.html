Does the church’s hypocrisy discredit Christianity?

The most common street-level objection in §20 — answered not by defending Christian behaviour, but by turning the word “hypocrite” into evidence for the standard it presupposes.

The question

If Christians — pastors and entire historical institutions alike — so visibly fail to live up to Christ’s teaching, doesn’t that failure discredit Christianity itself?

This is the objection most likely to surface in a street conversation, a podcast cross-examination, or from a wounded deconstructing believer. It wears three faces: the personal hypocrite (“Christians don’t practise what they preach”), the institutional scandal (Crusades, Inquisition, witch trials, clergy abuse, the fallen megachurch pastor), and the cynical charge that the church’s doctrine of human worthlessness is a marketing ploy to keep adherents subservient.

The convergent reply is not a defence of conduct. It is a criterion move: the very ability to call someone a hypocrite presupposes the objective standard he is failing — Christ’s own command. The standard is not refuted by the failure; it is ratified by it.

The convergent argument — all three experts

Premise 1

The charge of “hypocrite” only makes sense if there is a real, objective standard the person is failing.

Knechtle’s anchor. A non-Christian who calls a hating Christian a “hypocrite” is appealing — without a PhD in the New Testament — to Jesus’s command to love. You cannot be a hypocrite to a standard that does not exist. Grounded in 1 John: “if I say I love God and hate my brother I’m a liar.”

Premise 2

That standard is Christ’s teaching, not the conduct of his followers.

Lennox & Strobel. Lennox: those who take up weapons in Christ’s name “are not following him, they’re disobeying him.” Strobel: “our faith is not in the pastor. Our faith is in Jesus.” The faith is anchored in the founder and the evidence, never in the adherent.

Premise 3

Christ’s teaching explicitly forbids the very behaviour the hypocrite is condemned for.

The Pilate hermeneutical key (Lennox). Jesus’s answer to Pilate — “my kingdom is not of this world, otherwise my servants would have been fighting” — plus the healed ear at his arrest. The founder demonstrably forbids the violence done in his name.

Conclusion

The hypocrite ratifies the standard rather than refuting it; Christianity must be tested against its founder and source documents, not its failures.

The pivot to evidence. Knechtle: go to the gospels, not the hypocrite — “the grave of Muhammad is full, the grave of Jesus Christ is empty.” Lennox: “false coinage doesn’t prove the real stuff doesn’t exist.” All three redirect from the ethical objection to the Resurrection evidence base.

How each expert deploys it

Cliffe Knechtle 4 transcripts
The dominant voice — four distinct deployments
Signature moves
  • “Hypocrite is a compliment to truth”: the word a non-Christian reaches for to condemn a hating Christian itself presupposes Jesus’s command to love. Concedes his own struggle — “do I struggle with hatred? Yes — but the operative word is struggle.”
  • Islam-parity / 9/11 analogy: judging Islam by 9/11 makes you “a hypocritical bigot”; the identical standard sends you to the source documents — Matthew, Mark, Luke and John — not to the hypocrite.
  • “Marketing ploy” rebuttal: re-defines self-righteousness as “I am morally better than you,” then grounds human worth in the imago Dei and the cross — “you’re not worthless.”
  • Pivots to the evidence: faith in Christ “is not blind faith … it is based on solid evidence that Christ is reliable.”
Backfire — riskiest move

His “atheists are running away from God like a criminal doesn’t find the police” parity is called out by Julian Baggini as an ad hominem that cuts both ways. Knechtle backpedals to disavow targeting Baggini personally but never answers the symmetry critique.

John Lennox 3 transcripts
Institution-vs-founder — “christendom, not Christianity”
Signature moves
  • Costly personal anchor: Northern Ireland — “my brother was nearly killed by a bomb … in the name of Christianity.” Owns the shame before answering.
  • The Pilate hermeneutical key: Jesus was himself tried as a violent revolutionary and acquitted — “my kingdom is not of this world.” He stopped the two sword-bearing disciples and healed the severed ear.
  • The verdict: weapon-bearers in Christ’s name “are not Christians in any real sense at all … this is christendom … not true Christianity.”
  • False-coinage analogy: counterfeits don’t prove the genuine article doesn’t exist, only that it may be hard to find.
  • The Hitchens turn: “all your objections to bad religion I share” — Jesus attacked the religious rogues too; properly understood, Hitchens “should be on Christ’s side.”
  • Fundamentalist certainty vs confidence: atrocity flows from closed receptors; 70 years of being “prepared to be vulnerable” is the safeguard. “You cannot impose truth by force.”
Lee Strobel 1 transcript
The fallen pastor — re-grounding in evidence
Signature moves
  • Foundation is not the pastor: when his own mentor Bill Hybels fell, “our faith is not in the pastor. Our faith is in Jesus … Historical data. I go back to that.”
  • Re-grounding as inoculant: the scandal is a deconversion trigger, not an epistemic defeater. “I look at the four E’s … That has nothing to do with Bill Hybels.”
  • Accountability structures: elders who are employees of the senior pastor cannot hold him to account; concrete practice — travelling with his wife, Mark Mittelberg as accountability partner since 1987, the Modesto Manifesto.
Distinctive contribution

The only treatment of the modern fallen-megachurch-pastor face, and the only one offering concrete institutional safeguards rather than abstract advice.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

“Christians don’t practise what they preach”

The visible gap between Christian profession and Christian behaviour shows the faith is empty — or at least gives no reason to take it seriously.

Standard popular / street objection

Strongest response

The word “hypocrite” ratifies the standard

You cannot be a hypocrite to a standard that does not exist. The charge itself presupposes Christ’s command. The failure convicts the man against the standard — so go to the source documents, not the hypocrite.

Knechtle “hypocrite is a compliment to truth”

Objection 2

Christian history is a catalogue of atrocity

Crusades, the Spanish Inquisition, witch trials, slavery justified from Scripture, sexual abuse within the church — the horrific history of the Christian religion discredits its claims.

Veritas-Forum interlocutor; Dawkins-debate framing

Strongest response

Institution vs founder — christendom, not Christianity

The perpetrators were disobeying Christ’s explicit command. He was himself tried as a violent revolutionary and acquitted — “my kingdom is not of this world.” This is christendom, “one of the scandals of christendom,” not true Christianity.

Lennox Pilate hermeneutical key + healed-ear scene

Objection 3

Religion causes war; certainty licenses violence

Religious certainty spills into doctrine and moral practice, and that certainty justifies almost any behaviour — Christianity is a cause of war.

Christopher Hitchens (via Lennox); Alex (Oxford podcast)

Strongest response

Truth cannot be imposed by force

Jesus told Pilate his witness to truth “doesn’t involve violence.” The driver of atrocity is “fundamentalist certainty” with closed receptors, not faith. Lennox: “all your objections to bad religion I share” — Hitchens, understood, should be on Christ’s side.

Lennox Hitchens-against-Hitchens + certainty/confidence distinction

Objection 4

A famous pastor fell, so the faith is false

Hybels, Robert Morris, Swaggart, Bakker, Haggard — the parade of fallen leaders shows the whole enterprise is rotten.

PBD-podcast host

Strongest response

Trigger, not defeater — re-ground in evidence

Faith is anchored in the Resurrection evidence, not the leader. “I look at the four E’s … That has nothing to do with Bill Hybels.” Accountability structures — non-employee elders, accountability partners — are the practical safeguard.

Strobel re-grounding + accountability practice

Objection 5

“Worthlessness” doctrine is a marketing ploy

The teaching that you’re sinful and not self-righteous is a tool — “a marketing ploy” — that a few corrupted individuals use to keep adherents down.

#1212 self-righteous skeptic

Strongest response

Re-define the term; ground worth in imago Dei

Self-righteousness is not “I have value” — it is “I am morally better than you.” Christianity grounds human worth in being made in God’s image and bought back by the cross: “you’re so valuable that God sent his only son … you’re not worthless.”

Knechtle imago Dei + cross

Objection 6

The church is a manipulative power structure

Church leadership exists to dominate and keep ordinary believers subservient.

#1212 self-righteous skeptic

Strongest response

Concede the abuse; distinguish community from hierarchy

Power-as-domination in church leadership is an abuse — conceded. But legitimate pastoral leadership is influence within a community, not hierarchy: “it’s not a power relationship … we do it as a community.”

Knechtle community-not-hierarchy

Objection 7

Psychological diagnosis cuts both ways

If atheists “run away from God” to escape moral conviction, believers could equally be accused of “hiding from their own mortality.” Diagnosing your opponent’s motives is not an argument.

Julian Baggini, in response to Knechtle

Gap — not engaged

Knechtle disavows, never answers

Knechtle backpedals to clarify he was not targeting Baggini personally — “I don’t have the faintest idea what motivates you, Julian” — but never engages the symmetry critique that psychological diagnosis is not an argument. The corpus’s clearest unanswered objection.
