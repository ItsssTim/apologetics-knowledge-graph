The question

Did the “coming of the Son of Man” Jesus predicted in the Olivet Discourse already occur in AD 70 — or is it still future?

This is the interpretive axis the second-coming node (Q-12.2.1) raises through the Olivet Discourse but deliberately defers, and where the apocalyptic-genre question of §14.1.3 becomes a doctrinal fork. The stakes are the credibility of Jesus as a prophet. Mark 13:30 — “this generation will not pass away before all these things take place” — looks, on a futurist reading, like a near-term prediction that failed.

Preterism (G.B. Caird, R.T. France, and most notably N.T. Wright) dissolves the difficulty: the events did happen within that generation, in AD 70, so the prophecy was fulfilled. The price, Craig argues, is an invisible coming of the Son of Man — a heavenly enthronement rather than a visible return — which multiplies Christ’s comings exactly as the rapture view does. A taxonomic distinction runs through the whole node: partial preterism (Olivet events fulfilled in AD 70, the bodily resurrection still future) versus full / hyper-preterism (everything, the resurrection included, already past). Craig’s decisive “Achilles heel” refutes the full form outright; against the partial form it shows tension — and, awkwardly, his own AD 70 reading of Mark 13:30 shares the partial preterist’s ground.

The convergent core — a shared hermeneutic, a forked verdict

This is not one argument the experts converge on. It is a shared hermeneutical premise that then forks. All five treatments grant the genre point; they split on how far it reaches. The three columns below are the agreed ground; the tree beneath is where the verdict divides.

Fork A

Partial preterism — the Olivet “coming” is the AD 70 judgment; the resurrection and final coming are still future.

Advocates in corpus. Michael Jones (self-identified), R.C. Sproul (named), N.T. Wright (per Craig). Two distinct comings are already granted — which is precisely the “multiple comings” Craig charges against the view.

Fork B

Full / hyper-preterism — even the resurrection of the dead is already past, fulfilled in AD 70.

No advocate in corpus. The position functions only as the foil Craig’s “Achilles heel” argument destroys — the resurrection plainly did not occur in AD 70.

Fork C

Classical futurism — the “coming of the Son of Man” is the visible, public return to earth, future, with the resurrection of the dead.

Craig’s landing. Lennox refuses the fork altogether, calling Revelation “all three” — first-century, prophetic, and principial at once — a both/and that sidesteps the taxonomy.

How each expert deploys it

Honest density. This is a WLC-dominant node: two of the five transcripts (both Defenders 3 “Last Things” lectures) carry the entire spine. The other three are single-paragraph touches from adjacent loci — Bauckham and Jones reach it through Knechtle interviews, Lennox through a Revelation-hermeneutics segment.

William Lane Craig2 transcripts
The spine — sympathetic reconstruction, then three-point refutation
Part 5 · the steel-man
  • Names the best advocates — Caird, France, and “most notably” N.T. Wright, “very well-known and highly respected.”
  • Core claim — the Olivet coming already occurred in AD 70; the Son of Man was enthroned in heaven.
  • Cosmic imagery = apocalyptic symbolism, via Isaiah 13:10 and Ezekiel 32:7, clinched by Acts 2 / Joel at Pentecost — conceded as a strong card.
  • Daniel 7:13-14 = ascent, not descent — the son of man comes to the Ancient of Days to receive the kingdom; the “coming” is enthronement.
  • The real engine: Mark 13:30. What actually drives preterism is the need to solve the delay of the parousia — on this view the “knotty problem” verse is simply true.
Part 6 · the refutation
  • Argument 1 — “come” is perspectival. Acts 1:11 fixes the direction (“come … as you saw him go”); plus Mark 13:26, 14:62, Rev 1:7 and Gundry’s lightning-contrast with the secret false messiahs → a visible coming to earth.
  • Argument 2 — no 40-year wait. 1 Cor 15:23-28 (~AD 55) and Hebrews 2:7-9 show Christ already reigning before AD 70; he need not wait to be enthroned.
  • Argument 3 — the resurrection, the “Achilles heel.” All Paul’s letters predate AD 70 and look forward to the resurrection at Christ’s return; it did not happen in AD 70.
Hostile-witness climax

Asked at a conference whether the resurrection is therefore also past, N.T. Wright — preterism’s leading defender — answered no: Christ will come again at the end of the age and then the dead will be raised. His own concession re-introduces the multiple-comings division Craig objects to.

Partial-preterism caveat

Craig collapses partial vs full preterism. Argument 3 only contradicts the full form; against a partial preterist who already grants a future resurrection it shows tension, not refutation. And Craig himself reads Mark 13:30 as fulfilled in AD 70 (Q-12.2.1) — the partial preterist’s own move. The real distance from Wright is narrower than the lectures imply.

  • Ezekiel 32:7-8 — sun-blackening for a judgment on Egypt: “I must have missed the part where the sun stopped shining in the sky.”

John Lennox1 transcript
The “all three” both/and
Distinctive moves
  • Refuses futurism, preterism and idealism — Revelation is all three at once: first-century letter, genuine prophecy, and principial exposition.
  • The three / three symmetry — chapter 1 names the coming three times (“every eye shall see him”), chapter 22 three times (“behold, I come quickly”). The book is framed by the second coming.
  • Symbol stands for reality — lampstands are churches, monsters are empires or leaders; the dismissive “it’s all surreal symbolism” reading is “simply false.”
  • Name-claims C.S. Lewis on metaphor — locking the most authoritative modern apologist into “symbol must encode reality.”
Richard Bauckhamvia Knechtle interview
Virtually-entirely-symbolic + inaugurated eschatology
Distinctive moves
  • Pushes the symbolic reading furthest: “virtually nothing in the book of Revelation is literal.”
  • Inaugurated / amillennial — “people have been living in the end times ever since the resurrection… the kingdom is active but has not yet come in its fullness.”
  • The four horsemen describe “how history always is … business as usual,” not end-time markers; the pandemic is not exceptional.
  • Burden of proof on the literalist — “the onus [is] on the person who says you should take this literally.”
  • Keeps a future literal expectation: Jesus returns at the end of history “but he’s not going to ride on a white horse out of heaven” — aligning him against full preterism.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

“This generation will not pass away” is a failed prophecy

Mark 13:30 / Matthew 16:28 commit Jesus to a near-term prediction. The events should have occurred within his hearers’ lifetime; pushing them to the far future makes him a failed prophet (the delay of the parousia).

Skeptical / critical scholarship

Strongest response

The preterist solution — it was fulfilled in AD 70

The events did occur within that generation, in AD 70, so the prophecy was fulfilled. Craig concedes this is preterism’s strongest card — “it would be nice if it were true” — but rejects the price. Craig gives no positive futurist reading of the verse in either session (see gaps).

Jones partial-preterist caseCraig reports it, then declines the price

Objection 2

The cosmic language must be literal astronomy

Darkened sun, falling stars, the powers of heaven shaken — if Jesus predicted these and they did not literally occur, the prophecy failed.

Literalist / popular

Strongest response

It is standard apocalyptic idiom

OT control texts (Isaiah 13, Ezekiel 32, Isaiah 34, Isaiah 19:1) use the same imagery for ordinary historical judgments — and at Pentecost Peter declares Joel’s cosmic-portent prophecy fulfilled though none of it literally happened (Acts 2). Craig calls this “genuinely strong.”

Jones Ezekiel / Isaiah parallelsCraig the Acts 2 clincherBauckham symbolic reading

Objection 3

Daniel 7’s “coming” is an upward enthronement

In Daniel 7:13-14 the son of man comes to the Ancient of Days in heaven to receive the kingdom — an ascent. So the predicted “coming” need not be a return to earth; it can be the heavenly enthronement of AD 70.

The core preterist hermeneutic (Caird / France / Wright)

Strongest response

“Come” is perspectival — a coming to earth

“Come” and “go” are observer-relative. Acts 1:11 fixes the direction: Christ will come the way the disciples saw him go — to where the observers are, earth. Reinforced by the universal-visibility texts (Mark 13:26; 14:62; Rev 1:7) and Gundry’s contrast with the secret false messiahs.

Craig Argument 1 — perspectival grammar + Acts 1:11

Objection 4

AD 70 is when Christ was enthroned and received the kingdom

The fall of Jerusalem is the moment the Son of Man is vindicated and enthroned; that is what the “coming” refers to.

Preterist

Strongest response

He need not wait 40 years to reign

A gap (AD 30/33 → AD 70) in which the risen, ascended Son of Man is somehow not yet reigning is “preposterous.” 1 Cor 15:23-28 (~AD 55) and Hebrews 2:7-9 already present Christ reigning before AD 70, with the kingdom delivered to the Father reserved for his future coming.

Craig Argument 2 — pre-AD 70 enthronement

Objection 5

If the Son of Man came in AD 70, the resurrection is also past

Paul ties the resurrection of the dead to the coming of the Son of Man. If that coming is AD 70, consistency demands the resurrection happened then too.

Full / hyper-preterism (the position, no named advocate)

Strongest response

The “Achilles heel” — via hostile witness

The resurrection plainly did not occur in AD 70; Paul (pre-AD 70) anticipates it as future. Even N.T. Wright, preterism’s chief defender, concedes the resurrection is future — which re-introduces the very multiple-comings division the view was meant to escape. The natural reading: Paul and Jesus describe the same event.

Craig Argument 3 — Wright as hostile witness

Objection 6

Revelation is too symbolic to apply to anything specific

It is all surreal monsters, snakes and dragons — so it cannot have any concrete relevance to us or to history.

Dismissive / popular

Strongest response

Symbol is application

Metaphor and symbolism always stand for an underlying reality, never against it. The book itself tells us the monsters are empires or their leaders. “My heart is broken” does not mean the pump is defective; it points to a real experience. The symbol encodes reality (C.S. Lewis).

Lennox the Lewis metaphor key

Objection 7

The pandemic and current crises show we are in the end times

War, famine, plague and disaster are visibly intensifying — the four horsemen are riding, so the end must be near.

Popular (Knechtle’s setup)

Strongest response

Catastrophe is historically normal

The four horsemen describe “how history always is … business as usual.” The West’s recent ~100 years of comfort is the anomaly; COVID-19 is mild beside the Black Death or the 1918 influenza. The pandemic is not an end-times marker.

Bauckham historical-normalcy reframe

Objection 8

Revelation is “the most peaceful, loving book of the Bible”

The sanitised liberal reading empties the book of its judgments — treating it as gentle reassurance with no nightmare in it.

A liberal pastor (relayed by Knechtle)

Strongest response

There are genuine nightmare passages

The plagues and judgments are meant to be a nightmare; the sanitised reading is false to the text. Bauckham does not address the encounter directly, but his framing — there are terrifying passages — implicitly rejects it.

Bauckham implicit rejection

Persistent gaps in the corpus

Gap 1

No positive WLC reading of Mark 13:30.

The conspicuous open gap. Across both sessions Craig frames the “this generation” objection as preterism’s motivation and rejects preterism’s answer — but never says how he reads the verse on a futurist view. The constructive answer to the central skeptical objection is absent from this locus. Standard options (“this generation” = the Jewish race; the generation alive at the end; “all these things” = only the AD 70 signs) go unmentioned.

Gap 2

The partial-preterist rejoinder is never engaged.

A partial preterist (Wright, Jones, Sproul) already agrees the final resurrection is future. So Argument 3 refutes only full preterism; against the partial form it shows tension. Craig never answers the partial preterist’s actual claim — that Jesus spoke of both an AD 70 judgment-coming and a future final coming in one discourse — the sharpest available counter to Argument 3, sharpened further by Craig’s own AD 70 reading of Mark 13:30 (Q-12.2.1).

Gap 3

The genre point Craig conceded in Part 5 is left to undercut his Argument 1.

Having granted that “seeing”-the-coming language can be apocalyptic idiom (Isaiah 13 / Ezekiel 32 / Acts 2), Craig asserts visible publicity in Part 6 (Mark 13:26; Rev 1:7) without re-engaging whether those very “every eye will see him” texts are themselves idiom. Gundry’s lightning-contrast establishes vivid public imagery, not necessarily a literal visible descent. The rebuttal to his own argument is left implicit.

Gap 4

Full / hyper-preterism has no advocate in the corpus.

It functions only as the foil Craig’s Achilles-heel argument destroys. No transcript presents it in its own voice, so the strongest full-preterist replies — e.g. a “spiritual body” reading of 1 Cor 15 — are neither stated nor answered.

Gap 5

Lennox’s “all three” is asserted, not adjudicated.

He claims Revelation is first-century and prophetic and principial but never says how the three readings are weighted when they conflict — which is exactly the question the preterism / futurism debate turns on.

This is an attractive view because it solves the very knotty problem of verse 30, where Jesus says all these things will take place before this generation passes away.
