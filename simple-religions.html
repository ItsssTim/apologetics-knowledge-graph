The tolerant objection

Aren't all religions basically the same?

It is the kindest-sounding idea going: every religion is just a different path up the same mountain, blind men each feeling one part of the same elephant. It sounds humble and generous. But it quietly assumes something nobody could actually know, and it does not survive a close look at what the religions really teach.

First / Where they overlap
Step 1 of 4
Shared morals, shared values

The similarity is real, but it is on the surface.

Start by giving the objection its due, because there is something to it. At the level of basic ethics, the major faiths really do echo one another.

The overlap
Nearly every tradition condemns murder, theft, betrayal and cruelty, and some version of treat others as you would want to be treated turns up almost everywhere. On day-to-day right and wrong, a Christian, a Muslim and a Buddhist will often nod along together. This is genuine common ground, not a trick.
Shared values
They also tend to prize the same things: compassion, honesty, humility, self-control, caring for the poor. And nearly all of them share one deep instinct, that the world is not as it should be and that we ourselves ought to be better than we are. That shared ache is real, and it is exactly why the same mountain idea feels true.
Even here
But the agreement frays even at this level. Buddhism teaches that the route to peace is to let go of desire itself; Jesus says the opposite, to hunger and thirst for what is right, to want the good more, not less. So even on values the paths are already turning away from each other. Hold that thought.
The theory you'll hear
So you admit they teach the same good things, why quibble over the rest? Because shared ethics is the surface, not the substance. Agreeing that kindness is good is a long way from agreeing on who God is, what a human being is, or what happens when you die. And those are not footnotes. They change everything about how you live and what you hope for. The overlap is real; it is just not where the action is.
✓
Confidence: the religions do share a real moral floor, and that is worth honouring. But a shared sense of right and wrong is not the same as a shared account of reality.
Second / Where they split
Step 2 of 4
Origin, identity, purpose, destiny

On the questions that matter, they contradict.

Every worldview has to answer four big questions: where we came from, who we are, why we are here, and where it is all going. Here the faiths do not blur into one. They give answers that cannot all be true.

Origin
Where did everything come from? Christianity, like Judaism and Islam, says a personal God made it on purpose. Much of Hindu and Eastern thought says it flows out of an impersonal divine reality that everything is part of. The secular answer is matter and chance, with no one behind it. Those are three different stories, not one in three outfits.
Identity
What are you? Christianity says a person made in God's image, of real but dependent worth. Hindu thought says your separate self is finally an illusion to see past, since all is ultimately one. The secular view says you are a clever animal, a passing arrangement of atoms. Sacred image-bearer, illusion to dissolve, or accident of chemistry. They exclude each other.
Purpose
Why are you here? To know and love the God who made you (Christianity). To break free of the cycle of rebirth and the grip of craving (Buddhism, much of Hinduism). To submit to God and so earn paradise (Islam). Or to invent whatever meaning you like, since none came built in (the secular view). Those aim a life in opposite directions.
Destiny
Where is it all heading? Christianity says resurrection and life with God. The Eastern traditions say reincarnation, reborn again and again until you are finally released. Islam says paradise or judgement, weighed on the scales of your deeds. The secular answer is that the lights simply go out. They cannot all be what waits.
The theory you'll hear
If you'd been born in India you would be Hindu, so it is all just an accident of geography. Probably true, and beside the point. Where you picked up a belief says nothing about whether it is true. Born in a different century you would believe different science too, and that does not make today's science false. The question was never where did your belief come from. It is which of these accounts is actually right.
✓
Confidence: two plus two is four, not three and not five, and truth works the same way. The religions can all be wrong, or one can be right, but their opposite answers cannot all be true at once.
Third / What is unique
Step 3 of 4
Grace, the claim, the evidence

Christianity runs in the opposite direction.

Once you see they really do differ, the question becomes which one, if any, is true. And here Christianity is not just one more option on the shelf. It is built the other way around.

Done, not do
Every other system is, at heart, a ladder: do enough good to outweigh the bad, work off your karma, keep the Five Pillars, walk the Eightfold Path. It is about what you must do to climb up to God. Christianity alone says the ladder does not work, so God climbed down to us. It is not a religion of achievement, it is a rescue. Done, not do.
His claim
The founders of the other faiths pointed to a way: Buddha to a path, Muhammad to a book. Jesus said I am the way, and claimed to be God in person and to forgive sins (John 14:6). That is why you cannot quietly file him as one more wise teacher. As Knechtle puts it, either he is God in human form, as the eyewitnesses said, or just a good teacher, as the Quran says. His own words will not let you have it both ways.
Only one
The historian Gary Habermas notes something startling: of all the founders of the major world religions, only Jesus is reliably recorded as claiming to be God. Confucius and Lao-tzu taught morals, the Buddha was effectively non-theistic, and the Quran makes Muhammad a prophet, never deity. The nearest parallel, Krishna, fails the test, because the texts are late, non-historical, and unsure he even lived. Jesus called himself the Son of God and forgave sins as only God can (Matthew 11:27) (Mark 2:5-7).
Checkable
And Christianity bets everything on a public, checkable event: a real execution and an empty tomb, in front of witnesses, in datable history. Other faiths tend to rest on a private revelation or the beauty of a teaching. The titles of deity were applied to Jesus within a few years, in creeds his followers were already reciting (Romans 1:3-4), not legends grown over centuries. Christianity says go and investigate the evidence. That is a very different kind of claim, and a riskier one, which is part of why it is worth taking seriously.
The theory you'll hear
Jesus was just a great moral teacher, like the others. It is the one box his own words rule out. Good teachers do not generally claim to be God, to forgive sins committed against other people, or to be the one road to the Father. Either those claims are true, or they are the claims of someone badly deluded or dishonest. What they cannot be is the modest sayings of just another sage in the line-up.
✓
Confidence: grace instead of climbing, a founder who claimed to be God, and a faith staked on checkable history. On all three, Christianity is in a different category, not a variation on the theme.
Fourth / Why it fits best
Step 4 of 4
And isn't that arrogant?

It tells the truth about us we already half-know.

Being different is not the same as being right. So here is why so many find that Christianity, of all of them, fits the world as we actually live it.

Both at once
We all feel two things that are hard to hold together: that human beings have real worth, and that we are also genuinely broken. The secular story struggles to ground real worth in mere atoms. Some Eastern thought treats the self as an illusion, which dissolves the worth. Christianity says both at full strength: made in God's image, and truly fallen. It matches what you already sense about yourself.
Open to all
Yes, Christianity is exclusive, Jesus as the one way. But it is the most inclusive exclusive claim there is. Because you get in by grace and not by merit, the door is open to anyone: any country, any caste, any record, the respectable and the wreck alike. No good-enough scoreboard, no lucky birth required. Narrow door, but flung wide to everybody.
Rings true
Put it together and it has the ring of truth: it explains the moral law we keep failing, the worth we cannot shake, the brokenness we cannot fix, and it answers them not with more rules but with rescue. It does not just make sense in a lecture. It fits the actual texture of being human.
The theory you'll hear
Isn't it arrogant to say your religion is the only right one? Notice that all are equally valid is itself a claim that excludes everyone who says otherwise, which is most of the world's believers. So the pluralist is not standing above the argument, they have just made their own. And the blind-men-and-the-elephant story only works if the storyteller can somehow see the whole elephant, the very God's-eye view it insists nobody has. The honest question is not who is being exclusive. Everyone is. It is who is right.
✓
Confidence: claiming to be true is not arrogance, it is what every worldview does, pluralism included. Christianity backs its claim by fitting reality, holding our worth and our brokenness together, and opening the door to all.

The generous-sounding answer flattens away the very differences that matter most. Christianity does not ask you to climb; it says God came down, in person, in history, and threw the door open to anyone willing to walk through it.
