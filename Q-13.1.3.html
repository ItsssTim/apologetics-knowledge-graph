Does the image of God ground human worth?

The load-bearing claim of Christian ethics — that inherent dignity flows from the imago Dei, and that the secular world is living on borrowed capital it can no longer fund. Three experts, four substantive treatments.

The question

Is the image of God the actual foundation of human dignity — and is that grounding replaceable by a secular account?

If the imago Dei is the real source of human worth, a great deal follows: the abolition of slavery, the care of the disabled, the wrongness of slavery and the Holocaust, and the modern vocabulary of human rights all trace to it — and the contemporary secular world is living on borrowed capital it can no longer fund.

If the grounding is replaceable — Kantian autonomy, evolutionary kin-altruism, secular-humanist welfare — the apologetic loses its distinctiveness. The three experts converge on the same borrowed-capital move: the sceptic’s own moral judgements presuppose a grounding only the image of God supplies.

The convergent argument — all three experts

The shared structure is an inference to the best explanation, not a deductive syllogism. Its force comes from the gap between what the interlocutor believes morally and what their worldview can justify — doubled by a historical reductio: subtract the image of God and you get the Gulag and the transhumanist abolition of man.

Premise 1

Every human holds certain moral convictions as universal and authoritative.

Observed phrasings. Perry: “all human beings have a moral compass inside of them that says I deserve dignity and respect.” Lennox: “human values, human dignity, human rights, all of that flow from that statement.” Slavery and the Holocaust are known to be wrong — not provisionally, but absolutely.

Premise 2

The interlocutor’s own worldview cannot supply the grounding for those convictions.

The stress test. Perry: the agnostic knows the Holocaust is wrong “because her heart says it’s wrong,” not because society says so. Stuart Knechtle: humanism “does not travel well” cross-culturally. Society, majority opinion and secular humanism are each shown to under-determine the verdict.

Premise 3

The best — and historically the only durable — explanation is that humans are made in the image of God.

Positive grounding. Lennox: only humans bear the image, “so that gives them infinite value and dignity.” Perry: “deep down inside every human being knows that they were created in the image of God.” The image stamps real, indelible worth and moral knowledge into the person.

Conclusion

Therefore the image of God is the foundation of human dignity; the secularist lives by capital his worldview cannot fund.

Reinforced negatively. Perry: “we live like atheists but we hold to the Christian worldview.” Where the doctrine is removed, atrocity follows — Lennox’s Stalinist collectivisation, Stuart’s rationalised abortion of Down-syndrome children and abandonment of Alzheimer’s patients.

How each expert deploys it

John Lennox 1 transcript
Historical reductio + hostile witness (the fullest case)
Signature moves
  • Positive grounding: only humans bear the image — the stars show God’s glory but are not made in it. The telescope detail marks the categorical difference of the human.
  • Hostile witness — Jordan Peterson: a non-Christian who “stopped and hesitated” and called the image of God “the cornerstone of civilization.”
  • Historical reductio — Stalin: replace the individual-in-God’s-image with state-enforced “equality” and you get “the blood of hundreds of millions.”
  • Contemporary application: Harari’s Homo Deus and Lewis’s The Abolition of Man — germline modification yields “not human beings but artifacts.”
Distinctive structure

Lennox is the only one to argue in both directions — positive infinite value, plus a two-sided consequence argument with Stalin behind and transhumanism ahead.

Preston Perry 1 transcript
The borrowed-capital IBE
Signature moves
  • Runs it as a live street dialectic against an agnostic philosophy major — the theological pivot of the whole conversation.
  • Does not lead with the doctrine; asks questions until her relativism is visibly stressed, then introduces the image of God as the resolution.
  • Heart, not society: she knows the Holocaust is wrong “because her heart says it’s wrong.”
  • Signature formulation: “we live like atheists but we hold to the Christian worldview.”
  • Asymmetry punchline: “in my worldview I have a basis to say… enslaving me it’s wrong — in your worldview I don’t really know.”
Distinctive method

Dignity-first: Perry argues from the interlocutor’s existing moral knowledge back to its only adequate source, rather than asserting Scripture at her.

Cliffe Knechtle 1 transcript
Definitional correction (worth conferred, not earned)
Signature moves
  • A skeptic charges that Christian self-righteousness teaching is “a marketing employ… a tool to keep me down.”
  • Definitional re-set: self-righteousness is not “I have value” — it is “I am morally better than you.”
  • Opposite anthropology: “the Bible says you’re so valuable that God sent his only son… to buy you back — you’re not worth less.”
  • Worth is grounded supra-personally (image + redemption), so it cannot function competitively as self-deprecation.
  • Narrow concession on hierarchy abuse: “it’s not a power relationship… we do it as a community.”
Stuart Knechtle 1 transcript (Aron Ra / Tjump debate)
Cultural-durability genealogy (Holland / Ferry)
Signature moves
  • Being made in the image of a triune God “historically drove the abolition of slavery and care for the disabled.”
  • Humanism doesn’t travel: “chart the last 25 humanist conferences — one was outside a western country.”
  • Trinity premise: “you can’t experience love throughout time with just one person” — only a triune God grounds eternal relational love.
  • Contemporary reductio: without indelible worth, the abortion of Down-syndrome children and the abandonment of Alzheimer’s patients become rational.
Where it stalls

Aron Ra replies that Stuart “said nothing… about us actually having a soul” and “jumped to apologetics” — the charge that the case smuggles theism into a soul debate goes unanswered.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

“Christian worth-talk is a marketing ploy to keep adherents down”

A self-described self-righteous skeptic: “a few corrupted individuals are using that as a tool to keep me down… it’s like a marketing employ — that’s my beef.”

Cliffe’s street interlocutor

Strongest response

Definitional re-set

Self-righteousness is moral superiority, not having value. Christianity grounds worth in the image of God plus redemption, not in self-comparison — so it cannot function as competitive self-deprecation.

Cliffe Knechtle definitional correction

Objection 2

“The Bible says you’re worthless”

“You read the Bible… you are worthless, you shouldn’t be self-righteous” — the doctrine reads as systematic self-deprecation.

Cliffe’s street interlocutor

Strongest response

Worth is conferred, not earned

“You’re so valuable that God sent his only son… to buy you back — you’re not worth less.” The image of God “totally trumps” any verdict of worthlessness.

Cliffe Knechtle imago Dei + redemption

Objection 3

“Church hierarchy weaponises this doctrine”

Religious authority structures exploit teaching about human unworthiness to keep people subservient.

Cliffe’s street interlocutor

Strongest response

Narrow concession + community framing

Power-as-domination is itself an abuse the gospel rejects (“oh good, we don’t buy into that”). Legitimate leadership is community influence: “it’s not a power relationship… we do it as a community.”

Cliffe Knechtle pastoral concession

Objection 4

Nihilism — there is no inherent worth to ground

If naturalism is true there is no objective human value; the imago Dei is answering a question that doesn’t arise.

Aron Ra (debate opener)

Strongest response

Durability + meaning route

Stuart pivots to the imago Dei / meaning route and the Holland-style genealogy: the doctrine has demonstrably sustained human worth for 2,000 years and is “way more durable” than humanism, which “does not travel well.”

Stuart Knechtle cultural-durability genealogy

Strongest response

The twentieth-century controlled experiment

Lennox: state-enforced “equality” replacing the individual-made-in-God’s-image produced Stalinist collectivisation and “the blood of hundreds of millions.” Stuart: rationalised abortion of Down-syndrome children and abandonment of Alzheimer’s patients.

“Imago Dei is a cop-out — a refusal to do the philosophical work”

Perry’s agnostic interlocutor

Gap — thin response

Asserted, not developed

Perry insists it is “a logical argument,” concedes her parallel point that religious people sometimes use religion as a cop-out, then returns to the slavery test case. He offers no meta-defence of why the imago Dei is explanatory rather than evasive.

Every expert argues the moral data require a transcendent grounding — but why specifically the Christian God, rather than any other transcendent account of inherent dignity?
