The question

Is the Holy Spirit a personal divine agent — the third person of the Trinity — or merely an impersonal “active force” that God exerts?

This question carries two distinct claims that the corpus tends to run together: that the Spirit is a person (a self-conscious agent, a “who” not a “which”), and that the Spirit is God (sharing the divine name and divine attributes). The two are evidentially separable — one could in principle grant personhood while denying deity — and the strongest opponents exploit exactly that seam.

It is load-bearing against two named adversaries: the Jehovah’s Witness reading (the Spirit is God’s impersonal “active force,” per the New World Translation) and the biblical-Unitarian reading (Anthony Buzzard: the Spirit is God’s dynamic presence, not a distinct person). Both deny the same proposition, and both are answered from the same evidential base — personal action plus divine prerogative.

Coverage caveat. Of the three transcripts tagged here, only two engage the personhood/deity question directly. The third — Cliffe and Stuart Knechtle at Clemson — sits at the §10.1 address but answers the unforgivable sin question instead, and is treated below as a thin adjacent touch, not a third vote in the convergent case.

The convergent case — two evidence streams, not a syllogism

Unlike the Kalam (a deductive syllogism), this is a cumulative biblical case. Personhood and deity are each established by stacking proof-texts, and the two streams are designed to reinforce each other: a thing that acts personally and bears divine attributes is a divine person. Craig works the case from the New Testament; McLatchie works the identical structure from the Old.

Stream A · Personal agency

  • Speaks “me / I” (Acts 13:2)
  • Can be grieved (Isaiah 63:10)
  • Teaches & convicts (John 14–16)
  • Intercedes for the saints (Rom 8:26–27)
  • “Only a person can do” these

Stream B · Divine identity

  • Lied to = lied to God (Acts 5:3–4)
  • Omniscience (Isaiah 40:13)
  • Omnipresence (Psalm 139:7)
  • Creative power (Job 33:4; Ps 104:30)
  • Divine prerogatives, not a force

Stream C · Distinctness

  • Named with Father + Son (Matt 28:19)
  • Pauline benediction (2 Cor 13:14)
  • “Sent forth” by Yahweh (Isa 48:16)
  • Not the Father / Son in a guise
  • Rules out modalism
Stream A

The Spirit does what only a person can do.

Personhood. The Spirit speaks in the first person (“me,” “I” — Acts 13:2), is grieved (Isaiah 63:10), teaches, convicts the world, and intercedes for believers “according to the will of God” (Romans 8). John even uses a masculine pronoun of the neuter noun pneuma — a chosen signal that the Spirit is a “who,” not a “which.”

Stream B

The Spirit bears the divine name and divine attributes.

Deity. Lying to the Spirit is lying to God himself (Acts 5:3–4). The Spirit is omniscient (Isaiah 40:13), omnipresent (Psalm 139:7), and active in creation (Job 33:4; Psalm 104:30) — “divine prerogatives, defining attributes which are attributed to the Holy Spirit” (McLatchie).

Conclusion

A being that acts personally and bears divine attributes and is named distinctly alongside the Father and the Son is a divine person — the third person of the Trinity, not an impersonal force.

The cumulative verdict. Each proof-text is individually re-readable; the combination is meant to make the “active force” reading the worse explanation. The case is abductive and cumulative, not deductive — and, as the gaps section notes, its two halves (personhood, deity) are run together rather than defended separately.

How each expert deploys it

William Lane Craig1 transcript
The dominant voice — three coordinated theses (NT)
Signature deployment
  • Historical opener — the Spirit as “the forgotten person of the Trinity,” neglected by the “binarian” fathers and the bare creeds. A gravity prelude, the same instinct as his Kalam pedigree.
  • Thesis A — the Spirit is God: Acts 5 — lying “to the Holy Spirit” is, in the same breath, lying “to God himself.”
  • Thesis B — the Spirit is a person: first-person speech (Acts 13:2, “only a person can do”); the masculine pronoun used of neuter pneuma; teaching, conviction and intercession “according to the will of God.”
  • Thesis C — distinct from Father and Son: Matthew 28:19 baptismal formula + 2 Corinthians 13:14 benediction name all three together — ruling out modalism.
  • Lay mnemonic: “a who not a which” — “we should never refer to the Holy Spirit as it.”
  • Vocabulary correction: “Holy Ghost” is an archaic translation artefact, not the lingering ghost of Jesus.
Method & limitation

Pure expository proof-texting; no secondary scholarship and no adversarial moves in Craig’s own voice. The personhood premises and the deity premises are run together as mutually reinforcing — without flagging that an opponent might grant personhood while denying deity. The one adversarial move in the transcript (Hebrews 10 against the New World Translation) is supplied by a class member in the Q&A.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

The Spirit is God’s impersonal “active force,” not a person

The New World Translation and biblical Unitarians read the Spirit as God’s dynamic power or presence — something God uses, not someone who acts.

Jehovah’s Witnesses (NWT); Anthony Buzzard

Strongest response

A force does not speak, grieve, or intercede

Craig: the Spirit speaks in the first person (Acts 13:2), teaches, convicts, and intercedes “according to the will of God” (Rom 8) — acts “only a person can do.” McLatchie: the Spirit is grieved (Isaiah 63:10) — “a personal agency, not an impersonal active force.”

Craig NT personal ministriesMcLatchie OT grieved-Spirit

Objection 2

“Lied to the Spirit … lied to God” (Acts 5) doesn’t prove the Spirit is God

Lying to the Spirit could be lying to God because the Spirit represents or belongs to God — not because the Spirit is numerically identical with God.

Jehovah’s Witness / Unitarian parsing

Gap — largely unaddressed

Asserted, not defended

Craig moves straight from the Acts 5 parallel to “the Holy Spirit is God” without engaging the representational reading. A Witness interlocutor would press exactly here, and the corpus has no rebuttal.

Aggregation gap

Objection 3

Personal language is mere personification

Scripture personifies abstractions — Wisdom “cries out” and “was beside him” in Proverbs 8 — without being a literal person. The Spirit’s speaking and grieving could be the same figure of speech.

Standard Jehovah’s Witness / Unitarian

Gap — largely unaddressed

No Proverbs 8 rebuttal in the corpus

Craig asserts first-person speech and personal ministries as decisive without steelmanning the personification reading. This is the central counter to the personhood stream and no expert engages it.

Aggregation gap

Objection 4

The masculine pronoun proves nothing — pneuma gender is grammatical

Greek nouns carry grammatical gender with no metaphysical implication. Pronoun concord with pneuma (neuter) tells us nothing about whether the Spirit is personal.

Jehovah’s Witness / Unitarian grammarians

Strongest response

John violates concord deliberately

Craig: John uses a masculine pronoun (“he”) where neuter is expected — a chosen theological signal, not an accident of grammar. Critics dispute the force of the point; Craig asserts it as decisive without noting the dispute.

Craig masculine-pronoun-against-grammar

Objection 5

Singular-pronoun symmetry: if singular pronouns make the Spirit a person, they make God one person

Ismail: you use singular pronouns to argue the Spirit is a distinct person, yet deny that the same singular pronouns used of God prove God is a single distinct person rather than three. The criterion cuts both ways.

Ismail (vs McLatchie)

Partial — dodged, not dismantled

Relocate to divine prerogatives

McLatchie rests the case on omniscience, omnipresence and creative power rather than on pronoun grammar — the right ground to stand on, since it sidesteps the symmetry. But the reductio itself is not directly answered in the extracted material.

McLatchie prerogatives over grammarSymmetry charge unanswered

Objection 6

Spirit-as-person is a late development (Council of Constantinople, 381)

The doctrine of the Spirit as a distinct divine person was fixed only at Constantinople in 381 — post-apostolic ecclesiastical theology read back into the text.

Ismail (vs McLatchie); church-history reductio

Strongest response

It is read out of the Hebrew Bible

McLatchie: the personal-agency and divine-attribute texts (Isaiah 48:16, 63:10; Psalm 104:30, 139:7) are in the Tanakh, centuries before 381. The doctrine is exegetical, not a fourth-century invention — engaging the substance rather than the date.

McLatchie OT antedates the councils

Objection 7

The Spirit-as-person view was once condemned as Montanist heresy

Early treatments that emphasised the Spirit as an active personal agent were labelled heretical (Montanism) — so the doctrine cannot have been settled orthodoxy.

Ismail (vs McLatchie)

Gap — only indirectly addressed

Implied, not rebutted

McLatchie’s OT case implies the doctrine predates Montanism, but the specific heresy-label reductio is not directly answered in the extracted material. A clean reply would separate Montanist prophetic excess from the Spirit’s personhood as such.

Aggregation gap

Objection 8

“Holy Ghost” means the lingering ghost of Jesus

The archaic phrase “Holy Ghost” suggests the Spirit is Jesus’ ghost, still roaming the earth after the ascension.

Popular Christian misconception

Strongest response

A translation artefact, not a doctrine

Craig: “Holy Ghost” is an “unfortunate archaic translation” — the Spirit is “not Jesus’ ghost who’s somehow still roaming the earth.” A vocabulary tidy-up, characteristic of his pastoral pedagogy, not a concession on the doctrine.

Craig “Holy Ghost” correction

Objection 9

One could grant personhood but deny deity

The personal-agency texts (speaks, grieves, intercedes) at most establish a person — conceivably a created one, like an angel. They do not by themselves establish that the Spirit is God.

Sophisticated Unitarian wedge

Gap — unaddressed

The two claims are never separated

Craig runs the personhood premises and the deity premises together as mutually reinforcing without flagging that they are evidentially distinct. The angelic-person wedge — grant personhood, deny deity — is left unhandled across the corpus.

Aggregation gap

Persistent gaps in the corpus

Gap 1

The personification reading (Proverbs 8 Wisdom) is never engaged.

The most important gap. This is the central Jehovah’s Witness / Unitarian counter to the personhood stream — that the Spirit’s speaking and grieving is figurative, as Wisdom is personified in Proverbs 8 — and no expert in the corpus rebuts it.

Gap 2

The Acts 5 alternative parsing is not addressed.

Craig’s headline deity proof (“lied to the Spirit … lied to God”) is asserted without engaging the representational reading — that lying to the Spirit is lying to God because the Spirit belongs to God, not because the Spirit is God.

Gap 3

Personhood and deity are conflated.

The two claims are evidentially separable (one could grant a personal-but-created Spirit), but the corpus treats them as a single package and never defends the move from personal agency to divine personhood on its own terms.

Gap 4

The singular-pronoun symmetry charge is dodged, not dismantled.

McLatchie relocates to divine prerogatives — the right ground — but does not actually answer Ismail’s reductio that the pronoun criterion, applied consistently, would collapse the three persons into one.

Gap 5

Single-transcript thinness on the headline question.

Only Craig (NT) and McLatchie (OT) treat personhood/deity, one transcript each. There is no folk / street-level deployment — the Knechtle transcript tagged here answers a different question — so the node has no popular register and no third corroborating voice.
