The filling of the Holy Spirit

The question

If every believer is already baptised in and permanently indwelt by the Spirit from the moment of conversion, what is the “filling” of the Spirit — and how does one live the Spirit-filled life?

This is the practical-pneumatology question that sits downstream of the baptism question (Q-10.4.1). Craig holds that Spirit-baptism is a once-for-all initiatory act at conversion, true of every Christian — “the permanent possession of every believer.” That answer raises the pastoral problem this node addresses: if every Christian already has the Spirit in full, why does Scripture command believers to be filled (Ephesians 5:18), and why do so many Christians visibly lack the Spirit’s fruit?

Craig’s solution is a sharp indwelling-vs-filling distinction: the baptism / regeneration / indwelling is positional, once-for-all and universal among the regenerate; the filling is relational, repeatable and conditional — possessed in greater or lesser measure, grieved and quenched, lost and regained. The schema he teaches is explicitly the Bill Bright / Campus Crusade “Spirit-Filled Life” material — a twentieth-century discipleship tradition, not a neutral exegetical given.

The convergent case — evidence streams, not a syllogism

Because the question is practical-theological rather than a contested proof, Craig builds a cumulative case from converging streams, not a deductive chain. The streams are mutually supporting: the command to be filled presupposes the universal indwelling; the carnal-Christian category explains why the command is needed; fruit-not-gifts and the grieve / quench mechanics follow as the diagnosis and the cure.

Stream A · The positional baptism

  • By one Spirit all believers baptised into one body (1 Cor 12:13)
  • The Spirit is “the permanent possession of every believer”
  • Establishes the floor: every Christian already has the Spirit
  • So “filling” cannot be a second reception of the Spirit

Stream B · The command to be filled

  • “Be filled with the Spirit” (Eph 5:18) — present-tense, repeatable
  • Addressed to the already indwelt
  • Establishes the gap: a further, conditional work
  • Distinct from the once-for-all baptism

Stream C · The three-man taxonomy

  • Natural man — unregenerate, no Spirit, self on the throne
  • Spiritual man — Christ on the throne, Spirit in control, bears fruit
  • Carnal man — regenerate yet self re-enthroned, Spirit grieved, fruitless
  • Bill Bright / Campus Crusade diagram — locates the problem

Stream D · Fruit, not gifts

  • Evidence of filling = the fruit of the Spirit (Gal 5:22–23)
  • Love, joy, peace, patience, kindness… — Christlike character
  • Not the gifts (tongues, prophecy, healing)
  • Dissolves the Pentecostal “tongues = baptism” criterion

Stream E · The mechanics

  • Filled by surrender: confess sin, yield the throne, walk by faith
  • Same posture as conversion — repent and believe
  • Grieving the Spirit (Eph 4:30) — sin
  • Quenching the Spirit (1 Thess 5:19) — resisting his prompting

The single conclusion

  • Indwelling and filling are two distinct works of the one Spirit
  • Indwelling: binary, permanent, universal among the regenerate
  • Filling: a matter of degree, repeatable, conditional, commanded
  • The Christian life is giving the Spirit more of you, not getting more of the Spirit

How the one expert deploys it

William Lane Craig 4 transcripts · sole voice
The indwelling / filling architect — Defenders class
Signature deployment
  • Sets the floor first. Spirit-baptism is initiatory, universal, once-for-all: it is “the initiating act by means of which we are placed into the body Body of Christ.” The Pentecostal “second work of grace” reading is “completely wrong.”
  • The pivot. A class member, Steve, presses that the tidy “baptism = conversion” account is “too simple” — there is a difference between being a Christian and walking / being filled. Craig reframes it as a “very nice Segway” and moves from the positional baptism to the relational filling.
  • The core distinction. Once indwelt, a believer may be more or less filled — more or less under the Spirit’s actual control. Indwelling is binary and permanent; filling is a matter of degree, repeatable, conditional, commanded.
  • The Bill Bright three-man taxonomy. Natural man (no Spirit) → spiritual man (Christ on the throne) → carnal man (regenerate but self re-enthroned, Spirit grieved). The carnal man is the load-bearing category: genuinely saved yet visibly fruitless.
  • Fruit, not gifts. The diagnostic of filling is the fruit of the Spirit (Gal 5), not the gifts (tongues). Gifts are variously distributed and do not measure spirituality; fruit is universally commanded and does.
  • The mechanics. Entered and sustained by repentance and faith — confess sin, re-enthrone Christ, walk by faith in the Spirit’s control. Forfeited by grieving (sin, Eph 4:30) or quenching (resistance, 1 Thess 5:19); restored by confession.
  • Defenders register: references announced twice for note-takers; full treatments deferred to the syllabus; lay contributions (Tai, Steve) absorbed collaboratively; a pastoral benediction as the payoff.
The structural move

The indwelling / filling distinction is Craig’s device for granting the Pentecostal experience a legitimate home while denying the Pentecostal doctrine. The “fuller and deeper experience” the charismatic seeks is real — it is just misnamed. It is the repeatable filling open to every believer, not a second baptism reserved for an elite.

Concession

Pressed by Steve on the Samaritans (Acts 8), Craig grants it is “this peculiar case” where they “hadn’t received him yet until they came down and laid hands on them” — the very temporal gap Pentecostals press — then sets it aside “in the interest of time.” His strongest opponent-datum is granted, not answered.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

There is no opposing expert in the room. The “objections” are the rival Pentecostal doctrine Craig refutes, the live pushback from the class member Steve, and the counter-data Craig himself raises and then defers.

Objection 1

Baptism in the Spirit is a second work of grace after conversion

The indwelt convert still needs a later baptism — often evidenced by tongues — to enter the deeper, fuller Spirit-filled walk.

Pentecostal / charismatic theology

Strongest response

The deeper experience is real but misnamed

There is no second baptism; there is the repeatable filling. Spirit-baptism is the initiating act (1 Cor 12:13); the “fuller and deeper experience” the charismatic seeks is the filling, open to every believer at any time.

Craig the indwelling / filling distinction is the rebuttal

Objection 2

Tongues (and the gifts) are the evidence of Spirit-filling

Speaking in tongues is the sign that one has received the baptism / filling of the Spirit; a believer without the gifts has not entered the deeper life.

Pentecostal evidentialism

Strongest response

Fruit, not gifts, is the measure

The evidence of filling is the fruit of the Spirit (Gal 5:22–23), not the gifts. Gifts are sovereignly and variously distributed and do not measure spirituality; fruit — Christlike character — is universally commanded and does.

Craig Galatians 5 fruit criterion

Objection 3

“There’s more to it than that — we can’t be too simple”

There is a difference between being a Christian and walking / being filled. Perhaps sanctification is “more of a baptism.” The clean “baptism = conversion” thesis is too tidy.

Steve — live class, Part 5

Strongest response

Conceded — and answered with the second tier

Craig grants the point as a “very nice Segway” and supplies exactly the missing refinement: the indwelling / filling distinction. He does not retract the core thesis but accepts it needs this second tier.

Craig conceding to Steve

Objection 4

A “carnal Christian” can be saved yet fruitless — doesn’t that licence sin?

If a genuinely regenerate, indwelt person can live indistinguishably from the unregenerate, the category seems to empty assurance and excuse a fleshly life.

Implicit pastoral worry

Response — with a live tension

The carnal Christian is why the command exists

Yes — the carnal Christian is real (self re-enthroned, Spirit grieved), which is precisely why “be filled” is commanded. The remedy is confession and re-surrender, not a second baptism. But the relation to perseverance is left unresolved (see Gap 1).

Craig Bill Bright taxonomy

Objection 5

The Samaritans (Acts 8) received the Spirit only after conversion

They believed and were water-baptised, yet the Spirit came only later when the Jerusalem apostles laid hands on them — a temporal gap between conversion-faith and Spirit-reception, the classic Pentecostal proof-text.

Steve raises it; the standard charismatic appeal

Gap — half-conceded, not answered

Granted as “peculiar,” then deferred

Craig calls it “this peculiar case with the Samaritans” who “needed… the ratification of the Jerusalem Apostles” and “hadn’t received him yet until they came down and laid hands on them” — then sets it aside “in the interest of time.” The single most obvious unanswered datum.

An Old-Covenant Spirit-baptism typology that complicates a clean New-Covenant “conversion = baptism” thesis. If Israel was baptised at the Exodus, the category is not uniquely Christian.
