The modern objection

Hasn't science disproved God?

For a lot of people this is the one that settles it: we have science now, so we have outgrown God. But look closely and the two are not even answering the same question, and some of what science has actually found points the other way.

First / What is science?
Step 1 of 4
What it can and cannot reach

Science answers how, not whether there is a mind.

Start with what science even is, because the objection quietly assumes it can answer every question. It is a spectacular tool, but it has a lane.

The method
The scientific method is, roughly, this: observe something, guess at an explanation (a hypothesis), use that guess to predict what you should see next, then test it with experiments anyone else can repeat. Its real power comes from sticking to what is measurable, physical and repeatable.
Its range
That same strength is also its boundary. Questions like why is there a universe at all, is there a mind behind it, or what makes a thing right or wrong, are not measurable or repeatable, so science is simply silent on them. As Lennox puts it, knowing how a car engine works tells you nothing about whether Henry Ford, the man behind it, ever existed. The mechanism and the mind are two different kinds of explanation, not rivals. Saying the engine runs on combustion does not abolish the engineer.
Scientism
The claim really hiding inside has science disproved God is usually scientism, the belief that science is the only road to truth. But that belief quietly defeats itself. The statement only what science can prove is real cannot itself be proven by science, so by its own rule it does not count as real. And you already trust plenty of things this way. You cannot run an experiment to prove a friend loves you, yet believing it is hardly irrational.
The theory you'll hear
If you cannot test it in a lab, it is not knowledge, it is just faith. But almost nothing you actually live by passes that test. History, mathematics, whether a person can be trusted, none of it is settled by experiment, and you would not call them all blind guesses. Moreland adds that science cannot even get started without things it cannot prove by experiment: that there is an orderly, knowable world out there, that the laws of logic hold, that truth exists. Pretending science answers every question it was never built for, as Lennox says, is not science, it is philosophy wearing a lab coat.
✓
Confidence: science explains the mechanism of the world brilliantly. Whether there is a mind behind the mechanism is a real question, just not a scientific one. So it could not have disproved God; that was never in its lane.
Second / Where from?
Step 2 of 4
A cause, and the nothing trick

Whatever begins to exist has a cause.

Here is where science actually helps the believer. The evidence says the universe had a beginning, and beginnings are exactly the kind of thing that need a cause.

The rule
Two plain steps. First, whatever begins to exist has a cause; things do not just pop into being out of nowhere. Second, the universe began to exist; on the standard Big Bang picture it is not eternal, it had a starting point. Put those together and the universe has a cause, something beyond it that brought it into being.
From nothing
The popular escape is that the universe came from nothing, so no cause is needed. But watch that word. When physicists say nothing here, they usually mean a quantum vacuum, fields, the law of gravity, something with properties and potential. That is not nothing, it is a very busy something. Real nothing has no laws, no energy, no power to do anything at all. Lennox is blunt: to say nothing produced the universe and then quietly hand that nothing the power to create is just wordplay. Nonsense does not stop being nonsense because a brilliant physicist says it.
Who made God
The obvious comeback is, well then, what caused God? But notice the rule was not everything has a cause, it was everything that begins to exist has a cause. The whole point is that the chain has to stop at something that never began, something uncaused and eternal. A first cause beyond time, space and matter is not a cheat, it is exactly what the argument was driving at, and it is a fair description of God.
The theory you'll hear
Maybe the universe is simply eternal, so it never began and needs no cause. It is a fair move, and for a long time most scientists believed it. But the science itself cut the other way. The evidence for a beginning, an expanding universe that traces back to a single starting point, is the very reason the eternal-universe idea was abandoned. An eternal universe would dodge the need for a cause. The trouble is that is not the universe we appear to live in.
✓
Confidence: the universe looks like it began, and beginnings need causes. The cleanest explanation is a cause beyond the physical that did not itself begin. Far from burying God, the cosmology fits the idea.
Third / Why fine-tuned?
Step 3 of 4
The dials look deliberately set

The universe is balanced on a knife edge for life.

It is not just that the universe exists, it is that its settings are absurdly precise. Move them a hair and there is no life, often no universe at all. This is what people mean by the fine-tuning of the universe.

The dials
Physics is full of fixed numbers: the strength of gravity, the energy of empty space, and many more. Some are tuned with eerie precision. One of them, the cosmological constant, is balanced to roughly one part in ten with a hundred and twenty zeros after it, a figure too large to picture. Nudge several of these even slightly and you get no stars, no chemistry, no us.
The code
And it goes deeper than physics. Inside a single one of your cells sits more information than thirty volumes of an encyclopaedia, written in the four-letter chemical code of DNA. Knechtle puts it well: walking on a beach, you put ripples in the sand down to the waves, but the words John loves Mary you do not, because dense, meaningful information points to a mind, not to chance. DNA is exactly that, only far denser.
Not luck
The usual reply is that we should not be surprised, since if things were not fine-tuned we would not be here to notice. But picture facing a firing squad of fifty trained marksmen, and every one misses. Saying well, if they had not missed I would not be here to wonder is no answer at all. You would rightly suspect the whole thing was rigged. Wildly improbable life-permitting settings call for an explanation, they do not get to wave one away.
The theory you'll hear
Maybe there are countless other universes, and we just happen to live in the one that works (the multiverse). Maybe. But notice the cost. To avoid one designer you have to posit an infinity of unseen, untestable universes, which is a colossal act of faith with no direct evidence, and arguably a bigger leap than a single mind. Even Stephen Hawking admitted the universe looks designed. The multiverse is one attempt to explain that appearance away, not a discovery that removes it.
✓
Confidence: the universe and the life in it sit on settings of staggering precision. A mind behind it is at the very least a serious, rational explanation, not a fairy tale that science has ruled out.
Fourth / Faith vs reason
Step 4 of 4
The war that never really was

Faith and science were never the real enemies.

Underneath the whole objection sits a story we have all absorbed: religion and science at war, faith forced to retreat as science advances. The history does not actually support that story.

The myth
The picture of a long war between science and religion is largely a Victorian invention, pushed by a couple of popular books and repeated ever since. The deeper clash is not science against faith, it is one worldview against another, theism against atheism. Science itself sits on neither side, because it is a method, not a belief about whether God exists.
The founders
Modern science was very largely built by believers: Newton, Kepler, Galileo, Faraday, Maxwell. Lennox makes the sharp point that they did science because of their faith, not in spite of it. They expected the universe to be orderly and intelligible precisely because they believed a rational God had made it that way. The confidence that nature runs on discoverable laws grew in that soil.
But Galileo?
Surely Galileo proves the church crushed science? Not really. Mark Kalthoff notes that every major figure in the affair was a committed Christian, so it was never secular science against a backward church; it was a family quarrel between Christians over how to read passages like (Joshua 10:12) and (Psalm 93:1), which then read as a stationary earth. The Catholic Church of the day funded more astronomy than any other institution alive, and even at his 1633 trial it never made anti-Copernicanism an article of faith. Galileo, a layman, also lacked the conclusive proof of the earth's motion that nobody yet had.
Real faith
And faith is not the opposite of evidence. Blind belief is not what the word means in Christianity. Faith is trust grounded in reason, the same evidence-plus-trust you already place in a friend or a surgeon. Plenty of working scientists today are also Christians and feel no strain, because running the experiment and thanking the one who made an experiment-friendly universe are not in competition.
The theory you'll hear
Sure, but real scientists today are atheists; belief is for people who do not understand the science. It simply is not true. There are eminent scientists in every field who are religious believers, including some at the very top of their disciplines. The disagreement between them and their atheist colleagues is not about the data, it is about how to interpret it, and that is a philosophical question, not a scientific one. Intelligence is not the dividing line. Worldview is.
✓
Confidence: the war between science and faith is more slogan than history. Science largely grew out of belief, many of its best practitioners still hold it, and faith at root means trust grounded in reason, not the absence of it.
Putting it together
The bottom line
Four steps, taken together

So, has science disproved God?

1 · What science is
It explains how the mechanism works, not whether a mind is behind it. Disproving God was never in its lane.

2 · Where it came from

Put it together and the honest answer is no, science has not buried God, and in places it has done the opposite. The deeper you look at the beginning of the universe and the fine-tuning of its laws, the more there is to explain, not less. Science can tell you the universe is staggering. It cannot tell you there is no one to thank for it.
