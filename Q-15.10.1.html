The question

Is Christianity a European religion imposed on Black people – a tool of colonisation and slavery – rather than a faith that belongs to Africans and African Americans on their own terms?

This is the central objection of the §15.10 cluster and the live wedge of the Hebrew Israelite, Afrocentric, and “conscious community” movements. It is not primarily a philosophy-of-religion question; it is a question of history, demography, and ownership. The charge bundles four sub-claims: that Christianity originated in Europe, that it reached Africa only by colonial imposition, that white slave-masters used the Bible to enslave Black people, and that a Black Christian therefore worships the oppressor’s god.

What makes this Q-node distinctive is its inside-out posture. The strongest moves are not detached historical lectures but appeals from within the demographic the objection claims – in Covin’s phrase, “Africa where he looks like me and you.”

The convergent argument — four independent evidence streams

This is not a deductive syllogism but an abductive cumulative case. No single expert runs all four streams; each leans on one or two, and the streams reinforce rather than depend on one another. The objection has to be wrong on history and on lived Black faith and on Christianity’s social effect and on its own internal coherence – simultaneously.

Stream A · History & demography

  • Christianity began in Palestine, not Europe
  • Acts 2 & Acts 8 – multi-ethnic from the founding
  • Ethiopian (4th c.) & Nubian (7th c.) churches predate European missions
  • Global-South majority today
  • African-American women lead US Christianity

Stream B · Slave Christianity

  • The enslaved met Christ directly in their suffering
  • Sought scripture “in context” by night
  • Refused the slave-master’s version
  • Loved a “blatant enemy” while oppressed
  • The Holy Spirit “already made a home”

Stream C · Agency expansion

  • Celsus mocked it as the religion of “slaves, women and children”
  • Galatians 3:28 dissolves in-group / out-group
  • Rodney Stark – women’s agency tracks Christianity empirically
  • Holland’s Dominion, Dixon’s Bullies and Saints
  • Worth “by nature of being just human”

Stream D · Internal critique

  • Subtract white slavery → the Hebrew Israelite religion is gone
  • “Don’t let the master’s misuse define the Book”
  • Let the Bible speak for itself
  • OT “slaves” ≠ 1600s chattel slavery
  • When the Bible shows chattel slavery (Pharaoh), God destroys it

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

Africans only believe because of colonialism / recent Western missionaries

Christianity is growing in Africa only because Westerners came to evangelise, bringing a gospel Africa never originally had.

Recounted hecklers; skeptic in Covin interview

Strongest response

African Christianity is independently ancient

Apostolic-era North Africa was already Christian; the Ethiopian (4th c.) and Nubian (7th c.) churches predate any European mission – the Nubians strong enough to force a treaty with invading Arab Muslims. Paul and the apostles “traversed into northern Africa.”

Perry / Covin historical streamKnechtle Nigerian audience member, Acts 8 eunuch

Objection 2

Christianity is Western in origin

The faith is a European cultural product, so a Black person following it is adopting white culture.

Generic objection

Strongest response

Literacy parity — and Jesus was a Jew

Knechtle: “literacy is Western” is equally pompous and inaccurate – literacy began outside the West and its majority is non-Western, “and the same thing is true with Jesus Christ.” Christianity began in Palestine, not Europe. The closer undoes the whole frame: “Jesus was not a white man, he was a Jew.”

Knechtle parity analogy + demographic data

Objection 3

White slave-masters used the Bible to enslave Black people — so it’s the master’s book

From 1619, the white man took the sacred scriptures and perverted them to justify chattel slavery. The book is therefore the oppressor’s, not ours.

Street objector; Hebrew Israelite framing

Strongest response

Don’t let the master’s misuse define the Book

Perry grants the perversion but denies the ownership: “you cannot allow what man did with the Bible to define the Bible… you have to let the Bible speak for itself.” Letting a 17th–19th-century perversion have final say is itself a second slavery. Slaveholder Christianity is not slave Christianity – the enslaved met Christ directly and refused the master’s version.

Perry methodology + slave-ancestor registers

Objection 4

Specific facts about Black people were removed from the Bible

The white man took facts about life and things about Black people out of the Bible and brainwashed us.

Street objector (2019)

Strongest response

Grant the manipulation, deny the excision

Perry splits the claim: he grants that man used the Bible to manipulate, but denies that any text about Black people was actually excised, and asks for a citation: “if you can pinpoint me anything… I would love to see that.”

Perry hard/soft claim split

Objection 5

The Bible has been revamped so many times you can’t know what’s perfect

Modern publishers even print contradictory variant Bibles; the text is unstable, so you cannot trust any reading.

Street objector (2019)

Strongest response

Manuscript testability

Perry: you test the scrolls against each other, the same way we historically verify that Lincoln existed – “there is not a more consistent historical book in the whole world.” (Loose: cites “500 people saw him” without naming 1 Corinthians 15:6.)

Perry testability response

Objection 6

Christianity is socially regressive / the content is just abuse

The faith subjugates; its very content props up oppression of the marginalised.

Implicit; ancient critics

Strongest response

Agency expansion — hostile witnesses confirm it

Huff: the earliest critic, Celsus, mocked Christianity precisely for empowering “slaves, women and children.” Rodney Stark shows women’s agency rising empirically wherever Christianity spread. Galatians 3:28 is the textual core. “If there’s one worldview that… says everybody has worth by nature of being just human, it’s the Christian worldview.”

Huff Celsus + Stark + Holland + Dixon

Objection 7

The Hebrew Israelite alternative is the true Black faith

Christianity is the white man’s religion; the real faith of Black people is the Hebrew Israelite identity.

Hebrew Israelite movement

Strongest response

Subtract-the-founder reductio

Perry: “if you take what the white man has done to the black man out of the history of humanity you don’t even have a religion.” The alternative is parasitic on white slavery – founded “on man, not what God has said about himself.”

Perry founder-indispensability inversion

Objection 8

Christianity can’t actually resolve racial harm

Faith makes no real difference; non-Christians forgive too, so the cross is not necessary for racial healing. Correlation is not causation.

Majors / Richardson (post-Floyd debate)

Partial — correlation, not causation

Christ-grounded forgiveness, but the necessity is not shown

Cliffe offers Botham Jean and the Charleston AME families forgiving “based off of the cross of Christ,” anchored in Galatians 3:28 and Genesis 1:26. But pressed that non-Christians forgive too, he pivots to “what is your alternative” rather than demonstrating the cross is necessary. The argument stays illustrative.

Conceded as illustrative

Gap A

The Curse of Ham (Genesis 9) proof-text

The single most important text in the actual history of biblical pro-slavery argument – the canonical slave-master proof-text.

Historic pro-slavery apologetics

Gap — never engaged

Absent from the entire corpus

No expert touches the Curse of Ham. This is the most serious gap: the corpus distinguishes slaveholder from slave Christianity in principle but never disarms the specific verse that did the historical work.

Aggregation gap

Gap B

The theological version of the objection

The content of the gospel – sin, atonement, substitution, judgement – encodes European conceptual categories, not just European messengers.

Sophisticated Afrocentric critique

Gap — unaddressed

All responses are historical or experiential

The corpus answers “where did it come from and who follows it” but never “is its conceptual furniture white.” The African Church Fathers (Augustine, Tertullian, Origen, Athanasius, Cyprian) who would anchor the “Africa shaped the theology” reply are conspicuously absent by name; Frederick Douglass’s named slaveholder-vs-slave distinction is never cited.
