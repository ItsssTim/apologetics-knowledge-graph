Union with Christ

The question

How does the application of redemption work — what holds election, justification, sanctification and glorification together in the believer’s life?

This is a question of soteriological architecture, not a debate-floor apologetic. It asks where the saving benefits are located and by what mechanism a justified sinner grows. Craig’s answer is the historic Pauline and Reformed one: union with Christ — being “in Christ” — is the framework within which every other benefit is received and within which sanctification occurs.

The stakes are partly internal-systematic (it fixes the ordo salutis) and partly polemical at two edges: against pantheistic absorption (union is identification, not the dissolving of the self) and against a union-grounded atonement (Turretin’s), which Craig charges with vicious circularity.

The argument structure — evidence streams, not a syllogism

This is not a deductive proof but a cumulative biblical-theological case. Craig builds the doctrine by cataloguing what Scripture says the believer has “in Christ,” then reads sanctification as one item inside that catalogue. The Knechtle stream runs parallel and pastoral. Three streams converge; the one genuinely argumentative move is negative — that justification must be explanatorily prior to union.

Stream A · The “in Christ” catalogue

  • “In Christ / in Christ Jesus” — 164 times in Paul
  • Chosen, called, foreordained (Eph 1:4, 1:11)
  • Created to good works, sealed (Eph 2:10, 1:13)
  • Justified, sanctified (1 Cor 1:2, 1:30)
  • Co-crucified and risen with Christ (Rom 6)

Stream B · The union metaphors

  • Vine and branches — abiding (John 15:1–8)
  • One-flesh marriage (Eph 5)
  • Body of Christ
  • Two-yet-one identification
  • Real union without merger or absorption

Stream C · Sanctification in the union

  • Dying to the old sin nature (Rom 6:1–11)
  • “Newness of life,” bearing fruit (John 15)
  • Not autonomous moral effort
  • Real but non-linear progress (Cliffe)
  • “Botanical growth”; full cleansing at glorification

How each expert frames it

William Lane Craig 3 transcripts
Architect of the “in Christ” framework
Signature deployment
  • Union is personal identification, not absorption — the vine and branches (John 15): “the person who is abiding in Christ … is a fruitful disciple.” Two-yet-one without merger.
  • “In Christ” is Paul’s master category — used 164 times; the doctrine is built by enumerating what those texts predicate of the believer. “A marvellous bible study.”
  • The eight-fold catalogue — chosen, called, foreordained, created to good works, sealed, justified, sanctified, co-crucified and risen — spanning the whole ordo salutis.
  • Sanctification located in the union (Rom 6:1–11) — growth is participation, not autonomous effort: crucified with Christ, raised to resurrection life.
  • Marriage one-flesh and body-of-Christ metaphors (Eph 5) carry the conceptual load of organic, two-yet-one identification.
Load-bearing argument

Justification is explanatorily prior to union, not grounded in it. Against Turretin — on whose account the cross is effective because the elect are already united to Christ — Craig charges vicious circularity: if union makes the cross savingly applicable, but union is itself received through the cross, the account presupposes what it explains. The forensic verdict must come first. (Cross-ref Q-9.1.4 / Q-8.2.17.)

Density caveat

Only the 2020 Mystical Union lecture treats union as its explicit subject. The two 2016 Holy Spirit lessons (Pts 5 & 6) are tagged here only because they answer the adjacent “how does growth work?” question via the indwelling-vs-filling distinction; their real home is Q-10.2.1.

Cliffe & Stuart Knechtle 2 transcripts
The street-level growth frame
Distinctive moves
  • Sanctification is real but non-linear (Cliffe) — conversion is not instant perfection: “two steps forward one step back … that’s a process.”
  • Glorification completes it — full cleansing deferred to Christ’s return (1 John); heaven holds those who “genuinely want to do it God’s way,” not the coerced. The recursive swim-to-Japan illustration turns the holiness gap into a hopeful promise.
  • Sin as practical doubt of God’s goodness (Cliffe) — a relational rather than legal frame.
  • Botanical growth as evidence (Stuart) — saved by grace, but sincere faith yields the fruit of the Spirit (Gal 5). “The beautiful blend of Paul and James.”
  • Pharisee-fate diagnostic (Stuart) — works without grace do not save: the Pharisees out-tithed modern Christians yet were lost.
Relation to Craig

The Knechtles supply the phenomenology of growth (how uneven it feels, what evidences it); Craig supplies the mechanism (it occurs in union with Christ). Complementary, not competing — though neither side engages the other, and the two sit on opposite sides of the latent Lordship-vs-free-grace dispute.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

Union with Christ means absorption — the self is dissolved into the divine

If the believer is truly “in Christ,” individuality is obliterated, as in pantheistic or monist mysticism. Union collapses into identity.

Pantheist, Hindu / monist mysticism

Strongest response

Identification, not absorption

WLC: union is personal identification, not the obliteration of individuality. The vine-and-branches and one-flesh-marriage analogies are precisely “two-yet-one” — distinct persons in real union without merger. (Extracted separately as the Q-6.4.x contrast.)

WLC vine / marriage analogies

Objection 2

The atonement works because the elect are already united to Christ

Turretin: Christ’s death is reckoned as the believer’s because union with Christ is logically presupposed — the cross is effective in virtue of that prior union.

Francis Turretin (Reformed scholastic)

Strongest response

Viciously circular — justification comes first

WLC: if union grounds the saving applicability of the cross, but union is itself a benefit received through the cross, the account presupposes what it sets out to explain. The forensic, declarative verdict of justification must be explanatorily prior to union and ground it, not the reverse.

WLC internal critique (Q-9.1.4 / Q-8.2.17)

Objection 3

Once you’re saved, are you automatically cured of all sinful thought?

If conversion really unites you to Christ, surely lust, greed and the rest should simply vanish. Why does the Christian still struggle?

Lay questioner (Knechtle Q&A)

Strongest response

Real but partial — finished at glorification

Cliffe: no. Sanctification on earth is real but partial and non-linear (“two steps forward, one step back”); the “readiness-to-sin factor” (Dallas Willard) persists until full glorification at Christ’s return.

Cliffe Knechtle non-linear sanctification

Objection 4

“Cheap grace” — just sin, ask forgiveness, go to heaven

Christianity is too easy: do something bad, ask forgiveness, and you’re in. Grace makes holiness optional (the “Kiran nightly syllogism”).

Skeptic caricature

Strongest response

Paul and James blended — fruit is diagnostic

Stuart: a caricature of Paul. Paul and James stress not grieving the Spirit, sanctification and growth. Genuine faith yields “botanical growth”; its total absence is diagnostic of no faith — the Pharisees out-tithed most Christians yet were lost.

Stuart Knechtle botanical-growth diagnostic

Objection 5

A “saved but fruitless” believer licenses complacency

If a “carnal Christian” can be genuinely saved yet bear no fruit, the doctrine licenses cheap grace — and there is no way to tell the carnal Christian from someone who was never saved at all.

Lordship-salvation advocates (latent)

Partial — no criterion offered

Saved though works burn — but the line is unknowable

WLC (Holy Spirit Pt 6) reads 1 Cor 3:15 to mean the carnal Christian is genuinely saved though his works “burn.” But he declines to give a test distinguishing a carnal Christian from an apostate: “I don’t think we know when a person has crossed that line.” The practical question is left without a criterion.

Persistent gaps in the corpus

Gap 1

The core exegetical premise is never defended

Craig assumes all 164 Pauline “in Christ” occurrences denote a single mystical union. But the phrase is widely read as covenantal, instrumental (“by means of Christ”), or locative-corporate in many of them.

Exegetical / Pauline studies

Gap — unaddressed

The “164 times” count substitutes for the argument

The frequency tally does the rhetorical work of an argument without the exegesis an argument would require. No defence is offered that the language is mystical rather than covenantal or instrumental. This is the central gap.

Aggregation gap

Gap 2

The circularity critique assumes its own theory of justification

Craig’s charge against Turretin only bites if justification is forensic and declarative. On a participationist reading, union is constitutive of justification, not downstream of it.

Catholic, Orthodox theosis, New Perspective

Gap — unaddressed

Sound only relative to prior commitments

On Catholic, Eastern-Orthodox or New-Perspective (Wright, Hays) accounts, the alleged circle simply is the doctrine. Craig does not engage these accounts, so the critique holds only given his forensic-justification premises.

Aggregation gap

Gap 3

How union sanctifies — definitive vs progressive — is never adjudicated

Does union effect sanctification definitively (a once-for-all break with sin) or progressively? The Reformed-vs-Wesleyan dispute is never named.

Reformed vs Wesleyan

Gap — unaddressed

Romans 6 quoted, not located

Craig quotes Rom 6 for sanctification-in-union but does not say how the union effects it, leaving his position unplaced within the standard debate.

Aggregation gap

Gap 4

John 15:6 eternal-security tension left open

The branches “cast forth … and burned” (John 15:6) sit directly against the perseverance / conditional-security debate. Can a branch in the vine be lost?

Perseverance vs conditional security

Gap — deferred

The vine quoted for union, the burned clause skipped

Craig uses the vine passage for union but does not adjudicate the burned-branch clause, leaving the §9.4 connection open — the same seam the Holy Spirit Pt 6 Q&A explicitly defers.

Aggregation gap

Gap 5

Lordship vs free grace is latent but unflagged on both sides

Craig’s “carnal Christian, truly saved yet fruitless” framework is exactly what Lordship-salvation rejects, while Stuart’s “fruit-as-evidence” leans the other way.

Lordship vs free grace

Gap — unaddressed

Two experts, opposite sides, no contact

Neither names the controversy. The two experts in this node sit on opposite sides of it without ever engaging each other — an unmarked fault line running through the corpus.
