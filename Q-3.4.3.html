Jesus’ use of “Son of Man”

The one Christological title that even sceptical scholarship grants is Jesus’s own authentic self-designation — and the dispute over whether it means the “mortal” of Ezekiel or the worshipped cloud-rider of Daniel 7.

The question

When Jesus called himself “the Son of Man,” was he using a humble human title, or claiming the divine identity of the exalted figure of Daniel 7?

This is the lexical and historical sub-question beneath Q-3.4.1. Because even sceptical scholarship grants that “Son of Man” is Jesus’s own self-designation — a remembered speech-habit rather than a later church confession — it carries unusual evidential weight. The whole dispute turns on which son of man Jesus meant: the generic Ezekielian mortal (Hebrew ben adam), or the worshipped cloud-rider of Daniel 7:13–14.

This node lives almost entirely inside Christian–Muslim debate, so it is contested by a live adversarial voice (Shabir Ally) — giving the corpus an unusually clean objection-and-response structure rather than the strawman-against-self pattern of most apologetic material.

The argument — a multi-stream cumulative case

The four treatments converge on one case, built abductively from converging streams rather than as a deductive syllogism. Each stream is independently attested; their joint force is what makes the title hard to deflate.

Stream 1 · Authenticity

  • Q + M + L + Mark + John
  • 69× in synoptics, 13× in John
  • Criterion of dissimilarity (Dunn)
  • No later NT writer applies it to Jesus
  • A remembered speech-habit, not a confession

Stream 2 · Daniel 7

  • Receives latreuo (LXX) / pelach (Aramaic)
  • Worship reserved for Yahweh alone
  • Rides the clouds — a Yahweh prerogative
  • Ps 104:3, Deut 33:26, Isa 19:1
  • The divine variant, not the Ezekielian one

Stream 3 · The trial

  • Mark 14:62 fuses Daniel 7 + Psalm 110
  • “Seated at the right hand of the Power”
  • Gundry’s 5–6 marks of authenticity
  • “The Power” per Mishnah Sanhedrin 7:5
  • Too subtle for Christian fabrication
Step 1

“Son of Man” is Jesus’s own remembered self-designation, not a church retrojection.

Support. Multiple attestation across Q, M, L, Mark, and John (69× in the synoptics + 13× in John); the criterion of dissimilarity — no other NT writer ever applies the title to Jesus (James Dunn).

Step 2

The title points specifically to the Daniel 7:13–14 figure, not the Ezekielian “mortal.”

Support. Jesus pairs it with the cloud-coming image (Mark 14:62), the Daniel 7 variant; the Daniel figure receives latreuo / pelach — service due only to Yahweh — and rides clouds, a strictly Yahwistic activity (Ps 104:3, Deut 33:26, Isa 19:1).

Step 3

At the Sanhedrin trial Jesus fuses Daniel 7 with Psalm 110 enthronement, claiming Yahweh’s throne.

Support. Mark 14:62; Gundry’s five/six marks of authenticity; the “the Power” substitution and clothes-rending match Mishnah Sanhedrin 7:5 too subtly to be a Christian fabrication.

Conclusion

Jesus authentically claimed deity through a divine Son-of-Man self-identification.

Reception. The high priest’s blasphemy verdict is internal-Jewish, hostile-witness attestation that the claim was received as a claim to divinity.

How each expert deploys it

Jonathan McLatchie 4 transcripts
The dominant voice — authenticity before meaning
Signature moves
  • Authenticity first (Dunn). 69× in synoptics, 13× in John; criterion of dissimilarity — pointedly citing Dunn as “one of [the opponent’s] favorite Scholars.”
  • Daniel 7 worship-language. The figure receives latreuo (LXX) / pelach (Aramaic), “only ever ascribed to Yahweh,” and rides the clouds (Ps 104:3, Deut 33:26, Isa 19:1).
  • Trial scene (Gundry’s five marks). Mark 14:62 fuses Daniel 7 + Psalm 110; “the Power” substitution matches Mishnah Sanhedrin 7:5.
  • Malachi 3:1 / Matthew 11 supplement. John prepares the way for ha-Adon — a title “never used of anyone other than Yahweh.”
  • Defensive pivot. Not adoni (Ps 110:1, possessive) but Adonai (Ps 110:5), the divine name proper; backup via Ps 16:2 / 35:23.
Signature method

Establish the saying’s authenticity before arguing its meaning — sourcing the strongest material to Q and the Markan trial narrative so the standard Muslim retreat to “John is unreliable” never gets traction.

Shabir Ally adversary · within McLatchie src 3
The opposing case — lesser power, two-powers heresy
Counter-arguments
  • Subordination. The Son of Man “great as he is is not as great as the Ancient of Days” — an exalted but lesser power.
  • Two-powers heresy. Via Alan Segal’s Two Powers in Heaven: a “second power in heaven” was a doctrine the rabbis “railed against, calling it a heresy.”
  • Chilton’s three categories. The apocalyptic-future son-of-man sayings refer to “a figure to come” who “is not Jesus.”
  • The humility reading. Ezekiel 80+×; Job’s “son of man is a worm”; “do not trust in the son of man.”
Why it matters

This is the corpus’s only sustained counter-case, and it is built on its own hostile-witness scholarship (Segal, Chilton). McLatchie answers the identification and worship questions but does not refute the lesser-power thesis itself.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

“Son of Man” just means “a human being”

In Hebrew ben adam / Aramaic bar enasha, “son of man” is the generic word for a mortal — Ezekiel is called it 80+ times. So it is a title of humility, not deity.

Vania, Shabir Ally (audience Q&A)

Strongest response

Mark 14 locates the title in Daniel 7

Jesus pairs the title with the cloud-coming image — the Daniel 7 variant, not the Ezekielian mortal. “It’s not a matter of personal interpretation; it’s a matter of just executing the text consistently.”

McLatchie text-consistency move

Objection 2

Daniel 7’s figure is a lesser power, subject to the Ancient of Days

Daniel clearly distinguishes the Son of Man from the Ancient of Days, who is “obviously… the one true God.” Even an exalted Son of Man is subordinate — not God in the strong sense.

Shabir Ally, via Alan Segal, Two Powers in Heaven

Strongest response

The figure receives worship due only to Yahweh

The Daniel figure receives latreuo / pelach — the highest religious service, reserved for Yahweh alone. A creature could not. So this is not the Ezekielian human-prophet figure.

McLatchie worship-language lexical argument

Objection 3

The apocalyptic Son of Man is a future, third figure — not Jesus

Source-critical analysis sorts the sayings into categories; in the apocalyptic-future category Jesus speaks in the third person of a coming figure “that… is not Jesus.”

Shabir Ally, via Bruce Chilton (Bible Review)

Strongest response

The Mark 12 → Mark 14 identification chain

In Mark 12 Jesus identifies the Messiah as seated at Yahweh’s right hand; at Mark 14 he identifies the Son of Man as seated there. Both sides grant Jesus is the Messiah — so Jesus is the Son of Man. Matt 19:28 / Luke 22:30 put him on “his glorious throne.”

McLatchie verse-chain identification

Objection 4

“Sitting at God’s right hand” is honorific closeness, not deity

David sits there in Psalm 110; the disciples are promised it; Moses and Abraham are honoured similarly. Adoni is merely a term of respect. “An awful lot of gods” would follow.

Vania (audience Q&A)

Strongest response

The argument rests on Adonai (Ps 110:5), not adoni (110:1)

Psalm 110:1’s adoni is the possessive form and need not denote Yahweh; but verse 5’s Adonai “only denotes Yahweh.” Backup, if the vowel-pointing is contested: Ps 16:2 / 35:23, where David’s “Lord” is unambiguously God.

McLatchie adoni / Adonai distinction

Objection 5

Mark’s “I am” is a later redaction

Comparing Mark with Matthew and Luke, scholars think Mark’s emphatic “I am” is a later redaction; the historical Jesus gave a non-committal answer at the trial.

Ali (audience Q&A)

Strongest response

The combined charge brings divine pretension to “virtual certainty”

The combined temple-destruction + Son-of-Man accusation brings “to virtual certainty the entailment of a charge that he arrogated to himself Divine roles.” The “the Power” substitution and clothes-rending match Mishnah Sanhedrin 7:5 too subtly to be forged.

Craig via GundryMcLatchie Mishnah subtlety

Objection 6

Christians fabricated the trial scene

The Sanhedrin trial was held in private at night; no Christian was present. The whole blasphemy episode could be a later Christian invention to dramatise the divine claim.

Implied critical-scholar objection

Strongest response

Gundry’s marks of authenticity

The right-hand + clouds combination, the “Power” substitution, and a not-yet-fulfilled Sanhedrin prediction are details Christians were “unlikely to have fabricated or even been able to fabricate” — they correspond too subtly to later Jewish blasphemy procedure.

McLatchie Gundry six criteriaCraig Gundry commentary

Objection 7

Jesus’s audience didn’t grasp a divine claim

The blasphemy verdict could reflect ordinary messianic over-reach, not a perceived claim to be God. Reading divinity into the reaction is anachronistic.

General sceptical

Strongest response

The high priest tore his robes

The robe-tearing and “you have heard the blasphemy” show the original audience “knew exactly what he was saying.” The hostile reaction is itself attestation of the claim’s meaning — a witness with no motive to inflate it.

Persistent gaps in the corpus

Gap 1

Casey / Vermes “bar enasha” idiom

In some Aramaic usage “son of man” is simply an idiom for “a man like me” or “I myself” — not a divine title at all.

Geza Vermes, Maurice Casey

Gap — named but unrebutted

Huff flags it; no expert closes it

Huff openly notes he does not engage the idiom. McLatchie’s “execute the text consistently” asserts the Daniel-7 reference but does not meet the philology head-on. The linguistic counter-argument is absent from the corpus.

Aggregation gap

Gap 2

Segal’s two-powers / subordination thesis

An exalted second power could be historically real yet still be a recognised Jewish heresy and sub-Anselmian — great, but less than the Almighty.

Shabir Ally, via Alan Segal

Gap — deflected, not refuted

Worship answered; lesser-power survives

McLatchie answers the identification (Jesus = the figure) and the worship (the figure receives latreuo), but never engages Segal’s actual claim that an exalted second power was a known heresy. The “lesser power” objection is left standing.

Aggregation gap

Gap 3

Chilton’s source-critical categories

Chilton sorts the son-of-man sayings by method; the apocalyptic-future class genuinely reads as third-person reference to a coming figure.

Bruce Chilton (Bible Review)

Gap — answered with verses, not method

Verse-chain, not category critique

McLatchie answers with Mark 12 + Matt 19:28 but does not engage Chilton’s criteria for sorting the sayings, so a sceptic can re-assert the third-person-future reading of the specific apocalyptic sayings.

Aggregation gap

Gap 4

Margaret Barker “begotten god” reading

A subordinationist reading of Daniel 7 in which the exalted figure is a distinct, lesser divine being — Barker’s Great Angel (Q-3.4.5).

Margaret Barker

Gap — flagged, never engaged

Untouched on this node

McLatchie’s own Source 1 extraction flags this as unaddressed; Ally gestures at Barker elsewhere, but the subordinationist alternative is never engaged on Q-3.4.3.

Aggregation gap

Gap 5

Which Greek text-form of Daniel 7?

The worship-language argument depends on the text-form: latreuo (LXX), pelach (Aramaic), latreia (Theodotion) are not interchangeable, and the LXX of Daniel was little used.

Text-critical scholarship

Gap — asserted, not defended

Text-form step skipped

McLatchie cites the LXX, Aramaic, and Theodotion forms interchangeably without distinguishing them. The strength of the worship argument turns on that step, which is asserted rather than defended.
