The question

How can one person be both fully God and fully man — and what must any account of the incarnation avoid in order not to fall into heresy?

This is the doctrinal setup for the logical-coherence objection to the incarnation. The orthodox two-natures doctrine is famously defined negatively — not by a mechanism explaining how the union works, but by a set of boundary lines marking the ditches on either side of the road. Three early heresies stake out those ditches; the Council of Chalcedon (451) draws the safe channel between them.

The strategic payoff sits downstream. Chalcedon is the constraint every model of the incarnation must satisfy (Q-8.1.4), and the rule William Lane Craig invokes to disqualify rivals — including Lutheran consubstantiation. But on Craig’s reading it is only a boundary: it tells you where the cliffs are without driving the car. That silence on the “how” is exactly where he reopens a condemned option in refined form.

The convergent structure — a narrowing corridor, not a syllogism

Unlike the Kalam (a deductive syllogism), this node is a process of elimination. Craig frames the patristic period as a corridor narrowing from both walls until only a thin channel remains — and Chalcedon then fences that channel rather than paving a road through it. The three heresies are the boundary markers; orthodoxy is whatever survives.

Wall one · Too little humanity

  • Apollinarianism
  • The divine Logos replaces the human mind (noūs)
  • Christ’s humanity is truncated
  • “What is not assumed is not saved”
  • Condemned: Constantinople I, 381

Wall two · Too much separation

  • Nestorianism
  • Two complete natures, each a full subject
  • Collapses into two persons / two sons
  • The theotokos dispute
  • Condemned: Ephesus, 431

Wall three · Natures blended

  • Eutychianism / monophysitism
  • The two natures fuse into one hybrid
  • Neither nature is preserved intact
  • “Without confusion, without change”
  • Condemned: Chalcedon, 451
The bind

Reject too-little-humanity and you slide toward two persons; reject two-persons and you slide back toward truncated humanity.

The corridor narrows from both walls. Apollinarianism is condemned for too little humanity (the Logos replaces the human mind); Nestorianism for too much separation (two complete natures = two persons). Craig states the squeeze plainly: “given the rejection of Apollinarianism, it seems very very difficult… to affirm two natures in Christ without lapsing into nestorianism.”

The channel

Two complete natures, one person — with each nature retaining its essential properties intact.

What survives the elimination. The human nature must be complete (against wall one); the person must be one (against wall two); the natures must remain distinct (against wall three). Chalcedon fences all three at once with four negative adverbs.

Chalcedon (451)

One person in two natures — without confusion, without change, without division, without separation.

A boundary, not a mechanism. Two adverbs guard against blending (walls three), two against splitting (wall two); the completeness of the humanity (wall one) is secured by insisting Christ is “truly man… of a reasonable soul and body.” Both experts are emphatic: the councils tell you what not to say without telling you how the union is achieved — the point at which the coherence objection bites, and at which Craig’s own model enters.

The tell — rehabilitating a condemned view

Craig calls Apollinaris “one of the most creative of the Alexandrian thinkers” and his solution “ingenious,” and pre-loads the soul-body analogy (the Logos plays the role the soul plays) that is the literal core of his own Neo-Apollinarian model (Q-8.1.4). He defends the model’s intrinsic plausibility before presenting its condemnation — signalling that he thinks the condemnation is not decisive against a refined version.

Limits, not mechanism

Craig is candid that Chalcedon sets a boundary without explaining the metaphysical how. By reading the Definition as silent on mechanism, he opens the very door — a refined one-nature model — the conciliar tradition seemed to have shut. The node is best read as the doctrinal on-ramp to his model of the incarnation.

Distinctive moves
  • The three-factions correction — Nicaea (325) had three groups, not two: Orthodox (homoousios, same substance, Alexander & Athanasius), Arian (heteroousios, different substance), and Eusebian (homoiousios, similar substance).

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

A complete humanity plus a complete divinity is just God indwelling a man

If Christ has both a full human nature and a full divine nature, that “simply amounts to God indwelling a human being” — a failed incarnation, not a real union. This is Apollinaris’s motivating worry.

Apollinaris of Laodicea; the Alexandrian school

Strongest response

A hypostatic union, not a juxtaposition

The orthodox reply is not to truncate the humanity but to insist the union is in the person — one hypostasis subsisting in two natures. Craig reports the worry sympathetically (it is the engine of his own model), but the church’s answer is Chalcedon’s “one person.”

Craig reports Apollinaris’s worry; Chalcedon answers

Objection 2

Two complete natures must mean two persons / two sons

If each of Christ’s natures is complete — each with its own full set of rational faculties, mind, and self-consciousness — then there are two subjects in Christ, one human and one divine.

Cyril and the Alexandrians, against Nestorius

Strongest response

An internal critique — completeness just is personhood

The charge sticks because a complete rational nature with its own self-consciousness already constitutes a person; so two complete natures yield two persons. This is the wall Nestorianism hits, condemned at Ephesus (431). It is also why orthodoxy cannot simply “add” a complete human person to the Logos.

Craig Alexandrian reductio of complete-two-natures Christology

Objection 3

“I do not believe in two persons” — Nestorius’s self-defence

Nestorius protested that he affirmed a single Christ and never taught two persons. His stated intention was a real, unified Christ.

Nestorius, patriarch of Constantinople

Strongest response

The heresy follows from his premises, not his intentions

A union that is merely “a functional unity of love and will” — a common prosopon (the Greek-theatre actor’s mask) — is not a real union but play-acting at unity. Given that each nature bears its own hupostasis, the two-persons consequence holds whatever Nestorius says.

Craig internal critique via Theodore’s functional-union prelude

Objection 4

Calling Mary theotokos makes her the source of the deity

“Mary bore only the man Jesus; she did not bear the divine logos.” To call her “Mother of God” implies she originated the divinity — which is absurd.

Nestorius (the theotokos objection)

Strongest response

Theotokos concerns who was born, not the origin of deity

The title predicates of the one person what is true of him in his divine nature: the one she bore is the divine Logos in his humanity. It does not claim Mary originated the divinity. The whole dispute presupposes the Logos’s deity — which Nestorius himself accepted.

Craig reporting the operative Alexandrian position

Objection 5

The two natures fuse into one — so neither is preserved

If divinity and humanity combine into a single nature in the incarnate Christ, the result is a hybrid tertium quid — neither truly God nor truly man.

Eutyches / monophysitism

Strongest response

Chalcedon’s first two adverbs fence exactly this

“Without confusion, without change” preserves each nature with its essential properties intact. The natures are united in one person but never blended into a third thing. This is the wall Chalcedon was actually convened to condemn.

Craig via the Chalcedonian formula

Objection 6

Lutheran consubstantiation communicates divine attributes to the human nature

To ground the real presence in the Lord’s Supper, Lutheran theology attributes invisibility, immateriality, and ubiquity to Christ’s human nature via the communicatio idiomatum.

Lutheran sacramental theology

Strongest response

This “confounds the natures” — a Chalcedonian violation

Communicating divine properties to the human nature is precisely the blending Chalcedon prohibits, so consubstantiation is “unacceptable christologically.” The rule does not touch Catholic transubstantiation, which uses substance-accident metaphysics rather than the communicatio idiomatum.

Craig Chalcedon as live diagnostic tool

Objection 7

The Nicene vote was narrow — orthodoxy barely won

The deity of Christ was decided by a knife-edge council vote, so it is a contingent political outcome rather than a settled apostolic conviction.

Popular “Da Vinci Code”-style narrative

Strongest response

Three factions, not two

Huff’s correction: even the Eusebian “dissenters” (homoiousios) were orthodox on the theology — they worried only that “same substance” sounded modalist. The disagreement with the Orthodox was terminological. The ~312-to-3 vote reflects real consensus against Arius.

Huff three-factions myth-correction

Objection 8

“What is not assumed is not healed” — a Logos-replaced mind is unredeemed

If the divine Logos replaced Christ’s human mind, then the human mind — the very seat of sin — was never assumed, and so was never redeemed. This is the classic patristic refutation of Apollinarianism.

Gregory of Nazianzus (quod non est assumptum)

Gap — the decisive objection is not voiced

Condemnation stated, soteriological argument missing

Craig notes that Apollinarianism was condemned but — consistent with his sympathy for the model — does not actually press Gregory’s argument that an unassumed human mind is an unredeemed one. The single most important reason the church rejected Apollinarianism is named in the framing but not developed in the corpus.

Aggregation gap

Persistent gaps in the corpus

Being honest about the gaps matters here, because this node is unusually thin — two experts for so load-bearing a topic, and effectively Craig alone on the two-natures material proper. A reader should not infer the corpus answers the coherence objection; it sets the boundary the answer must respect.

Gap 1

Eutychianism / monophysitism is named structurally but barely treated.

The “natures blended” wall is the one Chalcedon was actually convened to condemn (against Eutyches), yet neither expert develops it. Craig’s taxonomy treats “monophysite” sympathetically — as the Alexandrian / Apollinarian family he is rehabilitating — and does not dwell on the Eutychian fusion error as a distinct heresy. The third wall is structural, not substantive.

Gap 2

Gregory of Nazianzus’s “what is not assumed is not healed” is missing.

The single most important patristic argument against Apollinarianism — that an unassumed human mind is an unredeemed human mind — is referenced by the framing but voiced by neither expert. Craig states the condemnation (Constantinople 381) but, consistent with his sympathy for the model, does not press the soteriological objection that sank it.

Gap 3

The historical-Nestorius vs “Nestorianism” question is set aside.

Craig presents the Alexandrian reading as operative and does not engage the modern scholarly debate (post the Bazaar of Heracleides) over whether Nestorius the man actually held the position condemned as “Nestorianism.” He treats the position, not necessarily the person.

Gap 4

The positive mechanism is deferred — by design.

Neither expert explains how two complete natures inhere in one person without blending or splitting, because that is the unanswered question Chalcedon brackets and Craig’s own model (Q-8.1.4) attempts. As a treatment of the coherence objection, this node is a setup, not a resolution.

Gap 5

Cyril’s own positive Christology is gestured at, not developed.

The raw extraction flags Cyril’s failed soul-body analogy as a “next extraction” not present here; the constructive Alexandrian alternative to Nestorius is thinner than the critique of Nestorius itself.
