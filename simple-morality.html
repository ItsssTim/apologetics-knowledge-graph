The moral argument

Where does morality come from?

You don't need to believe in God to be a good person, and the answer here never says you do. The real question is deeper and stranger: when we say something is genuinely right or wrong, what on earth is that based on? Follow it and it leads somewhere surprising.

First / Do they exist?
Step 1 of 4
Some things really are wrong

Some things are wrong, whatever anyone thinks.

Start with what almost everyone, believer or atheist, actually believes deep down: that some things are not just frowned upon, but genuinely, objectively wrong.

Really wrong
Torturing a child for fun. The Holocaust. Slavery. We do not think these are merely unfashionable, or wrong only for people who happen to dislike them. We think they are wrong, full stop, and would still be wrong even if every society on earth had approved of them. That conviction is the starting point, and you already hold it.
Not taste
This is different in kind from I prefer tea or that music is dull. Those are about you. But the wrongness of the Holocaust is not a report on your feelings; it is a claim about reality. As the truth card put it, the person who insists it is all just opinion never actually lives that way, and on this, least of all.
Above culture
And we sense that morality sits above culture, not under it. When Martin Luther King wrote from a Birmingham jail that there is a law above the law of Alabama, everyone felt the force of it. The Nuremberg trials only made sense because the Nazis had broken a law higher than their own. If morality were merely culture, there would have been nothing left to judge.
The theory you'll hear
Morality is just what helps a society function, so it's relative to the culture. But you do not believe that, and neither does anyone. If morality were only the rules a society makes, then a society that endorsed genocide would not be wrong, only different, and we know that is false. The very fact that we can judge an entire culture proves there is a standard standing above all of them.
✓
Confidence: objective moral values exist, some things are really wrong regardless of opinion or culture, and almost everyone, the atheist included, knows it. That is the agreed starting point. The question is what it all rests on.
Second / Without God?
Step 2 of 4
Where could they come from?

A godless universe explains behaviour, not obligation.

Grant that real right and wrong exist. The next question is what they could possibly be grounded in if there is no God. The usual answers all run into the same wall.

Evolution
The popular answer is evolution: morality is just instincts that helped our ancestors survive. That may explain why we feel certain things are wrong, but feeling something is wrong is not the same as it being wrong. The deeper problem is that natural selection rewards beliefs that are useful, not beliefs that are true, so it would have fixed the same conscience in us whether or not anything were really wrong. Honest defenders of the view admit as much: some concede that on this account ethics is an illusion fobbed off on us by our genes to get us to cooperate. It tells you what helped us survive; it never tells you what you actually ought to do.
Culture
The next answer is society: morality is whatever the group agrees on. But then the reformer is always wrong by definition. King was breaking his society's rules; the abolitionists were the deviants. If culture is the source, no culture can ever be judged, and the Holocaust was simply Germany's preference at the time. Nobody can actually live with where that leads.
Is and ought
Underneath both lies a gap philosophers have known about for centuries: you cannot get an ought from an is. Science can tell you what burns and what heals, that an act causes suffering or flourishing, but no fact about the world can tell you that you ought to care. The leap from how things are to how they should be needs something science alone cannot supply.
The theory you'll hear
Morality is simply about the well-being of conscious creatures, no God required. It is the best secular attempt, but it quietly smuggles in the very thing it needs to prove. Why ought I care about anyone's well-being beyond my own? Call well-being good and you have assumed a value, not discovered one. Science can describe what promotes flourishing; it cannot tell you that you are obligated to want it.
✓
Confidence: evolution and culture can explain why we have moral feelings and customs, but not why anything is genuinely binding. A purely physical universe gives you what is, never what you ought to do.
Third / Where from?
Step 3 of 4
A law points to a lawgiver

A moral law points to a mind behind it.

If objective morals are real, and matter cannot produce them, then they point beyond the physical to their actual source.

Like a law
Notice what morality is actually like. It does not behave like a fact of physics; it behaves like a law, binding, authoritative, making demands of you whether you like them or not. And a command is the kind of thing that comes from a mind. Knechtle's question is blunt: does anyone really think a wall, or any lump of matter, understands the difference between right and wrong?
A lawgiver
A law above every human law, King's law above Alabama, can only exist if there is a mind above every human mind to ground it. Real, binding moral obligations are best explained not by particles, but by a personal, good God whose own character is the standard. That is where the ought finally comes from: not from stuff, but from someone.
Real value
It also explains why people matter, which no culture took for granted. In the Babylonian creation story humans are a by-product of a war among gods, and under karma the weak are merely reaping what they earned. But if every person is made in the image of God (Genesis 1:27), then human dignity is a fact, and degrading anyone is objectively wrong.
The theory you'll hear
Is something good because God commands it, or does God command it because it's good? Either way, the argument sinks. This old dilemma has a third option that slips between its horns. God does not invent good by arbitrary command, nor consult a standard above himself. Good is simply his own unchanging nature, what he most deeply is. So morality is neither a whim he could reverse, nor a free-floating abstraction binding on no one. It is grounded in the character of a person, which is exactly where real obligation has to come from.
✓
Confidence: real moral obligation behaves like a law, and a law implies a lawgiver. The best explanation of binding, universal morality is a personal God whose character grounds it, and in whose image people have real worth.
Fourth / But atheists?
Step 4 of 4
Yes, and that was never the question

Of course atheists can be good. That was never the question.

This is where the argument is most often misheard, so be clear: it is not a claim that atheists are bad people. It is a claim about where goodness itself comes from.

Of course
Atheists can be deeply good, kind, honest, often more so than many Christians. Nobody is saying you must believe in God to behave well or to tell right from wrong. You can read beautifully without ever having studied the rules of grammar. The point was never about your behaviour, or your sincerity, at all.
Two questions
The trick is to separate two questions that constantly get jumbled together. Can a person be good without believing in God? Easily, yes. But what makes anything good or evil in the first place? That is the real question, and it is not answered by pointing to kind atheists. How you behave and what grounds morality are simply not the same issue.
Borrowed
So the honest picture is this: the moral atheist is living on borrowed capital. They hold tightly to human dignity, real justice, objective wrong, all of which make fine sense if there is a God and very little if there is not. They are, in the best possible way, living better than their own worldview can account for. That quiet gap is itself a kind of pointer.
The theory you'll hear
So you're saying I need religion to be a moral person. Not at all, and it would be insulting to say so. You already know right from wrong; that has never been in question. What is in question is what that knowledge rests on. The claim is not that atheists lack morals, but that morality itself lacks a foundation without God, and that the goodness you live by fits a world he made.
✓
Confidence: the moral argument never says atheists cannot be good. It says goodness itself needs a foundation, and a personal God supplies the very one that the moral life everyone already lives quietly requires.

So where does morality come from? Not from matter, which knows nothing of right and wrong, and not from culture, which we judge by a higher standard all the time. The objective good we all live by behaves exactly like a law, and a law points to a lawgiver. Atheists can be wonderfully moral; what they cannot easily do is explain, on their own terms, why anything is truly right or wrong at all. That pointer has a name.
