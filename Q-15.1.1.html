The question

What does Scripture actually teach about homosexuality — and, just as pressingly, how does a Christian engage a gay person with dignity rather than turning the topic into a battleground?

This Q-node carries two questions at once, and the corpus treats them as inseparable. The intellectual question is exegetical and doctrinal: what do the biblical texts say, how is Christian marriage defined, and is sexual orientation chosen or given? The relational question is the one the experts say actually decides most conversations.

On their own testimony, this is the single most-cited barrier to the gospel for a secular audience. Knechtle’s transcripts flag Greg Ganssle’s “Yale observation” that anti-homosexuality is perceived as the number-one gospel barrier; Preston Perry builds his entire method around the fact that a gay person’s identity has usually been totalised by their sexuality long before the Christian arrives.

The shared conviction across all four experts is a refusal of both poles — neither “you are going to hell for being gay” nor “anything goes.” Every voice clears that ground first, then builds. The pastoral playbook is given equal weight with the exegesis, because that is where the brief and the sources both point.

Two questions, treated as one

The corpus never lets the doctrinal question float free of the relational one. The strongest deployments hold both strands together: clear the ground, locate the issue inside universal sexual brokenness, and engage the person rather than the flashpoint.

The intellectual question

  • What the texts say (Gen 2, Lev 18, Rom 1, 1 Cor 6, Eph 5)
  • How Christian marriage is defined
  • Is orientation chosen or given?
  • The same-sex marriage debate
  • The revisionist / affirming reading (under-engaged)

The relational reality

  • The number-one perceived gospel barrier
  • Disagreement is not the absence of love
  • Engage the person, not the flashpoint
  • Name the church’s own failures honestly
  • Imago Dei as the floor of every encounter

The shared starting point

  • Not “hell for being gay”
  • Not “anything goes”
  • Salvation turns on lordship, not orientation
  • All sexual sin is in view, including the speaker’s own
  • Dignity is granted before disagreement

The sub-questions — four pooled strands

This is a consolidated tree: each sibling sub-question gets its own strand, with the core position and the key move. The relational strands are given full weight alongside the doctrinal ones.

What the Bible saysQ-15.1.1.1
Doctrinal foundation + the pastoral half
Core position
  • Doctrinal: God designed sex for one man and one woman in lifelong covenant (Genesis 2:24); same-sex practice falls outside that design, as does all sexual sin including heterosexual lust.
  • Relational: where the experts spend most of their words. Disagreement is not the absence of love. Knechtle grounds his credibility in gay friends in his wedding party; Huff turns the question outward; the model named is Sam Allberry, the celibate same-sex-attracted minister (“Side B”).
Key move
  • Clear the deck first, then locate the question inside universal sexual brokenness rather than singling out gay people.
Same-sex marriageQ-15.1.1.5
The covenantal-typological argument
Core position
  • Michael Jones: same-sex marriage is “covenantally wrong” within Christianity — not because the affection is wrong, but because Ephesians 5 (rooted in Genesis 2) reads marriage as a sign of Christ’s covenant with the church, which requires the man-and-woman structure to function as the sign.
  • The argument sidesteps “abomination” rhetoric entirely.
Key move
  • Stuart Knechtle’s civil / religious distinction: he holds the scriptural ethic yet says he “would never necessarily vote against” civil same-sex marriage — the most concession-laden move in the corpus.
Chosen or given?Q-15.1.1.6
The “born this way” parity move
Core position
  • Cliffe Knechtle concedes orientation is given, not chosen — and argues it does not settle the ethical question. A lesbian-convert friend “still has a sex drive for women; that has not changed,” but covenantal commitment is a separate decision.
Key move
  • Parity, applied to himself first. His own heterosexual drive does not license his own behaviour, so being “born this way” cannot by itself license anyone’s. The skeptic counter (Smalley): science treats attraction as innate; Knechtle trusts “bronze-age fishermen” over medicine.
The engagement playbookQ-15.1.1.9
Preston Perry — the most fully relational strand
Core position
  • When a gay person’s worldview is built from several non-Christian commitments, do not foreground the sexuality. It is a downstream symptom of upstream metaphysics; leading with it cedes the conversation to the flashpoint and reinforces the totalising of the person’s identity by their sexuality.
Key move
  • Sexuality as on-ramp, not cudgel. Use it as the entry hook, then pivot to metaphysics. Grant dignity on the flashpoint first; name the church’s failures directly. Credential is biographical — Jackie Hill Perry’s Gay Girl, Good God. The floor is Genesis 1:27.

Expert framings

Knechtle and Huff are the main voices; Michael Jones and Preston Perry each contribute one distinctive, load-bearing argument. The affirming / revisionist position is noted honestly as an in-house under-representation rather than a developed in-house view.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

“You’re telling gay people they’re going to hell.”

The conservative line damns people for their orientation, full stop. That is what Christianity amounts to.

Popular / skeptic

Strongest response

Salvation turns on lordship, not orientation

Knechtle and Jones both refuse the framing directly. “You don’t go to hell for being gay just like you don’t go to heaven for being heterosexual.” The criterion is self-lordship versus submission to Christ, not a specific sin.

Knechtle deck-clearing openerJones greedy-Christian parallel

Objection 2

“Disagreeing with my relationship means you hate me.”

Christian “family values” simply mean hatred of gay people and anyone on the genderqueer spectrum.

Aron Ra

Strongest response

Concern for flourishing increases love

Knechtle’s parenting analogy: caring about whether a choice helps someone flourish does not make you love them less — if anything more. Lived credibility: gay friends in his own wedding party.

Stuart Knechtle parenting analogy

Objection 3

“I was born this way, so it’s good — and questioning my identity is offensive.”

My orientation is innate; to call its expression wrong is to attack who I am.

Smalley; popular

Strongest response

Self-applied parity: born-with does not equal licensed

Knechtle: a heterosexual drive doesn’t license his own behaviour either, so the appeal to nature settles nothing for anyone. “If your identity is based on your sexual practice — scary.” The parity is applied to himself, not aimed at gay people.

Cliffe Knechtle heterosexual-drive parity

Objection 4

“Science understands same-sex attraction — you’re trusting bronze-age fishermen over medicine.”

Modern medicine has understood orientation for decades; the biblical authors could not have. Why privilege an ancient text over the science?

David C. Smalley

Conceded in part — not fully answered

Orientation granted; the is-ought step disputed

Knechtle agrees orientation is given. The disputed step is whether an innate desire settles the ethics, which he denies by parity. The deeper medical-authority and biblical-authority question underneath is not developed.

Partial — aggregation gap

Objection 5

“Christianity is uniquely cruel to gay people.”

Of all the worldviews on offer, the Christian one is the most hostile to a same-sex-attracted person.

Popular

Strongest response

Comparative-worldview reversal

Huff turns the question outward: naturalism offers “no intrinsic purpose” and a survival-of-the-fittest “weeding out”; Sunni orthodoxy debates only the method of killing. Christianity, by contrast, affirms image-bearing worth.

Huff internal critique of the alternatives

Objection 6

“Christianity makes outsized demands on gay people specifically.”

Celibacy for life is a burden no straight Christian is asked to carry. The cost is unequal.

Popular

Strongest response

Universal cross-bearing

Topping, via Allberry: “if you think I am called more to do that, you don’t understand what it means to submit to the lordship of Christ — we all have our own burdens to bear.” The demand is framed as universal, not gay-specific.

Topping / Allberry Side B model

Objection 7

“Christians don’t actually treat gay people with the dignity they preach.”

The lived record — people thrown out of churches, weaponised sermons — contradicts the talk of love.

Perry (self-raised)

Strongest response

Grant the wrong; distinguish gospel from cultural Christianity

Perry names it himself — the transgender man thrown out mid-sermon — and grants the wrong as a wrong: “my heart just hurts for him.” He does not defend the offenders; he separates the gospel from the culture that has weaponised it.

Perry dignity-grant + self-critique

Objection 8

“Same-sex marriage is just two people who love each other.”

If marriage is about love and commitment, the church’s objection looks arbitrary and exclusionary.

Smalley; popular

Strongest response

Marriage as the sign of Christ and the church

Jones’s covenantal-typological reply: Christian marriage is a sign (Ephesians 5 / Genesis 2); the objection is to the structure of the sign, not the worth of the love. Stuart adds the civil / religious distinction.

Jones typological argumentStuart Knechtle civil distinction

Objection 9

The affirming reading: the Greek words don’t mean that, and the writers didn’t know modern orientation

Arsenokoitai and malakoi are lexically contested; the biblical authors had no concept of a fixed, loving same-sex orientation, so the prohibitions don’t reach modern relationships.

Matthew Vines, James Brownson (affirming / revisionist)

Gap — largely unaddressed

Named everywhere, engaged nowhere

Every expert flags this in their “objections not addressed” section; no one in the corpus engages the lexical or revisionist-exegesis case on its own terms. This is the single most important gap for fairness and the priority gap-fill target.
