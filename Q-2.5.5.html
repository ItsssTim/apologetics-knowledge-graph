The question

How can an eternal hell be a proportionate penalty for the finite choices of a finite life?

This is the proportionality objection, and it is distinct from the questions either side of it. It is not Q-2.5.1 (“is hell compatible with a loving God?”) and not Q-2.5.6 (“what about those who never heard?”). The challenge is narrower and sharper: even granting that hell exists and that God is loving, the arithmetic looks unjust — an infinite sentence for a finite crime. Aron Ra puts the popular version at full volume: “ten thousand Hitlers Times Square — you can’t be bad enough to be worth hell.”

This node is named for Knechtle’s reply: a defence that relocates the moral variable away from the duration of the offence and onto the dignity of the persons — the dignity God conferred on the offender (which makes their free choices genuinely weighty) and the authority of the God offended (against whom the offence is measured). A paired sub-argument carries the same logic onto the atonement: Christ’s finite suffering can pay an infinite debt because its measure is the intensity of relational rupture, not clock-time.

The convergent case — one defence, two axes, several moves

Knechtle does not run a single syllogism. He stacks a cluster of mutually-reinforcing moves, all of which relocate the question from quantity (how long, how much) to quality (whose dignity, whose authority, what kind of relationship). Treat it as a cumulative case with four streams rather than a deductive proof.

Stream A · Dignity of the offender

  • Rational mind, conscience, free will
  • Conferred dignity → real responsibility
  • “Not quid pro quo”
  • The “god-shaped vacuum”
  • “Fair because God gave you dignity”

Stream B · Authority of the offended

  • Gravity scales with authority
  • The kindergarten-to-judge ladder
  • Same slap, rising penalty
  • God’s authority is maximal
  • “Flip off the creator of the cosmos”

Stream C · Intensity, not duration

  • The cry of dereliction
  • Rupture of an eternal relationship
  • “That is hell”
  • Measure is who, not how long
  • Atonement adequacy = identity
Axis 1 · Dignity

A being God endowed with such dignity bears genuine responsibility, so its choices about God carry proportionate — eternal — weight.

The offender’s side. Eternal stakes are not a punitive tariff; they are the dignity of being taken seriously as a free agent. “The point is not quid pro quo… God has given you so much dignity, so much responsibility, that you are responsible… to decide where you want to spend eternity.”

Axis 2 · Authority

The gravity of an offence scales with the authority of the one offended; God’s authority is maximal, so the penalty is maximal.

The offended party’s side. The same slap earns detention (a classmate), the principal’s office (a teacher), arrest (a police officer), prison (a judge). The act is identical; only authority rises. Sin is “the attitude of slapping God in the face” — hence the maximal penalty. Built by Anselmic step-by-step extension, not syllogism.

Conclusion

Eternal separation is “fair” — the cost of being a dignified, free agent whose choices about God genuinely matter — and the atonement is adequate for the same reason: who the persons are sets the weight, not how long.

The shared logic. Both axes, and the atonement pivot, relocate moral seriousness from the finitude of the act to the status of the persons. Hell is also self-chosen: “God doesn’t throw people into hell” — the theme song is “I did it my way,” and God honours that into eternity.

Honest density — single-voice, deep, but unrebutted

The entire substantive corpus is Knechtle’s, refined across street Q&A, formal debate and long-form conversation. That depth lets us see the defence from many angles — but there is no second expert to supply the rebuttal Knechtle himself never gives to the objection his own premise invites (see gaps).

Notable internal tension — the duration retreat (2023, Aron Ra)

Under Aron Ra’s self-negation reductio, Stuart Knechtle distances the position from eternal conscious torment altogether: “I don’t think it goes on forever.” He pivots to justice-for-victims and the revenge-cycle argument rather than defend the traditional duration — striking for the same ministry that elsewhere mounts a confident proportionality defence of hell’s severity. It is the clearest sign that the duration question is the soft point the dignity/authority defence never armours.

Heaven and hell are infinite states granted as reward or punishment for the finite actions of one short life. The mismatch between a finite cause and an infinite consequence looks straightforwardly unjust.

Objection 4

“I didn’t decide to not believe in God, I came to that conclusion.” If belief is involuntary, the choice-frame for hell is incoherent — one cannot be justly condemned for an honest conclusion reached on the evidence.

Persistent gaps in the corpus

Because the node is single-voice, no second expert ever supplies the missing rebuttals. These are the objections the §2.5.5 corpus does not adequately answer.

Gap 1 · the load-bearing one

The proportionality premise is asserted, never defended against its standard objection.

Status of the offended ≠ culpability of the offender. Both axes rest on the claim that offence-gravity scales with the status of the persons. The classic objection to the Anselmic / Edwardsian “infinite-being-offended” defence is that desert is normally fixed by the offender’s knowledge, intent and capacity — not by a scale-factor read off the victim’s rank. And the judge-ladder equivocates: a judge’s authority is institutional and finite, so the ladder shows only that penalties increase with authority, not that they reach infinity at maximal authority — the very step the argument needs. Knechtle never engages this.

Gap 2

The duration question for the damned is dodged, not answered.

Intensity-not-duration, dignity and the judge-ladder all defend the severity or justice of hell — none defends the eternal duration of the lost’s punishment. When the 2022 student presses on “a countable amount of time” vs infinity, the reply re-describes hell as relational separation rather than defending why it must be everlasting; in 2023 Stuart abandons the claim outright.

Gap 3

The involuntarism objection is unresolved.

Baggini’s “I concluded, I didn’t choose” directly threatens the hell-as-self-chosen stream. Knechtle concedes belief can’t always be willed, then pivots to conscience without closing the gap between honest intellectual non-belief and willful rejection. “No one is condemned for honest ignorance” sits in tension with “you chose separation” whenever the unbeliever is sincere.

Gap 4

No formal engagement with the live alternatives.

Neither annihilationism / conditional immortality, nor the natural-consequences view (Geach, Kinghorn), nor hopeful universalism is named and assessed — even though Stuart’s “I don’t think it goes on forever” implies one of them. The corpus defends “hell is fair” without ever fixing which model of hell it is defending.

Gap 5

The internal inconsistency across transcripts is never reconciled.

A confident proportionality defence of hell’s severity (2014, 2022) sits beside an explicit retreat from eternal conscious torment (2023) with no acknowledgement. The ministry’s position on duration is, on the evidence of the corpus, unsettled.
