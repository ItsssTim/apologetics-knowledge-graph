Panel Prep — Apologetics Teaching Kit

Distilled from 81 cross-expert argument trees (Craig, Lennox, Huff, Meyer, McLatchie, Knechtle, Perry, Strobel). 23 teaching cards across four clusters, plus two safety sheets. For the ~58 questions not carded here, use the searchable hub: browse.html.

How to use this on the day

  • Each card is one screen: the case in 4 beats → the signature illustration (your teaching hook) → a quotable line → what to say if challenged → your honest soft spot.
  • Lead with the illustration, not the syllogism. The freezing-water analogy, Leslie’s firing squad, the 100,000-piece puzzle, “what is God doing on a cross?” — these are what an audience remembers.
  • ⚠️verify marks every quote, statistic, name, and date. They came from auto-transcribed video and some are wrong. Do not read a ⚠️verify item in quote marks or cite a number from it on stage without checking it first. The recurring offenders are in the Quote-Caution sheet at the back.

⚠️ Check these three BEFORE you walk in

  1. Martyrdom numbers are inflated and wrong. “360,000 killed in one Colosseum” and “over 500 apostles martyred” recur in the source material and are historically false — a prepared skeptic will catch it and use it to discredit everything else you say. Retreat to the defensible claim: the apostles were demonstrably willing to suffer and die for their eyewitness testimony (only Peter and the two Jameses are securely attested as martyred). That claim is enough; the inflated one is a trap.
  2. Never let “the gospels were written 80+ years later” stand. Mark is c. AD 65–70; the 1 Cor 15 creed is within a few years of the crucifixion. Correct it immediately.
  3. The JAMA medical-death article is 1986, not 1968. If you cite the medical case that Jesus was dead before the spear, get the year right.

Your five most dangerous soft spots (know these cold — full list at the back)

  1. The bridge from “a necessary/first cause” to the personal God of theism — the cosmological arguments under-defend this last step.
  2. The resurrection’s Bayesian-prior seam — skeptics attack on “any natural story beats a miracle”; only McLatchie answers on that ground. Don’t counter a probability objection with mere explanatory power.
  3. Faith-vs-trust equivocation — the “faith is evidence-based trust” move never actually rebuts the charge that religious commitment persists beyond the evidence.
  4. Divine hiddenness (Schellenberg) and natural evil / the “options-menu” theodicy — both left genuinely unanswered.
  5. The Kalam assumes the A-theory of time (Craig concedes it fails on the B-theory). Don’t claim it’s airtight on the philosophy of time.

Cluster 1 — Existence of God

Did the universe begin to exist, and does that require a cause? · Q-1.1.1

Teach it in 4 beats: 1. Whatever begins to exist has a cause (not “everything has a cause” — that qualifier pre-empts “who made God”). 2. The universe began to exist — back it philosophically (you can’t cross an actual infinite) and scientifically (Borde-Guth-Vilenkin theorem, thermodynamics, Big Bang). 3. Therefore the universe has a cause: uncaused, timeless, spaceless, immaterial, enormously powerful. 4. → That cause must be personal — only a free agent can produce a temporal effect from a timeless state.

Signature illustration: The freezing-water analogy. If “below 0°C” had held from eternity past, any water would already be frozen from eternity — it could never begin to freeze. A timeless cause that produces an effect with a beginning therefore needs a will. (Hilbert’s Hotel is the backup hook for “you can’t have an actual infinite.”)

Quotable ⚠️verify: “To claim that something can come into being from nothing is worse than magic. When a magician pulls a rabbit out of the hat, at least you’ve got the magician — not to mention the hat.” — William Lane Craig.

If challenged: “What caused God?” → It misfires as a “complex question”: premise 1 is “begins to exist,” and God by definition did not begin. Lennox reverses it: “You believe the universe created you — so who created your creator? I’m still waiting for the answer.”

Your soft spot: Craig himself concedes the Kalam fails on a B-theory of time (it needs the A-theory, that time genuinely “flows”). Most popular versions assume the A-theory without defending it. Don’t claim the Kalam is airtight on the philosophy of time.

Does the fine-tuning of the universe point to a designer? · Q-1.2.1

Teach it in 4 beats: 1. The constants and initial conditions sit on a razor’s edge for life, and they’re independent of the laws of nature. 2. There are only three options: physical necessity, chance, or design. 3. Necessity fails (the constants could have been otherwise); chance fails (the odds are absurd, and the multiverse rescue collapses). 4. → Therefore the fine-tuning is best explained by design — a transcendent mind.

Signature illustration: Leslie’s firing squad. Fifty marksmen all miss; saying “I shouldn’t be surprised I’m alive, since if I were dead I couldn’t notice” misses the point — your surprise is that they all missed. Survival doesn’t dissolve the wonder, it relocates it. (Backup: Henry Ford — you won’t find him by studying the engine, but you can’t explain the car without him. This is “an argument from knowledge, not from gaps.”)

Quotable ⚠️verify: “A common-sense interpretation of the data suggests that a super-intellect has monkeyed with physics, as well as chemistry and biology.” — Fred Hoyle (hostile witness). And ⚠️verify Penrose’s initial-entropy figure: 1 in 10(10123).

If challenged: “A multiverse makes a tuned universe inevitable.” → Two replies: (a) Craig — a random member of a world-ensemble should be a single fluctuating Boltzmann brain, not a finely-tuned cosmos, so the ensemble is disconfirmed; (b) Lennox — “God can create as many multiverses as he likes,” so a multiverse doesn’t get rid of God anyway.

Your soft spot: Stovold’s unfalsifiability charge — that theists would claim victory whether the constants were tunable or not — goes substantively unanswered. Likewise the “fine-tuned for our kind of life” reference-class problem. Don’t pretend the chance-vs-design step is fully closed.

Does the information in DNA point to a designing mind? · Q-1.2.3

Teach it in 4 beats: 1. In all uniform experience, specified information (language, code) always traces back to a mind. 2. DNA stores genuine digital, sequence-specific information — chemistry doesn’t fix the order, so the sequence carries meaning like alphabetic text. 3. Chance, necessity and self-organisation all fail to produce that kind of information. 4. → Therefore a designing intelligence is the best explanation — using Darwin’s own “present is the key to the past” method to reach a non-Darwinian conclusion.

Signature illustration: The Rosetta Stone. Walking into the British Museum, no one says the inscriptions were carved by “wind and erosion” — you instantly infer a mind. (Lennox’s scaling version: you read “exit” (four letters) and infer mind instantly; the genome is a 3.5-billion-letter word, yet people credit “chance and necessity.”)

Quotable ⚠️verify: “In the 19th century we thought everything was matter and energy, and we now know there’s a third fundamental entity in nature, and that’s information — and information is something you can’t account for apart from mind.” — Stephen Meyer. (Backup hostile witness ⚠️verify: Bill Gates — “DNA is like a software program, only much more complex than any we’ve ever created.”)

If challenged: “It’s just chemistry — the sequence is physically determined.” → McLatchie: “You can arrange the amino acids in any order — there’s no physical limitation; that is a biological fact.” Order isn’t information (a bathtub vortex is spontaneous order, not specified complexity), so the specificity can’t come from chemical law.

Your soft spot: The Shannon-vs-specified-information distinction is asserted but never rigorously closed against the technical critique (“Dembski’s berry fallacy”). And no expert gives a positive account of how a disembodied mind could sequence matter. Flag the distinction, don’t over-claim it’s settled.

Do objective moral values and duties require God? · Q-1.3.1

Teach it in 4 beats: 1. If God does not exist, objective moral values and duties do not exist (a long roster of atheists concedes this). 2. Objective moral values and duties do exist — torturing a child for fun is really wrong, not just unfashionable. 3. This is about moral ontology (what grounds morality), not moral epistemology (whether atheists can be good — they obviously can). 4. → Therefore God exists.

Signature illustration: Nuremberg / the “law above the law.” The Nazi defence was cultural (“we followed our own culture’s orders — who are you to judge?”) and it stalled the trials. Then prosecutor Robert Jackson stood and said there is a law above culture, by which slaughtering Jews is absolutely evil. Real moral judgement needs a standard outside culture. (MLK’s Letter from Birmingham Jail is the American-key version.)

Quotable ⚠️verify: “Morality is a biological adaptation no less than our hands and feet and teeth… considered as a rationally justifiable set of claims about an objective something, ethics is illusory.” — Michael Ruse (hostile witness for premise 1).

If challenged: “Atheists can be moral without God / I don’t need God to be good.” → That’s epistemology, not ontology. Craig: “I would never think of saying atheists can’t live good moral lives… what I’m saying is there’s no transcendent foundation for the values we both want to affirm.”

Your soft spot: Atheistic non-natural moral realism (Wielenberg, Enoch, Ahmed — “brute moral facts”) is the genuinely live alternative and the corpus keeps collapsing it into “moral Platonism” or not engaging it. The third-horn Euthyphro reply (“goodness flows from God’s character”) is usually asserted, not defended against “why isn’t God’s character itself an arbitrary brute fact?”

Contingency vs Kalam: which cosmological argument actually works? · Q-1.1.3

Teach it in 4 beats: 1. Whatever exists has an explanation — in the necessity of its own nature or in an external cause (note: exists, not “begins to exist”). 2. If the universe has an explanation, that explanation is God (the only non-physical necessary candidates are abstract objects or an unembodied mind, and abstract objects can’t cause anything). 3. The universe exists, and exists contingently (a different collection of quarks would be a different universe). 4. → Therefore the explanation of the universe is God.

Signature illustration: The translucent ball in the woods. You’d ask what explains a ball you stumbled on — and blowing it up to the size of a planet, a galaxy, or the whole universe never removes the need for an explanation. (Barron’s companion line: matter is by its nature contingent, since it only ever exists in some particular form or configuration that itself needs explaining — “the appeal to matter doesn’t solve the problem, it postpones it.”)

Quotable ⚠️verify: “Whatever exists has an explanation of its existence, either in the necessity of its own nature or else in an external cause… If the universe has an explanation of its existence, that explanation is God.” — William Lane Craig. (Note ⚠️verify: an agnostic interlocutor, “Nathan,” conceded “that one works.”)

If challenged: “Why not just call matter itself the necessary being?” → Barron: matter never exists “as such,” only in a particular form/intensity/position — each of which needs explaining — so matter is contingent, not necessary.

Your soft spot: The bridge from “a necessary being” to “the personal God of classical theism” is the corpus’s biggest gap, named by both the friendly agnostic (“a far cry from an incarnational God or a Trinity”) and hostile critics (“is that a mind we can pray to?”). The argument’s only patch is the cumulative case — don’t claim contingency alone delivers a personal God.

Does naturalism undermine confidence in human reason? · Q-17.5.5

Teach it in 4 beats: 1. On naturalism, the mind is the brain, and the brain is the end-product of a mindless, unguided process. 2. That process selects for survival and reproduction, not for truth — and the two come apart. 3. So there’s no reason to trust any belief the faculties produce — including belief in naturalism itself. 4. → Naturalism is self-defeating; theism grounds reason because the mind “in here” and the universe “out there” trace to the same rational Creator.

Signature illustration: Lennox’s Socratic set-piece + the computer analogy. “I ask my colleagues what they do science with. ‘My brain.’ What’s the brain? ‘The end product of a mindless unguided process.’ And I just smile and say — and you trust it?” The trap: if you knew your computer was the end-product of a mindless process, you wouldn’t trust it either.

Quotable ⚠️verify: “With me the horrid doubt always arises whether the convictions of man’s mind, which has been developed from the mind of the lower animals, are of any value or at all trustworthy.” — Charles Darwin (hostile witness — lead with this to neutralise “you got it from creationists”).

If challenged: “But natural selection would track truth — true beliefs help you survive.” → Best reply on offer: selection optimises for survival, not truth (Gray), and assuming it produced reliable cognition is an “evolution of the gaps” just-so story. (See soft spot — this reply is gestured at, not won.)

Your soft spot: This is the gap — the detailed reliabilist reply (that fitness and truth substantially overlap in survival-relevant domains; Plantinga–Fodor–Dennett) is never actually refuted. Every expert states “survival not truth”; none defeats the overlap case. State the worry; don’t claim to have closed it.

Can life have meaning without God? · Q-17.2.2

Teach it in 4 beats: 1. On naturalism, we’re produced and annihilated by purposeless forces and the universe ends in heat death. 2. So there’s no ultimate meaning — any meaning is self-invented and arbitrary (everyone grants atheists have local/subjective meaning; the claim is only about cosmic meaning). 3. The honest atheists (Camus, Russell, Nietzsche, Sartre) concede exactly this; the inconsistent ones smuggle in meaning their worldview can’t fund. 4. → No one can actually live the consistent conclusion — the universal hunger for meaning and justice points beyond naturalism to a personal God, supremely the God whose resurrection underwrites hope past the grave.

Signature illustration: “Everybody worships” (David Foster Wallace, “This Is Water”). “The only choice we get is what to worship… pretty much anything else you worship will eat you alive.” Stuart Knechtle pulls it up verbatim and treats Wallace’s own suicide as a tragic verification. (Backup: Craig’s heat-death set-piece — “only the corpses of dead stars and galaxies expanding into the endless night.”)

Quotable ⚠️verify: “If there is no immortality then life is without ultimate meaning, value or purpose… everything will wind up the same… futile and fleeting gestures against the inevitable fall of darkness.” — William Lane Craig.

If challenged: “Subjective meaning is real and motivating — hunger doesn’t prove food (Dillahunty).” → Best handling is McLatchie’s term-control: concede subjective meaning at the outset, stipulate the debate is about objective/cosmic meaning; and Knechtle’s durability point — self-made meaning is fragile and collapses under real suffering.

Your soft spot: The contingent-meaning reframe (Baggini, Parsons: “ultimately meaningless ≠ completely meaningless”) is the corpus’s weakest point — the apologists assert that cosmic meaninglessness drains local meaning of value but never argue the dependence. Note too the selection effect: the atheist side is stocked with existentialists who concede, not analytic naturalists (Wolf, Metz) who construct meaning. Don’t overreach on “atheists can’t have meaning.” ## Cluster 2 — Jesus & the Resurrection

Panel-prep study digest. One card per argument tree. Teach the positive case and the illustration first; keep “if challenged” crisp. ⚠️verify flags every quote and number — these trace to auto-transcripts and need a source check before you say them on a panel.

The five §3.3 cards overlap by design. Keep them distinct: 3.3.1 is the whole cumulative case, 3.3.2 is the empty tomb / women, 3.3.3 is the early 1 Cor 15 creed, 3.3.4 is the disciples’ belief / martyrdom, 3.3.5 is the demolition of the alternatives. Cross-reference rather than repeat.

Did Jesus actually claim to be God? · Q-3.4.1

Teach it in 4 beats: 1. Words — Jesus uses the divine name: “Before Abraham was, I AM” (John 8:58); “I and the Father are one” (John 10:30). 2. Deeds — he does what only God does: forgives sins (Mark 2), accepts worship, is Lord of the Sabbath. 3. Reactions — his Jewish audience reaches for stones to kill him for blasphemy; the trial convicts him for the claim (Mark 14:62, Son of Man + Psalm 110). 4. → The cumulative force of words + deeds + enemy reaction makes the claim unambiguous. He claimed divine identity.

Signature illustration: The Jewish stoning reaction as enemy attestation — his hostile audience understood the claim as a claim to be Yahweh, so no later church invention is needed. (Wes Huff’s HANDS acronym is the clean memory hook: Honour, Attributes, Names, Deeds, Service — each reserved for God alone, each given to Jesus.)

Quotable ⚠️verify: “Before Abraham was born, I AM… There was no misunderstanding. The Jews picked up stones to stone him for blasphemy.” — Cliffe Knechtle. (⚠️verify on ANY quote or number — auto-transcripts.)

If challenged: “High Christology was invented later — John is a late gospel” → 1 Cor 15 and Philippians 2 are early creeds that already carry divinity, and the Mark 14:62 trial scene has Q-source attestation plus the criterion of dissimilarity — so the claim is in the earliest layer, not bolted on by a late John.

Your soft spot: No expert in the corpus engages the Synoptic-vs-Johannine critical distinction head-on (Borg/Crossan: historical-Jesus tradition vs Johannine theology), nor Ehrman’s How Jesus Became God development thesis by name. Don’t get drawn into “Synoptics never make the claim” — the trees don’t give you a clean rebuttal.

Did Jesus rise from the dead? (the whole case) · Q-3.3.1

Teach it in 4 beats: 1. He really died — Roman crucifixion was lethal expertise; the watery serum from the spear wound signals heart failure (defeats swoon). 2. He was buried in a known tomb — Joseph of Arimathea, a Sanhedrist, so the site was traceable and checkable. 3. The tomb was found empty, by women — and demoralised deserters were suddenly willing to die for what they saw. 4. → Run those four facts through “best explanation”: “God raised Jesus” beats every naturalistic rival on scope and power.

Signature illustration: “They died for what they SAW, not just what they believed.” Cliffe Knechtle’s eyewitness-vs-belief distinction — many die for a sincere belief; almost no one dies for what they know to be a lie, and these men were positioned to know. (This card owns the cumulative four-stream frame; see 3.3.3 for the creed, 3.3.4 for martyrdom in depth, 3.3.5 for the rebuttals.)

Quotable ⚠️verify: “as an historian I cannot explain the rise of early Christianity unless Jesus rose again leaving an empty tomb behind him” — N.T. Wright (cited by Craig). (⚠️verify on ANY quote or number — auto-transcripts.)

If challenged: “Miracles are by definition the least probable event — a historian can never establish one” (Ehrman/Hume) → Craig’s reply: the probability of the resurrection isn’t its intrinsic prior alone; it must include the failure of the naturalistic alternatives in the denominator. Ignoring that is “Ehrman’s egregious error.” The agnostic philosopher John Earman calls Hume’s argument “an abject failure.”

Your soft spot: The “no contemporary evidence Jesus even lived” challenge goes unmet at the popular level — Knechtle pivots to anthropology instead of deploying the standard Tacitus/Josephus/creed toolkit. Also: the medical-death point only defeats swoon, not hallucination or legend — don’t over-claim it.

Was the tomb really empty — and why do women matter? · Q-3.3.2

Teach it in 4 beats: 1. All four gospels make women the first to find the tomb empty — testimony a first-century court would have discounted. 2. No one writing to persuade a sceptical, patriarchal audience invents discredited witnesses; this is the criterion of embarrassment. 3. The earliest Jewish counter-claim — “the disciples stole the body” (Matt 28) — concedes the tomb was empty (enemy attestation). 4. → The empty tomb is a fact every theory must explain; the women detail and the Jewish polemic both point to authentic memory, not fabrication.

Signature illustration: The women-as-first-witnesses embarrassment — the single most-cited application of the criterion in the whole corpus. N.T. Wright’s line that putting Mary Magdalene there is “like shooting themselves in the foot” for an apologist, “but to us as historians this kind of thing is gold dust.” (This card owns the empty tomb; the broader case is 3.3.1.)

Quotable ⚠️verify: “nobody faking a story would have ever claimed that women were the main bearers of the testimony” — John Lennox. (⚠️verify on ANY quote or number — auto-transcripts.)

If challenged: “The women-at-the-tomb detail is Mark’s literary theology, not history — in Mark the marginalised understand, so he’d invent it that way” (Ehrman) → it’s independently attested in roughly five sources (so it isn’t only Markan), and the women are named followers, not anonymous outsiders. Honest flag: this answers that the tradition is multiply attested but doesn’t fully dismantle Ehrman’s literary-motive reading on its own terms.

Your soft spot: The clean logical gap — an empty tomb is consistent with theft, relocation, or wrong-tomb error, so it is not by itself a resurrection — is never directly closed. The experts lean on the cumulative case to bridge it. Concede the gap and pivot to the cumulative case rather than over-defending the single fact.

Why the resurrection? The early 1 Corinthians 15 creed · Q-3.3.3

Teach it in 4 beats: 1. The case rests on one document everyone — including hostile scholars — grants is early: the creed in 1 Cor 15:3–8. 2. Paul says he “received” and “passed on” this (rabbinic transmission language) and traces it to Peter and James in Jerusalem (Gal 1:18) — dating it to within a few years, arguably months, of the cross. 3. The creed names checkable eyewitnesses (Cephas, the Twelve, James, 500 at once) and Paul adds “most of whom are still alive” — an open invitation to verify. 4. → A multiply-attested, falsifiable, near-eyewitness list this early outruns legend; the best explanation of the reports is a real resurrection.

Signature illustration: The 1 Cor 15 early creed itself — “a news flash that goes right back to the beginning” (Strobel). The “still alive” clause is a live verification invitation: go ask them. Pair with the mass-hallucination rebuttal — you cannot get 500 people to hallucinate the same thing at the same time over 40 days.

Quotable ⚠️verify: “this Creed this tradition we can be entirely confident was formulated… within months of the death of Jesus” — James D.G. Dunn (via Strobel). (⚠️verify on ANY quote or number — auto-transcripts.)

If challenged: “A creed only tells us what people believed, not that it happened” (Paulogia) → redirect to the appearance to the Twelve and the 500: the Corinthians could verify by going to Jerusalem, and the testimony “clearly comes from a lot of people, not just one person” (Bauckham). Honest flag: the strongest form — getting James independently confirmed as a witness by James himself — is deflected, not fully answered.

Your soft spot: The Damascus-road experience is leaned on (Paul as un-primed convert) but its nature (visionary vs physical) is never engaged — Allison and Paulogia exploit that. And Kappel’s consensus-asymmetry charge (Craig defers to historians’ consensus on the facts but not to philosophers’ consensus elsewhere) goes unanswered.

What caused the disciples to believe — and die for it? · Q-3.3.4

Teach it in 4 beats: 1. Even hostile critics grant the datum: the disciples sincerely believed they saw the risen Jesus (Lüdemann, Fredriksen, Ehrman all concede it). 2. The belief was sudden, overturned a deeply resistant Jewish paradigm (no category for a crucified, individually-risen Messiah), and converted enemies — Paul the persecutor, James the sceptic brother. 3. The naturalistic causes fail: hallucinations are private and individual; liars make poor martyrs; the creed is too early for legend. 4. → The best explanation of the sincere, costly, paradigm-breaking belief is that they actually encountered the risen Jesus.

Signature illustration: “They died for what they SAW, not just believed” — sharpened here with the Watergate/Colson analogy: brilliant lawyers cracked and confessed the cover-up under mere threat of prison; first-century Jews would not die under threat of execution to maintain what they knew was a lie. (This card owns the disciples’ belief and martyrdom; 3.3.1 owns the overall case.)

Quotable ⚠️verify: “They were willing to die not for a belief… they were willing to die for what they claim to have seen — the dead Christ risen from the dead.” — Cliffe Knechtle. (⚠️verify on ANY quote or number — auto-transcripts.)

If challenged: “People die for false beliefs all the time — 9/11 hijackers, kamikaze pilots, Jim Jones” → concede and flip it: those people died for what they were convinced was true, not for a known fabrication. The argument requires dying for a known lie, and the disciples were uniquely positioned to know whether they’d seen him — that structural feature is exactly what the parity cases lack.

Your soft spot: The martyrdom traditions are mostly late and legendary for everyone except Peter and the two Jameses. Retreat to the defensible claim — willingness to suffer and risk death, which is far better attested — and do not inflate it (“over 500 died,” “360,000 in one Colosseum” are overstated/contested numbers). Craig also never produced a martyrdom citation when Ehrman directly demanded one.

If he didn’t rise, what’s the best alternative — and does any work? · Q-3.3.5

Teach it in 4 beats: 1. Frame it as elimination: the data (appearances, empty tomb, origin of belief) has four mutually exhaustive explanations — legend, lie, honest mistake, or it happened. 2. Legend fails (creed too early); lie fails (liars make poor martyrs, and why proclaim in Jerusalem where the body could be produced?); honest mistake/hallucination fails (appearances were poly-modal — touch, eating, groups, 40 days — and “you can’t hallucinate an empty tomb”). 3. Swoon, wrong-tomb, stolen-body each collapse on motive, opportunity, medical fact, or anachronism. 4. → Knock down all three rivals and the fourth — resurrection — stands by elimination.

Signature illustration: The mass-hallucination rebuttal (no such thing — 500 people don’t share identical content over 40 days), capped by the unknown-identical-twin theory: a sceptical professor, unable to deny the four facts, posited a secret twin separated at birth. Craig wields it as a reductio of naturalism itself — “it shows to what desperate lengths skepticism must go.” (This card owns the demolition; the positive case is 3.3.1–3.3.4.)

Quotable ⚠️verify: “Five hundred people having the same hallucination at the same time would be a bigger miracle than the resurrection itself.” — Gary Collins, psychologist (via Strobel). (⚠️verify on ANY quote or number — auto-transcripts.)

If challenged: “Any naturalistic story, however unlikely, beats a miracle on prior probability” (Ehrman, Law, Blais) → McLatchie’s move: concede the Bayesian framework, then contest the priors — independent natural-theology evidence for God plus the messianic context make the prior on a resurrection “certainly not astronomically low,” so sufficient evidence can overcome it.

Your soft spot: Most experts only knock down the caricature “mass hallucination” and barely engage the sophisticated bereavement-vision hypothesis (Allison). Worse, Craig and Knechtle answer the probability objection with an explanatory-power argument — a methodological mismatch that lets the strongest sceptics slip away. Only McLatchie meets them on Bayesian ground; if you’re pressed on priors, that’s your lane, not the explanatory-scope reply. ## Cluster 3 — Bible Reliability & Other Religions

Can we trust the New Testament text? (manuscript reliability and transmission) · Q-4.1.1

Teach it in 4 beats: 1. Name the objection: the “telephone game” / 400,000+ variants means the text is hopelessly corrupted (Bart Ehrman). 2. Invert it. Variant abundance is leverage, not liability. Three streams: abundance (~5,800 Greek manuscripts), early dating (P52, P66 within a generation or two), and cross-checks (named witnesses, archaeology). 3. Apply parity: judge the NT by the standard we already use for Homer, Caesar, Genghis Khan. F.F. Bruce: if it were secular, authenticity would be “beyond all doubt.” 4. → All the original wording survives somewhere in the apparatus. The only live question for any substantive variant is which reading is original, never that one is missing.

Signature illustration: The 100,000-piece puzzle vs the telephone game. The telephone game is a single degrading line; the NT is multiple independent copies, so we have ~110,000 pieces for a 100,000-piece puzzle. Surplus pieces make errors easier to spot. (Pair with the 6,600-ft manuscript stack / CN Tower image.)

Quotable ⚠️verify: “we don’t have a hundred percent of the text of the Bible we have a hundred and ten percent of the text of the Bible… we sift out we put the puzzle together and we figure out where there are pieces that don’t belong” — Wes Huff. (⚠️verify on ANY quote/number — auto-transcripts.)

If challenged: “Manuscript count doesn’t equal divine inspiration.” → Correct, and concede it openly. Huff says so himself: the count is a consistency/parity argument, not a proof of inspiration. Apply the standard you use for Plato and Herodotus, nothing more.

Your soft spot: The easy “99% of variants are trivial” win dodges Ehrman’s stronger move, which grants the trivial 99% and presses the doctrinally-loaded fraction (Mark 1:41 “angry” vs “compassion”; the orthodox-corruption thesis). Only Huff’s 2016 webinar engages this fully; most treatments bracket it off.

What counts as a “contradiction” in the Bible, and what does inspiration mean? · Q-4.5.1

Teach it in 4 beats: 1. Define your terms first. A contradiction must break the law of non-contradiction: A and not-A, same thing, same sense, same time. Most alleged contradictions are just differentiation in detail. 2. Separate two senses of “error.” The textual critic’s “error” (any divergence between manuscripts, e.g. a spelling slip) is not the theologian’s “error” (a false claim about fact or doctrine). 3. Reframe variation as evidence: independent eyewitnesses should differ; identical detail would signal collusion. Genre matters too (poetry is not failed science). 4. → The threshold for “contradiction” is rarely met, “error” is equivocated, and no doctrine rests on a contested verse. Doctrine is tota scriptura, taken from the whole.

Signature illustration: The Socrates-biographies parity. Socrates has three biographies (Aristophanes, Xenophon, Plato) that differ in detail, yet classicists give the authors the benefit of the doubt. “New Testament scholars do the exact same thing as classicists do for the life of Socrates.”

Quotable ⚠️verify: “god doesn’t err in communicating to us but a scribe you know making a spelling difference well that’s not god’s fault that’s a scribe’s fault” — Tim Mitchell (with Wes Huff). (⚠️verify on ANY quote/number — auto-transcripts.)

Do all religions lead to God? (pluralism + Buddhism, Hinduism, Islam) · Q-6.7.1

What is the doctrine of the Trinity, and is it coherent? · Q-7.1.1

Teach it in 4 beats: 1. Clear the caricature first. Most objections attack modalism (one person wearing three masks), a heresy Christians already reject. Steelman what is actually being defended before defending it. 2. Lay the load-bearing distinction (James White): “being” is what something is; “person” is who someone is. God is one in being, three in person. 3. Apply non-contradiction precisely: the law is only broken if God is one and three in the same sense. Different senses, no contradiction. 4. → One being in three co-equal, co-eternal persons, equidistant from modalism, tritheism, and partialism. You cannot divide a qualitative infinite by three, so each person has the fullness of deity.

Signature illustration: The Trinity-of-love (Lennox): “God is a fellowship. Did Christianity give birth to modern science? · Q-5.7.4

Teach it in 4 beats: 1. The founders (Galileo, Kepler, Newton, Boyle, Maxwell, Faraday) were overwhelmingly believers who said faith motivated the work — “the motor that drove it.” 2. Belief in a rational Creator generated the expectation of law in nature, and the conviction (imago Dei) that we can read it — the two preconditions of doing science at all. 3. Doctrine supplied the method: creation is not divine (so study it, don’t worship it) and contingent (so go and look, don’t deduce a priori). 4. → Negative control: technologically superior China produced no abstract science because it “lacked the unifying concept of a single Creator” (Needham). So the conflict thesis is a historical inversion — science is a Christian cultural offspring.

Signature illustration: Lennox’s triangulation — “you need laws up above, minds in here, and a world out there, and you need those three to triangulate.” Christianity uniquely grounds all three. (Pair with the university-mottos reversal: “it gave you your University as well.”)

Quotable ⚠️verify: “men became scientific because they expected law in nature and they expected law in nature because they believed in a lawgiver” — Lennox (via Whitehead/Lewis). (⚠️verify on ANY quote/number — auto-transcripts.)

If challenged: “Everyone was religious then, so it’s no special argument” → the Needham China control defeats it: an equally religious civilisation lacking the single-Creator concept produced technology but not abstract science. The relevant variable is which theology, not religion as such.

Your soft spot: The Islamic counter-case. Islam is also strict monotheism with a single rational Creator and a rich medieval scientific tradition — yet no scientific revolution there. The corpus excludes Islamic contexts without argument. Also unresolved: the correlation-to-causation gap (Lennox concedes the founders’ belief “doesn’t make God real”).

Does Hume’s argument rule miracles out in advance? · Q-5.5.2

Teach it in 4 beats: 1. Name the two arguments: the metaphysical (“a miracle violates inviolable natural law”) and the epistemic (“testimony to a miracle is always less probable than the testimony being false”). 2. Reframe the law: laws of nature describe what normally happens, they don’t prescribe like a state law holding God prisoner. God feeds a new event into an open system — breaking nothing. 3. Hume contra Hume: he rejected causation and induction, so he can’t help himself to “firm and unalterable experience”; and to know experience is uniform he’d need access to “every event of the history of the universe” — he assumes what he wants to prove. 4. → Clear the gate, and openness to miracle becomes rational; knowing the normal law is precisely what lets you recognise the exception. Whether a given miracle occurred is then a live historical question.

Signature illustration: The hotel-drawer parable. Put £100/$100 in the drawer two nights, find $50 the third morning — you don’t conclude the laws of arithmetic were broken; you conclude the laws of the land were broken, and the arithmetic is exactly what lets you detect the theft. The drawer is an open system; an agent (a thief) can act in it without breaking the maths.

Quotable ⚠️verify: “You cannot recognize a miracle unless you know the normal laws to which nature operates.” — Lennox. (⚠️verify on ANY quote/number — auto-transcripts.)

If challenged: “Always more reason to disbelieve the report than believe it” (Hume’s Maxim) → Hume predates the probability calculus; even Hume scholar Peter Millican concedes the Maxim fails; sufficient quantity and quality of testimony can mathematically overcome any finite prior (Craig). Hostile witness: Antony Flew, Hume’s leading expositor, recanted — “I would have to write all my books again.”

Your soft spot: The Bayesian/Earman epistemic formalisation goes largely unmet. Lennox answers only the metaphysical “violation” version, never Hume’s probability-on-testimony point on its own terms. And no expert supplies a metaphysics for how a non-physical agent inputs an event into physical regularity — the analogy carries the weight alone.

Is “your faith is delusional because it isn’t scientifically provable” coherent? · Q-20.2.1

Teach it in 4 beats: 1. P1: scientism claims science (or scientific provability) is the only route to truth. 2. P2: but that claim is not itself a deliverance of science — it’s a philosophical claim about science. 3. C: therefore it fails its own test — if it’s true, it’s false. Why does God allow children to suffer? · Q-2.4.1
