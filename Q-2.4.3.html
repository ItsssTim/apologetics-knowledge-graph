The question

If God is good and powerful, why does he allow believers to go on suffering — trapped in besetting sin, addiction, mental illness, bereavement, and death — rather than simply intervening to fix it?

This is the pastoral form of the problem of evil. Not the abstract logical question, but the lived complaint of someone already inside the faith: I have repented, I have prayed, and the affliction is still here. Why? It surfaces in three registers across the corpus — besetting sin and addiction (Knechtle), bereavement and one’s own mortality (Lennox, in the COVID moment), and the apparent randomness of who suffers most (Topping).

Because it is asked from inside belief, the answer cannot be the bare free-will defence aimed at the atheist. The experts converge instead on a process-and-formation answer: God works through a sanctifying process rather than a magical fix, uses the affliction itself to form character and equip the sufferer as a healer of others, and underwrites the whole thing with the guarantee of resurrection.

The convergent case — three evidence streams

Unlike a deductive theodicy, the corpus answers this with three converging streams, no one of which is meant to carry the load alone. The strongest deployment stacks all three.

Stream A · Omnipotence & free will

  • “All-powerful” excludes the logically impossible
  • God respects the free will he gave
  • No obligation to intervene on demand
  • True love requires freedom
  • Sin’s consequences are cosmic, not private

Stream B · Formation, not retribution

  • Suffering does not run on karma
  • Job over Job’s friends — no one-to-one sin/suffering
  • Soul-making: virtue needs resistance
  • The wounded healer equips others
  • “Beauty in the pain”

Stream C · Resurrection-hope

  • Final healing reserved for the resurrection
  • Scripture gives principles, not topical chapters
  • “Death is not the end”
  • Without it, “no hope in face of my death”
  • Questions land hardest when the chips are down

How each expert deploys it

Cliffe Knechtle1 transcript (with Stuart Knechtle)
The definitional reframe + the wounded healer
Signature delivery
  • Refuse the hidden premise about omnipotence first. “You better be real careful the way you define that” — it never meant a square circle, overriding free will, or intervening on demand.
  • Sin’s consequences are cosmic, not private: “if we think that only affects you and me, we are naive” — repercussions “go down through the generations.”
  • Sanctification is a process, not a fiat: repentance, prayer for the Spirit, accountable community, and “me waking up and smelling the coffee.”
  • Job over his friends: no one-to-one correspondence between sin and suffering; we are “born into a cursed, messed up world.”
  • On-the-spot parity rejoinder to “that’s baloney”: UNF wasn’t built “last summer” — good decisions trickle down through the years just as bad ones do.
Stuart Knechtle — extension

The mental-health / addiction angle: healing only Christians would entrench “a works righteousness”; final healing is eschatological (“all are going to be healed ultimately in heaven”); affliction makes wounded healers — “beauty in the depression, beauty in the drug addiction.” His own clinical anxiety and depression now equip him as a therapist.

John Lennox1 transcript
Resurrection-hope as the distinctive resource
Distinctive moves
  • Principles, not a coronavirus chapter: Scripture “doesn’t talk about mathematics either” — it gives principles at a moral, spiritual, intellectual, and emotional level.
  • The answer to suffering is resurrection-hope: it tells us “death is not the end” and offers “the tremendous hope of a resurrection.”
  • Existential stake: without Christ’s victory over death he “would have no hope in the coronavirus crisis, no hope in face of my death.”
  • Moments of truth: people respond to the big questions “especially when the chips are down.”
Method note

Lennox does not defend the resurrection here — he stakes his own hope on it and reports where the question lands hardest. Existential testimony as criterion, not syllogism.

Wes Huff (with John Topping)1 transcript
Free-will defence + soul-making, against karma
Distinctive moves (Topping)
  • “Karma is not a thing.” The “were they worse sinners?” questioners Jesus rebukes are asking karma — “that’s not how this works.”
  • Collectively borne: “we’re all ruining it together and we all suffer consequences together… and it’s not as though it’s deserved.”
  • Free-will defence: a robot programmed to say “I love you” isn’t love; freedom to love entails the option to sin.
  • Soul-making: “the concept of virtue is impossible without some sort of suffering” — no temptation, no self-control.
  • Both defences cohere: God allows “this brief moment” of suffering to bring us out “stronger and better people.”
Tactical note

Topping separates the emotional from the intellectual problem (Q-21.3.2). His wedge is his own family’s bereavement — a bystander told his grieving parents “you must have had a sin in your life” after his newborn sister died. “No, that’s not how it works.”

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

“God is all-powerful, so he could just remove the lust or addiction — why doesn’t he?”

If God can do anything, and refusing to free a repentant believer is bad, then either he cannot or he will not — and both look damning.

Implicit in the question (Knechtle’s interlocutor)

Strongest response

Define omnipotence rightly; sanctification is a process

“All-powerful” never entailed the logically impossible, overriding free will, or intervening on demand. God works the affliction through a process — repentance, the Spirit, accountable community, personal responsibility — not a magical removal.

Knechtle omnipotence reframe + process

Objection 2

“Sin doesn’t trickle down through generations — that’s baloney.”

The claim that one person’s rebellion has consequences for others, even across generations, looks like a convenient fiction.

Knechtle’s interlocutor (verbatim: “oh Cliff that’s baloney”)

Strongest response

Parity counterexample from the host university

UNF wasn’t built “last summer so you could be here this year” — many people sacrificed across many years. Good decisions trickle down through the generations exactly as bad ones do.

Knechtle on-the-spot parity move

Objection 3

“You suffer because you sinned” — karma / retribution

Job’s-friends folk theology: a one-to-one correspondence between personal sin and personal suffering. Deployed cruelly — told to grieving parents after a child’s death.

Topping’s bystander; the “worse sinners?” questioners Jesus rebukes

Strongest response

Job was right; “karma is not a thing”

No one-to-one sin/suffering correspondence; we are “born into a cursed, messed up world.” Suffering is collectively borne, “sometimes some more than others, and it’s not as though it’s deserved.”

Knechtle Job over his friendsTopping karma demolition

Objection 4

“Why does God heal some and not others?” — the bias objection

If God selectively healed believers of illness or addiction, that would be evidence of him — so why doesn’t he?

Stuart Knechtle (raised internally)

Strongest response

Selective healing would entrench works-righteousness

Healing only Christians “would be a level of bias and could connect a works righteousness.” Final, complete healing is eschatological — “all are going to be healed ultimately in heaven.”

Stuart Knechtle works-righteousness guard

Objection 5

“Why do some people suffer far more than others?”

Even granting a fallen world, the distribution of suffering looks arbitrary and unfair — some lives are crushed while others are spared.

Huff / Topping audience question

Strongest response

Free will + fall = uneven, undeserved consequences

Freedom and the fall mean consequences fall unevenly and undeservedly; God can still use even disproportionate suffering for soul-making and to make wounded healers.

Topping free-will + soul-making cumulative

Objection 6

“The Bible says nothing about my situation”

Scripture has no chapter on COVID, or addiction, or this particular grief — so what use is it as a guide to suffering?

Lennox’s interviewers (COVID context)

Strongest response

It gives principles, not topical chapters

“It doesn’t talk about mathematics either” — God means us to work much out ourselves. Scripture gives principles at a moral, spiritual, intellectual, and emotional level. The principle for suffering is resurrection-hope.

Lennox principles-not-chapters

Objection 7

Suffering shows there is no hope — death is the end

In the face of one’s own mortality or a pandemic, suffering looks like proof that there is nothing to hope for.

Implicit existential despair (Lennox’s frame)

Strongest response

Resurrection: “death is not the end”

The distinctively Christian resource is resurrection-hope. Without Christ’s victory over death, Lennox says, he “would have no hope in the coronavirus crisis, no hope in face of my death.”

Lennox resurrection-hopeStuart Knechtle eschatological healing

Objection 8

Natural evil — suffering with no human-freedom cause

Predation, congenital disease, and natural disaster predate humanity and cannot be pinned on any creature’s free choice. The free-will defence does not reach them.

Standard problem-of-evil literature

Gap — largely unaddressed

Every stream routes through human sin

All three experts locate suffering downstream of human sin and freedom (“the reason there is evil and sin is because of us”). The fall is invoked as a cosmic mechanism but never defended against the natural-evil objection.

Aggregation gap

Objection 9

Soul-making’s dark side — suffering that destroys

Much suffering embitters, crushes, or kills before any growth — the sufferer never reaches the “stronger and better” stage. Why permit suffering that ennobles no one?

Gratuitous-evil objection (Rowe-style)

Gap — largely unaddressed

The payoff is asserted, not defended

“God brings us out stronger and better people” is stated against the best case, not the counter-evidence. Neither the specific distribution of suffering nor its destructive outcomes is engaged.
