How to read the apostasy warning passages

The question

When Hebrews, John 15 and 2 Peter warn believers against falling away, what are those warnings actually doing — and which reading of them is correct?

This is the meta-level question that sits one floor above the exegetical fight over Hebrews 6 and 10. Granting that the warning texts say what they say, how do they function? Are they addressed to genuine believers about a real danger, or to nominal members about a danger only they face — and if the elect cannot in fact fall, what work is a warning even meant to do?

The answer is load-bearing for the whole perseverance debate. Both sides claim the same texts; the dispute is not over whether the warnings are in Scripture but over the hermeneutic that decides who is warned and to what end. Get the hermeneutic right and the exegesis of the individual passages largely follows.

The argument structure — three readings on trial

Because this is a single-voice node, there is no cross-expert convergence to tabulate. The structure is a contest of three readings of one body of texts. Each is internally coherent; the question is which survives Craig’s scrutiny — adjudicated by a single scalpel, the modal (can the elect fall?) versus de facto (will any elect person fall?) distinction.

Reading 1 · Nominal-only

  • The warned were never truly regenerate
  • “Tasted” the gift, never imbibed it
  • Their fall continues unbelief, not loss of salvation
  • Calvinist — saves eternal security by denying the warned are saved
  • Strains the “partakers / sanctified” language of Heb 6, 10

Reading 2 · Warnings-as-means

  • Warnings target the genuine elect
  • They are the Spirit’s causal mechanism of perseverance
  • Real and operative — but not evidence the elect could fall
  • Calvinist — saves eternal security by making warnings causal, not modal
  • Self-destructs on Craig’s reductio; reduces to Molinism

Reading 3 · Face-value (Craig)

  • Written to genuine believers (“holy brethren,” indwelt, enlightened)
  • About a real, not hypothetical, danger
  • The danger is apostasy, not everyday sin
  • Arminian — conditional security read at face value
  • Requires reading John 15 / 2 Peter 2 addressees as genuine

The contest, adjudicated

Craig’s verdict is abductive-by-elimination — not a deductive syllogism but a narrowing of live options, where Reading 2’s internal collapse is the decisive move and Reading 3 is left as the reading that takes the texts’ addressees and danger at face value.

Reading 1 · verdict

Survives as logically possible, but strains the texts’ own language.

The nominal-Christian reading must square “holy brethren... who share in a heavenly call,” people who “are his house” and were “enlightened” — and, from Hebrews 6 and 10, “partakers of the Holy Spirit” and “sanctified by the blood of the covenant.” Read at face value, these are regenerate believers, not Christians in name only.

Reading 2 · verdict

Self-refuting on its own terms — and not Calvinism at all.

The dilemma. If it is impossible for the elect to fall, the warnings are superfluous; if the warnings are the genuine means by which God secures perseverance, the elect can fall. Either way the view concedes the modal claim it was built to deny. And a God who ordains the very warnings He knows the elect would freely heed is operating by middle knowledge — Molinism disguised as Calvinism (see Q-7.4.2).

Reading 3 · verdict

Left standing: read the warnings at face value — to and about regenerate believers, of a real danger of apostasy.

The scope limit. The danger is “not a matter of slipping in and out of salvation by sinning” but “the very, very serious sin of apostasy” — a deliberate, conscious renunciation of Christ. Assurance one will persevere remains fully compatible with the ability to apostatise: the modal and de facto questions answer differently.

The single voice — how Craig works the node

This is a single-voice deep-dive: Craig is the only expert in the corpus here, the material drawn from three sequential Defenders lectures that build on one another. Not thin treatment by many, but layered treatment by one — tracked below as his three distinct moves.

William Lane Craig 3 transcripts
The modal scalpel & the “Molinism in disguise” charge
Move 1 · Steelman both Calvinist construals (Part 18)
  • Construal 1 — nominal Christians. Those in Hebrews 6 were “at best nominal Christians”; the “tasted” language implies sampling, not imbibing, so their apostasy continues an unregenerate state.
  • Construal 2 — warnings as a means of grace. The Spirit preserves the elect through means — word, prayer, preaching, the Lord’s Supper, the exercise of gifts — and these warnings are one such means. They “are not meant to show that it is a real possibility” of falling but are the mechanism that guarantees the elect will not.
  • The upshot a Calvinist must accept: visible apostates “were never really Christians to begin with.” Stated neutrally — but it sets the pastoral stakes the later lecture exploits.
Move 2 · The face-value criterion & cumulative case (Part 20)
  • The interpretive rule: take the warnings at face value — written to regenerate Christians (“holy brethren,” those who “are his house,” the “enlightened”), and entailing a real, not hypothetical, danger.
  • Perception-shift: once the lens is granted, “you suddenly find... the New Testament is filled with warnings like this. Maybe you never notice them because you never read them in this light.”
  • Cumulative stack: 2 Peter 2:20–22 (“the dog turns back to his own vomit”) and John 15:1–6 (branches that do not abide are burned) reinforce Hebrews.
  • Scope limit (pastorally load-bearing): the danger is “the very, very serious sin of apostasy,” not slipping in and out of salvation by daily sin.
Move 3 · The modal/de-facto reductio + Schreiner (Part 21)
  • The distinction: “will any elect person fall away?” (de facto) and “can an elect person fall away?” (modal) are “two very distinct questions” the Calvinist conflates.
  • The adultery illustration: a prophetic assurance one will never commit adultery does not entail one cannot. “He can commit adultery, but he won’t.” Assurance ≠ inability.
  • Foreknowledge-freedom: “Even if God knows that you will not apostatize, it does not follow that you cannot.” The fatalist inference is fallacious (see Q-17.1, Q-7.4).
  • The reductio: impossible-to-fall ⇒ warnings superfluous; warnings-as-means ⇒ the elect can fall. The view presupposes the modal possibility it denies.
  • The charge: ordaining warnings God knows the elect would freely heed is middle knowledge — “a hidden Molinist view disguised as a Calvinistic view.”
Hostile witness — Schreiner

The Calvinist Thomas Schreiner concedes “the warning passages are so strong” and that “If I were not convinced of unconditional election, I would surely be an Arminian.” A leading Reformed exegete grants the exegesis — leaving only unconditional election (which Craig rejects, Q-9.3.1 / Q-9.3.2) propping up eternal security.

Tentative suggestion — do not over-read

Craig floats, only as a question (“Could it be that apostasy is a form of blasphemy against the Holy Spirit?”), that apostasy may be the unpardonable sin — which would explain why “there no longer remains a sacrifice for sins.” Offered as a possibility, never a settled position.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Single-voice node: the “sources” are the positions Craig engages rather than rival experts, and every strongest response is his.

Objection 1

The warned were never really regenerate (nominal Christians)

Those in Hebrews 6 “tasted” the heavenly gift but never imbibed it. Their falling away is the continuation of an unregenerate state, not the loss of a salvation they never had.

Some Calvinists — Construal 1

Strongest response

The addressees are described as believers

The texts address “holy brethren... who share in a heavenly call,” people who “are his house” and were “enlightened” — and elsewhere “partakers of the Holy Spirit,” “sanctified by the blood of the covenant.” Read at face value, this is regenerate, not nominal, status.

Craig face-value criterion (Part 20)

Objection 2

The warnings are God’s means of securing the elect, not proof they can fall

The Spirit preserves the elect through means; these warnings are one such means. They are real and operative — but precisely the mechanism that guarantees the elect will not fall, not evidence that they could.

Some Calvinists — Construal 2

Strongest response

Reductio — the view presupposes apostasy is possible

Either falling is impossible (then the warnings are superfluous) or the warnings are the genuine means of perseverance (then falling must be possible). “What you’re saying is that the elect can fall away” — the very modal claim the position denies.

Craig dilemma + reductio (Part 21)

Objection 3

Assured he will never apostatise, yet may — isn’t that incoherent?

How can a believer be assured he will never apostatise and at the same time hold that he may commit apostasy? Schreiner’s own challenge to the conditional-security reading.

Thomas Schreiner’s challenge

Strongest response

Modal vs de facto — the adultery illustration

“Very easy to explain, because one is a modal question and the other is a de facto question.” God could assure a man he will never commit adultery; it would not follow that he cannot. He is able to; he simply won’t. Assurance is not inability.

Craig modal distinction (Part 21)

Objection 4

God foreknows the elect will persevere, so they cannot fall

If God infallibly knows the elect will not apostatise, then their apostasy is impossible — so the warnings cannot point to any genuine possibility of falling.

The fatalist inference behind the Calvinist reading

Strongest response

Foreknowledge does not entail necessity

The same logically fallacious move dealt with under divine omniscience: “Even if God knows that you will not apostatize, it does not follow that you cannot apostatize. You could apostatize, but you won’t.”

Craig omniscience-and-freedom (Q-17.1, Q-7.4)

Objection 5

Doesn’t conditional security mean you lose salvation every time you sin?

If genuine believers can fall away, then ordinary daily sin would put salvation in constant jeopardy — a pastorally intolerable, salvation-by-works treadmill.

Implicit pastoral worry / eternal-security rejoinder

Strongest response

Apostasy is not everyday sin

The warnings concern “the very, very serious sin of apostasy” — a deliberate, conscious renunciation of Christ — “not... slipping in and out of salvation by sinning” in one’s daily living.

Craig scope limitation (Part 20)

Objection 6

Eternal security without unconditional election (the Baptist position)

Many hold “once saved, always saved” while rejecting unconditional election — affirming eternal security on grounds other than the Calvinist decree.

Implicit, via Schreiner

Strongest response — hostile witness

A Calvinist says it “just doesn’t make sense”

Schreiner, quoted approvingly: the warning passages are too strong to ground security on anything but unconditional election; holding security while rejecting that election “just doesn’t make sense.” The exegesis forces the choice.

Craig / Schreiner hostile-witness concession

Persistent gaps in the corpus

Gap 1

The compatibilist rejoinder to “superfluous”

A Calvinist determinist can answer that means are not superfluous merely because the end is certain — God decreed the warnings as the causal means of perseverance, just as He decrees food as the means of nourishment.

Compatibilist Calvinism

Gap — unaddressed

The “why give warnings?” charge assumes libertarianism

Craig’s reductio presupposes a libertarian construal of the warnings’ efficacy and never engages the determinist alternative head-on. This is the single most serious gap — the obvious reply, left un-rebutted.

Aggregation gap

Gap 2

From “foreknown responses” to “therefore Molinism”

A compatibilist denies that God’s use of foreknown responses requires middle knowledge: on determinism God knows the elect’s responses because He causes them — no counterfactuals of libertarian freedom needed.

Compatibilist Calvinism

Gap — asserted, not defended

The crypto-Molinism inference is stipulated

Craig asserts that warnings-as-means is Molinism rather than defending the inference against the determinist construal, on which the same mechanism needs no middle knowledge at all.

Aggregation gap

Gap 3

The Reformed reading of John 15 and 2 Peter 2 addressees

The fruitless branches “in me” may be merely professing, externally-attached members; the 2 Peter “they” may be the chapter’s false teachers, whose initial status is itself contested.

Standard Reformed / eternal-security reading

Gap — assumed, not rebutted

Craig treats the addressees as genuine without argument

The cumulative case leans on reading these addressees as true believers, but the dispute over their status — the very hinge of the Reformed reply — is not engaged.

Aggregation gap

Gap 4

Apostasy = the unpardonable sin?

Craig suggests apostasy may be the blasphemy against the Holy Spirit Jesus called unforgivable — but the Gospel context locates that sin in attributing Jesus’ exorcisms to Beelzebul.

Gospel-context reading of Matt 12 / Mark 3

Gap — tentative only

Offered as a question, never defended

“Could it be...?” The identification is suggestive, not established, and is not defended against the exorcism-context reading of the unpardonable sin.

Aggregation gap

Gap 5

The de facto question is left open

Has any genuine believer actually apostatised? The question is pushed onto the disputed status of the biblical apostates — Judas, Demas, Hymenaeus, Alexander — without resolution.

Internal to the argument

Gap — by design

Modal point won; historical point declined

Craig establishes that the elect can fall while explicitly declining to settle whether any in fact has — so the case stops one step short of the concrete claim.
