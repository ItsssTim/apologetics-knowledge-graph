Is penal substitution coherent and just?

The question

Can the suffering of a sinless Christ even count as punishment — and if it can, is it not flatly unjust for a perfectly just God to punish an innocent in the place of the guilty?

This is the philosophical heart of §8.2. Penal substitution (PSA) — the claim that Christ bore the punishment due to sinners so that justice and mercy could meet at the cross (see Q-8.2.1) — is attacked on two fronts that are easily conflated and must be kept apart. The coherence objection says God could never condemn a sinless Christ, so any suffering inflicted on him would not be punishment at all, rendering PSA not false but incoherent. The justice objection — the ancient Socinian charge, the “biggie” — grants that Christ could be punished but insists it is an axiom of retributive justice that punishing the innocent is unjust.

Craig is the sole substantive voice here, and he treats it as a legal philosopher would. His defence, drawn from his monograph Atonement and the Death of Christ (2020), turns on a handful of load-bearing distinctions: definition vs justification, imputation, punished vs endured, vicarious liability and respondeat superior, and substitution vs representation. The decisive move — that divine-command theory frees God from the moral duties that bind us — Craig concedes pushes the objection back only “a notch”, leaving the real question (is retributive justice essential to God’s nature?) deferred to Q-8.2.13. That deferral is the headline gap of the whole unit.

The argument — a cumulative legal brief in four streams

This is a single-voice deep-dive, so the structure is not a clash of experts but Craig’s own multi-stage defence. He reduces each objection to a numbered syllogism, formulated charitably, then shows several premises are independently defeasible. The defence runs as four converging streams — a cumulative, defeater-deflecting case rather than a deductive proof.

Stream A · Coherence

  • Punishment = definition + justification; definition comes first
  • Reject expressivism — strict-liability crimes
  • Or formulate it right — censure of the act, not the person
  • Imputation — Christ is legally guilty (Turretin)
  • Weaker PSA — suffering that would have been punishment

Stream B · Representation

  • Pinch-hitter — pure substitute
  • Sports agent — pure representative
  • Corporate proxy — both at once (the payload)
  • Romans 5 Adam–Christ correlation
  • Romans 6 union — “what my representative did, I did”

Stream C · Retribution

  • Retributivism vs consequentialism
  • Consequentialism a 20th-century “disaster”
  • Divine judgment is eschatological (Rom 2:5)
  • Scripture grounds desert (Rom 1:32, Heb 10:29)
  • “Given with one hand, taken with the other”

Stream D · Justice answered

  • Six-step Socinian chain formalised
  • Voluntariness does not dissolve it
  • Vicarious liability / respondeat superior
  • Punished-vs-endured distinction
  • DCT — God has no moral duties to violate

How Craig builds the defence — the four streams

Stream A · Coherence Defenders Pt 18–19
The expressivism (Feinberg) objection — three vulnerable premises
Craig’s moves
  • A theory of punishment has two parts — a definition (what counts) and a justification (when permitted); the definitional question comes first.
  • Reads the Stanford Encyclopedia’s four conditions; the fourth is Feinberg’s expressivism — harsh treatment must express condemnation or censure to count as punishment.
  • Formalises the objection as three premises, then attacks all three. Premise 3: weaker PSA needs no punishment of Christ, only the suffering that would have been ours — “the argument falls apart.”
  • Premise 2, two ways: reject expressivism (strict-liability crimes are punished without expressing censure); or formulate it correctly — the fourth condition never requires censure of the person; Feinberg himself says it expresses disapproval of “what the criminal did.”
  • Premise 1: imputation (Turretin) makes a sinless Christ legally guilty; a student adds Romans 8:3 (“condemned sin in the flesh”).
  • Verdict: every premise is “eminently challengeable”; the objection “can be put to rest.”
Stream B · Representation Defenders Pt 12
Substitution vs representation — the analogy ladder
Craig’s moves
  • Refuses to let Christ be “just a mere substitute, like that pinch-hitter”; builds the distinction from three escalating analogies.
  • Pinch-hitter (pure substitute): bats instead of you, but never touches your batting average.
  • Sports agent (pure representative): acts on your behalf so his acts count as yours, but does not stand in your place.
  • Corporate proxy (both): signs in your stead and casts your vote as your own — the payload, because it fuses both roles.
  • Grounded in Romans 5 (Adam–Christ) and Romans 6 (union): “what my representative did, I did.” So God punishes me in my representative.
  • On the Trinity: only the Son has a crucified human nature and undergoes imputation; the Father administers justice; “the Father and the Spirit are not the sin-bearers.”
Stream C · Retribution Defenders Pt 18 & 20
Why divine justice is retributive, not consequentialist
Craig’s moves
  • Theories of punishment derive from theories of justice: retributivism (the guilty deserve it) vs consequentialism (justified by extrinsic benefits).
  • Consequentialism dominated 20th-century penology and “was a disaster” — women received longer sentences than men because thought more “reformable.”
  • A consequentialist could trivially justify punishing Christ (it saves the human race) — which is why it is the wrong framework for a just God.
  • Divine justice is eschatological (Rom 2:5): tares grow with wheat until the end; punishment then “could serve no other purpose than retribution.”
  • Scripture grounds desert — Romans 1:32 (“deserve to die”), Hebrews 10:29 (“deserved”).
The rhetorical hinge

What is given with one hand is taken with the other. The legal academy’s renaissance of retributivism vindicates the biblical picture — but “it is precisely retributive justice that holds that the innocent ought not to be punished, and hence for the retributivist this objection to penal substitution arises in spades.” Craig hedges: divine justice is retributive “in some significant measure”, not necessarily exclusively.

Stream D · Justice answered Defenders Pt 20 & symposium
The Socinian objection — and the divine-command move
Craig’s moves
  • Formalises the moral objection as a six-step chain (perfectly just → cannot punish an innocent → Christ was innocent → PSA false).
  • Voluntariness does not dissolve it — the nobility of a selfless act “wouldn’t do anything to annul the injustice.” (A candid concession.)
  • Imports the legal categories of vicarious liability and respondeat superior (a master is liable for his servant), plus the punished-vs-endured distinction, to show the law already countenances liability transfer.
  • The decisive move — divine-command theory. Our objective duties derive from God’s commands; God issues no commands to himself, so “he literally has no moral duties to fulfil.” Even if God forbids humans from punishing the innocent, “God himself is not so forbidden.”
  • Bearing our sin exalts rather than violates God’s gracious nature: “the Injustice objection becomes almost ludicrous.”
  • Burden-flip: to defeat the reply, critics must defeat DCT itself — against Adams, Alston, Quinn, Baggett, “good luck.”
Major concession — the headline gap

Craig knows the DCT move is not a knock-down. He concedes it pushes the objection back only “a notch”: even if God has no duties, the deeper question is whether retributive justice is essential to God’s nature — for if it is, God’s own being might require that the guilty, not an innocent substitute, be punished. He defers this to Q-8.2.13 and never resolves it here. The whole defence rests on an open metaethical question Craig himself flags.

Objections handled — master list

Objection 1

Coherence: a sinless Christ cannot be condemned, so it is not punishment

On expressivism, harsh treatment must express condemnation or censure to count as punishment. God could never censure the sinless Christ — so any suffering inflicted on him would not be punishment at all, and PSA is incoherent.

Joel Feinberg (expressivism); Stanford Encyclopedia “Retributive Justice”; Socinus

Strongest response

Three premises, all challengeable

(3) Weaker PSA needs no punishment of Christ, only the suffering that would have been ours. (2a) Strict-liability crimes are punished without expressing censure, so expressivism is false. (2b) Even granting it, the fourth condition never requires censure of the person — Feinberg himself says it expresses disapproval of “what the criminal did.” (1) Imputation makes Christ legally guilty (Turretin, Rom 8:3).

Craig definition/justification splitCraig the Feinberg “lacuna”

Objection 2

Justice: a perfectly just God cannot punish an innocent person

The Socinian “biggie.” It is an axiom of retributive justice that punishing the innocent is unjust. Christ was innocent. Therefore a perfectly just God could not punish him — and PSA is false.

Faustus Socinus; the “axiom of retributive justice”

Strongest response

Attack premises 1–2; ultimately DCT

Reduce to six premises. Deny premise 6 (weaker PSA), or attack premises 1–2: vicarious liability and respondeat superior show the law transfers liability; the punished-vs-endured distinction; and ultimately DCT — God has no moral duties, so cannot act unjustly in this.

Craig reduce-to-premisesCraig DCT defence (symposium)

Objection 3

Voluntariness is irrelevant — freely accepted punishment of the innocent is still unjust

It is no defence that Christ willingly took the punishment (“your will be done”); a noble, freely-chosen act cannot make an unjust punishment just.

Implicit in the Socinian objection; raised for the defence by a student

Concession — Craig agrees

Voluntariness does not annul the injustice

Craig grants the objector this point: “the nobility of his selfless act wouldn’t do anything to annul the injustice.” He therefore does not lean on voluntariness — the heavy lifting is shifted entirely to the metaethical (DCT) move.

Craig candid concession

Objection 4

You cannot transfer guilt — imputation is a “legal fiction”

Guilt is personal; it cannot be reckoned to someone who did not commit the act. Imputing our sin to a blameless Christ is mere forensic make-believe.

Socinian tradition; Oliver Crisp’s “forensic fiction” worry (symposium)

Strongest response

A recognised legal category, not a fiction

Vicarious liability and respondeat superior show the law already transfers liability between persons; imputation is of a piece with that. Caveat: Crisp’s fuller Romans-5 metaphysical-realism critique was cut short in session — logged as a gap, not answered.

Craig vicarious-liability analogy

Objection 5

Substitute, not representative — PSA severs me from Christ’s act

If Christ merely stood in for me, his death is not mine in any meaningful sense — he is a bare stand-in, and the benefit’s transfer to me is left unexplained.

Pastoral / exegetical worry

Strongest response

The pinch-hitter / agent / proxy ladder

Christ is both substitute and representative. A corporate proxy stands in for you and casts your vote as your own. Grounded in Romans 5 (Adam) and Romans 6 (union): “what my representative did, I did.”

Craig analogy ladder (Rom 5–6)

Objection 6

Consequentialism makes it cheap — punishing Christ just saves the human race

If punishment is justified by good consequences, then God’s punishing the innocent Christ is trivially justified — you cannot think of a better consequence than saving everyone.

Utilitarian framing

Strongest response

Divine justice is not consequentialist

Concede a consequentialist could justify it — but God’s judgment is eschatological and desert-based (Rom 1:32, Heb 10:29), so it is retributive, and the cheap consequentialist exit simply does not apply to a just God.

Craig grant-then-disqualify

Objection 7

Hell is pointless — “you don’t come out a better person”

Eternal punishment reforms no one and deters no one once history is over, so it is gratuitous — a cruelty with no purpose.

Atheist (student Cody)

Strongest response

Diagnose the buried consequentialist premise

The charge presupposes punishment must be instrumental. On a retributive view the “pointlessness” objection never arises: punishment is deserved, not a means to an end. The objector has absorbed the consequentialism dominant until the 1970s.

Craig hidden-premise diagnosis

Objection 8

Hell is a natural consequence of sin, not a punishment

Death and separation simply follow from rejecting God, the way ill health follows abuse of the body. No one need be punished, so no penal substitute is required.

Students Taiwan, T-one (universalist-leaning)

Strongest response

The NT calls them punishments — but it can be both

Hebrews 10:29 explicitly speaks of punishment that will be “deserved”, which is retributive. Craig concedes it “doesn’t need to be an either/or”: death and hell can be both consequences of sin and punishments for it.

Craig consequence-and-punishment

Objection 9

God upholds justice by infusing righteousness, not by punishing

God establishes justice by making the wicked righteous (demonstrating or infusing right), not by inflicting penalties — so Christ was “not punished”, and the penal model is unnecessary.

Student Taiwan (infusion / demonstration view)

Partial response

That just is denying premise 6 — but does it satisfy retributive justice?

The move is permissible (it corresponds to the weaker-PSA escape), but it remains to be shown that it satisfies God’s retributive justice — “the demands of God’s retributive justice need to be satisfied as well, and I think that that calls for a penal substitute.”

Craig retributive-demand reply

Objection 10

Divine command theory is widely disputed

Grounding the defence in DCT is no help, because DCT is itself a contested minority metaethic — you cannot rescue PSA by leaning on a theory most philosophers reject.

Joshua Thurow (symposium, reply 2.1)

Strongest response

Burden-flip — “good luck”

Many eminent ethicists defend DCT — Adams, Alston, Quinn, Baggett. To defeat the response, critics must first defeat DCT itself: “let him have a go at … many other eminent ethicists. Good luck.” The cost of the objection is raised, not the case proven.

Craig appeal to authority (symposium)

Objection 11

A plausible DCT is restrained by God’s essential goodness

The best versions of DCT admit God’s commands are constrained by his essential goodness — and that goodness might itself forbid punishing the innocent.

Joshua Thurow (symposium)

Strongest response

Agreed — but goodness exalts, not forbids

“I concur wholeheartedly”, but Thurow shows nothing to make God’s essential goodness inconsistent with Christ being a penal substitute. On the contrary, condescending to bear our sin exalts that goodness.

Craig goodness-exalted reply

Objection 12

DCT eliminates justice as the reason for the atonement

If whatever God wills is just, then justice cannot be why God chose the cross — any means would have been equally just, so the crucifixion looks arbitrary.

Joshua Thurow (symposium)

Strongest response

Acceptation + the display of holiness and love

Invoke acceptation (Scotus): Christ’s death has unlimited value, more than proportionate to human sin. Why crucifixion specifically is answered by Abelard / Grotius — it exhibits, in an unparalleled manner, both the holiness and the love of God.

Craig Scotus / Abelard / Grotius

Persistent gaps in the corpus

Gap 1 — the headline gap

Is retributive justice essential to God’s nature?

The whole defence culminates in DCT: God has no moral duties, so cannot act unjustly in punishing Christ. But if retributive justice is essential to God (not merely commanded of us), God’s own nature might require that the guilty be punished — not an innocent substitute.

The deeper objection behind the Socinian charge

Gap — deferred by Craig himself

DCT only pushes it back “a notch”

Craig openly concedes the DCT move is not decisive and that this is the real question — then defers it to Q-8.2.13. The entire edifice rests on an unresolved metaethical question. This is the single most important datum on the page.

Aggregation gap

Gap 2

Thurow’s “roadblock to reconciliation” argument

The perceived moral wrongness of PSA could itself impede the relational aim of the atonement — reconciliation cannot proceed through a mechanism that strikes the conscience as unjust.

Joshua Thurow (symposium, Part 2)

Gap — dismissed, not engaged

“Utterly implausible”, but unanswered

Craig dismisses the worry as “utterly implausible” but never engages the structural point — that a doctrine’s felt injustice could frustrate the very reconciliation it aims at.

Aggregation gap

Gap 3

Crisp’s Romans-5 metaphysical-realism critique

Craig’s forensic account fails to take seriously the metaphysical realism of Pauline Adam-Christology (Rom 5:12–19) — real participation in Christ, not merely a legal reckoning.

Oliver Crisp (symposium paper, read by Andrew Torrance)

Gap — cut short in session

Delivered only in the abstract

Crisp’s section on Romans 5 was cut off live (“his time is out”), so the corpus carries the charge but not Craig’s rebuttal. The published paper presumably contains the full argument.

Aggregation gap

Gap 4

“Legal fiction” deflected, not dissolved

Naming human-law categories (vicarious liability, respondeat superior, imputation) shows the law transfers liability — but a critic can reply these are conventional and do not establish that real moral guilt transfers.

Implicit in the forensic-fiction critique

Gap — category asserted, not grounded

Existence vs moral legitimacy

The reply establishes that such categories exist in law; it does not ground their moral legitimacy when transposed onto divine justice. The objection survives one level down.

Aggregation gap

Gap 5

Voluntariness conceded away

Many lay defenders treat “Christ chose it” as the obvious answer to the justice objection — but Craig grants it does nothing to annul the injustice.

The popular consent-based defence

Gap — removes the intuitive reply

All weight on DCT

By conceding voluntariness, Craig removes the answer most people reach for first and rests the entire defence on the contested DCT move — which (per Gap 1) he admits is not decisive.
