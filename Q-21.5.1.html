The question

What is the right way to prepare for, and conduct yourself in, a public debate — and what should you actually be trying to achieve?

This is not a content Q-node but a practice Q-node: it concerns method, posture, and purpose rather than any particular argument. It sits near the strategic centre of §21, because every other argument in the corpus is delivered through some encounter — a debate, a moderated discussion, a long-form podcast, a street conversation — and the method governs whether the argument lands.

The corpus is small but coherent: three experts describe different sub-genres of the same activity and arrive at convergent commitments — prepare asymmetrically, serve the audience rather than the opponent, and prefer formats that reward truth over speed of thought. The tension underneath is between the confrontational debate Lennox now rejects and its alternatives.

The convergent case — four method strands

This is not a syllogism. It is a cumulative practitioner consensus assembled from four converging streams. No single expert owns all four; each contributes one or two, and the shared spine across all of them is listener autonomy — present the case as well as you can and trust the audience to judge.

Strand A · Purpose

  • Audience over opponent
  • The long-tail conversion effect
  • “Trust people to make up their own mind”
  • Not converting the opponent ≠ failure

Strand B · Format

  • Confrontation has serious defects
  • Moderated discussion over debate
  • Boxing-match analogy (O’Connor)
  • Tests speed of thought, not truth

Strand C · Preparation

  • Over-prepare asymmetrically
  • Mine the host’s prior content
  • Follow the host’s other interests
  • Counting the cost (Luke 14)

Strand D · Method on stage

  • Say what you came to say
  • Predict-and-test framing
  • Internal critique of the opponent
  • Differential evidence (McGrew)

How each expert deploys it

John Lennox2 transcripts
The convert from confrontation — purpose & format
Signature moves
  • “Say what you came to say.” An unnamed senior newspaper writer’s rule: make sure that when the debate is finished you have said what you came to say — do not simply be reactive.
  • Audience over opponent. The metric is the long-tail effect — people who write years later, the research scientist who began her journey on a single evening — not the on-stage scoreline.
  • Failing to convert the opponent is not failure: “there was a man called Saul of Tarsus, after all.”
  • Confrontation has serious defects: it cuts corners, does not really listen, and rewards trying-to-win over inquiry.
  • Resolution: refuse debate, insist on moderated discussion (names Justin Brierley as a good moderator).
Live demonstration

Near the end of the Oxford Union interview Lennox confesses he moderated his own pressure on the agnostic interviewer — “I decided not to push you on certain things beyond limits.” He performs the gentleness he is advocating in the same breath he advocates it.

Wes Huff1 transcript
Asymmetric over-preparation for long-form
The five-step method
  • Long-form has no question list. “The conversation has the ability to go in absolutely any direction” — Rogan to cosmology, Schulz to the unevangelised, Bartlett to whether animals have souls.
  • Mine the host. Used AI tools to ingest five years of Rogan content, counted Christianity / Jesus / Bible mentions, isolated repeated topics, and rehearsed his own engagement.
  • Follow the host’s interests outward. Knowing Joe was intrigued by Genghis Khan, he researched the earliest biographies — when and by whom.
  • Unused prep is still right prep. He used essentially none of it on Rogan and regrets none of the late nights.
  • Scriptural warrant: the prudent ant, the prudent who see danger and shelter, and Jesus on counting the cost (Luke 14:28–32).
Caveat — scalability

The method presupposes time, AI tooling, and scholarly background the everyday Christian-in-conversation does not have. Huff does not address how it degrades for a layperson with an hour and no tools.

Jonathan McLatchie2 transcripts
Method as a weapon — predict-and-test & internal critique
Distinctive moves
  • Predict-and-test. “If Jesus preached Islam, what should we expect to see? If Jesus preached Christianity, what should we expect to see?” Then derives testable predictions for each hypothesis.
  • Rhetorically devastating because the opponent’s open admission — “I select the verses that agree with the Quran” — becomes the live exhibit of the circularity.
  • Closing refrain: “you have to explain the origins of the disciples’ beliefs.”
  • Internal critique (via Timothy McGrew). Dillahunty’s “I don’t know what would convince me” is a category error: on a k-criterion definition, cumulative cases become incoherent.
  • Dillahunty lacks the concept of differential evidence — evidence that points toward without establishing — so he keeps moving the goalposts.
Attribution note

The on-stage analysis in the 2020 review is Timothy McGrew’s, which McLatchie platforms and frames. The “differential evidence” point — the single most transferable idea in this node — belongs to McGrew.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

“You didn’t convert your opponent — so the debate failed.”

If Dawkins walks away an atheist, the whole exercise was pointless.

Popular audience challenge to Lennox

Strongest response

The opponent is not the target

The metric is the long-tail audience effect — the people who write years later, the research scientist who began her journey that evening. And conversion of the opponent is not even impossible: “there was a man called Saul of Tarsus, after all.”

Lennox audience-over-opponent metric

Objection 2

“If you don’t use the preparation, the preparation was wasted.”

Hours of research that never surface in the conversation are hours thrown away.

Wes Huff, posed to himself

Strongest response

Flip the asymmetry

Being under-prepared when something does surface is far worse than being over-prepared and using nothing. “It’s always better to be over prepared and use nothing than to be under prepared and needing to have something.”

Huff asymmetric-cost argument

Objection 3

“Confrontational debate is the proper test — it shows who is right.”

The adversarial format puts both cases under maximum pressure; the better case wins.

The debate-circuit culture Lennox left

Strongest response

It tests speed of thought, not truth

Confrontation cuts corners and rewards trying-to-win over listening. The boxing-match analogy: it shows the better boxer, not the better fighter, and “doesn’t tell you what the truth is.” Switch to moderated discussion.

Lennox confrontation-defects critiqueO’Connor boxing-match analogy

Objection 4

“I’ll only accept the verses that agree with my prior framework.”

Karim openly states he keeps the gospel verses that align with the Quran and discards the rest — and claims Christians do exactly the same.

Ayoob Karim (offered as method)

Strongest response

Predict-and-test makes the admission self-defeating

The frame converts the opponent’s hermeneutic into a live exhibit of circularity: the open admission to selecting agreeable verses is the demonstration that the method begs the question.

McLatchie predict-and-test frame

Objection 5

“I don’t know what evidence would convince me — nobody could specify the evidence for black holes in advance either.”

An open-ended refusal to name a threshold, defended by analogy to scientific discovery.

Matt Dillahunty

Strong response — but the corpus does not arm you to pre-empt it

A category error about evidence

On a k-criterion definition, cumulative cases become incoherent (a conjunction of non-evidence becoming evidence). Dillahunty lacks the concept of differential evidence, so he cannot hear a cumulative case and keeps moving the goalposts; unspecified “sufficient evidence” is unfalsifiable personal incredulity dressed as argument.

McGrew platformed by McLatchieno pre-emption strategy given

Persistent gaps

Gap 1

Scalability of Huff’s method

Over-preparation presupposes time, AI tooling, and scholarly background the everyday Christian-in-conversation lacks. The pastoral-apologetics context (Q-21.3.x) is unaddressed.

Flagged in the raw extraction

Gap 2

When discussion is the wrong format

Lennox abandons confrontational debate wholesale, but never asks whether some opponents or audiences are reachable only adversarially — or whether refusing to debate cedes ground to opponents who debate anyway.

Format choice argued one-directionally

Gap 3

The audience-over-opponent metric is unfalsifiable

Lennox’s evidence is anecdotal long-tail correspondence. The corpus offers no way to distinguish a debate that moved the audience from one that produced a few selection-biased letters — the same unspecified-criterion problem McGrew levels at Dillahunty, here on the apologist’s own side.

Unexamined

Gaps 4 & 5

Uncooperative and bad-faith opponents

Predict-and-test works against an opponent who admits the circularity (Karim); the corpus is silent on the opponent who refuses the frame. And the Dillahunty review diagnoses slipperiness only after the fact — nothing tells you how to prepare for, or conduct yourself against, an opponent whose method is evasion.

No preparation strategy for evasive opponents
