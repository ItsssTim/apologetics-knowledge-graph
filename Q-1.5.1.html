The question

Can a person’s inner experience — of God, of love, of consciousness itself — count as a rational starting point for belief in God?

The cosmological and design arguments work outward from the world; this Q-node works inward from the experiencing subject. Two distinct claims live here. The metaphysical claim: conscious, valuing, purposive experience is the most certain thing we have and the hardest to reduce to matter, so reality is at bottom mental. The epistemological claim: belief grounded in experience of God can be properly basic — rational without an argument, like belief in the external world or other minds.

The strategic difficulty is that the same move that makes experience a warrant for the believer makes it look like an unshareable feeling to the outsider. Every critic in the corpus presses that seam: parity, counter-testimony, and subjective indistinguishability.

The argument structure — multi-strand, not syllogistic

Unlike the Kalam, this is a cumulative case built from four converging streams. No expert deploys all four; each stakes out a different stream, and they reinforce one another. The case is strongest when the de jure / de facto distinction is kept clean — experience grounds the believer’s warrant; argument is for showing others.

Stream A · Consciousness

  • Idealism over materialism
  • Subjective experience is epistemically primary
  • Reality is bigger than physics
  • Value & purpose are basic (Ward / Knechtle)

Stream B · Properly basic

  • Self-authenticating witness of the Spirit
  • Parity with external world / other minds
  • Brain-in-a-vat licence
  • De jure vs de facto (Craig)

Stream C · Transformation

  • Faith comes by experience, not argument
  • Stan Ford the boxer-preacher
  • The experience of love
  • “Meeting people where they are at” (Lennox / Knechtle)

Stream D · Defeater-handling

  • Mormon parity is not a defeater
  • Hallucination-parity reply
  • “Super naturalist”: not all supernatural is from God
  • Inner witness vs cumulative case (McLatchie / Huff)

How each expert deploys it

William Lane Craig4 transcripts
The architect of properly basic belief
Signature delivery
  • Belief in God is properly basic — foundational, not inferred, grounded in experience like belief in the external world, the past, and other minds.
  • Parity with perception: “there’s no way to get outside your sensory data… in the absence of any defeater you simply accept them.”
  • Self-authenticating experience — the falling-in-love analogy: “you just know that you’re in love.”
  • Against Lessing’s ditch, reaches for Kierkegaard: every generation is contemporaneous with the eyewitnesses through the Spirit.
Load-bearing distinction

The de jure / de facto split: the witness of the Spirit grounds the believer’s own warrant and outranks argument for the believer; but to show others, “we appeal to argument and evidence… these are deductive arguments,” not retrospective evidentialism. Hitchens reads his Reasonable Faith text aloud; Craig affirms it candidly.

Cliffe & Stuart Knechtle3 transcripts
Experience as evidence — love & the author of life
Signature moves
  • Cliffe’s argument from love: love is “a very real, intangible, non-material value… built on trust,” not just “a chemical reaction.” “One of my pieces of evidence that God exists is this experience of love.”
  • Distinguishes “different kinds of evidence” — scientific vs historical / experiential — against the demand for objective verifiability.
  • Stuart’s Shakespeare–Hamlet analogy: God “is the author of life” who entered his own work as Jesus Christ.
  • Faith is “counting up the evidence and putting your trust in… a person” — “I needed the evidence and then the trusting relationship.”
Known weakness

Cliffe asserts love is “non-material” without engaging naturalist accounts (pair-bonding, oxytocin, kin selection), and jumps from “love is real” to “Jesus claimed God loves you” — which Aron Ra flags as a non-sequitur.

Keith Wardvia Cliffe Knechtle
Idealism over materialism
Distinctive moves
  • Idealism — “consciousness, value, and purpose are the basic reality” — is more defensible than Dennett’s materialism.
  • Subjective experience is more certain than matter as described by physics: “that’s where our knowledge begins from.”
  • Physics describes a world with “no consciousness… no value… no purpose” — so reality exceeds physics; the rest is “a spiritual conscious valuable purposive world.”
  • Genealogical flank: most philosophers across history accepted a spiritual basis to reality; Dawkins calls theism delusion only because he “knows nothing about philosophy.”
John Lennox1 transcript
Faith comes by experience, not argument
Distinctive moves
  • “Most people don’t believe in God because of an argument… the belief itself came through some kind of experience.”
  • Turns the high intellectual bar against itself: a God reachable only by mastering philosophy would not be “worth believing in.”
  • Stan Ford — illiterate ex-fairground boxer turned preacher who finished “the entire parable” of the sheep and goats from memory against a cynical theology student.
  • Pairs Ford (experiential route) with Anthony Flew and McGrath (intellectual route): “meeting people where they’re at… is one of the biggest evidences for God.”
Jonathan McLatchie1 transcript
The parity-defeater specialist
Distinctive moves
  • Presses the Mormon parity objection — as a Christian: how to distinguish the inner witness from the “burning in the bosom” objectively?
  • A known-false experience “raises the probability that such an experience can be produced by naturalistic means.”
  • Does not think mystical inner-witness is normative: “I certainly don’t have any such experience… and I know plenty of christians who would say the same.”
  • Conflation diagnosis: an “inarticulate sense of the power of the whole case” is really “a rational response to a cumulative case argument,” mistaken for a mystical witness.
Best defence (Rauser, same transcript)

Both Mormon and Christian could have prima facie warrant; the difference is defeaters (Joseph Smith’s huckstery, patristic history, mass apostasy, materialist incoherence). And hallucination-parity: one person’s false horse-sighting “does not provide sufficient evidence to undercut the other person’s prima facie justification.”

Wes Huff1 transcript
Taking non-Christian experience seriously
Distinctive moves
  • “I’m a super naturalist… there’s a realm beyond what we can see” — but “there are powers… that are not from God.”
  • Makes experience common ground, not a conclusion: truth is “that which corresponds to logic and reality” — so what does the experience mean for how you live?
  • Own testimony disarms naivety: healed from paraplegia, “a very powerful supernatural experience — and yet later in my life I still had doubts.”
  • Theological cap: belief is necessary but not sufficient — “you can believe in God and not be saved… the demons believe.” Closer: “if Christianity were true would you believe it?”

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

Love / experience is just a biological reaction

Love is pair-bonding, oxytocin, kin-selection altruism — a chemical and evolutionary process, nothing more.

Aron Ra (vs Cliffe Knechtle)

Strongest response

Love is relational and non-material

Love is “an intangible, non-material value… built on trust, on caring for somebody” — resisting pure biological reduction. But the standard naturalist accounts are not engaged.

Cliffe Knechtle asserts but does not argue it

Objection 2

Subjective evidence is only evidence to you

“Subjective evidence is only evidence to you, not to me. It has to be objectively verifiable facts.”

Aron Ra; Keith Parsons

Strongest response

Different kinds of evidence; de facto switch

There are distinct evidence categories — scientific vs historical / experiential. Craig: experience is the de jure warrant for self; when showing others you switch to deductive argument.

Cliffe Knechtle evidence-pluralismCraig de jure / de facto split

Objection 3

How do you tell the Holy Spirit from a warm fuzzy feeling?

“How do I know that’s not just some psychologically caused warm fuzzy feeling? How do I tell the difference between the Holy Spirit acting and a warm fuzzy feeling?”

Keith Parsons (vs Craig)

Partial response — no outsider criterion

Self-authentication + perception-parity

Craig: the experience is self-authenticating (“you just know”); and no one can step outside their sense data either. But this gives the outsider no criterion — the indistinguishability challenge stands.

Craig self-authenticationGap: no shareable test

Objection 4

Mormon “burning in the bosom” parity

A Mormon’s inner witness has the same structure as the Christian’s. A known-false experience raises the probability such experiences are naturalistically produced.

McLatchie (vs Rauser)

Strongest response

Defeaters, not the bare experience

Rauser: both can be prima facie warranted; the difference is defeaters (Joseph Smith’s huckstery, patristic history, mass-apostasy claims, materialist incoherence). Hallucination-parity: one false sighting does not undercut another’s prima facie justification.

Rauser defeater taxonomyMcLatchie presses the objection

Objection 5

Ghost / leprechaun / glossolalia parity

“Why should I take that more seriously than people’s experience of ghosts, demons, leprechauns?” Speaking in tongues is “purely a psychological phenomenon… I observed it at a Beatles concert.”

Keith Parsons (vs Craig)

Strongest response

Demarcate by defeaters and source

Rauser (transferred): defeater-handling does the work. Huff: supernatural experience ≠ experience of God; some sources are not God. Parsons’s specific cases get no direct reply from Craig.

Rauser defeater principleHuff source-demarcation

Objection 6

I sought God honestly and did not find

“Jesus says seek and ye shall find. Well, I sought, I didn’t find, so Jesus is wrong.” Many people search openly and earnestly and meet only silence.

Keith Parsons (vs Craig)

Gap — no clean answer

Counter-testimony left standing

Craig can only deny or reassert. This is the argument from non-resistant non-belief in experiential form. Lennox’s “meeting people where they are at” deepens the problem rather than resolving it.

Aggregation gap

Objection 7

Religious experience = “I felt good” — that’s William James

“You’ve had a revelation and it’s making you feel good” — James’s Varieties of Religious Experience as a byword for self-soothing irrationality.

Jerry Coyne / Sam Harris

Strongest response

Internal critique — an utter mischaracterisation

Craig: James attacked the Cliffordian evidentialism Coyne and Harris are reviving. The Will to Believe defends rational belief under a genuine, forced, momentous option where evidence is balanced.

Craig defends James against the caricature

Objection 8

This is just fideism — Spirit ranked over evidence

Hitchens reads Craig’s own text: “should conflict arise between the witness of the holy spirit… and beliefs based on argument… it is the former which must take precedence.” “That’s not evidentialism, that’s just faith.”

Christopher Hitchens (vs Craig)

Strongest response

Distinguish knowing from showing

Craig affirms the text but separates de jure (knowledge via the Spirit) from de facto (showing others): “this isn’t retrospective evidentialism, these are deductive arguments.”

Craig de jure / de facto distinction

Objection 9

“The Spirit tells me” would license every religion

“To me that is meaningless… it’s like saying there is only one God and Allah is His Messenger. It’s gibberish to me.” The same structure licenses a Muslim’s witness of Allah.

Christopher Hitchens (vs Craig)

Gap — unanswered

No criterion supplied

Craig gives no principled way to distinguish his self-authenticating witness from a Muslim’s analogous witness. Rauser’s defeater-taxonomy is the nearest tool but is deployed only against Mormonism, not Islam.

Aggregation gap

Objection 10

If non-Christians experience the supernatural, all religions are valid

A Mormon, Muslim, or JW friend reports answered prayer or a powerful supernatural experience — doesn’t that vindicate their religion?

Implicit (Huff’s questioner)

Partial response

Supernatural ≠ experience of God

Huff: some supernatural sources are not God; even genuine experience of God is not saving knowledge (“the demons believe”). But the demonic-vs-of-God demarcation (Q-22.4) is only gestured at.

Huff super-naturalist frameGap: no demarcation

Objection 11

Consciousness is a by-product of neurons

A human being is “just neurons and matter in the brain and consciousness is a byproduct” that “plays no important part in life.”

Daniel Dennett (via Ward)

Strongest response

Subjective experience is more certain than matter

Ward: we are most certain of conscious experience and least sure of matter as physics describes it; a world “with no consciousness… no value… no purpose” cannot be the whole of reality. Asserted as inference to best explanation, not argued against Dennett’s account.

Ward idealism / consciousness-first
