Why doesn’t God make his existence more obvious?

The problem of divine hiddenness — where the strongest articulations come from the unbelievers, and the corpus’s most honest voice concedes it is unsolved. Four experts, seven substantive treatments.

The question

If God exists and wants a relationship with us, why does he leave so much room for doubt — why doesn’t he simply show up undeniably?

In the popular corpus this arrives in two registers. The philosophical register is J.L. Schellenberg’s argument from non-resistant non-belief: a perfectly loving God would leave no honest seeker in doubt; honest seekers plainly exist; therefore the loving God does not. The existential register is the felt complaint of the deconvert and the activist — “just come down and say hello,” “show up in a way that’s hard for us to deny.”

What makes this Q-node distinctive is that the strongest articulations come from the unbelievers, and the defenders are visibly uneven: Lennox and Craig engage it at full strength; the Knechtles engage it pastorally and repeatedly decline the philosophical core.

The convergent argument — the calibrated-revelation defence

The four defenders converge on one master move: God has deliberately calibrated the evidence to be sufficient for the willing seeker but resistible by the closed heart, because coercive proof would destroy the very thing God wants — freely given love. This is the Pascalian “enough light / enough obscurity” frame.

Premise 1

God’s overarching aim is loving relationship, not bare assent to a proposition.

Observed. Baker-Hytch: “God’s overarching aim in terms of human beings is for relationship with humans.” Craig: knowledge of God is “personal / relational knowledge, not impersonal data” — sui generis, conditioned by moral and spiritual disposition.

Premise 2

Coercive, doubt-eliminating evidence would override free will and produce fear or self-interest, not love.

Observed. Knechtle: demanding proof is “playing serious manipulative games.” Baker-Hytch (Kierkegaard’s king-and-maiden): overwhelming evidence creates a power-imbalance that coerces a response “out of fear” rather than love.

Premise 3

So God calibrates revelation: visible to those who seek with an open heart, obscure to those with a contrary disposition.

Observed. Stuart Knechtle (Pascal): “revealed himself enough for those who truly seek him and not enough for those who want to turn over their lives to him.” Craig quotes the Pensées “enough light … enough obscurity” passage verbatim.

Premise 4

Revelation is in fact widely available — in nature, conscience, the incarnation, and contemporary phenomena.

Observed. Knechtle: nature, conscience, and “thousands … having dreams of Jesus.” Stevenson: Middle-Eastern dreams and visions. Cliffe: the incarnation is God showing up — “he became a Jewish man 2,000 years ago.”

Conclusion

Perceived hiddenness is partly false (general revelation exists) and partly intentional. The live question is not whether the evidence coerces but whether it is sufficient to make belief rational and trust warranted.

The reframe. Craig: “Is there enough evidence for faith to be rational? … yes, there certainly is.” Lennox pivots from “why doesn’t God show up?” to “is there evidence to trust him?” The argument is abductive and theodicy-shaped — its load-bearing premise (a loving God values uncoerced love above universal belief) is precisely what Schellenberg denies, and the corpus does not adequately defend it against him.

How each expert treats it

John Lennox 1 transcript
The sympathetic concession — dominant voice
Signature moves
  • The only defender to engage the non-resistant non-believer as a real category. To O’Connor’s steelman he answers: “I share that question with you. I’ll be totally open.”
  • Names “a real mystery” and “a residual problem here, Alex. I’m with you on that” — refuses to score points off a sympathetic interviewer.
  • Vulnerability-to-counter-evidence as a model: the Why I Am Not a Christian anecdote against himself — buying Russell’s book strengthened his faith.
  • Grants the honest-rejecter category (Thomas Nagel: “I don’t really want there to be a God”).
  • Some “intellectual” rejection is wounding: the Siberian-conference politician’s irrational tirade masked abuse by “professing Christian clerics.”
  • James Tour as a live counter-example — a standing weekly online resurrection challenge.
  • The criterion-shift: Christianity “doesn’t solve it like a question to be solved, but gives us a way in to cope with it.”
Honest limit

Answers anecdotally and pastorally; never engages Schellenberg’s premises as a formal argument, and never addresses the sincere seeker who never even encounters a James Tour. He concedes this is “the hard objection.”

William Lane Craig 1 transcript
Apatheism diagnosis + Pascal calibration
Signature delivery
  • Opens with Russell’s judgment-day line: “Not enough evidence, God. Not enough evidence.”
  • Apatheism diagnostic: most great atheists “lost their faith when they were around 11 or 12 … reject Christianity based on the objections of a 12-year-old.”
  • Decodes the complaint: “not enough evidence” really means “not enough to coerce me out of my indifference … the evidence isn’t going to grab me by the lapels.”
  • Knowledge of God is sui generis: “a spiritually indifferent person cannot know God,” though he may master physics or theology.
  • Biblical warrant: Jeremiah 29:13; Matt 7:7 — finding conditioned on whole-hearted seeking.
  • Pascal verbatim, then the reframe: the live question is whether belief is rational, not whether the evidence coerces.
Cliffe & Stuart Knechtle 4 transcripts
The relational reductio — most-tested, weakest
Signature deployment
  • “Fly to New York” reductio (Cliffe’s signature): demanding on-call manifestation is “playing a game with your head … manipulative … Don’t treat God that way.”
  • Incarnation as God showing up: “he became a Jewish man 2,000 years ago.” Stuart: the cross shows love at cost.
  • Pascal probation-theology (Stuart) + a marriage analogy: “we eventually have to take that step to fully know.”
  • Parenting-reversal (Cliffe): loving parents routinely have children who deny they were loved — the failure-mode is not always the parent’s.
  • “Even the demons believe” (Stuart): compelling proof yields fear, not saving trust.
Recurrent weakness

Across all four hostile encounters the interlocutor states he is non-resistant (“I would prefer that a loving powerful God did exist” — Steingard), yet the Knechtles answer the resistant objector. Steingard’s sharpest rejoinder — the marriage analogy fails because “nowhere is there any doubt that your wife exists” — goes unanswered.

Jonathan McLatchie 1 transcript (panel)
Baker-Hytch’s Kierkegaard parable
Distinctive moves
  • Substantive work by panellists Max Baker-Hytch and Sarah Stevenson, not McLatchie himself.
  • Kierkegaard’s king-and-maiden (signature analogy): a king cannot win genuine love “in the clear” without coercing — so he disguises himself as a peasant.
  • Trust > certainty: “what’s key for relationship isn’t certainty so much as trust” — meaningful commitment involves risk.
  • Stevenson reframes the asymmetry: “God loves everyone and loves everyone equally … we box God’s ability,” citing cross-cultural Jesus-dreams.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

Non-resistant non-belief (Schellenberg)

An honest seeker — “no boiling rage, no hatred, no resistance” — simply cannot believe. This is incompatible with a God who loves and wants to be known by all.

Alex O’Connor; implicitly Steingard, Smalley, Apostate Prophet

Concession + reframe — not a solution

Granted as “the hard objection,” then deflected

Lennox concedes the residual mystery and shifts to “is there evidence to trust?” Craig and Baker-Hytch argue a loving God values uncoerced love above universal belief, so calibrates evidence. No one rebuts the formal premises.

Objection 2

“Just come down and say hello”

An omnipotent God could simply appear at the lake and demonstrate his existence. That he doesn’t is itself evidence he doesn’t exist.

Mike Newdow

Strongest response

Relational reductio + incarnation

Demanding on-call manifestation is manipulative, not loving — “don’t treat God that way.” And God did show up: he entered history as Jesus, at the cost of the cross. The gospels are the access.

Objection 3

The modern-media argument

The internet makes a universal, public, verifiable miracle trivially easy for an omnipotent being — and it costs God nothing. Christian miracles are only ever private, personal, vague.

Apostate Prophet (Aaron Adair)

Weak — misses the framing

Curated highlights + “demons believe”

The Bible is “the top 10 nominees,” not a daily catalog; and compelling proof yields fear, not saving trust (“even the demons believe and shudder”). But Apostate Prophet is explicitly non-resistant, and the reply answers the resistant objector instead.

Stuart Knechtle highlights + demonsDoes not meet non-resistant framing

Objection 4

The parenting analogy

A present father’s adult child would not doubt he exists. If God’s children doubt him, the hiddenness is “on” the parent — on God.

Jon Steingard

Strongest response

Reverse the analogy

Loving parents routinely have children who reject them and say “you never really loved me.” The failure-mode in a parent-child relationship is not always the parent’s — so perceived hiddenness need not be God’s fault.

Cliffe Knechtle rebellious-children reversal

Objection 5

Psychic-parity / verifiable test

God, like a psychic who backpedalled when asked to read a word on a notepad, “has every opportunity in the world to show himself to me in a way that I would believe” — and never does.

David C. Smalley

Gap — not engaged

Knechtle does not address it

Knechtle offers his own hospital testimony in the Q&A but never engages the parity move or the verifiable-test challenge. The load-bearing rhetorical analogy goes unanswered.

Objection 6

Nature gives generic spirituality, not Christ

A feeling of connectedness in nature “could be eastern pantheism … there’s nothing about that feeling that says ‘hi, I’m Jesus, Christianity is true.’”

Jon Steingard

Partial — genericity stands

Cross-cultural Jesus-dreams

Stevenson and Knechtle appeal to Middle-Eastern Jesus-dreams for specific Christian content. But the dreams phenomenon is invoked, not defended evidentially, and Steingard’s charge that nature yields only “a higher being” is not closed.

Stevenson dreams as specific contentGenericity unrebutted

Objection 7

The marriage analogy fails on existence

In the marriage / relationship analogies, the partner’s existence is never in doubt. Hiddenness is doubt about existence itself — “a much more fundamental doubt.”

Jon Steingard

Gap — unanswered

The sharpest rejoinder in the corpus

Every relational analogy the defenders use presupposes the partner exists. Steingard names the disanalogy explicitly, and neither Knechtle replies. This quietly undercuts the central marriage / trust framing.

Objection 8

NDEs are neurologically explicable

Near-death experiences are “very much scientifically explained … within the findings of neuroscience” — so appeals to them as revelation are deflated.

Apostate Prophet

Gap — not addressed

Knechtle pivots to scale

Rather than engage the neuroscience, Knechtle appeals to the sheer scale of testimony. The reductionist explanation is left standing.

Objection 9

“Not enough evidence” (Russell)

The honest agnostic’s judgment-day reply: there simply was not enough evidence to warrant belief.

Bertrand Russell (via Craig)

Strongest response

Decode the complaint

“Not enough evidence” really means “not enough to coerce me out of my indifference” — the wrong success criterion. The right criterion is rational warrant for the willing seeker, and that the evidence supplies.

Craig apatheism decoding
