Jesus, Paul, and the faith

Did Paul invent Christianity?

It is a popular claim, made by Muslim and secular sceptics alike: that Jesus taught a simple ethical message, and it was really Paul, who never met him, who turned him into a dying and rising God and founded the religion we call Christianity. If it were true, it would change everything. The dates and the documents make it very hard to hold.

First / The Jesus-vs-Paul theory
Step 1 of 4
What is actually being claimed

The idea of two rival founders.

The theory has a tidy shape, which is part of its appeal. It splits the faith in two, and lays the second half at the feet of one man.

The theory
The claim runs like this. There was the real Jesus, a Jewish teacher with a simple message about God and how to live, who never claimed to be divine. And then there was Paul, a latecomer who never walked with him, who bolted on the cross, the resurrection, the divinity and the whole machinery of salvation, and so invented a new religion in Jesus's name.
Jesus vs Paul
It is sometimes put as a slogan: Christianity is really Paulianity. On this view Paul is the genius, or the villain, who hijacked a Galilean preacher and built an empire of doctrine the man himself would never have recognised. It is a serious charge, and it deserves a serious look rather than a brush-off.
Two Jesuses?
But the whole theory rests on one assumption: that beneath Paul there was a simpler, merely human Jesus, later painted over. So the real question is whether that earlier, plainer Jesus actually exists in the evidence, or whether he is something the theory has to invent in order to work. The dates decide it.
The theory you'll hear
Jesus preached a simple ethic; Paul turned him into a dying-and-rising God. But the dying-and-rising core is not Paul's invention. He quotes it as a creed he received from those before him, (1 Corinthians 15:3-8) datable to within a few years of the crucifixion, and the Gospels, written independently, present the same Jesus. There is no earlier, simpler Jesus hiding beneath Paul. The crucified and risen Jesus is there from the very start.
✓
Confidence: the theory claims a simple, human Jesus was overwritten by Paul's invented, divine Christ. Everything turns on whether that plainer Jesus is actually in the evidence, and the timeline leaves no room for him.
Second / Too early to invent
Step 2 of 4
The problem of the dates

Paul is too early to have invented it.

The first thing that sinks the theory is simply the calendar. Paul is not a late embellisher. He is the earliest Christian writer we have, and even he is passing on something older still.

The early source
Paul's letters, written in the 50s, are the oldest Christian documents in existence, earlier than the Gospels. If anything he stands closer to the events than the writers usually accused of adding legend. So he cannot be the late layer that quietly transformed a simple Jesus, because there is no earlier Christian voice for him to have transformed.
He received it
And Paul is explicit that he is not the source. He tells the Corinthians that he passed on to them what he himself first received: that Christ died for our sins, was buried, rose, and appeared to Peter and the rest. (1 Corinthians 15:3-8) Scholars across the spectrum date that creed to within a few years of the crucifixion. So the death, the resurrection and the worship of Jesus predate Paul. He inherited the gospel; he did not manufacture it.
Too early
This is fatal to the legend version. Legends need generations to grow, and the core of Christianity is already fixed in a formula older than the earliest letter we possess, traceable to the first handful of years. There was no quiet century in which a human teacher slowly swelled into a divine saviour. The high view of Jesus is in the foundations, not in later paint.
The theory you'll hear
The Gospels came decades after Paul, so the legend had plenty of time to grow. Run the dates the other way. Paul is the early source, and even he is handing on something older still, the creed he received. The resurrection and the worship of Jesus are not late additions anyone bolted on; they sit in the earliest material we have, traceable to within a few years of the cross, far too early for legend to do its work.
✓
Confidence: Paul is the earliest Christian writer, and he explicitly passes on a creed older than himself, dated to within a few years of the crucifixion. The death, resurrection and worship of Jesus predate Paul, so he cannot be the man who invented them.
Third / The eyewitnesses vetted him
Step 3 of 4
Who checked his work

The eyewitnesses vetted him.

If Paul had been spinning a new religion, there were people perfectly placed to expose him: the men who had actually known Jesus. So what did they do?

He met them
Paul did not operate in isolation from them. In his letter to the Galatians he describes going up to Jerusalem, staying with Peter for over two weeks, and meeting James. (Galatians 1:18-19) These were not strangers; they were the inner circle, the men who had walked the roads of Galilee with Jesus. Paul sat with them, at length, comparing notes.
They signed off
And the outcome was endorsement, not exposure. Paul says the leading apostles, having examined his gospel, added nothing to it and gave him the right hand of fellowship. (Galatians 2:9) Had he been preaching a Jesus they did not recognise, this is exactly where it would have blown up. Instead, the eyewitnesses shook his hand. You could not ask for a tougher fact-check, or a clearer result.
James the brother
Most striking of all, one of those who affirmed him was James, the brother of Jesus, who grew up in the same house. A man does not endorse a stranger's fairy tale about his own brother. That James, a sceptic during Jesus's lifetime, ended up leading the Jerusalem church and backing Paul's message is among the hardest facts for the invention theory to swallow.
The theory you'll hear
Paul never even met Jesus; he just made up his own version. He never walked with the earthly Jesus, that is true. But he went to Jerusalem and spent time with the men who did, Peter and Jesus's own brother James, and they affirmed his gospel rather than denouncing it. An inventor of a rival religion does not get the blessing of the dead man's brother.
✓
Confidence: Paul met, and was vetted by, the eyewitnesses, Peter and James the brother of Jesus, who endorsed his gospel rather than exposing it. An invented religion does not survive inspection by the very people who knew Jesus, including his own family.
Fourth / The theory collapses
Step 4 of 4
Where the theory falls apart

There's no hidden, simpler Jesus.

Put the pieces together and the Jesus-versus-Paul theory has nothing left to stand on. The simpler Jesus it needs never turns up, and the inventor it imagines makes no sense.

No hidden Jesus
The whole idea requires a plainer, merely human Jesus underneath, later overwritten. But peel back Paul and you do not find him. You find a creed, older than Paul, that already proclaims the death, resurrection and lordship of Christ. The simple ethical teacher is not the earliest layer waiting to be uncovered. He is a figure the theory has to imagine into existence.
Gospels agree
And the Gospels are not Paul's puppets. They are independent accounts, and they show the same Jesus he does: one who forgives sins, claims divine authority, predicts his death, and rises. The divinity is not a Pauline import. It runs through sources Paul did not write and could not control, as the cards on Jesus's claims and the resurrection set out.
Not from paganism
Nor was it borrowed from the Greek mystery religions, the other common escape. Ronald Nash showed that an embarrassingly high percentage of the alleged pagan parallels is dated long after the New Testament, so the borrowing runs the wrong way. The remainder turn on exaggeration, on loose description, or on retelling pagan rites in borrowed Christian language. The New Testament thought-world is thoroughly Jewish, not Greek.
Nothing to gain
Finally, look at Paul himself. He began as a violent enemy of the church, then threw away status, safety and finally his life for the message, beaten, imprisoned and executed for it. People invent religions for power, money or comfort. Paul got the opposite of all three. That is not the behaviour of a con man. It is the behaviour of a man convinced he had met someone real.
The theory you'll hear
Paul, not Jesus, is the real founder, and he built it out of Greek mystery religion. Then he founded it on a message that predates him, with the blessing of the eyewitnesses, matching Gospels he did not write, and he did it knowing it would cost him beatings, prison and execution. And the pagan parallels do not hold: most are dated long after the New Testament, and the rest dissolve on a close look. That is not the profile of an inventor. It is the profile of a man who believed, with good reason, that he had met the risen Jesus.
✓
Confidence: there is no simpler Jesus beneath Paul; the earliest creed is already high and Christ-centred, the independent Gospels agree, and Paul suffered and died for a message he received rather than invented. The founder of Christianity is Jesus, not Paul.

So did Paul invent Christianity? The dates say no. Paul is the earliest voice we have, and even he is handing on a creed older than himself, one that already worships a crucified and risen Lord within a few years of the events. The men who actually knew Jesus, including his own brother, checked Paul's message and put their names to it. The Gospels he did not write tell the same story. And Paul himself traded power for a prison cell and a martyr's death. There is no simpler Jesus hiding underneath it all. There is only the one the first Christians, Paul included, were willing to die for.
