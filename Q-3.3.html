The question

Did Jesus rise bodily from the dead, and is resurrection the best historical explanation of the agreed facts?

This is the load-bearing node of the entire corpus. Knechtle states the stakes plainly: “if Jesus is not risen from the dead, we’re fools for believing in him… you can falsify it or you can verify it.” Stuart adds the hinge: “if that happened then you have to accept all of the Christian faith; if it didn’t happen then you have to discard all of it.”

Unlike the Kalam (Q-1.1.1), this is not a deductive syllogism but an abductive cumulative case — an inference to the best explanation of facts that even sceptical historians concede. Every expert builds it the same way: establish the facts on neutral historical grounds, then argue that “God raised Jesus” outperforms every naturalistic rival.

The convergent argument — four converging streams

The corpus is unusually unified at the structural level. All eight experts deploy a minimal-facts / maximal-data inference to the best explanation: four streams of conceded historical fact, followed by the elimination of naturalistic rivals. Each stream is contested independently; the cumulative force is hard to evade.

Stream A · The Death

  • Roman crucifixion (executioner competence)
  • Spear-wound: serum + clotted blood = heart failure
  • Honourable burial by Joseph of Arimathea
  • Defeats swoon / apparent-death theory

Stream B · The Tomb

  • Empty tomb, multiply attested (≥5 sources)
  • Women as first witnesses (embarrassment)
  • Jewish polemic concedes it (Matt 28)
  • Known location — body was checkable

Stream C · The Appearances

  • Polymodal: sight, touch, eating, 40 days
  • 1 Cor 15 creed dated within months
  • 500+ named witnesses, “go ask them”
  • Seen by skeptics, unbelievers, enemies

Stream D · The Belief

  • Disciples transformed from deserters to martyrs
  • Skeptic converts: James the brother, Paul
  • Overnight Jewish paradigm shift
  • “Died for what they claimed to have seen”
Inference

“God raised Jesus from the dead” is the best explanation of all four streams.

Tested against McCullagh’s six historiographical criteria — explanatory scope, power, plausibility, not ad hoc, accord with accepted belief, outstripping rivals (Craig / Stuart Knechtle); or framed as four mutually exhaustive hypotheses — legend, conspiracy, honest mistake, or resurrection — the first three eliminated (McLatchie). The argument is abductive: the premises are conceded facts; the controversy lives in which explanation wins.

How each expert deploys it

Cliffe & Stuart Knechtle22 transcripts
Popular willingness-to-die case
Signature moves
  • The crucial qualifier: the disciples died “not for a belief but for what they claimed to have seen” — dying for what you know to be a lie is the rare, probative case
  • Watergate analogy — three brilliant lawyers cracked under prison pressure; first-century peasants would not die to cover up a known hoax
  • The 9/11 inversion — concedes the parity then flips it: hijackers died for what they were “convinced was the absolute truth,” not a known lie
  • Enemy attestation — the Sanhedrin’s stolen-body rumour presupposes the empty tomb (Stuart)
  • Overnight paradigm shift (Kuhn) — Jews had “the most hoops to jump through”
  • C.S. Lewis on the gospels: “too uncreative… they don’t use embellished language”
Known weaknesses

Often abandons the resurrection mid-debate for morality / inerrancy. The “360,000 in one Colosseum” figure is unsourced and inflated; Larry Hurtado is repeatedly mis-placed at “Marquette” (he was at Edinburgh). Against Baggini, effectively concedes by running out of time.

William Lane Craig14 transcripts
Architect of the Minimal Facts inference
Distinctive moves
  • Treat the NT as ordinary documents — neutralises circularity before it is raised
  • Facts by scholarly headcount — Kremer (empty tomb), Lüdemann (appearances, hostile), N.T. Wright (origin of belief)
  • McCullagh’s six tests — the resurrection passes all six
  • Bayesian rebuttal of Hume — Millican (hostile) concedes the maxim is fallacious; improbability attaches to a natural resurrection only
  • The twin-Jesus reductio — the UC-Irvine professor’s secret-twin theory shows “to what desperate lengths skepticism must go”
  • Lapide anchor — a Jewish theologian convinced “the God of Israel raised Jesus”
  • The 1 Cor 15 creed is “a list of witnesses, not appearances”; women omitted for lacking legal standing
Concession / gap

Leaves the Kappel asymmetry unanswered (defers to historians’ consensus but dismisses philosophers’). In one Law debate, never engages the resurrection attack before the moderator pivots.

Jonathan McLatchie14 transcripts
Maximal-data four-hypothesis frame
Distinctive moves
  • Four mutually exhaustive hypotheses — legend, conspiracy, honest mistake, resurrection; the Lewis trilemma transposed onto the disciples’ testimony
  • “Polymodal” appearances — touch, conversation, eating, across 40 days → not hallucination
  • Criterion of restraint (his distinctive) — the gospels never narrate the private Peter / James appearances; silence = faithful reporting
  • James the brother — “imagine your older brother claimed to be the Yahweh of the Old Testament”
  • Temple-saying undesigned coincidence — Synoptics give the accusation, John 2:19 the original; neither copied
  • Bayesian background probability — not “any old miscellaneous Jew” but the typological culmination of the Hebrew scriptures
Methodological note

Explicitly rejects “Minimal Facts” for “Maximal Data” (with the McGrews). Waves off the sophisticated bereavement-vision objection: “the objections are really poor.” Attributes “undesigned coincidence” to Paley (likely J.J. Blunt / McGrew).

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

“People die for false beliefs all the time”

Jim Jones, Branch Davidians, kamikaze pilots, 9/11 hijackers — willingness to die proves only sincerity, not truth.

Skylar Fiction, Tom Jump, Newdow, Adair

Strongest response

Dying for what you know to be a lie

The disciples were uniquely positioned to know whether they had fabricated the claim. Watergate: brilliant lawyers cracked under prison pressure rather than maintain a known lie. The 9/11 parity is conceded then flipped — the hijackers died for what they were convinced was true. The corpus’s single most repeated move.

Cliffe / Stuart Knechtle qualifier + WatergateStrobel unique epistemic access

Objection 2

Extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence (Hume)

There is always more reason to disbelieve the report of a miracle than to believe what it reports.

Dillahunty, Atkins, Ehrman, PZ Myers

Strongest response

Hume predates the probability calculus

Millican (hostile Hume scholar) concedes the maxim is fallacious — Hume neglected the probability that we would have the evidence we do if the resurrection had not occurred. The improbability attaches to a natural resurrection; given independent grounds for God, raising Jesus is “an ordinary event in his economy.” Lennox: miracles are inputs, not violations (the £100 analogy).

Craig Bayesian likelihood ratioStrobel God-economyLennox input-not-violation

Objection 3

Mass / group hallucination explains the appearances

Grief-induced collective vision produced the conviction that Jesus had appeared.

Modern critical scholars, PZ Myers

Strongest response

Polymodal appearances — no such phenomenon

Huff: “there’s no such thing as a mass hallucination” — 500 people can’t share identical content over a month. McLatchie: the appearances were polymodal (touch, eating, conversation, 40 days), not one confusing episode. Schreiner: hallucinations are individual and don’t explain the conversion of enemies like Paul.

Huff mass-hallucination denialMcLatchie polymodalSchreiner skeptic-convert

Objection 4

The empty tomb is a later Markan invention

Joseph of Arimathea appears only in Mark, 35–40 years on; the women fit Mark’s literary theme that marginalised outsiders understand Jesus — so Mark would invent them.

Bart Ehrman

Response — partly blunted

Multiple attestation, but the criterion is dented

Craig: the empty tomb is attested in ≥5 independent sources, not Mark alone, and Ehrman himself moved toward conceding burial + empty tomb. But Ehrman’s reversal of the embarrassment criterion (Mark’s theme motivates inventing the women) is answered only by retreating to multiple attestation — the sharpest counter in the corpus.

Craig multiple attestationembarrassment criterion left dented

Objection 5

The resurrection is a legend that developed over time

Decades of oral transmission turned a failed messiah into a risen Lord.

Standard skeptical, Paulogia

Strongest response

The creed dates “within months”

Strobel: the 1 Cor 15 creed traces to within 1–6 years (Dunn: “within months”); Sherwin-White showed two generations is too short for legend to overwrite fact. McLatchie: legend is eliminated by the early creed plus Luke as Paul’s travelling companion with access to living witnesses.

Strobel creed-dating + Sherwin-WhiteMcLatchie Luke as eyewitness companion

Objection 6

Stolen body / Sanhedrin conspiracy

The disciples (or someone) moved the body and fabricated the resurrection.

Newdow, Atkins, audience questioners

Strongest response

Anachronistic — and enemy-attested against

Stuart: the Jewish polemic (Matt 28:11–15) presupposes the empty tomb — enemy attestation. Craig: this is dead 17th-century deism; no first-century Jew had the category of an individual resurrection within history, so they would “either go home or get a new Messiah.” Schreiner: Roman guards, and later martyrdom disconfirms fraud.

Stuart Knechtle enemy attestationCraig anachronismSchreiner guards + martyrdom

Objection 7

Swoon / apparent death

Jesus survived the cross and later revived, faking the resurrection.

Implicit across debates

Strongest response

Forensics of certified death

Knechtle: the spear-wound signature — watery serum separated from clotted blood — indicates massive heart failure; Roman executioners “knew when this person was dead.” Schreiner: soldiers pierced his side rather than break his legs, having verified death.

Cliffe Knechtle spear-wound forensicsSchreiner verification

Objection 8

“You just believe what’s written in a book” (circularity)

All the evidence is Christian testimony; independent corroboration from non-believers is required.

Greydon Square, Smalley, Baggini, PZ Myers

Response — uneven

Reliability is an external question

Cliffe: gospel reliability is testable on neutral historical criteria; demanding only non-Christian sources is “intellectually dishonest” (you don’t bar Jewish historians on the Holocaust). But Baggini’s order-of-reasoning reversal goes unworked, and Cliffe concedes by running out of time.

Cliffe Knechtle reliability reframeorder-of-reasoning left open

Objection 9

Pagan dying-and-rising-god parallels

The risen Jesus is a syncretism of Osiris, Horus and the mystery cults.

Mythicist, Kappel

Strongest response

Genre distinction + the scholarship is dead

Cliffe: myths claim myth; the gospels claim history, and the Osiris account is “radically different.” Craig: per Mettinger, “people who think there were pagan myths of dying and rising deities are like dinosaurs today.”

Cliffe Knechtle genre distinctionCraig Mettinger

Objection 10

Matthew 27 “zombie apocalypse” — argument from silence

Dead saints walking Jerusalem would be reported everywhere; only Matthew records it, so the gospels are unreliable.

Yusuf Ismail

Strongest response

Arguments from silence are notoriously weak

McLatchie: Josephus and Philo pass over the Claudius expulsion; Vesuvius is reported by only Pliny; even the AD 70 temple destruction has one first-century non-Jewish source. Single-source attestation of a remarkable event is not disqualifying.

McLatchie three-silence parity

Objection 11

Islamic assumption theory (vision, not resurrection)

God assumed Jesus to heaven before death and granted the disciples a genuine vision (Surah 3:55).

Shabir Ally, Badawi

Strongest response

A vision yields assumption, not resurrection

Craig: visions would have led Jews to preach assumption, in line with Jewish categories — not bodily resurrection contrary to Jewish modes of thought. The hypothesis “turns God into a deceiver,” and comes “too late” once Jesus is already in the tomb.

Craig four-count refutation

Objection 12

Naturalism is not a presupposition — it’s a conclusion

“I haven’t decided there’s no supernatural and therefore no resurrection; I reason from the evidence to my naturalistic worldview.”

Julian Baggini

Gap — standoff

The order of reasoning is never worked through

Knechtle charges that atheist rejection is “parasitic on prior commitment to naturalism.” Baggini ably inverts the order, and Knechtle does not adjudicate which direction is epistemically correct — a genuine unresolved standoff.

Aggregation gap — epistemic order unaddressed

Objection 13

Bayesian layering does not raise the posterior

The “flying grandfather” thought experiment: at each added layer a cheaper non-miraculous explanation remains more probable, so stacking evidence never gets you to a literal resurrection.

Nathan (Digital Gnosis)

Gap — spoken past

Paradigm-shift rhetoric, not likelihood ratios

The Knechtles answer with Kuhnian paradigm-shift framing rather than engaging the probabilistic structure. McLatchie’s likelihood-ratio approach (used elsewhere) is the right counter but is not brought to bear here.

Aggregation gap — probabilistic framing not engaged
