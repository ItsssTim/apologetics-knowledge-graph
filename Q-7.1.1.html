Stating the doctrine precisely enough to escape three heresies — and answering the charge that “one God who is three” is a flat contradiction.

The question

What does Christianity claim when it says God is one being in three persons — and does the claim survive the charge that 3 = 1 is a contradiction?

The node carries a double load. The first task is definitional: stating the orthodox doctrine sharply enough to distinguish it from the three heresies it is constantly confused with — modalism (one person in three masks), tritheism (three separate gods), and partialism (each person a third of God). The second is logical: answering the objection that “one God who is three” breaks the law of non-contradiction.

The corpus shows a pattern. Against unitarian opponents — Muslims, Jehovah’s Witnesses, Latter-day Saints — the experts spend most of their energy clearing away the caricature before defending anything, because, as Perry observes, the opponent is almost always attacking modalism, a heresy Christians already reject. The coherence answer, when it comes, is strikingly uniform: one in one sense, three in another — and non-contradiction only forbids being both in the same sense.

The convergent coherence argument — five of six experts

Premise 1

“Being” and “person” are distinct categories: being is what something is; person is who someone is.

The James White distinction. McLatchie and Huff both quote it verbatim. Perry renders it in popular register: “what he is is God; who he is exists in three persons.” This move relocates the oneness and the threeness into different categories.

Premise 2

God is one in the sense of being (one divine essence) and three in the sense of person (Father, Son, Spirit).

Observed phrasings. McLatchie: “one in one sense and three in another.” Huff: “the one being of God exists in three co-equal co-eternal persons.”

Premise 3

Non-contradiction is violated only if God is both one and three in the same sense. The two predications differ in sense — so no contradiction.

The same-sense rider. McLatchie’s one-line reply to “you affirm X and non-X”: “x and non-x in different senses.” To break the law of contradiction “we would need to say that in the same sense.”

Premise 4

The divine essence is a qualitative infinite and cannot be divided; each person fully possesses it (blocking partialism) while being one of three persons (blocking tritheism).

McLatchie’s signature move. “You cannot divide a qualitative infinite by three.” Each person has the fullness of deity, not a third — so partialism dies at the level of the essence, tritheism at the level of the persons.

Conclusion

Therefore the Trinity — one being eternally subsisting in three co-equal persons — is internally coherent and equidistant from modalism, tritheism, and partialism.

Perry’s corollary. Distinct roles do not entail unequal deity: as a husband and wife are equal in essence yet differ in role, the Father–Son language cannot be pressed into ontological inequality.

A second, dissenting stream runs alongside this. Keith Ward (the lone non-orthodox voice) would abandon “three persons” for “three ways of being” — a move the orthodox stream reads as modalist-adjacent. And William Lane Craig opens an internal Christian dispute over whether divine simplicity rescues or wrecks the coherence case.

How each expert treats it

Jonathan McLatchie3 transcripts
The coherence anchor — being / person under hostile fire
Distinctive moves
  • Being / person distinction (James White) — relocates oneness and threeness into different categories.
  • The same-sense rider — contradiction requires opposites in the same sense; “x and non-x in different senses.”
  • Qualitative infinite — the essence “cannot be divided by three,” blocking partialism while each person keeps the fullness of deity.
  • The balance: each is “one-third of this group of persons” yet shares the whole nature.
  • Quran-eternality parity — the eternal-yet-instantiated Quran is a structurally parallel “two natures” inside Sunni theology (anti-Islam wedge).
  • Positive OT case (vs Shabir Ally): Spirit, Son, and Father each bear divine attributes across the Hebrew Bible.
Clarifying formula (retrospective)

The line McLatchie wishes he had said live, offered in commentary: “Jesus is fully God, but God is not fully Jesus.” Distinguishes full possession of the essence from being the whole triune godhead.

Preston Perry5 transcripts
The dignity-first definer — clarify before defending
Signature deployment
  • Clarify before defending — opponents “think we believe in this thing called modalism, and we do not.” Steelman the position first.
  • Modalism definition (his most-deployed line, near-verbatim across four transcripts): the persons “cannot take turns being one another.”
  • Marriage role / essence analogy — Socratic assent: “you’re equal in essence with your husband… but he is greater than you in role.”
  • Genesis-1 positive case — Father initiates, Son speaks creation, Spirit hovers (eagle-over-nest semantics from Psalms / Proverbs).
  • “Who he is / what he is” — popular-register being/person formula.
  • Vs LDS: John 1:1–3 makes Jesus creator of all spirit-beings, so he and Lucifer cannot be brothers (“So Jesus created his brother?”); monogenes = unique, not created.
Wes Huff1 transcript
Trinity vs Tawhid — the Hebrew lexical front
Distinctive moves
  • Tritheism has always been heresy — “the church passed down from the Apostles unanimously said this is heresy.”
  • Affirms the Shema — “Christianity has always proclaimed that… we have never said we believe in more than one God.”
  • Echad as plural unity — one flesh (Gen 2:24), one heart (2 Chron 30), one temple of many components.
  • Being vs person — clean restatement of the White distinction.
  • Royal-we is anachronistic — the majestic plural “is not a concept that exists in ancient” Hebrew / Aramaic.
John Lennox1 transcript
Monotheism by parsimony — the Trinity of love
Distinctive moves
  • Parsimony against dualism — more than one ultimate reality “gets you into some kind of dualism”; Ockham’s razor favours one.
  • Solves “was God lonely?” — “God is a fellowship. He has relationship within himself, but he is one God.” Relationality is internal, so creation is not driven by divine need.
William Lane Craig1 transcript
The simplicity–coherence dispute (intra-Christian)
Internal critique
  • Subsistent relations are too thin — Aquinas distinguishes Father and Son “like the distinction between I and me”; “not robust enough to capture what we mean by a person.”
  • And self-violating — positing relations within God “is to violate the doctrine of divine simplicity”; he calls the reconciliation “a desperate attempt.”
Counter-position on the same panel

Howe holds the opposite: simplicity is “the safest guard” against collapse into tritheism or partialism, “precisely because God is not made of parts.” Huffling adds the Fathers saw a problem to reconcile, not a contradiction. One doctrine read as both helping and hurting another.

Keith Wardvia Knechtle · 1 transcript
The dissenting reformulation — flagged, heterodox
Distinctive moves
  • Reformulate to “three ways of being” — creator, living human expression, indwelling spirit; “person” misleads modern hearers.
  • “Three people sitting in heaven talking to each other… that’s just baby talk.”
  • Credentialed by Rahner and Barth — a deliberate Catholic-plus-Protestant authority pairing.
Aggregator flag

“Modes of existence” is the historic vocabulary of modalism (Sabellianism). Ward never distinguishes his view from it, never treats the eternal immanent relations, and reads as purely economic — the modalist failure mode. Documented as an in-corpus position, not the orthodox framing. Knechtle does not press.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

“You affirm X and non-X at once — it’s a contradiction”

You say each person is fully God and yet there is one God. That is having your cake and eating it — X and non-X at the same time.

Mansoor Ahmed, Speakers’ Corner

Strongest response

“X and non-X in different senses”

One in the sense of being, three in the sense of person. Non-contradiction is only broken if both are asserted in the same sense — which the doctrine never does.

McLatchie being/person + same-sense rider

Objection 2

“If each is fully God, that’s three gods” (tritheism)

They are all fully God, lacking no divine attribute — so that is three gods.

Paul Williams; Ahmed Sraa

Strongest response

Tritheism needs each to be the whole godhead

It would be three gods only if each person were the totality of the triune godhead. Each is one of three persons yet fully possesses the one undivided essence — trinitarianism, not tritheism.

McLatchie totality distinctionHuff tritheism always heresy

Objection 3

“If the Father is fully God, he must be the one God by himself”

Being fully God, the Father just is the one God — so where do the others fit?

Mansoor Ahmed

Strongest response

Fully God, but not the whole godhead

Yes in the sense of possessing the divine essence; no in the sense of being all three persons — that latter claim would be modalism. The Father is not the Son and not the Spirit.

McLatchie essence vs godhead distinction

Objection 4

Each person has only a third of God (partialism)

If the one essence is shared three ways, each person holds only a fraction of deity.

Implicit in the tritheism press

Strongest response

A qualitative infinite cannot be divided

“You cannot divide a qualitative infinite by three.” Each person possesses the fullness of the divine essence, not a portion of it. Partialism is blocked at the level of the essence itself.

McLatchie qualitative-infinite argument

Objection 5

The Trinity just is modalism — God in three masks

God plays three roles in turn: Father, then Son, then Spirit — one person switching modes.

JWs, Muslims, Hebrew Israelites

Strongest response

The persons cannot “take turns being one another”

Modalism is a heresy Christians reject. The persons are genuinely and simultaneously distinct — the Son is not the Father, the Father is not the Spirit. Clarify this before defending anything.

Perry clarify-before-defendingMcLatchie “we are not modalists”

Objection 6

OT plurals are just the “royal we” (majestic plural)

“Let us make man” is the way kings and high authorities speak — a literary majestic plural, not plural persons.

Ahmed Sraa

Strongest response

The royal we is anachronistic

The majestic plural “is not a concept that exists in ancient” Hebrew or Aramaic. Applying a modern monarchical convention to the ancient text is anachronistic. Hebrew echad already carries plural unity.

Huff anachronism + echad plural unity

Objection 7

Father–Son language proves the Son is lesser or created

A son is subordinate to and later than his father; “begotten” means made. So Jesus is a creature.

Jehovah’s Witnesses, Latter-day Saints

Strongest response

Role-difference is not essence-difference

Husband and wife are equal in essence yet differ in role. Monogenes means unique, not created. And the Pharisees sought to kill Jesus for claiming equality with God, not mere sonship — a fact both parties share.

Perry marriage analogy + monogenes + Pharisee-reaction test

Objection 8

“Person” misleads — drop it for “modes of being”

Modern hearers read “three persons” as three centres of consciousness. Better to say “three ways of being in God.”

Keith Ward (citing Rahner, Barth)

Response from the orthodox stream

“Modes of being” slides toward Sabellianism

The reformulation is modalist-adjacent: it omits the eternal immanent relations and reads as purely economic. The orthodox stream keeps “person” precisely to preserve genuine, simultaneous distinctness.

PerryMcLatchieHuff hold the line on distinct persons

Objection 9

Simplicity makes the persons mere “subsistent relations”

To square the Trinity with divine simplicity, Aquinas reduces the persons to subsistent relations — too thin to be persons, and itself a violation of simplicity.

William Lane Craig (intra-Christian)

Disputed in-house

Simplicity as guard vs simplicity as wrecker

Howe replies that simplicity is the safest guard against tritheism and partialism, since God has no parts. Huffling: the Fathers saw a tension to reconcile, not a contradiction. A live intra-Christian dispute, not a settled answer.

Craig critiqueHowe / Huffling defence

Objection 10

No closure — why three, not four or seven?

If “one” can include “three,” why not four? Revelation names “seven spirits”; a doctoral thesis documents many spirits in the NT. “Multi-unity” is more accurate than “trinity.”

Shabir Ally (citing Clint Tibbs)

Gap — largely unanswered

No principled account of “exactly three”

McLatchie does not engage closure in the debate; by Shabir’s account his answer shifted between “Scripture” and “reason” and was “not very coherent.” No expert in the node supplies a principled reason for the number three.

Aggregation gap

Objection 11

Death-of-God dilemma — persons are expendable

If “the Word became flesh” and the Word is the second person, then when Jesus died the second person died — so persons are expendable; the other two can run the world.

Shabir Ally

Gap — not engaged in-node

Answered only at Q-8.1.1

The resolution lives in the hypostatic union (the human nature dies, not the divine essence). In this node the dilemma stands unrebutted — no expert deploys the two-natures reply against it here.

The Trinity is “originally a pagan concept”

The doctrine was imported from pagan triads, not drawn from Scripture.

Anton (Jehovah’s Witness)
