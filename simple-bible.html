First / Were they there?
Step 1 of 4
Or was it written centuries later?

The writers were close enough to know.

The first worry is that the Gospels were written so long after Jesus, by people so far removed, that they are basically rumour passed down the centuries. The dates do not allow for that.

When
All four Gospels were written inside the first century, within the lifetime of people who had actually been there. The earliest summary of the resurrection, the creed Paul quotes in 1 Corinthians 15, traces back to within a few years of the events, not generations later.
Eyewitness
They read like first-century reporting, not legend. The New Testament scholar Richard Bauckham points out that the Gospels get the incidental things right that a later outsider could never have faked, down to the exact frequency of Jewish personal names in that specific time and place. That is the fingerprint of people who knew the world they were describing.
In use
By the end of the first century the earliest church leaders, men like Clement of Rome and Ignatius of Antioch, were already quoting the Gospels in their own letters. You could nearly rebuild the Gospels from their quotations alone. The material was in circulation and being used almost immediately, with no quiet gap for a legend to grow in.
The theory you'll hear
The anonymous-legend theory: that the Gospels were written by unknown people generations later and just attached to famous names. But anonymous does not mean unknown, and the content itself, the names, places, customs and offhand details, fits first-century Palestine with a precision a distant later writer could not have reverse-engineered. And the timeline is simply too tight, since the church was already quoting these texts within a single generation.
✓
Confidence: the Gospels sit close to the events, inside living memory, with eyewitness detail all through them. This is not centuries-later rumour.
Second / Survived intact?
Step 2 of 4
Or is it a game of telephone?

It isn't a game of telephone.

This is the big one. Surely after centuries of copying and translating, the original wording is long gone. It is the most common objection, and it is also the one the evidence answers most decisively.

The myth
People picture a single chain: a copy of a copy of a translation of a translation, drifting further from the original at every step, like a message whispered around a room. That is not how it worked. Modern Bibles are translated straight from the earliest Greek manuscripts, not from a long line of English passed hand to hand.
The numbers
There are more than five thousand Greek manuscripts of the New Testament, plus many thousands more in other ancient languages, with the earliest fragments landing within about a century of the originals. Nothing else from the ancient world comes remotely close. Richard Bauckham notes that a play by Sophocles may survive on a single copy made over a thousand years later, and no one doubts we can read Sophocles.
Cross-check
Having so many copies is a strength, not a weakness. Because the manuscripts come down through many independent lines, scholars can lay them side by side and spot exactly where a scribe slipped, the way you would catch one person's typo by comparing everyone else's copy. Mistakes get filtered out rather than baked in.
The theory you'll hear
The corruption theory: that the text has been changed so many times we cannot know what it first said. This is the case Bart Ehrman is best known for. But here is the part even he concedes: the differences between manuscripts are overwhelmingly trivial, things like spelling and word order, and no central Christian teaching depends on any disputed passage. We can reconstruct the original wording to a very high degree precisely because we have so much to compare.
✓
Confidence: the New Testament is the best-preserved text from the ancient world by a wide margin. What you read today is, to a remarkable degree, what was first written down.
Third / Contradictions?
Step 3 of 4
Aren't there thousands of errors?

Most contradictions dissolve on a closer look.

Two separate worries get bundled together here: differences between the manuscripts, and apparent clashes between the four accounts. They have different answers, and neither does the damage people assume.

The variants
Critics love the figure of hundreds of thousands of variants across the manuscripts. But that number is only so large because the manuscripts are so many. Sort them and the overwhelming majority are spelling slips and word order. The tiny handful that genuinely affect meaning change no central teaching of the faith.
Perspective
When the Gospels seem to clash, it is usually four witnesses describing one event from different angles, not four stories that cannot both be true. Two people at the same accident report different details. That is the mark of independent witnesses, not invention. Where one writer mentions a single angel at the tomb and another mentions two, both can be true. One simply focused on the one who spoke.
Too tidy
Here is the twist. If all four accounts matched word for word, that would be the real warning sign, the fingerprint of people who got together and agreed on a story. The small, surface-level differences are exactly what honest, independent testimony looks like.
The theory you'll hear
The riddled-with-errors theory: that because there are discrepancies, the whole thing collapses. Watch the move, though. A difference in a minor detail is treated as if it sinks everything, a standard no historian applies to any other ancient source. And the alleged contradictions sit in peripheral details, never in the core the accounts all agree on: that Jesus lived, was executed, and was reported alive again.
✓
Confidence: the variants are mostly trivial and touch no doctrine, and the apparent contradictions are the normal texture of real, independent eyewitness reports.
Fourth / Who decided?
Step 4 of 4
Didn't Constantine pick and change the books?

Nobody voted the Bible into existence.

The popular version, made famous by The Da Vinci Code, is that the Emperor Constantine and the Council of Nicaea chose which books got in and edited out the rest. Almost none of that survives contact with the history.

Nicaea
The Council of Nicaea in 325 met to settle a fierce dispute about who Jesus was. It did not draw up the list of biblical books, and the canon was not even on its agenda. The claim that Nicaea voted the Bible together is a modern story you can trace back through a chain of garbled and even forged sources, not through any historical record.
Recognised
The books that became the New Testament were already being read and circulated by churches across the empire long before any council weighed in. The later lists were confirming what was already in widespread use, the way you recognise a genuine signature rather than inventing it by decree. Recognition, not imposition.
Couldn't
Even if Constantine had wanted to rewrite it, he could not have. By his day copies were already scattered across thousands of locations in several languages. There was no single master copy to edit. The earliest fragments we hold predate him by roughly two centuries, and they match.
The theory you'll hear
The suppressed-gospels theory: that the church buried other gospels, like Thomas, Judas and Mary, that told a rival story. But those Gnostic texts were written much later, often a century or more after the eyewitnesses had died, and they read nothing like the first-century accounts. They were not so much banned as never recognised, because everyone could see they had arrived far too late to come from anyone who knew Jesus.
✓
Confidence: the Bible was recognised over time by the wider church, not handed down by an emperor or a single council. Constantine did not write it, and could not have rewritten it.
