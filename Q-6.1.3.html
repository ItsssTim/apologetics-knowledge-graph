Does naturalism undermine itself?

The Evolutionary Argument Against Naturalism — Lewis’s argument from reason, sharpened by Plantinga: if the mind is the end-product of a mindless process, the naturalist cannot trust the faculties he uses to argue for naturalism.

The question

If the human mind is the end-product of a mindless, unguided process, can the naturalist consistently trust the faculties he uses to argue for naturalism?

This is the Evolutionary Argument Against Naturalism (EAAN) — the contemporary descendant of C.S. Lewis’s argument from reason and Alvin Plantinga’s formal probability version. It is not the claim that atheists cannot do science; manifestly they do brilliant science. It is the sharper claim that the atheist worldview cannot supply the warrant for the cognitive reliability that science and rational argument presuppose.

Its strategic value is that it is self-referentially explosive: if sound, it does not merely fail to support naturalism — it takes naturalism’s own conclusion down with it. The naturalist, in Lennox’s image, is “sitting on the branch of a tree cutting off the branch on which they’re sitting.”

The convergent argument — a self-undermining reductio

All four experts deploy a recognisable version of the same reductio. The framing differs — Socratic, deductive, Bayesian — but the spine is identical. All the action lives in the move from P2 to P4: whether unguided selection for survival can be trusted to track truth.

Premise 1

Science and rational argument presuppose that the mind has reliable access to truth.

Variants. Lennox grounds this in the rational intelligibility of the universe (Einstein, Wigner) as “a matter of faith” that science itself cannot give you. Huff: “why should this two pounds of gray matter… have any purchase on ultimate reality?”

Premise 2

On naturalism, the mind is the brain, and the brain is the end-product of a mindless, unguided process that selected for survival, not truth.

Variants. Lennox’s signature phrase, “the end product of a mindless unguided process.” Huff: processes “that favor merely reproduction and survival.” Craig states it formally: “the probability that our cognitive faculties are reliable given naturalism and evolution is low.”

Premise 3

No one would trust a device they knew to be the product of such a process.

The computer analogy — the delivery vehicle. Lennox: “if you knew that your computer was the end product of a mindless unguided process you wouldn’t trust it for one minute.” The audience-accessible step that makes the abstract worry concrete.

Premise 4

So the naturalist has a defeater for any belief his faculties produce — including belief in naturalism itself, and the science used to support it.

Variants. Craig’s formal three-step defeater chain: a defeater for reliability is a defeater for any belief those faculties yield. Lennox: an argument that “purports to derive rationality from irrationality… is logically incoherent.”

Conclusion

Naturalism is self-undermining; theism supplies the missing warrant — the same rational God grounds both the mind in here and the cosmos out there.

Positive resolution. Lennox: “the same God who created the universe is ultimately responsible for the human mind.” Huff names the doctrine — the Logos became flesh. Craig: “God can supervise the evolutionary process… to guarantee the fundamental reliability of our cognitive faculties.”

The dominant voice — Socratic computer analogy
Signature moves
  • The Socratic reductio. “What do you do science with?” → “my brain” → “and what’s the brain?” → “the end product of a mindless unguided process” → the pause-and-smile: “And you trust it?” Built for the room, not the page.
  • The computer analogy as the audience-accessible kill-shot.
  • “The branch you’re sitting on” (vs Dawkins) — naturalism self-amputates; deriving rationality from irrationality is “logically incoherent.” Dawkins never responds.
  • Hostile-witness chain, stacked chronologically: Darwin (1860s) → Haldane → Lewis (1940s) → Nagel & Gray (2010s). Pre-empts “that’s a Christian argument”: “No… it’s the kind of argument that Charles Darwin would come up with.”
  • Warrant, not ability. Concedes atheists do brilliant science, then redirects via Plantinga and the imago Dei: what’s lacking is the warrant.
  • The framing flip: not science vs God but science vs atheism — “science is burying atheism.”

William Lane Craig 1 transcript (vs Wielenberg)
The formal Plantingian — applied to moral knowledge
Distinctive moves
  • The only premise-numbered formal version: P(R | N&E) is low → defeater for reliability → defeater for any belief, “including his moral beliefs.”
  • Causal closure makes it worse. On Wielenberg’s view mental states have no effect on brain states, so “the content of our beliefs is irrelevant to our survivability.”
  • The question-begging rebuttal — his strongest move: the very observation that evolution produced reliable believers presupposes the reliability of the observing faculties.
  • Theism as supervisor: God can “guarantee the fundamental reliability of our cognitive faculties.”
Erik Wielenberg 1 transcript (opposing)
The naturalist reply — inspect the products
Distinctive moves
  • The Bic lighter analogy. Empirical track-record trumps armchair probability: a lighter that ignites 99 of 100 times is reliable “even if I’m aware of some guy’s armchair speculations.”
  • Inspect the widgets. Dogs and humans demonstrably form many true beliefs; Plantinga’s low-probability premise is “armchair speculation” refuted by observation.
  • Does not answer Craig’s circularity charge — the central unresolved exchange in the corpus.
Why this matters

Wielenberg is the only sustained naturalist rebuttal in the corpus. Lennox and Huff bypass this objection by quoting atheists who concede the problem; only Craig engages it directly.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

Natural selection would select for reliable faculties

Reliable beliefs aid survival, so unguided evolution would drag along largely truth-tracking faculties in the relevant domains — the standard reliabilist reply.

Fodor / Dennett tradition

Gap — mostly bypassed, not refuted

Hostile-witness short-circuit; Craig engages alone

Lennox and Huff quote past this objection (Gray, Darwin concede the worry) rather than engaging it. Only Craig answers head-on, via the circularity charge below — powerful but contested.

Craig circularity rebuttalLennox / Huff bypass

Objection 2

“Look at the widgets the widget-maker made”

To know what a process produces, inspect its actual products. Creatures produced by unguided evolution — dogs, humans — reliably form many true beliefs. So Plantinga’s low-probability premise is empirically refuted.

Erik Wielenberg (Bic lighter analogy)

Strongest response

Question-begging

The very observation that evolution produced reliable believers is itself a deliverance of the faculties under suspicion. “If naturalism were true, we could never know… it has produced reliable believers, since that conclusion assumes that our observations and inferences are reliable.”

Craig vs Wielenberg

Objection 3

“That’s just a Christian argument / wishful thinking”

The whole worry is a piece of apologetic special pleading — the kind of thing a Christian would invent to make naturalism look bad.

Generic skeptic

Strongest response

Pre-empted by hostile witnesses

“No, it isn’t. It’s the kind of argument that Charles Darwin would come up with.” Lennox stacks Darwin, Nagel, Gray and Haldane — all atheists — voicing the doubt themselves, chronologically from the 1860s to the 2010s.

Lennox hostile-witness chainHuff John Gray concession

Objection 4

Brilliant atheists do brilliant science

If atheism undermined the rationality science needs, atheists couldn’t be doing first-rate science — yet some of the best scientists and mathematicians are atheists.

Stated by Lennox himself (Duke)

Strongest response

Warrant, not ability

A category error. It is not a question of whether atheists can do science (they obviously can — every person is rational, made in God’s image) but whether the atheist worldview supplies the warrant for the rationality presupposed.

Lennox Plantinga warrant + imago Dei

Objection 5

Evolution gave us brains — maybe limited ones

Our brains evolved and may simply not be strong enough to understand absolutely everything (quantum entanglement may forever exceed us).

Peter Atkins (Southampton debate)

Strongest response

Capacity, not limits, is the issue

Lennox accepts the limits concession but distinguishes: limits of understanding are no warrant for “God of the gaps.” The point is the capacity itself — that an evolved mind has any reliable purchase on truth at all — that naturalism cannot explain.

Lennox vs Atkins

Objection 6

Causal closure rescues the naturalist

On a fully physicalist account, mental states are determined by physical brain states — so cognition runs on physics regardless of worldview.

Implicit in Wielenberg’s physicalism

Strongest response

It makes the problem worse

Craig turns the premise around: if mental states have “absolutely no effect on our brain states,” then “the content of our beliefs is irrelevant to our survivability” — so selection cannot track belief-content at all. Causal closure deepens the defeater.

Craig internal critique of Wielenberg
