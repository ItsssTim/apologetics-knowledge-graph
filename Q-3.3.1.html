The question

Given Jesus’ death, burial, the disciples’ transformation, and the women’s discovery of the empty tomb — is bodily resurrection the best explanation, and can a historian say so?

This is the one in-principle falsifiable event on which Christianity stands or falls. Paul stakes the whole faith on it: “if Christ is not raised, we are fools.” The striking feature of this Q-node is that the argument is almost never contested at the level of the facts. The contest is over method and metaphysics.

So this is two debates wearing one coat: a first-order historical case (burial, empty tomb, appearances, origin of belief) and a second-order methodological war — Minimal Facts vs Maximal Data, Bayesian priors, and the charge of naturalism smuggled into historiography.

The convergent case — four evidence streams, not a syllogism

This is an abductive inference to the best explanation. Every expert builds from a small set of facts that hostile critics also grant, then argues that “God raised Jesus from the dead” outstrips every naturalistic rival. The conclusion is then run through McCullagh’s six tests and, in the sophisticated deployments, an explicit Bayesian likelihood ratio.

Stream 1 · Death

  • Real medical death (Knechtle: watery serum → heart failure)
  • Crucifixion as Roman expertise (Craig: the agreed fact)
  • Defeats swoon a priori
  • Conceded by virtually all scholarship

Stream 2 · Burial

  • Honourable burial by Joseph of Arimathea
  • A known tomb — a Sanhedrist’s, not a secret one
  • A checkable site rules out cover-up / wrong-tomb
  • Knechtle, Craig, Stuart Knechtle

Stream 3 · Empty tomb + women

  • Tomb found empty by women followers
  • Female testimony legally inadmissible
  • Criterion of embarrassment: no fabricator invents it
  • Kremer: “by far most exegetes hold firmly”

Stream 4 · Transformation

  • Demoralised deserters suddenly, sincerely believe
  • Appearances to individuals and groups
  • Belief held at mortal cost (“died for what they saw”)
  • Lüdemann concedes appearances “historically certain”

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

“Miracles are by definition the least probable occurrence; historians can’t establish them.”

A resurrection is the most improbable event, so the historian can never say it probably happened. Belief is theology, not history.

Bart Ehrman, David Hume

Strongest response

The probability calculus refutes it

Pr(R|B&E) includes the explanatory power of the resurrection and the failure of the naturalistic alternatives in the denominator. Ignoring the denominator is “[Ehrman’s] egregious error”; conflating Pr(R|B) with Pr(R|B&E) is “Bart’s blunder.” And the position self-refutes: if the historian can say nothing of God, he can’t call the resurrection improbable either.

Craig Bayesian rebuttalEarman agnostic hostile witness

Objection 2

Extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence

A supernatural claim must clear a far higher evidential bar than the testimony of ancient documents can supply.

Carl Sagan, Matt Dillahunty

Strongest response

The real question is whether the event is conceivable

Bauckham (via Knechtle): if naturalism is presupposed, no evidence will suffice; if it is not, the evidence is “really very strong.” Craig (vs Atkins): if God exists, raising Jesus is “not at all improbable” — background information resets the prior.

Knechtle / Bauckham conceivability reframeCraig background-information move

Objection 3

“Probability is irrelevant after the fact.”

Historical events have already happened, so probabilistic reasoning about them is beside the point.

Matt Dillahunty

Strongest response

“Bayes’ theorem doesn’t have an arrow of time”

Tim McGrew: we invoke probability about the past constantly — this stance would, if consistent, gut evolutionary biology and the Big Bang, both inferred from present residue.

McGrew / McLatchie internal-consistency critique

Objection 4

Alien-abduction / Elvis-sighting parity

Living, multi-witness alien-abduction reports today — and even more Elvis sightings — are not believed. So multiple ancient witnesses prove nothing.

Matt Dillahunty, Peter Atkins

Strongest response

Wrong shape to explain the data

Craig: Elvis sightings are lies or hallucinations; neither explains the diversity of appearances, the willingness to die, or the empty tomb. The resurrection ran contrary to Jewish expectation — unlike miracle-craving paranormal reports.

Craig disanalogy on scope, cost, and unexpectedness

Objection 5

Hallucination / bereavement vision explains the appearances

Grief-stricken followers projected visions of the leader they had lost — a well-documented psychological phenomenon.

Christopher Hitchens, naturalist scholarship

Strongest response

Lacks scope and produces the wrong belief

Craig: hallucinations leave the empty tomb unexplained and would have produced “assumed into heaven,” not “risen” — contrary to Jewish categories. They don’t fit the diversity of witnesses. Huff: possible but not plausible; needs multiple hypotheses where the resurrection needs one.

Craig three-fold disanalogyHuff parsimony

Objection 6

Stolen body / disciples-moved-the-stone conspiracy

The disciples (or someone) removed the body and fabricated the resurrection — “Golgotha-gate.”

English deism (via Atkins)

Strongest response

17th-century deism, and anachronistic

Craig: “completely dead” in scholarship. No first-century Jew had the conceptual category of an isolated within-history resurrection. “They would either simply have gone home or else got themselves a new Messiah” — not steal a body and lie.

Craig anachronism argument

Objection 7

Swoon / wrong-tomb / unknown-twin theories

Jesus never really died, the women went to the wrong tomb, or an identical twin impersonated the risen Christ.

Popular skeptic; a UC-Irvine professor

Strongest response

Medically dead; secular scholarship has “written them off”

Knechtle: heart failure defeats swoon. Stuart: these are “off the table” in modern scholarship. Craig: the unknown-twin theory, advanced by a scholar who could not deny the four facts, is a self-undermining reductio of skeptical desperation.

Knechtle medical deathCraig twin-theory reductio

Objection 8

The disciples were honestly mistaken (breaks the Lewis trilemma)

A Bayesian fourth horn: Jesus — or the witnesses — could have been sincerely, innocently wrong about his identity and fate.

Daniel Howard-Snyder

Strongest response

A mistaken messiah leads a rebellion, not a crucifixion

McLatchie: a Jew honestly mistaken about messianic credentials would seek a Roman-rebellion messiahship, not crucifixion (cf. Justin Martyr’s Trypho, the Alexamenos graffito). Huff: the miracles themselves are incompatible with honest mistake.

McLatchie messianic-expectation mismatchHuff the miracles consideration

Objection 9

Law’s UFO/Venus background-frequency case

A 1967 UFO report — trained police, a magistrate, a corroborating radar blip — was multiply attested, hard-dataed, and false (it was Venus). Baffling reports crop up anyway, whether or not miracles occur, so a baffling case carries little evidential weight.

Stephen Law

Gap — under-engaged

Answered in 30 seconds, never fully

Craig gestures that the resurrection is no “bald anomaly” — it climaxes Jesus’ unparalleled life, and its un-Jewish unexpectedness is what paranormal-craving cases lack. But the moderator cut him short; his full multiply-attested-and-religiously-significant rebuttal never gets play.

Objection 10

“There’s no contemporary evidence Jesus even lived.”

All the evidence comes after the fact; nothing is written by a named eyewitness during Jesus’ lifetime.

Julian Baggini

Gap — under-answered

Knechtle pivots rather than rebuts

Knechtle shifts to the anthropological universality of religion instead of deploying the standard toolkit (Tacitus, Josephus, the 1 Cor 15 creed dating, Pliny). The corpus has this material elsewhere but does not bring it where it is most squarely demanded.

Aggregation gap

Objection 11

Minimal Facts is too thin — the bait-and-switch / bottleneck critique

A method that brackets gospel reliability cannot establish bodily resurrection over honest mistake; the claimed scholarly consensus on the group appearances is overstated. (This is an intra-Christian objection.)

Lydia & Tim McGrew; Paulogia

Gap — unresolved within the corpus

The two camps talk past each other

McLatchie prosecutes Maximal Data and concedes Minimal Facts “won’t cut it.” But no Minimal Facts defender answers him on his own ground — that the method is calibrated for hostile audiences who won’t grant gospel reliability. The dispute is left open.

Aggregation gap

Objection 12

Martyrdom doesn’t differentiate Christianity

Mormon witnesses and other groups also died for sincere first-person claims. “Died for what they saw” proves nothing unique.

Standard skeptical parity

Gap — asserted, not secured

The eyewitness-vs-second-hand distinction is left undefended

Knechtle insists the apostles died for a first-person observation, not a mere belief — but never defends that distinction against the obvious parity cases. The differentiator is asserted rather than argued through.
