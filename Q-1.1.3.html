Contingency vs Kalam: which one actually works?

Two cosmological arguments, routinely confused. One turns on the universe’s beginning, the other on its contingency — and the corpus quietly prefers the second, even when an agnostic is the one who says so.

The question

Of the two cosmological arguments — Kalam and contingency — which one actually carries the weight, and how far does it get you?

Where Q-1.1.1 defends the Kalam, this node asks the prior strategic question: which cosmological argument should an apologist reach for, and what does each one cost?

Kalam turns on a temporal premise — the past is finite, the universe began — and so inherits a dependence on the A-theory of time. Contingency turns on a modal premise — the universe might not have existed — and asks for a sufficient reason, sidestepping whether the past is finite. The corpus is unusual in two ways: the dominant voice (Lennox, 11 of 18 extractions) is almost entirely negative, and the cleanest case for why contingency works comes from an agnostic conceding ground.

The convergent argument — the Leibnizian contingency syllogism

Premise 1

Whatever exists has an explanation of its existence — either in the necessity of its own nature, or in an external cause.

Note the wording. Exists, not begins to exist — Craig audibly corrects himself from the Kalam phrasing. This is the principle of sufficient reason, not the causal principle. It is what lets the argument bypass the A-theory dependence that (per Nathan) sinks the Kalam.

Premise 2

If the universe has an explanation of its existence, that explanation is God.

Why a mind. A necessary, non-physical cause of all physical reality leaves only two candidates — an abstract object or an unembodied mind. Abstract objects (numbers) are causally impotent. Therefore: a transcendent, necessary, unembodied mind.

Premise 3

The universe exists, and exists contingently.

Intuition pumps. Craig’s translucent ball in the woods (scaling it to the size of a galaxy never removes the need for an explanation) and the steel shoes (a different collection of quarks would be a different universe) establish contingency against the “maybe it exists necessarily” move.

Conclusion

Therefore the explanation of the universe’s existence is God — a necessary being.

The honest limit. Both Kalam and contingency terminate at a necessary thing. The corpus’s candid disagreement — named by the agnostic Nathan and the philosopher Kappel alike — is the bridge from “a necessary thing” to the personal God of classical theism.

How each expert deploys it

John Lennox 11 transcripts
The demolition specialist — internal critique of “something from nothing”
Signature moves
  • Hawking, three levels of self-contradiction. The claim presupposes something (a law of gravity) while asserting creation from nothing; “X creates X” is circular; a law of nature depends on nature existing. “Nonsense remains nonsense even when it’s talked by world famous scientists.”
  • Krauss, equivocation on “nothing”. “Since something is physical, nothing must be physical” is incoherent; he traces Krauss’s “progressively speculative redefinitions” from nothing to quantum vacuum to “everything not forbidden must happen” (rebutted with the London-earthquake reductio).
  • Laws describe, they do not create. “Newton’s laws of motion never moved a billiard ball.” The Atkins anecdote: “two plus two equals four” never put four dollars in his pocket — “creative accounting.”
  • Hostile-witness signature. Guth at MIT (“when you use the word nothing do you mean absence of anything? No, we do not”), and Atkins corroborating on stage: “yeah, I think that’s nonsense.”
  • IBE closer. Not “I have proven God” but “the primary reality is Spirit; mass-energy is derivative” — “in the beginning was the Word” against “in the beginning were the particles.”
William Lane Craig 4 transcripts
The contingency architect — cleanest forward construction
Signature delivery
  • States the Leibnizian syllogism as contingency, not Kalam, keeping the two arguments separate even though Kalam is his “favourite”.
  • Intuition pumps: the translucent ball in the woods and the steel shoes for the contingency of the universe.
  • Swinburnean reply to Kappel’s “the universe just has no explanation”: where no scientific explanation is possible, a personal explanation remains — so the cause is an agent.
  • Cross-tradition note: Craig (Protestant) defaults to Kalam; Barron (Catholic) to contingency, and supplies the best answer to “why not call matter the necessary being?”
Major concession

The cosmological arguments are evidentially strong but existentially evadable: “you can shut your eyes to the evidence of contemporary cosmology... but every day you answer by how you live the question do you think other people have intrinsic moral worth.” The moral argument outperforms both.

Cliffe Knechtle 2 transcripts (with Stuart Knechtle)
Folk compression — uncaused → eternal → non-physical → personal
Signature delivery
  • Four-move popular form: “Nothing comes from nothing, so God is an uncaused cause... as uncaused he is eternal... as eternal he is non-physical, a spiritual being.”
  • Reserves the rhetorical weight for the gospel turn: the uncaused cause “actually gives a rip about you and me — now that’s either crazy or the most important news on the planet.” A Lewis-trilemma dichotomy applied to the gospel claim.
  • Declines the composition framing. To “what is God composed of?” he answers via uncausedness — a non-physical being is not a composite in the questioner’s sense, so the question dissolves.
Known weakness

Stuart’s positive case (“the best physical evidence for God is that the universe has a beginning”) is a popular Kalam — notably less technically careful than the agnostic Nathan he is in dialogue with, and exposed to exactly the A-theory worry Nathan raises.

Stephen Meyer 1 transcript
The matter-first vs mind-first primitive
Distinctive move
  • Frames the whole debate as a contingency-style “which is the Primitive?” question. The Dawkins “who designed the designer?” objection is symmetric: “where did those unguided undirected processes come from? You can always ask a prior question.”
  • Two candidates, “debated back to the ancient Greeks”: matter-first and mind-first. His tilt: “in light of modern cosmology, matter and energy is not a good candidate to be the Primitive... we know there was a definite beginning.”

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

“Who created God?” — infinite regress

If you ask who created God, you must ask who created the God that created the God, ad absurdum.

Richard Dawkins

Strongest response

A complex question — then reverse it

The question presupposes God was created; it only applies to created gods (idols). “You believe the universe created you — so who created your creator?” The real question is not whether there is an ultimate reality, but which reality is ultimate.

Lennox complex-question diagnosis + reversalMeyer symmetric “prior question”

Objection 2

“The universe creates itself from nothing”

“Because there is a law of gravity, the universe can and will create itself from nothing.”

Stephen Hawking, The Grand Design

Strongest response

Three levels of self-contradiction

A law of gravity is something, not nothing; “X creates X” presupposes X to explain X (“Alice in Wonderland, not science”); and a law of nature depends on nature existing, so it cannot be the creative cause of nature.

Lennox the signature demolition

Objection 3

“A universe from nothing” (quantum vacuum)

Quantum mechanics creates all observed matter from no matter without violating physics — “the first kind of miracle that science has shown is not a miracle.”

Lawrence Krauss

Strongest response

Equivocation on “nothing”

“Since something is physical, nothing must be physical” is incoherent — you cannot attribute physicality to the absence of something. The quantum vacuum is something. Guth at MIT concedes “nothing” is not the absence of being.

Lennox verbatim demolition + hostile-witness Guth

Objection 4

Net-zero energy / credits equal debits

The universe’s charges and energy sum to zero, so “nothing” separating into opposites is a simpler scientific task than positing a creator.

Peter Atkins

Strongest response

A balanced ledger is not nothing

“Because my credits equal my debts” does not mean “nothing is going on” — the charged system is real, and space-time is still smuggled in. The argument simplifies the question without answering where the charges came from.

Lennox (Atkins concedes the Krauss point on stage)

Objection 5

“Why not just call matter the necessary being?”

What you call God, I’ll call matter or energy — it has always been there, just assuming different forms.

Sophisticated atheist

Strongest response

Matter is by its nature contingent

Matter never exists “as such” — only in some particular form, configuration, intensity, or position, each of which needs explaining. The appeal to matter doesn’t solve the problem; it postpones it.

Barron Aquinas-style contingencyCraig endorses

Objection 6

Maybe the universe exists necessarily (denies P3)

Grant that contingent things need explanation — why think the universe is contingent rather than the necessary being itself?

Klemens Kappel

Strongest response

Conceivability + the shoes analogy

A different collection of quarks would be a different universe, so this one is contingent. No contemporary philosopher or physicist holds that the universe exists metaphysically necessarily.

Craig translucent ball + steel shoes

Objection 7

The universe might have no explanation

If the laws came into being with the universe, and explaining an event means subsuming it under laws, then there is no explanation of that event at all.

Klemens Kappel

Strongest response

Swinburne’s two modes of explanation

Explanation can be scientific (laws + initial conditions) or personal (an agent + his volitions). At an absolute origin no scientific explanation is possible — therefore the cause must be a personal agent.

Craig the Swinburnean reply

Objection 8

“First cause” is a placeholder for ignorance

“Supernatural” is just a linguistic placeholder for “I don’t know” — a conversation-stopper, no better than “dark energy.”

Michael Shermer

Strongest response

The buck stops on both sides

Both worldviews terminate explanation somewhere — eternal matter or eternal Mind. “In the beginning was the Word” is a better inference than “in the beginning were the particles.” Shermer concedes the regress is genuinely hard for both sides.

Lennox matter-or-Mind IBE

Objection 9

Cosmic waste undermines the “privileged planet”

The universe is heading to nothingness — accelerating expansion, Andromeda collision, sun-death. What deity claims credit for the nothingness?

Christopher Hitchens

Strongest response

The universe was never meant to be permanent

Biblical eschatology already predicts heat death; the universe is “a stepping stage,” not a permanent phenomenon, and God’s “new manifestation” (new heavens and earth) is the larger context.

Lennox eschatological reframe

Objection 10

Quantum events happen without a cause

Vacuum fluctuations and quantum tunnelling are cases where particles — or the universe itself — come into existence by chance, without any external cause, throwing P1 into doubt.

Arif Ahmed (Cambridge Union)

Gap — not addressed

Craig structurally locked out

Ahmed delivers this in the final speech; Craig has no opportunity to respond. The standard “virtual particles are not from literal nothing” reply never appears in the corpus.

Aggregation gap

Objection 11

A necessary being is not the personal God of theism

Even granting a transcendent cause, “is that a mind we can pray to?” It’s “a far cry from an incarnational God or a Trinity” — the explanation raises more questions than it solves.

Nathan (agnostic); Klemens Kappel

Gap — deferred, not closed

Defer to the cumulative case

Craig’s only reply is that the other arguments aggregate to specify the attributes of God. Within the cosmological argument itself, no expert bridges “a necessary thing” to even a personal cause. This is the corpus’s largest gap — named by its friendliest opponent.

Craig cumulative-case fallbackBridge unproven
