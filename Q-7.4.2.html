Does middle knowledge reconcile sovereignty and freedom?

Molinism’s claim — that God knows what any free creature would freely do in any circumstance — offered as the mechanism that makes foreknowledge compatible with freedom. An in-house Christian dispute, treated by three experts.

The question

Can God exhaustively foreknow and sovereignly order free creaturely choices without determining them?

This is an in-house Christian dispute, not a believer-versus-sceptic one. Molinism sits between Calvinist determinism (God decrees what creatures will do) and open theism (God does not foreknow free future choices at all). Its claim is that God possesses a third category of knowledge — between his natural knowledge of necessary truths and his free knowledge of what he has decreed — namely counterfactuals of creaturely freedom: what any free creature would freely do in any possible circumstance.

The stakes run beyond the doctrine itself. Middle knowledge is load-bearing for the free-will defence against the problem of evil and for Craig’s fate-of-the-unevangelised argument. If middle knowledge fails, both inherit the damage.

The convergent argument — all three experts

Premise 1

God knows counterfactuals of creaturely freedom — what each free creature would freely do in each possible circumstance.

Observed variants. Craig states it with deliberate understatement: “is C true? God knows! At least if he has middle knowledge.” Jones says God “predestined from his middle knowledge.” Lennox grounds the same premise not in philosophy but directly in Christ’s words at Matthew 11:21.

Premise 2

God surveys the feasible worlds and actualises one — but he cannot actualise a world in which a creature acts contrary to what it would freely choose.

Load-bearing distinction. Feasible worlds are a subset of possible worlds — those God can actualise given the counterfactuals he is handed (Craig). Jones’s vernacular version: God “could not predestine that world without … determining me.”

Conclusion

Therefore foreknowledge is compatible with freedom: free actions fix what God foreknows and providentially orders, not the reverse.

Prediction is not causation. Jones: “omniscience isn’t fatalism … what we just do and how we act decides” what God foreknows. Craig applies it: God co-actualises responsive people where the Gospel reaches them.

How each expert deploys it

William Lane Craig 1 transcript
The architect — the providence machinery
Signature delivery
  • Establishes P1 with deliberate understatement: “is C true? God knows! At least if he has middle knowledge” — the pun concedes the hinge assumption while asserting the counterfactual has a truth-value.
  • Possible vs feasible worlds is load-bearing throughout: feasible worlds are a subset God can actualise given the counterfactuals he does not get to choose.
  • The “you can’t lift a segment out of a world” rebuttal — his signature refutation of the heaven objection.
  • Co-actualisation through missions: God places people who would believe where the Gospel reaches, and would have created different (responsive) people had the missionary gone elsewhere.

Michael Jones 1 transcript (Inspiring Philosophy)
The popular Molinist — barometer analogy
Signature moves
  • Predestination from middle knowledge, not from decree: God surveys the possible worlds where free creatures choose freely and actualises the best one.
  • Fireman / hairdresser illustration: a world where Jones became a fireman is on the table; a world where he became a hairdresser may not exist, and God “could not predestine that world without … determining me.”
  • The infallible weather barometer — the node’s defining analogy: omniscience “always going to predict the weather but it doesn’t cause the weather.”
  • Presents the Molinist frame as the structural reply to a determinist “you can’t choose to believe” run by some atheists. Cites his own video The Omniscience Paradox Debunked.
John Lennox 1 transcript
The provisional, label-severing affirmation
Distinctive moves
  • Label-resistance is sociological, not theological: people react against the -ism, not the idea.
  • Christ himself affirms the thesis: in the upbraiding of the cities (Matthew 11:21) Jesus states what Tyre, Sidon, Sodom and Gomorrah would have done under different evidence — the node’s only exegetical anchor.
  • Affirms it “whether you call that molinism or not” — severing the school label from the biblical commitment to lower the cost of agreement for wary evangelicals.
  • Credibility-by-humility: he might “modify a little bit” of Determined to Believe, but correspondence has given “no reason … to retract very much.”
Declined engagement

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

Omniscience entails fatalism — if God knows it, you must do it

If God infallibly foreknows your choice, then it is fixed in advance, so you cannot do otherwise — foreknowledge collapses into determinism.

Popular / atheist determinist (flagged by Jones)

Strongest response

Prediction is not causation — the barometer analogy

An infallible barometer always predicts the weather but never causes it. Omniscience tracks free choices; it does not fix them. “What we just do and how we act decides” what God foreknows, not the reverse.

Jones infallible-barometer analogy

Objection 2

God could just create heaven — a world where everyone freely does right

If a world exists where free creatures always choose the good without overriding their freedom, an all-good God should simply actualise it — just create heaven from the start.

Facebook student, in Craig’s Q&A

Strongest response

You cannot lift a segment out of a possible world

Two errors. (a) It begs the question by assuming there is freedom to sin in heaven, which is not obvious. (b) More fundamentally, you cannot take a sub-segment of a world and re-instantiate it as a standalone possible world while assuming the same counterfactuals of freedom carry over — the isolated world is a new possible world with potentially different counterfactuals.

Craig segment-lifting rebuttal

Objection 3

Molinist “predestination” is just Calvinist determinism in disguise

If God picks which world to actualise, he is still picking your choices for you — the freedom is cosmetic.

Implicit Calvinist objection

Strongest response

Predestination is from middle knowledge, not over freedom

God “could not predestine that world without … determining me.” The worlds available to God are constrained by what creatures would freely choose; he selects among feasible worlds rather than imposing choices. By construction this is not determinism.

Jones feasible-worlds constraint

Objection 4

“Molinism” is a sectarian philosophical novelty unmoored from Scripture

It sounds like a speculative -ism imported from philosophy, foreign to the biblical text — why should an evangelical adopt it?

Evangelical suspicion of philosophical theology

Strongest response

Christ affirms the idea, whatever you call it

The underlying claim — that God knows what people would freely do under counterfactual circumstances — is stated by Jesus at Matthew 11:21, “whether you call it molinism or not.” The label is dispensable; the biblical commitment is not.

Lennox Matthew 11:21 anchor

Objection 5

Missions are pointless if God has already ordered who responds

If sovereignty has fixed who will believe, evangelism changes nothing — the elect are saved regardless.

Standard determinism worry

Strongest response

Co-actualisation — missions are the means, not a redundancy

God places responsive people where the Gospel reaches because he knew the missionary would go; had the missionary gone elsewhere, God would have created different, responsive people there. Missions are the providential mechanism by which sovereignty operates.

Craig co-actualisation through missions

Objection 6

The grounding objection

What makes a counterfactual of creaturely freedom true, prior to God’s decree and prior to the creature even existing? If nothing grounds these truths, God has nothing to know — and Molinism collapses.

Robert Adams, William Hasker (academic literature)

Gap — never raised

The central academic objection is absent

Craig asserts the counterfactuals have truth-values (“God knows!”) but the popular corpus offers no defence of that assertion. The single most-discussed objection to Molinism in the philosophical literature does not appear anywhere in these three transcripts.

Aggregation gap

Objection 7

The Calvinist counter-position

Reformed determinism holds that God decrees creaturely choices directly, and that libertarian freedom is neither necessary nor biblical. Why prefer Molinism to it?

James White, Reformed theology (Craig-vs-White exchange)

Gap — declined

Named by the host, never engaged

Lennox explicitly declines Calvinist objections; the Craig-vs-White exchange is referenced by Brierley but its substance never enters the corpus. The most relevant in-house opponent is gestured at and dropped.
