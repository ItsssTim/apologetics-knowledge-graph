The question

Were Neanderthals (and Denisovans) fully human image-bearers descended from Adam — and how far back does humanity reach?

A §13 Anthropology question that straddles §5 Science and Faith. It is the empirical hinge on which one model of the historical Adam turns: if the cognitive and symbolic signatures of humanness appear in Neanderthals and their predecessors, then humanity is not co-extensive with Homo sapiens, and Adam — the first image-bearer — must be pushed back to the common ancestor of sapiens and Neanderthals, Homo heidelbergensis (~750 kya).

The case does double duty. It fixes the date of Adam, and it inoculates the imago Dei against discriminatory misuse: any model that locates Adam later than the Neanderthal/sapiens split “writes the Neanderthals out of the human race” — and the same exclusionary logic, applied to living populations, has underwritten history's worst racism.

This is a single-voice deep-dive. The load-bearing lecture is Craig's Defenders Part 15; two panel sources show the same thesis stress-tested by sympathetic evangelical colleagues who hold a historical Adam but resist his exclusivist conclusions. The corpus is dense and the dating evidence is contested — this page marks that density honestly.

The cumulative case — one genetic stream, three discoveries

Not a deductive syllogism but an inference to the best explanation. Anatomy supplies the necessary condition (near-identical skeleton, equal brain capacity); the four streams below supply the sufficient condition — behavioural humanness. No single stream is decisive; the cumulative weight is the argument. Each discovery is sourced to its lead secular investigator.

Stream 1 · Interbreeding

  • 2010 Neanderthal genome reconstruction
  • Sapiens interbred with Neanderthals & Denisovans over tens of thousands of years
  • ~2% Neanderthal DNA outside Africa; extra Denisovan DNA in Oceania
  • Repeated intercourse “plausibly requires communication and hence language”
  • Cited: Kai Witting

Stream 2 · Bruniquel Cave

  • 176 kya — Neanderthals Europe's only humans
  • 336 m deep; sealed since the Pleistocene, undisturbed
  • ~400 stalagmite pieces in rings and heaps, traces of fire
  • Designed, discussed, task-divided project → social organisation, ritual
  • Cited: Jacques Jaubert

Stream 3 · Spanish cave art

  • ≥64,800–66,700 years ago (U-Th dating)
  • Maltravieso, La Pasiega, Ardales — stencils & non-figurative paintings
  • ~20,000 years before sapiens reached Europe → artists were Neanderthals
  • Pigment/shell use elsewhere >115 kya
  • Cited: Hoffmann et al.

Stream 4 · Schöningen spears

  • 400,000–300,000 years ago — Homo heidelbergensis
  • Sculpted 6–7 ft throwing spears, weight forward
  • Matched modern Olympic javelins when reproduced
  • Found with butchered horse remains → systematic hunting, foresight, planning
  • Cited: Hartmut Thieme
Necessary condition

Anatomical near-identity and equal brain capacity.

Neanderthal–sapiens skeletal differences “are not major” — a cold-adapted, reinforced physique with brain capacity equal to or exceeding modern man's. (Stringer & Andrews, The Complete World of Human Evolution.)

Conclusion

Neanderthals — and Heidelberg man — exhibit the cognitive signs of human behaviour, and so belong to the human family descended from Adam.

The payload. Humanity is not confined to Homo sapiens; Adam is located at Homo heidelbergensis, the common ancestor of sapiens and Neanderthals (~750 kya). The verdict arrives from mainstream palaeoanthropology, not apologetic special pleading.

How the case is built — and contested

The architect — three-discovery cumulative case
Signature delivery (Defenders, 2020)
  • Establishes the necessary condition (anatomy + brain size), then “home[s] in now on just three archaeological discoveries that confirm this conclusion.”
  • Reads interbreeding as evidence of communication and language over tens of thousands of years.
  • Sources each discovery to its lead secular investigator — Jaubert, Hoffmann, Thieme — a deliberate neutral-witness credibility move.
  • Keyword “prejudicial”: denying the humanity of the Neanderthal artists, given identical behavioural signatures, is framed as a prejudice.
  • Structural imago Dei: humans resemble God as “self-conscious rational free persons”; functional and relational views both presuppose this ontological substrate.
  • Time-machine thought experiment: a Neanderthal brought into the present “we want to treat… as members of the human family.” Chauvet/Lascaux artists are “one of us”; Australopithecines and Homo habilis were “just bipedal apes.”
The hard corollary, stated plainly

The parity argument that includes Neanderthals simultaneously excludes their non-Adamic contemporaries: “these are sub-human beasts in effect… they're not in the image of God… not people for whom Christ died.” The corpus's hardest edge — asserted as the price of the model, not separately defended.

The multi-definition foil
Distinctive moves
  • Affirms a historical Adam but rejects Craig's single definition of “human.” There is no scientific consensus on the term.
  • Semantic pluralism: Craig's substantive definition and the scriptural definition (“Adam and Eve and their descendants”) are both valid simultaneously.
  • Normalises a “non-human person” category: “not human in the same way that Klingons are not human… in the same way that angels are not human.”
  • On bestiality: viable offspring proves the partner is “human in critical scientific senses” — a third category, neither beast nor theologically human.

Objections handled — cross-source master list

Objection 1

Bigger brains don't mean more intelligence

Elephants and whales have much larger brains than humans, so equal-or-larger Neanderthal brain capacity proves nothing about cognition.

Facebook follow-up to Defenders Part 14

Strongest response

Within hominins, brain size does track cognition — and EQ confirms it

Among hominins specifically, brain size is positively correlated with cognitive capacity. The encephalisation quotient (brain-to-body-mass ratio) is small for whales and elephants but ~7 for humans — so both EQ and absolute size confirm the correlation.

Craig EQ + absolute-size reply

Objection 2

Interbreeding may have been non-consensual / non-communicative

If the encounters were coercive, interbreeding would not imply shared humanity, mutual recognition, or language.

Implicit (Defenders)

Strongest response

Unreasonable to assume all were coercive

It is “quite unreasonable to assume that all sexual encounters… were of the non-consensual variety”; at least some interbreeding was the result of communication and mutual recognition over tens of thousands of years.

Craig / Kai Witting consent-and-communication reply

Objection 3

The spears might be crude sticks like chimps make

Sharpened sticks are within ape capability; sophisticated tools would be needed to demonstrate human cognition.

Implicit (Defenders)

Strongest response

Olympic-grade engineered javelins, not sharpened twigs

These are 6–7 ft sculpted, weight-forward throwing spears on a par with modern Olympic javelins when reproduced — nothing like chimp-sharpened sticks. (The auto-caption drops a “not” in this sentence; see footer.)

Craig tool-sophistication reply

Objection 4

The “bestiality” objection

If Adam's descendants interbred with non-Adamic hominins, that is bestiality — humans coupling with beasts.

Marcus Ross (EPS panel)

Strongest response

Viable offspring proves the partner is human

Swamidass: scriptural bestiality is humans trying to breed with beasts that cannot produce offspring. Anything that interbreeds with humans and produces viable offspring is “human in critical scientific senses” — a third category. Loke adds: anatomical humanness alone defeats the charge.

Swamidass viable-offspring criterionLoke anatomical-sufficiency reply

Objection 5

The racial-exclusion analogy equivocates

Denying Neanderthals full humanity is not equivalent to denying Africans full humanity. Paleo-species categories are categorially distinct from contemporary racial categories, with direct social consequences “right here and now.” The parity argument does not transfer.

Joshua Swamidass (Capturing Christianity)

Response — concedes the analogy would otherwise bite

Only extinction spares us the problem

Craig: the analogy fails to bite hard “just because the Neanderthals have gone extinct”; were they alive, we would face the discrimination problem. This concedes Swamidass's categorial point rather than refuting it — the disagreement is left standing.

Unresolved — sharpest live disagreement in the corpus

Objection 6

Functional / relational imago Dei makes image-bearing contingent

If the image is a vocational calling (election to dominion), it could be separable from structural humanity — so Neanderthals might have the capacities yet lack the image.

Implicit / Loke (vocational view)

Strongest response

Structure underlies function — “almost unavoidable”

Craig: whatever view of the image one adopts — structural, functional, or relational — there must be “something about the ontology… that enable[s] them to carry out that function or stand in that relation.” The structural view underlies the others.

Craig structuralist-priority reply

Objection 7

“Human” has no single agreed definition

There is no scientific consensus on what defines “human,” so Craig's substantive verdict is not the only legitimate reading. Multiple definitions — substantive and genealogical — can be valid at once.

Joshua Swamidass (EPS panel)

Gap — genuinely contested

Conceded in part by all parties

Craig holds the substantive definition is decisive for moral worth and the image; Swamidass and Loke hold multiple definitions coexist. No party resolves the semantic dispute; it is the unargued background of the whole exchange.

Unresolved — definitional, not evidential

Persistent gaps in the corpus

Where the case leans on still-debated archaeology, or on philosophical commitments its sympathetic critics decline to share.

Gap 1

The broader-breeding-population problem is left implicit

Pushing Adam to Homo heidelbergensis (~750 kya) makes him very ancient and raises whether non-Adamic Heidelberg men existed and whether interbreeding with them was bestiality. The Defenders lecture stays on the “Neanderthals were human” thesis and defers this corollary (engaged only in the genealogical-Adam material, Q-13.4.7).

Aggregation gap

Gap 2

The dating of behavioural modernity is contested

The Spanish cave-art U-Th dates (≥64–66 kya) that make the artists Neanderthals have been challenged since (contamination, U-Th reliability on carbonate crusts). Craig presents the chronology as settled; the inference “art predates sapiens in Europe, therefore artists were Neanderthals” is only as strong as the disputed dates.

Aggregation gap

Gap 3

The encephalisation-quotient argument is asserted, not defended

The reply to Objection 1 treats EQ ~7 and absolute brain size as jointly confirming cognition, but EQ is a coarse, debated proxy across taxa. The corpus asserts the correlation rather than defending it against the comparative-cognition literature.

Aggregation gap

Gap 4

Denisovans are never treated on their own terms

Denisovans appear only as interbreeding partners. Whether the symbolic-cognition evidence that carries the Neanderthal verdict transfers to a population known mostly from genetics and a few fragments is not addressed.

Aggregation gap

Gap 5

The equivocation charge is unresolved

Swamidass's claim that paleo-species categories are categorially distinct from racial categories — so the anti-racism parity argument does not transfer — is answered only with the extinction rejoinder, which concedes the analogy would otherwise have force. The disagreement is left standing.

Aggregation gap

Gap 6

“Sub-human beasts” is asserted, not defended

Craig's claim that non-Adamic contemporaries lack intrinsic moral worth and are not those “for whom Christ died” is the model's hardest edge. It receives no independent argument — it is the price of the model stated plainly, not a conclusion separately established.
