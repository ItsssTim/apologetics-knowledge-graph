“Extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence”

The Bayesian rebuttal of Sagan’s slogan — the methodological keystone of the historical apologetic. A low prior is not a veto. Three experts treat it in seven substantive extractions.

The question

Does “extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence” actually defeat the case for a miracle — or does it collapse under a proper probability analysis?

Almost every resurrection debate eventually reaches this slogan. The skeptic invokes Hume, Sagan, Ehrman or Dillahunty to argue that a miracle is by definition the least probable explanation, so no historical evidence could ever make it the best explanation. If that move stands, the entire minimal-facts apologetic is dead on arrival.

The reply is not “we have extraordinary evidence.” It is a structural rebuttal: the slogan misunderstands how evidence works on Bayes’s theorem. A low prior is not insuperable; it can be overwhelmed by a sufficiently top-heavy likelihood ratio. And for Jesus specifically the prior is not vanishingly small once the theo-historical backdrop is loaded in.

The convergent argument — all three experts

Working at different registers — Craig the formal probabilist, McLatchie the self-described “card-carrying Bayesian epistemologist,” Stuart Knechtle the street-level rhetorician — all three deploy a recognisable version of the same probabilistic structure.

Premise 1

Probability is always relative to background knowledge and evidence; “evidence” is whatever raises the probability of a proposition above its prior.

Observed forms. Craig: P(R | B&E) and P(R | B) are two different probabilities. McLatchie’s forensic murder-weapon: the weapon doesn’t prove guilt, but it is evidence because it is “rendered more probable if we suppose that he is guilty.”

Premise 2

A low prior is not insuperable. A top-heavy likelihood ratio can swamp it.

Supports. McLatchie: “a top-heavy ratio… greater than one” — a Bayes factor of 100 means the evidence is 100× more likely on your hypothesis. Craig: as the naturalistic-alternative term y → 0, the posterior → 1 (certainty).

Premise 3

The slogan’s error is to weigh only the miracle’s intrinsic improbability and ignore the strength of the naturalistic alternatives.

Named fallacies. Craig calls this “the airman’s egregious error” — ignoring the denominator. Stuart calls the slogan “a spectrum of subjectivity” and a goal-post-mover.

Premise 4

The resurrection’s prior is not as low as claimed, because independent theo-historical evidence raises it specifically for Jesus.

Prior elevators. Messianic prophecy, Jesus’s divine self-identity, Passover-lamb typology (Exodus 12), Isaiah 49 / 53, and the “vanishingly small” odds of a persecuted pre-313 sect becoming a global religion. McLatchie: “we’re not dealing with some miscellaneous bloke.”

Conclusion

Therefore the resurrection is an explanation that should be on the table — a live contender whose posterior can be high even though its prior on background knowledge alone is low.

The pivot. The argument is probabilistic, not deductive. Its force depends on the comparative weakness of the naturalistic alternatives — which is exactly where Craig says the skeptic must “erect a positive case of his own.”

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

A miracle is by definition the least probable explanation

So a historian can never infer it as the best or most probable explanation — the deck is stacked before any evidence is weighed.

Hume, Ehrman, Dillahunty, E.H. Carr

Strongest response

It conflates two different probabilities

P(R | B&E) is not P(R | B). A low prior can be overwhelmed by a top-heavy likelihood ratio. There is no contradiction in “the most improbable event is the most probable” — the two probabilities are about different things.

Craig “Bart’s blunder”McLatchie top-heavy ratio

Objection 2

You must use the resurrection’s intrinsic improbability to calculate its probability

Because historians can only establish what probably happened, and the resurrection is wildly improbable, they cannot say it occurred.

Bart Ehrman

Strongest response

You ignored the denominator

You must also weigh the intrinsic probability and explanatory power of the naturalistic alternatives (term Y). As Y → 0 the posterior → 1. The skeptic must “erect a positive case of his own,” not merely tear down the evidence.

Craig the airman’s egregious error

Objection 3

The slogan settles it — extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence

A resurrection is the most extraordinary claim imaginable; ordinary historical documents could never meet the bar.

Sagan, the Inquisitor, O’Connor

Strongest response

The slogan is subjective and goal-post-moving

It is “a spectrum of subjectivity.” A stack of ordinary evidence amounts to extraordinary evidence; and any logically possible explanation can in principle be confirmed by sufficient evidence.

Stuart Knechtle subjectivity / goal-postsMcLatchie any explanation confirmable

Objection 4

The Hitchcock parity test

If the same historical facts were presented for Alfred Hitchcock rising from the dead, none of us would be convinced — so the document evidence is insufficient for Jesus too.

Alex O’Connor

Strongest response

Jesus is not “some miscellaneous bloke”

Hitchcock has no theological backdrop; Jesus’s prior is independently raised by messianic and divine credentials and the Passover typology, so the parity fails. The prior for raising Jesus is not equivalent to the prior for raising a random person.

McLatchie prior-elevation against parity

Objection 5

Methodological naturalism is foundational to history

Remove naturalism and you remove the efficacy of the historical method itself — and besides, we make this kind of argument all the time.

Granado, E.H. Carr (What is History)

Strongest response

Distinguish science-naturalism from history-naturalism

Ordinary history may default to naturalism, but the prior can be independently raised in specific cases. “We make this argument all the time” is not a defence of the argument against the charge that it is fundamentally flawed.

McLatchie internal critique

Objection 6

Slippery slope — accept Jesus and you must accept every miracle

If miracle-reasoning works for Jesus, you must accept Muhammad’s miracles, Buddha’s miracles, and every uncle who saw an alien.

Granado

Strongest response

Case-by-case, prior tracks divine motivation

Each claim is investigated individually; the prior depends on antecedent evidence of divine motivation, which Jesus uniquely has. This is not an “open the gates” policy — no miracle claim should be allowed to stand by default.

McLatchie case-by-case investigation

Objection 7

Historians can’t presuppose God — so resurrection is theology, not history

Historians of any persuasion must reason from what is “available among us”; Craig’s hypothesis requires belief in God, so it is not history.

Bart Ehrman

Strongest response

Self-refuting on God-talk

If the historian can say nothing about God, he cannot say the resurrection is improbable either. And physics routinely postulates unobservables; we are not bound by professional constraints in our “off hours.”

Craig internal contradiction + unobservables

Objection 8

Objective evidence outranks testimony

DNA, archaeology and forensics are objective and “don’t lie”; testimony is second-class — and first-century Jews were pre-scientific and superstitious anyway.

John Loftus

Strongest response

“Cargo cult epistemology”

Physical objects are not self-interpreting (the x-ray); most knowledge — even archaeological fact — reaches us via testimony; and the Innocence Project shows “objective” forensics convict the innocent. Birth, death and execution are universal human competencies.

Tim McGrew x-ray / metal detectorLydia McGrew Innocence ProjectGilson panel

Objection 9

Probability is irrelevant “after the fact”

Once a past event has either happened or not, probability talk no longer applies to it.

Matt Dillahunty

Strongest response

Bayes has no arrow of time

Past, present and future are not components in Bayes’s theorem. All historical science — evolutionary biology, the Big Bang — invokes probability about the past from present residue. To deny it is to bin all historical science.

Tim McGrew no arrow of timeMcLatchie consistency across historical science

Objection 10

Is “deviation from uniform experience” itself a justified prior-setter?

Hume’s actual core claim: uniform experience against miracles rightly sets a near-zero prior. The experts show the prior can be lifted, but should the base prior be that low to begin with?

David Hume (the deeper premise)

Gap — not refuted on its own terms

Every expert lifts the prior; none defends the base prior

McLatchie (vs Greenberg) is explicitly flagged as leaving the Ehrman / Carr definition unrefuted on its own terms. The corpus answers “the prior can be raised” without answering “should the base prior be near-zero at all?”

Aggregation gap

Objection 11

Why not a natural cause we just don’t understand?

Even granting the theo-historical backdrop, why prefer “God raised Jesus” over “Jesus rose by some unknown natural means”? The comparative-prior calculation is owed.

Implicit in Granado’s and O’Connor’s pressure

Gap — gestured at, not spelled out

The comparative prior is asserted, not calculated

McLatchie claims “God raising Jesus” has a much higher prior than any other cause “because of the theo-historical context,” but the raw notes flag that the comparative calculation “isn’t spelled out.” The selection-bias / survivorship cousin of the slippery-slope is never pre-empted.
