The question

Is the Qur’an textually preserved better than the Bible — and does the Muslim “your scripture is corrupted, ours is perfect” charge survive its own evidence?

The standard Muslim case runs in two moves: the Bible was originally revealed but has been textually corrupted (tahrif), with Christian scholars themselves admitting it; and the Qur’an, by contrast, has been perfectly preserved — “not a single letter changed in fourteen centuries.” If both halves stand, Christianity’s evidential foundation collapses and Islam inherits it.

The Christian counter is not primarily a defence of biblical preservation. It is a parity reversal: every textual problem the Muslim presses against the Bible applies with greater force to the Qur’an, on Islam’s own traditional sources. The argument is won or lost on consistency of method — apply one yardstick to both books and see which survives.

The argument structure — a cumulative parity case

Unlike a deductive syllogism, this is a cumulative parity case built from four converging streams, capped by McLatchie’s one genuinely deductive move (the Qur’an Dilemma). Each stream is contested independently; the cumulative force is that one method, applied consistently, breaks the Qur’an before it breaks the Bible.

Stream 1 · Claim is false

  • Sanaa palimpsest — washed-out under-text
  • Birmingham Qur’an (2015) — variants
  • Hafs (97%) vs Warsh (~3%) divergence
  • 1924 Egyptian Hafs is one recension
  • Refutes Surah 15:9 / Qazi “not one letter”

Stream 2 · Islam’s own sources

  • Verse lost at Battle of Yamama (Abu Dawud)
  • Ibn Umar: “much of the quran has been lost”
  • Uthman destroys variant codices
  • Ubai ibn Kaab’s codex suppressed
  • al-Kindy: leaves “boiled in vinegar”

Stream 3 · Parity / tu quoque

  • No Qur’anic autographs either
  • At least 26 Arabic Qur’ans today
  • Marginal NT variants openly footnoted
  • Inerrancy applies to the autographs
  • “Your argument backfires on you”
Stream 4

The New Testament is a freely transmitted text; the Qur’an is a controlled text.

Huff’s organising thesis. ~24,000 NT manuscripts, multi-vocal, dispersed beyond central control — which makes the original recoverable. The Qur’an is a single-thread, centrally-edited line — a “bean pole” rather than a “family tree” — which makes it unverifiable. Counter-intuitively, more visible variants mean more confidence, not less.

Dilemma P1–P3

Either the Bible is the word of God or it is not — and on both horns the Qur’an is not.

McLatchie’s deductive spine. If the Bible is God’s word, the Qur’an contradicts it (the crucifixion) and so is not. If the Bible is not God’s word, the Qur’an (Surah 10:94; 5:68/47) affirms the Torah and Injil are God’s word — and so is still not.

Conclusion

Apply one textual-critical method to both books, and the Qur’an’s preservation claim fails first — on Islam’s own sources.

The cap. The cumulative streams soften the ground; the dilemma closes it. Even a secular Islamicist (Fred Donner) concedes the field is “in a state of disarray.”

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

The Bible is corrupted — and Christian scholars admit it

Cragg, James White (pericope adulterae), Licona (the Matt 27 “zombies”), William Lane Craig — Christian scholars themselves concede the text has been changed.

Aqil Onque (Muslim debater)

Strongest response

Footnoted, doctrine-neutral, autograph-recoverable

The admitted passages (1 John 5:7; John 7:53–8:11; Mark 16:9–20) are openly footnoted; no doctrine depends on them. Inerrancy applies only to the autographs, which textual criticism recovers.

McLatchie autograph + footnote argument

Objection 2

The Qur’an is perfectly preserved (Surah 15:9)

“No change has occurred in a single word, a single letter… during the last fourteen centuries.” God promised to guard it from corruption.

Mazhar Kazi; traditional Sunni orthodoxy

Strongest response

Empirically false on extant manuscripts

The Sanaa palimpsest shows correction “in the text as a whole”; the Birmingham Qur’an shows variants; and the Hafs (97%) and Warsh (~3%) text-forms still co-exist and differ. The apologetic claim is contradicted by the manuscript record.

Huff manuscript evidenceMcLatchie 26 recensions

Objection 3

No autographs → no reliable Bible

We do not possess a single original manuscript of any biblical book, so we cannot know what the autographs said.

Aqil Onque

Strongest response

The attack backfires

“Where are the original autographs of the Quran? We don’t have any.” The objection, applied consistently, destroys the Qur’an’s case as thoroughly as the Bible’s — and the NT’s manuscript depth makes recovery far easier.

McLatchie Socratic reversal

Objection 4

The numerical miracles (7 / 19) prove divine authorship

The word “seven” occurs 28 times (7×4), the Arabic alphabet has 28 letters, 28 is a “perfect number,” and one word’s placement has a “1 in 117,000” chance — no human placed these.

Shabir Ally

Strongest response

Debunked by Muslims; Christian parallels equally weak

Muslim scholars themselves rejected the patterns (Bilal Philips on the number 19); Christian apologists (Chuck Missler) made parallel claims for the Bible that are just as weak. And the patterns rest on the 1924 Cairo edition — itself one recension among many.

McLatchie internal-Muslim refutation + parity

Objection 5

Multiple Bible recensions; the canon was imposed

The Bible has competing recensions, and forgeries circulated early (2 Thess 2:2; Gal 1:6–7). Who authorised the people who decided what counts as God’s word?

Aqil Onque

Strongest response

Recognised, not imposed — and parity on recensions

The canon was recognised by apostolicity, orthodoxy and catholicity, not invented; Paul himself flags forgeries, showing the church was already filtering. Meanwhile there are at least 26 Arabic Qur’ans, none pre-1924 matching the 1924 Hafs.

McLatchie canon criteria + recension parity

Objection 6

Are the Islamic traditional sources even reliable?

If the hadith reports of lost verses and Uthmanic destruction are themselves unreliable, the Christian cannot use them to attack Qur’anic preservation.

Anticipated Muslim reply

Strongest response

Bracket it — argue from within

“Whether that’s true or not is a debate in of itself.” Huff deliberately reasons from within the sources Muslims themselves treat as historically reliable, so the conclusion binds the orthodox Muslim regardless of how the reliability debate resolves.

Huff internal-source method

Objection 7

The two books aren’t comparable

The Bible is 66 books written over centuries by many hands; the Qur’an is one book codified in 25 years in one location. Comparing their transmission is apples to oranges.

Self-raised by Huff

Strongest response

Concede it — it favours free transmission anyway

Huff grants the asymmetry openly, then shows it cuts the wrong way for the Muslim: the dispersed, multi-authored, harder-to-control text is exactly the one whose original is recoverable.

Huff conceded-fairness framing

Objection 8

The cake analogy — God still calls it “scripture”

A partly-eaten cake is still “the cake.” So when the Qur’an calls the prior writings “scripture,” it endorses their identity, not their textual integrity — defeating the dilemma’s third premise.

Aqil Onque

Gap — not answered on its own terms

Dilemma advanced, analogy untouched

McLatchie presses the formal dilemma but never dismantles the cake analogy directly. It remains the Muslim’s live escape from P3, and the corpus leaves it standing.

Objection 9

Tawatur — mass oral memorisation preserves the Qur’an

Hafiz memorisation transmits the Qur’an independently of any manuscript line. So palimpsests, lost folios and Uthman’s recension miss the point — the living oral chain is the real preservation.

Standard Muslim defence (Q-6.2.2)

Gap — the single biggest hole

Whole case targets parchment, not memory

Both Huff transcripts critique the written collation; neither engages the strongest Muslim claim, which is oral. The “controlled text” argument never touches tawatur. This is the priority gap-fill target.
