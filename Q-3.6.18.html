The question

Why do Jews reject Jesus as the Messiah — and do the anti-missionary objections defeat the Christian claim?

This is the oldest live objection to the gospel: the people from whose Scriptures the messianic hope arose are also its longest-standing dissenters. The Jewish anti-missionary case is not historical — it concedes Jesus existed — but exegetical and prophetic. The claim is that Jesus failed the messianic job description the Hebrew Bible itself lays down: world peace, the ingathering of Israel, a rebuilt temple, universal knowledge of God, the end of war.

The node is structurally adversarial. Unlike “Did Jesus claim to be God?”, the Christian here is on defence against a catalogue of unfulfilled prophecies. The dominant move is the two-comings response — that the Old Testament itself anticipates a first coming (the pierced one) and a second (Yahweh on the Mount of Olives) — defended not as ad hoc special pleading but as the very methodology Orthodox Jews already use for the return-from-exile prophecies.

The convergent argument — a defensive cumulative case

This is not a syllogism but an abductive weight-of-evidence defence. The Christian concedes some anomalous data (apparent unfulfilled prophecies), argues each is only weakly disconfirming because a coherent alternative reading exists, and claims the cumulative positive case outweighs the residue. The most candid moment in the corpus is McLatchie conceding that Ezekiel 45 is the single strongest argument against his view — and that he has no complete answer.

Stream A · Two-comings is biblical

  • Zech 12:10 — look on “me whom they have pierced” presupposes a prior coming
  • Zech 14 — Yahweh’s feet on the Mount of Olives is the second
  • Zech 9 — the donkey-king with dominion sea-to-sea
  • One figure, two arrivals (Isaiah servant songs 9, 42, 49, 52–53; Zephaniah 3)

Stream B · Internal-critique parity

  • Orthodox Jews split the return-from-exile prophecies (Jeremiah, Ezekiel)
  • Some fulfilled at the Babylonian return; some still eschatological
  • Two-stage fulfilment is their method, not Christian ad-hockery
  • Probabilistic asymmetry (Lydia McGrew: benign vs malignant ad-hocness)

Stream C · Honest residue + pastoral frame

  • Ezekiel 45 granted as “some evidence against my own view”
  • Possibly not Messianic at all; possibly Eucharistic; weak vs positive case
  • Strobel: the rejection was leadership, not the people
  • Christian anti-Semitism is doctrinally incoherent

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

Jesus failed the messianic job description

No world peace, no ingathering of Israel, no third temple, no universal worship, no resurrection of the dead, no end of war. The Hebrew Bible’s own criteria are unmet — and any single failure debunks an all-knowing being’s prophecy.

Tom Jump (citing emailed Jewish scholars); Orthodox Jewish anti-missionaries

Strongest response

Two-comings — these are second-coming items

Zech 12:10 (the pierced one) presupposes a first coming; Zech 14 (Yahweh’s feet on the Mount of Olives) is the second. The same figure who rides the donkey (Zech 9) is pierced (Zech 12) and is Yahweh (Zech 14). The methodology is biblical, not ad hoc.

McLatchie two-comings intertextual argument

Objection 2

“Two-comings” is special pleading and unfalsifiable

You can ambiguate any text to make a failure look like a future fulfilment. That is just “our psychological proclivity to make things fit” — not evidence.

Tom Jump; Orthodox Jews (named by McLatchie)

Strongest response

Internal-critique parity + benign ad-hocness

Orthodox Jews already split the return-from-exile prophecies into fulfilled-at-Babylon and eschatological. And being upfront about a probabilistic hit while holding strong confirming data elsewhere (Lydia McGrew’s benign ad-hocness) is legitimate — only malignant ad-hocness hides the hit.

McLatchie parity moveLydia McGrew ad-hocness distinction

Objection 3

Ezekiel 45 — the prince offers sin offerings for himself

In the eschatological kingdom the “prince” offers animal sacrifices for sin, including for himself. A sin-offering for oneself implies guilt — so the prince is sinful, and cannot be the sinless Jesus.

TenakTalk’s Greg & William

Conceded as the strongest point

Three layers — and an honest residue

(a) The “prince” may not be the Messiah at all; (b) if he is, the sacrificial system can be read Eucharistically — Christ the high priest offering himself; (c) even granting it as anomalous, it is weak against the cumulative positive case. McLatchie grants this is genuine evidence against his view.

McLatchie steelmanned concessionVladimir Eucharistic reading (conditional)

Objection 4

Davidic descent is impossible via virgin birth

With no paternal father — “he’s the son of God, he doesn’t have a father” — Jesus cannot be a descendant of David, which the messianic prophecies require.

Tom Jump; Sheikh Mumtaz (Romans 1 “seed of David”)

Gap — unanswered

McLatchie does not engage it at all

The standard Christian answers (Joseph’s legal adoptive line in Matthew; Mary’s Davidic descent; the Luke genealogy) appear nowhere in the corpus. This is the most conspicuous unmet objection in the node.

Objection 5

Luke 1:33 — the Davidic throne went unfulfilled in his lifetime

The angel said Jesus would take David’s throne and rule the house of Jacob forever. But Pilate sat on the throne, Jesus was crucified, and the house of Jacob did not submit. The prophecy failed.

Sheikh Inamullah Mumtaz

Partial response

Throne-and-dominion is eschatological

By extension of the two-comings frame, dominion language (Zech 9, sea-to-sea) belongs to the second coming. McLatchie answers the general unfulfilled-prophecy form but does not address Luke 1:33 by name.

McLatchie two-comings (by extension)

Objection 6

Short-range-prophecy default (Matt 24)

Most prophets were short-range. If Jesus said he would return “within this generation,” the near-term reading is more probable than a delay of thousands of years. The natural reading makes it a failed prediction.

Tom Jump

Strongest response

Matt 24 sets up a long wait

McLatchie: the alternative reading is plausible — “wars, rumours of wars, earthquakes and famines” are explicitly not the signs of the end (“do not be alarmed”); “this generation” can mean the generation that observes the end-time signs.

McLatchie alternative reading of the Olivet discourse

Objection 7

“Just ask the Jews / the academic consensus says he didn’t”

“The vast majority of historians say he didn’t fulfil the prophecies — it’s a Christian myth that he did.” The list itself came from emailed Jewish scholars.

Tom Jump

Gap — weakly contested

No counter-scholars named

McLatchie stays at the methodological level and does not name counter-scholars, distinguish senses of “fulfilment,” or challenge the survey claim. The consensus appeal goes effectively unrebutted.

Aggregation gap

Objection 8

The Christian messianic reading is anti-Semitic

Loaded framing from the interviewer: “modern-day Jews who created their own Talmud… the 600-plus commandments” — an invitation to treat the Jewish people as a fair target.

The PBD interviewer (loaded framing)

Strongest response

Anti-Semitism is incoherent for a Christian

Strobel: Christianity is an outflow of Judaism fulfilled “like a fingerprint”; the execution was instigated by specific leaders, not the people; contemporary anti-Semitism is spiritually-driven and socially profitable. He acknowledges the legalism critique without converting it into anti-Semitism.

Strobel pastoral rebuttal + spiritual-warfare frame

Persistent gaps in the corpus

Objections the corpus does not adequately answer — stated honestly, as the deep-dive demands of an objection-shaped node.

Gap 1

Davidic descent / virgin birth is never engaged

Jump raises it directly; McLatchie does not respond. The Matthew adoptive-line, Mary’s descent, and Luke genealogy answers are simply absent.

Why it matters

It is a first-order messianic requirement and the most conspicuous unmet objection in the node — an interlocutor pressing it leaves empty-handed.

Target for gap-fill extraction

Gap 2

No text-by-text rebuttal of the specific prophecies

McLatchie deliberately stays methodological (two-comings + probabilistic asymmetry) and never works through Isa 11:12, Ezek 39, Mic 4:3, Isa 2:4 or the third-temple texts individually.

Why it matters

The defence is a frame, not an exegesis. It answers the form of the objection but leaves every individual proof-text untouched.

Target for gap-fill extraction

Gap 3

Ezekiel 45 is explicitly conceded as unresolved

McLatchie calls it “more a question than an objection” and grants he has no complete answer. The Eucharistic reading is offered only conditionally, and Vladimir’s patristics search found nothing.

Why it matters

“Origen mentions it in one of his letters but not in the context we’re talking about — so there really isn’t a commentary on it.” The strongest objection has no settled Christian reply.

Open question in the corpus

Gap 4

The “academic consensus” claim goes uncontested

Jump asserts the majority of OT/NT scholarship holds Jesus did not fulfil the prophecies. No expert names a counter-scholar or challenges the survey claim.

Why it matters

An unrebutted appeal to authority does real rhetorical work on a lay audience and is the kind of claim a prepared apologist should be able to dismantle.

Target for gap-fill extraction

Gap 5

Scope drift in the 2016 source

The Sheikh’s case is predominantly an Islamic deity-denial argument (Isaiah 43, Surah 5:116) with several misattributed proof-texts (John 9:5 for John 9:15; Matthew 23:9 misapplied).

Why it matters

It overlaps the Jewish messianic question only at the Davidic-throne and Isaiah-43 points; the bulk belongs to Q-3.4.x / Q-6.2.x, so it under-supplies this node.
