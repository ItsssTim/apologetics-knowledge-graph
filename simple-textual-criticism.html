What textual criticism shows

How do we know it matches the original?

We do not have the original copy of any ancient book, not Plato, not Caesar, not the New Testament. So how do scholars work out what was first written? The method is called textual criticism, and it runs the same four tests on every ancient text. We are applying them to the New Testament, because that is where the real fight is: critics rarely bother with the Old Testament, they go after the New, since it carries the testimony about Jesus.

First / Sources
Test 1 of 4
Where did the information come from?

It traces back to people who were there.

The first test of any ancient account is its sources. For the New Testament, the trail leads straight back to eyewitnesses, and it is backed up from outside the faith.

Who saw it
The Gospel writers drew on people who had seen Jesus teach, be executed, and be encountered alive again. In a world where almost nobody could read and parchment was expensive, you only bothered to write down what mattered enormously, and you hired trained scribes to do it. Paul dictated to a scribe named Tertius; Peter used one named Silas. These were deliberate records, not campfire rumours.
Outside too
And it is not only Christian sources. Three non-Christian writers from completely different backgrounds confirm the core: Josephus, a Jewish historian; Tacitus, a Roman statesman; and Pliny the Younger, a Roman official. Between them they attest that Jesus lived, was executed under Pontius Pilate, that his movement exploded after his death, and that his followers were worshipping him as God remarkably early.
Not telephone
Critics like Bart Ehrman say it became a game of telephone, garbled over decades. But the scholar Darrell Bock points out this was a culture of memory: people routinely memorised long, complex texts. And unlike telephone, where the whole point is to get it hilariously wrong, these were life-altering events that eyewitnesses were fiercely motivated to retell accurately, to other eyewitnesses who would correct any slip.
The theory you'll hear
The Gospels are just propaganda, written by insiders with an agenda. If so, they are remarkably bad at it. People inventing a flattering legend make their heroes look good, yet these writers record their own leaders fleeing, doubting and denying Jesus, and they make women the first witnesses to the resurrection in a culture that would not even accept a woman's word in court. To invent a believable story you mirror the values of your day; the Gospels keep doing the opposite, which is the fingerprint of awkward fact, not tidy fiction.
✓
Confidence: the New Testament traces back to eyewitnesses, corroborated by neutral and hostile outside writers. By any historian's standard, the events are well-sourced.
Second / Early
Test 2 of 4
How soon was it written down?

Too early for legend to take over.

The second test is timing. The shorter the gap between an event and the writing about it, the less room there is for myth to creep in. By that test the New Testament is almost freakishly good.

The gap
All 27 books of the New Testament were written in the first century, within one or two generations of the events, while eyewitnesses were still alive to push back. That is nowhere near enough time for a legend to quietly replace the facts. The people who had been there were still in the room when it was written.
Vs Alexander
Compare any other ancient figure. The earliest surviving biographies of Alexander the Great were written by Arrian and Plutarch around four hundred years after he died, and no historian throws those out. A gap of mere decades, like the New Testament's, is almost unheard of in the ancient world. It is the gold standard, not the weak link.
Earliest there is
These are also the earliest Christian writings in existence. There is no older, competing version of the story sitting underneath that the New Testament supposedly buried. What we have are the first accounts, not a later rewrite. The trail goes cold exactly where you would want it to: right back at the beginning.
The theory you'll hear
Decades is still long enough for the story to drift. Only if it drifted by slow rumour, which the first test already answered. Within those decades the eyewitnesses were alive, the events were seismic, and the earliest summaries (like the creed Paul quotes in 1 Corinthians 15) trace back to within a few years of the events themselves. The window for legend to overwrite fact was never really open.
✓
Confidence: written within living memory, in a world where a four-century gap is normal. The New Testament is early enough to be reporting, not legend.
Third / The copies
Test 3 of 4
How many manuscripts survived?

We have more copies than of anything else, by miles.

Here is the heart of the method. Since the originals are long gone, scholars reconstruct the wording by comparing the surviving handwritten copies, called manuscripts. The more copies, the better, and the New Testament has an embarrassment of them.

The count
There are somewhere between 5,400 and 5,600 Greek manuscripts of the New Testament, plus more than 8,000 in Latin and thousands more in other ancient languages. Every one was written and copied by hand, and an astonishing number have survived the centuries.
Vs the rest
Set that next to the rest of the ancient world. Most classical works survive on a handful of copies, anywhere from one to twenty. The next best-attested ancient book, Homer's Iliad, has around 650. The New Testament has nearly five thousand more manuscripts than any other document from antiquity. It is not in the same league. It is in a league of its own.
Why it helps
And here is the part people get backwards: more copies makes the text more secure, not less. Because the manuscripts come down through many independent lines, scholars can lay them side by side and recover the original wording, spotting precisely where any single scribe slipped. It is the opposite of one fragile chain that no one can check.
The theory you'll hear
But they're copies of copies of copies, so the errors just pile up. They would, if it were a single chain. It is not. With thousands of independent copies from different regions, a mistake in one shows up instantly against all the others that lack it. Having so many witnesses is exactly what lets scholars filter errors out rather than bake them in, which is the fourth test.
✓
Confidence: with over five thousand Greek copies and thousands more besides, the New Testament is the best-attested text of the ancient world by a vast margin.
Fourth / The variants
Test 4 of 4
What about the differences between them?

Yes, there are differences. No, they don't matter.

This is where textual criticism actually lives: the differences between the copies, called variants. The number sounds alarming, right up until you see what they actually are.

The number
Sceptics like to point out that there are more variants across the manuscripts than there are words in the New Testament, around two and a half times as many. It sounds devastating. But that figure is huge only because the number of manuscripts is huge. More copies inevitably means more tiny differences to count up.
What they are
Sort them and the alarm drains away. Around 90 percent are spelling slips and quirks of Greek grammar, the ancient equivalent of a typo. Another 9.5 percent make no real difference to the meaning. That leaves roughly 0.5 percent that are both meaningful and genuinely in question, and not one of those puts a single core Christian belief in doubt.
The agreement
With so many copies, quotations and translations to cross-check, the level of agreement between them is remarkable. Scholars reckon the original wording can be reconstructed to better than 99 percent, and the small fraction still in question touches no point of doctrine. The handful of real question marks are not hidden; they are debated in the open and printed in the footnotes of your own Bible.
The theory you'll hear
This is the case Bart Ehrman built a career on: the text has been changed. But here is the part even Ehrman concedes: the differences are overwhelmingly trivial, and no central Christian teaching hangs on a disputed verse. The variants exist precisely because we have so much to compare, and that abundance is what lets scholars reconstruct the original, not lose it.
✓
Confidence: the variants are real, public and overwhelmingly trivial. None threaten a core doctrine, and the sheer volume of copies is exactly what makes the original recoverable.
Putting it together
The bottom line
Four tests, taken together

So, does it match the original?

So put it through the very same method scholars use on every ancient document, and the New Testament passes on all four fronts: solid sources, written early, copied more than anything else in history, and with differences that turn out to be trivial. The words you read today match the originals to a degree no other ancient text can claim.
