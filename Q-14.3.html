The question

The Bible records — and, in the Torah, legislates — slavery, concubinage and polygamy. Does it therefore endorse them, and if it does, how can it be the word of a good God?

This is one objection wearing three faces. The atheist and the troubled believer press the same internal critique: a perfect God gave laws regulating who may be owned, beaten, inherited and bedded, and never once said thou shalt not own people. Either God approves of what the text permits, or the book is not from him.

The Christian cluster answers on two axes at once. A hermeneutical axis: recording and regulating a fallen practice is not the same as commending it — the accommodation / “hardness of heart” move from Matthew 19. And a historical-genealogical axis: the Bible’s own trajectory — Exodus liberation, the man-stealing death penalty, Galatians 3:28, Philemon — is what drove abolition, and abolition was overwhelmingly a Christian achievement.

The honest finding of this consolidation: the defensive case (regulation ≠ endorsement; OT servitude ≠ chattel slavery) is strong on the pastoral side and weak under expert OT cross-examination, where Dr Josh Bowen’s Leviticus 25:44–46 foreign-chattel case repeatedly went un-exegeted. The polygamy answers are cleaner and more confident than the slavery answers.

The six sub-questions

Each sub-question, the core Christian answer in a few beats, and the key move or illustration that carries it — with the counter-pressure named honestly.

Q-14.3.20 · What “slavery” meant

  • Hebrew eved, ~800× Genesis–Malachi; used of David and Moses before God, and of Hagar
  • Mostly war captives + debt servitude, never the ideal
  • Move: “If I tell a soldier follow orders, that’s not my whole view of war” — instruction ≠ approval
  • Pressure: Bowen — Leviticus 25 context demands the chattel sense

Q-14.3.21 · Vs American chattel slavery

  • American slavery was race-based and perpetual; OT/Roman servitude was debt-based and time-bound
  • Exodus 21:2 seventh-year release; Exodus 21:16 death penalty for man-stealing
  • Move: the Cornell narrative — a Black Christian woman supplies the Roman-vs-American disanalogy live
  • Pressure: Bowen — same legal rationale as antebellum law; protections cover Israelites only

Q-14.3.23 · Why not abolish it?

  • Accommodation is the pattern — Matthew 19, “because your hearts were hard”
  • A hard-hearted people taken through a process; overnight abolition was not feasible
  • Move: the petroleum analogy — you can’t reasonably ban an embedded dependence overnight
  • Pressure: Bowen — Leviticus 25 is the latest stratum yet expands foreign slavery

Q-14.3.27 · Chattel slavery & complicity

  • Smalley: God “ordered take slaves”; humans, not God, fixed it
  • 2 Timothy 3:16 + Malachi 3:6 → God still holds these positions today
  • Move: none — Knechtle concedes “moral repugnancy” and pivots to NT reliability
  • The cluster’s weakest node: the standard tools went un-deployed

Q-14.3.29 · Polygamy of the patriarchs

  • Genesis 2:24 sets the creation norm — “the two shall become one”
  • Genesis 3 (the fall) explains the deviation; polygamy “resulted”
  • Move: the counting riff — “not the 10, not the 20… Solomon’s 700/300… the two become one”
  • Description, not prescription — the patriarchs narrated as failure

Q-14.3.34 · Why not prohibit polygamy?

  • Genesis sets the monogamy ideal; Jesus’ hardness-of-hearts framing (Matthew 19)
  • God “met the Israelites halfway”; the NT reveals the original intent
  • Move: Stevenson’s “as a woman… I find that confusing” → “look at Jesus”; 2 Samuel 12:8 read as accommodation
  • Gap: the favourable David evaluation is left unresolved

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

“The Bible legislates slavery, so it condones it”

To condone just means to permit, and the Torah permits ownership, inheritance and beating. Legislating an institution is endorsing it.

Matt Dillahunty

Strongest response

Instruction / regulation ≠ endorsement

On the Matthew 19 divorce model: Moses permitted a sub-ideal “because your hearts were hard.” Permission is not the ideal — “if I tell a soldier to follow orders, that does not reveal my whole perspective on war.”

Knechtle four-options dialectic

Objection 2

Leviticus 25:44–46 is explicit foreign chattel slavery

Owned as ahuzzah (landed property), bequeathed to children as inheritance, held le’olam (forever). Perpetual, hereditary, race-based ownership — in the text.

Dr Josh Bowen

Gap — largely unanswered

Trajectory and lexical range, not the verse

Knechtle answers with the eved semantic range and the abolition trajectory; Huff cites the man-stealing death penalty. Neither exegetes 25:44–46 directly. Bowen’s ahuzzah / le’olam reading goes unrebutted — the cluster’s hardest text.

“No verse you cite even contains the word slavery”

Galatians 3:28, imago Dei and Revelation 7:9 are about soteriology, not the institution. The texts adduced against slavery never legislate against it.

Matt Dillahunty

Gap — largely unanswered

The trajectory answers the NT, not the OT silence

The genealogy / abolition answer addresses the New Testament and church history; it does not close the argument-from-silence Dillahunty is pressing about the Torah’s legislative density.

Persistent gaps

1. Leviticus 25:44–46 is never exegeted. The single hardest text — explicit, perpetual, hereditary foreign chattel slavery — is the load-bearing weakness of the whole cluster. Bowen’s ahuzzah / le’olam reading goes unrebutted.

2. Exodus 21:20–21 (“the slave is his property… not punished if the slave recovers after a day or two”) is gestured at as “instruction not endorsement” but never engaged.

3. The “regulation vs endorsement” critique itself. Dillahunty’s strongest move — that legislating an institution within the most law-dense corpus in antiquity is not neutral recording — is answered by analogy (divorce) but never by showing why slavery is structurally unlike divorce as accommodation.

4. Argument from silence. “No verse you cite even contains the word slavery” exposes that the Christian texts adduced are soteriological, not legislative-prohibitive. Unclosed.

5. The NT household-codes / Philemon tension. Philemon is read as proto-abolitionist, but Bowen notes the scholarly consensus that it is not an institutional declaration and “this view… is no longer held by anyone.” The household codes (Eph 6, Col 3, 1 Pet 2) are read charitably; the tension is real.

6. Numbers 31 (the Midianite captives) is answered by denial only; the “why must they be virgin?” question is unmet.

7. Christian complicity (Q-14.3.27) is never substantively defended — the apologist concedes and pivots.

8. The favourable David evaluation under polygamy is acknowledged but unresolved.
