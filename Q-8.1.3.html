Does the incarnation require the Son to give up divine attributes?

Kenotic Christology and the meaning of “he emptied himself” (Philippians 2:7). Three experts converge on a verdict — the incarnation is addition, not subtraction — from analytic metaphysics, the Jehovah’s Witness front, and the Muslim debate.

The question

When Philippians 2:7 says the Son “emptied himself” (Greek kenōsis), did he divest himself of omniscience, omnipotence and omnipresence to become incarnate — or is the incarnation addition rather than subtraction?

Kenotic Christology answers yes: to be genuinely human, the Logos relinquished the omni-attributes incompatible with a finite human life. The orthodox Chalcedonian answer is no: the Son added a complete human nature without subtracting anything from the divine nature he eternally possesses.

The stakes run in three directions. Against Islam, the objection “God cannot be ignorant or die” presupposes that any human limitation disproves deity — so kenosis-as-divestment hands the Muslim the win. Against Jehovah’s Witnesses, the text is read as proof Christ “stopped being God” on earth. And internally, kenoticism is held by some orthodox Protestants — so the apologist must show it is not merely unnecessary but, on Craig’s analysis, incoherent.

The convergent case — three anti-kenotic streams

This is not a single syllogism but a cumulative anti-kenotic case. The three experts approach from different rooms — Craig from analytic metaphysics, Perry from the JW dispute, McLatchie from the Muslim debate — and arrive at the same Chalcedonian verdict: the kenōsis is a change of status and privilege, never of essential attributes.

Stream A · Metaphysical

  • Nature, not person, determines deity
  • Essential change = ceasing to exist (the cremation-to-powder device)
  • A typical member of the kind deity is essentially divine
  • Necessity, aseity, eternality cannot be divested even temporarily
  • Carried solely by Craig

Stream B · Exegetical

  • “Emptied” = took a servant-form he was not (Perry)
  • Laid aside the privilege, not the attributes (McLatchie)
  • Two-states schema: humiliation then exaltation
  • A change of status, not of possession
  • Carried by Perry & McLatchie

Stream C · Reductio

  • The “thin God” that survives full divestment is too thin to be worshipped (Craig)
  • “Humble yourself to death” is impossible for one already subject to death (Perry)
  • Divest an essential attribute ⇒ no longer God
  • Keep the un-divestable ones ⇒ straight back to Chalcedon

How each expert deploys it

William Lane Craig1 transcript
The analytic demolition — “addition, not subtraction”
The four-stage argument
  • Slogan (stated thrice): the incarnation is “not a matter of subtraction… It is a matter of addition” — the Logos assumes a human nature in addition to the divine.
  • Stage 1: it is one’s nature, not person, that determines deity — so divesting an essential attribute changes the deity.
  • Stage 2: essential change = ceasing to exist (the cremation-to-powder device); Christ is a typical member of the kind deity, so losing deity means ceasing to exist — impossible for a necessary, eternal being.
  • Stage 3: the “thin God” escape (omni-attributes are merely contingent) yields a being no greater than an ordinary human, who could not be worthy of worship — hence not God.
  • Stage 4: necessity / aseity / eternality cannot be shed even temporarily; the only coherent way to say Christ died is reduplicatively — which is just Chalcedon, not kenoticism.
Named verdict

Kenotic christology “is theologically unacceptable and… really incoherent in the end.” In the same class he tells a questioner his position is “heresy… contrary to the council of Chalcedon” — the one place in the corpus an expert names a lay view heresy.

Preston Perry1 transcript
The JW front — emptying as addition + the death-reductio
Distinctive moves
  • Emptying is addition: Jesus “did not empty himself by stop being God but… by becoming something that he wasn’t previously” — a human servant. On earth he was “100% man and… 100% God.”
  • Humility cleanup (load-bearing): real humility is not acknowledging rank but having “equal rights” and “not holding tightly to these things… for the sake of serving others” — letting him hold both equality and humbling at once.
  • The death-reductio (finishing move): “it is impossible… to become obedient to death when they are already held subject to death” — only a being superior to death could humble himself to it, so the text proves pre-existent deity.
  • Reproduced as live dialogue from a real JW encounter who “just looked at me” — question-based methodology in concentrated form.
Jonathan McLatchie3 transcripts
The Muslim front — and a qualified kenosis
Distinctive moves
  • Two natures, divine nature does not die: “natures don’t die, human persons die”; the whole God-man died, though the divine nature “cannot be killed.” One-liner: “He is a man but not only a man.”
  • Kenosis = laying aside the privilege, not the attributes — “at least partially”: the geographic example is omnipresence. The qualifier is a genuine (minimal) kenotic concession Craig does not make.
  • Mark 13:32 ignorance: either a subliminal / divine exhaustive knowledge behind a limited waking knowledge, or Augustine’s idiom — “to know” means “to reveal.”
  • The fig tree (Mark 11:13): reframed as a deliberate Jeremiah 8:13 intertext — Jesus as Yahweh-the-inspector — so it “points to the deity of Christ rather than detracting from it.”
Internal seam

McLatchie explicitly endorses a limited kenosis (“he laid aside the privilege of the divine attributes at least partially”), where Craig rejects any divestment. Reconcilable on the privilege / possession distinction — but the corpus never states the reconciliation.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

“He emptied himself” means he ceased to be God on earth

Philippians 2:7 says Christ “emptied himself” (kenōsis). The plain reading is that he divested his divinity — so he was not God during his earthly ministry.

Vania (Muslim), Jehovah’s Witnesses, general kenotic reading

Strongest response

The emptying is addition, not subtraction

Perry: he emptied himself by becoming a human servant he previously was not — not by ceasing to be God. McLatchie: he laid aside the privilege of the attributes, not the attributes. The incarnation adds a nature; it removes nothing.

Perry emptying-as-additionMcLatchie privilege not possessionCraig addition not subtraction

Objection 2

The omni-attributes are merely contingent properties

Omnipotence, omniscience and omnipresence are not essential to deity — they are contingent properties God happens to have, so the Logos could abandon them without ceasing to be God.

The kenotic theologian (Craig’s generic target)

Strongest response

The “thin God” reductio

That entails a concept of God “far too thin to be theologically acceptable.” A being no more powerful, intelligent or unlimited than an ordinary human could not be worthy of worship — and so could not be God. An Anselmian worthy-of-worship criterion.

Craig “just incredible” that such a being is worship-worthy

Objection 3

Christ had to give up attributes in order to die

An eternal, necessary being cannot die. So if Christ truly died on the cross, he must have divested at least some divine attributes to make mortality possible.

Kenoticist (implied)

Strongest response

The un-divestable attributes force the fork

Necessity, aseity and eternality cannot be shed even temporarily. The only coherent way to say Christ died is reduplicatively — he died in his human nature while the attributes are preserved in the divine. But that is two-natures Chalcedon, not kenoticism.

Craig “right back to Chalcedon again”

Objection 4

Mark 13:32 — Jesus doesn’t know the hour

Jesus says “of that day or hour no one knows… nor the Son.” If he is not omniscient, he is not God.

Muslim apologetic

Strongest response

Two compatible readings, either sufficient

McLatchie: either (a) exhaustive knowledge in the subliminal / divine mind behind a limited waking consciousness; or (b) Augustine’s Hebrew idiom — “to know” means “to reveal,” so Jesus does not reveal the hour. Vladimir favours (b); McLatchie favours (a).

McLatchie subliminal-knowledge modelVladimir Susic Augustine’s “not to know”

Objection 5

The fig tree — Jesus didn’t know it wasn’t the season

Mark 11:13 says “it was not the season for figs,” yet Jesus cursed the tree for bearing none. He was ignorant — so not omniscient, so not God.

Shabir Ally

Strongest response

A deity-pointing intertext, not ignorance

McLatchie: the cursing deliberately echoes Jeremiah 8:13, where Yahweh inspects Israel for figs and finds none. Jesus stepping into Yahweh’s inspector role “points to the deity of Christ rather than detracting from it.” The two-natures subliminal-knowledge model is the fallback.

McLatchie Jeremiah 8:13 reframe + two-natures fallback

Objection 6

“Humility” just means recognising you are lower

If Philippians 2 shows Jesus humbling himself, that simply means he acknowledged he was lower than the Father — which makes him inferior, not equal.

JW / popular misreading

Strongest response

That is rank-acknowledgment, not humility

Perry: acknowledging you are lower is not humility — it is just stating a fact. Real humility is having equal standing and voluntarily not pressing it to serve others. That is exactly what the hymn depicts, and it presupposes Christ’s equality with God.

Perry redefinition of humility

Objection 7

Jesus was a mere man, recreated as Michael the Archangel

Jesus was only a man on earth; when he died he died as a man, and in heaven he was recreated as Michael the Archangel — a created being throughout.

Jehovah’s Witnesses

Strongest response

The death-reductio from their own commitments

Perry: obedience-to-death presupposes superiority-over-death, which presupposes uncreated being. A created being is already subject to death, so it cannot “humble itself” to what is inevitable. The JW’s own premise (Christ-as-creature) makes Philippians 2 incoherent — unless Christ is God.

Perry internal critique — “she just looked at me”

Gap 1

The corpus’s own internal seam — strict vs privilege-kenotic

McLatchie affirms a qualified kenosis (“laid aside the privilege of the divine attributes at least partially”); Craig rejects any divestment. Are these the same position?

Internal to the corpus

Gap — unreconciled

No expert states the reconciliation

“Privilege of an attribute” is not the attribute itself, so the two are reconcilable — but neither expert performs the reconciliation. The “convergent” position has a strict wing (Craig) and a privilege-kenotic wing (McLatchie) that are never explicitly harmonised.

Aggregation gap

Gap 2

Orthodox kenotic Christology is never engaged

Sophisticated kenoticism (the relative-vs-essential-attributes distinction; Forrest, Davis) is held by respectable orthodox theologians, not just JWs and Muslims.

Kenotic-Christology literature

Gap — unaddressed

Craig argues only against a generic target

Craig engages “the kenotic theologian” in the abstract; Perry does not name kenotic Christology as a position held by any orthodox theologian at all. The most defensible form of the view — selective divestment of relative attributes — is not directly met.

Gap 3

Ally’s “latent omniscience = confessed ignorance”

If omniscience sits in a “latent consciousness,” then Jesus as God is “not omniscient in his consciousness” — “if you ask me now I have to confess I don’t know it.”

Shabir Ally (closing rebuttal)

Gap — left standing

The subliminal model’s deepest objection is unanswered

Ally’s counter directly attacks McLatchie’s two-levels model and receives no rejoinder in the source. The model would need an independent account of how knowledge possessed sub-consciously still counts as omniscience for the conscious subject.

Aggregation gap

Gap 4

1 Timothy 6:16 against Perry’s eternity inference

Perry argues from Christ’s eternity (“God is the only one that is eternal”) to his deity. But 1 Timothy 6:16 says the Father “alone has immortality” — which a JW could turn against that very inference.

Jehovah’s Witness counter-move

Gap — unaddressed

Perry does not anticipate the counter

The eternity-to-deity inference is left exposed to the “who alone has immortality” rejoinder. A full answer would distinguish underived immortality (the Father’s) from shared-essence eternity (the Son’s), but Perry does not raise it.

Aggregation gap

Gap 5

Craig’s typical-member asymmetry is asserted, not defended

Stage 2 leans on Christ being a typical member of the kind deity but a non-typical member of the kind man. Why the asymmetry?

Structural dependency on Plank 1

Gap — flagged

Follows from the prior lesson, not free-standing

The asymmetry follows from the essential / contingent distinction Craig defends in the two-natures lesson (Q-8.1.2). A kenoticist who rejected that distinction would not be moved here. A structural dependency rather than a true gap — but worth flagging.
