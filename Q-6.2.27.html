The question

When the Qur’an commands Jews and Christians to “uphold the Torah and the Injil,” does Islam’s own doctrine of scriptural corruption make that command self-refuting?

The narrow argument that names this node is Jonathan McLatchie’s circular-argument reductio. Surah 5:68 tells the People of the Book to stand on their own scriptures, yet mainstream Islam holds those scriptures (tahrif) corrupted. So either Allah enjoins judgement by a corrupted text — absurd — or judgement by the text only where it already agrees with the Qur’an — circular. Either reading discredits the command, and the Qur’an that issues it.

Around that core sits a wider corpus. The same nine transcripts deploy a family of internal critiques — Islam’s own sources against Islamic claims — on the disciples (Surah 3:52 / 61:14), the crucifixion (Surah 4:157), Muhammad in the Old Testament (Deut 18), and salvation (the Bukhari “99 killings” hadith). The unifying tactic is internal critique, not a single shared syllogism.

The convergent argument — the Surah 5:68 reductio

Premise 1

Surah 5:68 commands the People of the Book to “uphold the Torah and the Injil.”

A Quranic command, granted by the Muslim interlocutor. The whole critique runs inside Islam’s own commitments.

Premise 2

On the standard Muslim view (tahrif), the present Torah and Injil are corrupted.

The load-bearing premise. The corruption doctrine is itself a Muslim premise, not a Christian imposition — which is exactly what makes the critique internal. All the force lives here, which is why McLatchie returns to it whenever his opponent will not answer.

Horn A

If Allah means “judge by your scriptures as they stand,” he commands judgement by a corrupted source.

The absurd horn — no rational lawgiver enjoins a verdict from a text he holds falsified.

Horn B

If Allah means “judge by your scriptures only so far as they agree with the Qur’an,” the command is viciously circular.

The circular horn. The recommendation-letter / job-interview analogy lands here: you hand over a reference that says good and bad things, then instruct the interviewer to read only the positive lines.

Conclusion

Either horn discredits Allah’s wisdom and the Qur’an’s authority. The only non-absurd reading — that the scriptures are preserved — collapses into McLatchie’s primary preservation syllogism.

Why the escape route is fatal. Reading Surah 5:68 as presupposing sound scripture defeats tahrif and feeds straight into the main anti-Islam argument (Q-6.2.6). The dilemma has no safe exit.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

“Muslim” isn’t a religious title — it just means one who submits to God’s will

So Surah 3:52 calling the disciples “Muslims” says nothing about Islamic doctrine; it only means they submitted to God.

Sheikh Inamullah Mumtaz

Strongest response

Shift from label to content

The disciples preached the deity of Christ and the crucifixion — both denied by the Qur’an (Surah 4:157). Whatever the word means, the disciples’ message was not Islamic.

McLatchie label-to-content pivot

Objection 2

Mark, Luke and John weren’t real disciples — they never met Jesus

So the high-Christology Gospels can’t testify to what the original disciples actually believed.

Sheikh Inamullah Mumtaz

Strongest response

Verified apostolic chains

Mark’s link to Peter is attested by Justin Martyr, Papias-via-Irenaeus and Bauckham; the apostolic fathers (Polycarp from John, Clement, Ignatius) carry the chain forward.

McLatchie patristic transmission

Objection 3

Paul vs Peter — Acts is late propaganda smoothing a real split

Paul corrupted Jesus’s message; the rift with the Jerusalem church (Tyson, Hengel) is the real history.

Shabir Ally

Strongest response

No theological rift

The apostolic fathers treat Paul and Peter together; the Antioch dispute (Gal 2) was over circumcision practice, not theology. Michael Kruger as hostile witness: “no split existed.”

McLatchie Kruger hostile-witness citation

Objection 4

“Brothers” in Deut 18:15 = Ishmaelites, so Muhammad is prophesied

The prophet “like Moses... from among your brothers” is an Ishmaelite (Abrahamic) descendant — Muhammad.

Standard Muslim apologetic

Strongest response

Read the chapter against itself

Deut 18:1-2, the very same chapter, uses “brothers” of fellow Israelite kinsmen (the Levites among the tribes). The term denotes kinsmen within the nation, not external descendants.

McLatchie same-chapter internal critique

Objection 5

God needs no atoning sacrifice — if he’s God he can just forgive

The Qur’an’s most-repeated line is “most merciful”; the cross is “nonsense.” Allah forgives at will.

Muslim apologists (in Huff)

Strongest response

Then justice is abandoned

The Bukhari “99 killings” hadith shows Allah shrinking the earth to save a murderer — mercy without justice for the 99 dead. Arbitrary, not perfectly just. The cross resolves both.

Huff internal-Islamic-source reductio

Objection 6

The Qur’anic human-prophet Jesus is intrinsically more believable

A mere human prophet is easier to credit than a risen divine Son — the cross is a stumbling block to Jews and folly to pagans.

Muslim debaters

Strongest response

Intrinsic prior ≠ conditional probability

On the evidence, first-generation NT sources written while eyewitnesses lived beat a book written 600 years later with no independent historical source. Conditional probability favours the NT portrait.
