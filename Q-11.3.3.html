The Lord’s Supper and the real presence

The question

When Jesus said “This is my body,” is Christ’s body and blood really present in the bread and wine — and if so, in what sense?

This is the most divisive question in the theology of the sacraments and the historic fault line of the Reformation. The answer decides whether the Lord’s Supper is a sacrament — an actual means of grace in which the communicant receives the body and blood of Christ — or an ordinance, a memorial of a grace already accomplished at the cross. The same six words of institution are claimed by every tradition; the adjudication turns on what kind of presence the elements carry, and on what one believes about the nature of Christ’s resurrection body.

WLC’s distinctive contribution is to make the doctrine of the resurrection body the organising hinge. His eucharistic conclusion is downstream of his doctoral work on the resurrection, not of a prior sacramental theology — which is why he rejects every real-bodily-presence view and lands “almost in between Zwingli and Calvin.”

Honest density. All six extractions are William Lane Craig’s Defenders systematic-theology lectures — a four-part 2021 run plus a 2014 variant that adds the live Q&A. This is in-house Christian doctrine taught to a sympathetic lay class, not adversarial apologetics; the “objections” are intramural cross-tradition disputes. WLC steel-mans four rival views in the third person (Parts 9–11), then delivers his verdict in his own voice (Part 12). His settled position is the memorialist / ordinance view — he is not endorsing the real-presence views he voices only to refute.

The four-view spectrum — strongest to weakest presence

This is not a syllogism but a cross-tradition fork that WLC collapses onto a single axis: how strongly does each tradition affirm Christ’s presence in the elements? He steel-mans each view on its own terms before adjudicating. The four positions descend from full bodily transformation to bare memorial.

1 · Transubstantiation

  • Catholic & Eastern Orthodox
  • Substance of bread & wine transformed into Christ’s body & blood; accidents remain
  • Mechanism: Aristotelian substance / accidents
  • Fourth Lateran 1215; Trent
  • WLC: rejected — no scriptural support; confused about the resurrection body

2 · Consubstantiation

  • Lutheran
  • Bread & wine not transformed, but body & blood present “in, under, through” the elements
  • Mechanism: communicatio idiomatum (ubiquity)
  • Formula of Concord 1577
  • WLC: rejected — inherits the above plus confuses the two natures (Chalcedon)

3 · Spiritual presence

  • Calvin / Reformed
  • No transformation, no consubstantiation; a real spiritual presence to the believer
  • Confirms spiritually what happened physically at the cross
  • A means of grace, not a physical reception
  • WLC: rejected — a dilemma (unintelligible, or collapses into ordinance)

4 · Memorial / ordinance

  • Zwingli; typically Baptist
  • No bodily presence and (Zwingli) no spiritual presence of body / blood at all
  • A memorial meal: remember, examine, confess, reflect
  • Marburg Colloquy 1529
  • WLC: adopted — “the ordinance view… almost in between Zwingli and Calvin”

The four positions — claim, mechanism, and WLC’s objection

View 1 · Transubstantiation · Catholic & Orthodox

The consecrated bread and wine are actually turned into the body and blood of Christ — “the strongest and most radical interpretation.”

Mechanism. Aristotelian substance / accidents: the substance of bread and wine becomes Christ’s body and blood while the accidents (colour, taste, consistency) remain, so it still looks and tastes like bread and wine. Vatican II (Lumen Gentium §11) calls it “the fount and apex of the whole christian life.” WLC’s objection (internal critique). He raises the natural “why isn’t it used up?” question; a Fordham philosopher-priest answers that the communicant consumes only the accidents, not the substance — but that guts the devotional point. A young Catholic woman liked the doctrine because it let her feel close to Christ by eating his flesh; on the classic doctrine “that’s not really true.”

View 2 · Consubstantiation · Lutheran

The bread and wine are not transformed, but Christ’s body and blood are present “in, under, and through” the elements — a middle way between transformation and a merely spiritual presence.

Mechanism. The communicatio idiomatum: in Christ’s exaltation the attributes of the divine nature (ubiquity, invisibility) are communicated to the human nature, so the body is really, if invisibly, present everywhere. WLC’s red-hot-poker illustration: as a poker in the fire takes on the fire’s heat and glow, the exalted human nature takes on divine ubiquity (Formula of Concord, 1577). WLC’s objection (Chalcedonian critique). It inherits all the objections to transubstantiation plus a fatal one — communicating ubiquity and immateriality from the divine to the human nature is exactly the confusion of natures Chalcedon forbids: “neither divide the person nor confuse the natures.”

View 3 · Spiritual presence · Calvin / Reformed

Not a transformation, not even consubstantiation — a real spiritual presence of Christ to the believer that “confirms spiritually what has already happened physically at the cross.”

Mechanism. A genuine spiritual communion and a means of grace, but not a physical reception of body and blood. (WLC notes several Reformed sub-views; the “major Rift” is Zwingli the Swiss vs Calvin the Frenchman, with Knox and Bucer alongside.) WLC’s objection (a dilemma). Either Calvin means the body and blood are present non-physically — “a body present non-physically and non-bodily just is not a body; that is a contradiction in terms” — or he means Christ is present in his divine nature, which is true but no different from his presence in prayer, worship, and witnessing. So the Supper is an ordinance, not a sacrament.

View 4 · Memorial / ordinance · Zwingli & Baptist

The weakest view: an ordinance, not a sacrament — for Zwingli not even a spiritual presence of body and blood, but a memorial meal of remembrance.

Mechanism. None claimed. We participate to remember Christ’s sacrifice, examine ourselves, confess sin, and reflect on what he has done. Zwingli and Luther held a famous colloquy over its nature (Marburg, 1529). WLC’s assessment. This is where he lands — with the qualification (his interlocutor James’s framing, which he accepts) that his own position sits “in between Zwingli and Calvin.”

WLC’s verdict

The ordinance view, for both baptism and the Lord’s Supper — a memorial of remembrance, self-examination, confession, and spiritual communion with the ascended Christ in his divine nature.

The controlling premise. All three rejections turn on one Christological fact: the genuinely physical, finite, now-departed resurrection body. Christ “is present not in his human nature, which is ascended to heaven and will not come again until the return of Christ, but he is present in his divine nature, his omnipresent, spiritual, immaterial, divine nature.” WLC is careful to deny he is “depreciating the significance of the Lord’s Supper.”

Objections handled — cross-tradition master list

Objection 1

“This is my body” must be taken literally

At the Last Supper Jesus identifies the bread and wine with his body and blood. The plain words establish a real presence; to read them figuratively is to evade them.

Catholic, Lutheran

Strongest response

Bodily present when he spoke — so “is” is imagery

Jesus was physically there, his blood “coursing through his veins,” so literal identity is impossible. “Is” is Semitic imagery, paralleled in Paul (1 Cor 10:4 “the Rock was Christ”; Gal 4:25 “Hagar is Mount Sinai”). The act is a symbolic prophetic sign interpreting his death via Isaiah 53.

Craig Part 12 cumulative case

Objection 2

If Christ’s body is eaten, why isn’t it “used up”?

If communicants worldwide eat the body and drink the blood of Christ, it should have been consumed long ago. Is there an infinite supply of body and blood?

Natural objection WLC raises

Response — turned into a critique

“You only consume the accidents”

A Fordham philosopher-priest’s answer: the communicant consumes only the accidents, not the substance — which is why it is not used up. WLC accepts this on its own terms, then shows the cost: it removes the literal eating of Christ’s flesh that motivated the doctrine.

Craig internal critiqueFordham priest opponent’s own admission

Objection 3

Transubstantiation gives intimate union with Christ’s flesh

The devotional appeal of the doctrine is that you are genuinely, literally eating the flesh of Christ and drinking his blood — the closest possible union.

Catholic devotional motive

Strongest response

“That’s not really true”

On the classic doctrine the communicant consumes only the accidents of bread and wine, never the substance of the Lord’s body and blood. So the intimate literal-flesh-eating that attracts believers to the doctrine is not in fact what the doctrine delivers.

Craig reductio of the devotional motive

Objection 4

The communicatio idiomatum grounds a real bodily presence

In Christ’s exaltation the attributes of the divine nature (ubiquity, invisibility) are communicated to his human nature, so his body really can be present everywhere — including in the elements.

Luther, Formula of Concord

Strongest response

It violates Chalcedon

The watchword of Chalcedon is “neither divide the person nor confuse the natures.” Communicating ubiquity and immateriality from the divine to the human nature is exactly that confusion — “not acceptable christologically.”

Craig Chalcedonian internal critique

Objection 5

Spiritual presence is a real, sacramental presence (Calvin)

Calvin’s view is not bare memorialism: the body and blood are spiritually present, and there is a genuine spiritual communion that makes the Supper a means of grace.

Reformed, Calvinist

Strongest response

A dilemma — bodiless body, or mere ordinance

Either the body and blood are present non-physically — “a body present non-physically and non-bodily just is not a body; that is a contradiction in terms” — or Christ is present in his divine nature, which is true of prayer, worship, and witnessing too. Either way the Supper is an ordinance, not a special sacrament.

Craig Part 12 dilemma

Objection 6

You ignore John 6 — the strongest real-presence text

The bread-of-life discourse (“unless you eat the flesh of the Son of Man and drink his blood”) is the most powerful New Testament passage for a real presence in the body and blood of Jesus.

Catholic, Lutheran

Strongest response

Wrong context, anti-retrojection, and Jn 6:63

It is mid-ministry, not the Last Supper. John uniquely has no Lord’s Supper narrative, so the discourse could trivially have been placed there but was not. “Son of Man” marks it dominical; John repeatedly uses literal-misunderstanding-of-a-spiritual-symbol (Nicodemus, the Samaritan woman, Lazarus “asleep”); Sirach 24 supplies an eat/drink-of-Wisdom parallel; and Jesus glosses it himself: “the flesh profits nothing.”

Craig seven-layer rebuttal

Objection 7

A finite resurrection body could not feed the worldwide church

Granting a real bodily presence, how can one physical body be eaten by all communicants everywhere at once? Either it is infinite, or it is spiritualised away.

Implied by real presence

Strongest response

The body has departed this universe

Christ’s resurrection body is a physical, humanoid organism that “is now departed from our space-time universe.” It “wouldn’t be large enough to feed all of the persons who are taking the Lord’s Supper at any one time,” and to spiritualise it “is to fail to do justice to the doctrine of the resurrection.”

Craig decisive resurrection-body objection

Objection 8

1 Cor 11 — “discerning the body / unworthy manner”

If the Supper is merely a memorial no different from other worship, why does Paul attach uniquely severe warnings — “guilty of the body and blood of the Lord,” judgement for failing to “discern the body”?

Objection 9

John 6: Jesus didn’t correct them — and shifted to trōgō

Jesus let disciples leave over a literal understanding without correcting it, and intensified to trōgō (“to gnaw / chew”) — evidence he meant a literal eating.

Catholic congregant (John, 2014)

Gap — only parried in 2014

Argument from silence; fits John’s pattern

In the 2014 run WLC answers briefly that this is an argument from silence and fits John’s established pattern of non-correction after a misunderstanding. It is not engaged at all in the 2021 monologue, and the lexical trōgō point is not directly rebutted.

Objection 10

Ignatius of Antioch — the patristic real presence

Early-church heretics were identified by denying the real bodily presence (Ignatius, c. 107 AD). The earliest post-apostolic witness favours a literal view.

Catholic congregant (John, 2014)

Briefly parried; Cyprian offered as counter

Hebrews “once for all” vs the Mass-as-sacrifice

Objection 12

The Thomist substantial-reception rejoinder

“You only consume the accidents” does not deflate the intimacy: the res (the substance of Christ) is truly received, though not digested. Substantial reception is exactly what grounds the union.

Thomist reply to WLC

Gap — not addressed

Treated as fatal; the rejoinder is unanswered

WLC treats accidental ingestion as deflating the devotional claim, but a Thomist would say the substance is truly received though not physically digested. This rejoinder — and the Anglican mediating / receptionist view that sits near WLC’s own landing spot — are not on his map.
