The second coming & the delay of the parousia

The question

Did Jesus predict his return within his hearers' lifetime — a prophecy that failed — and is a literal, sudden, cosmic second coming credible at all?

The doctrine of the second coming is not contested among these experts. What is contested — and what the corpus spends almost all its energy on — is the delay of the parousia: the charge, pressed since Reimarus, Schweitzer and Weiss, that Jesus announced an imminent return that never arrived. The crux text is Mark 13:30, “this generation will not pass away before all these things take place.”

The coverage here is unusual. Nine of the ten extractions are one voice — consecutive instalments of William Lane Craig's Defenders 3 series (Parts 1, 7–14), delivered to a sympathetic, catechetical audience of believers, not to skeptics. John Lennox supplies the only second voice, and on an adjacent question. There is no cross-expert consensus to report, because there is barely a second expert. This page foregrounds Craig's developed argument and flags the density honestly.

The convergent case — evidence streams, not a syllogism

The doctrine is established cumulatively (a biblical-data survey) and its central objection is met by an interlocking set of moves, not one syllogism. Four streams carry the weight. The argument is abductive; its strategic signature is Craig's stated method — sharpen rather than soften the objection, then dissolve it as an undercutting defeater.

Stream A · The doctrine

  • OT Messianic-kingdom hope (Isaiah 9, 11)
  • Daniel 7 “son of man” hinge
  • ~250 NT references; Olivet Discourse
  • Nature: personal, glorious, decisive, sudden
  • Purpose: redeem, raise, judge, gather

Stream B · The dilemma

  • Horn A: “far-off” Olivet signs + Paul
  • 2 Thess 2 antichrist-first; Rom 11 harvest
  • Horn B: Mark 13:30 “this generation”
  • Mark 8:38–9:1; Matt 16:28
  • Matthew “sharpens” the imminence reading

Stream C · The resolution

  • Reject preterism (implausible)
  • Reject provisional prophecy (disanalogy)
  • Contextual ambiguity (Matt 10:23 knock-down)
  • Mark 9:1 perfect tense → resurrection
  • Mark 13:30 “all these things” → AD 70

Stream D · Positive + credibility

  • Four delay-parables (Matt 24–25)
  • Sheep & Goats “explicitly contradictory”
  • 2 Peter 3 “scoffers” objection
  • False-vacuum decay parity (Adams & Laughlin)
  • “Quite on a par” with theology

The resolution chain — Craig's delay-of-parousia argument

The load-bearing apologetic (Parts 9–11) runs as a single problem→response unit. He states the dilemma at full strength, rejects two solutions, then wins the principle on uncontested ground before transferring it to the crux text.

Problem

A handful of sayings (Mark 13:30; Mark 9:1; Matt 16:28) seem to predict the return within the hearers' lifetime — against abundant “far-off” data from the Olivet signs and Paul.

Method. Craig deliberately strengthens the skeptic's horn, noting that Matthew's wording makes the imminence reading “even more obvious” than Mark's. The strategy is to sharpen, not soften.

Reject A

Preterism (all fulfilled in AD 70) is conceded a real strength but rejected as exegetically implausible.

“That is certainly a strength of the preterist view — it just completely solves the problem — but I've already expressed my reservations… I just don't find it a plausible interpretation.” (See gaps: his own Mark 13:30 reading is itself partial-preterist.)

Reject B

Provisional prophecy (revised like Jonah / Hezekiah) fails on disanalogy.

Those OT prophecies changed because circumstances changed — Nineveh repented, Hezekiah prayed. In Christ's case nothing changed; the predicted signs proceeded as given. So the revision model does not fit.

Solution C

Contextual ambiguity: the evangelists relocate sayings with editorial freedom, so a coming-saying can take on a false appearance of predicting imminence purely from its placement.

The knock-down argument (Matt 10:23). Matthew inserts Olivet persecution material into the Mission of the Twelve, producing “you will not have gone through all the towns of Israel before the Son of Man comes.” Matthew himself then narrates the disciples returning — so he plainly did not read it as a fulfilled imminence prediction. The context manufactured the meaning.

Apply

Transfer the mechanism to the crux texts.

Mark 9:1: the perfect tense (“the kingdom of God has come with power”) looks backward — most plausibly to the resurrection; Matthew's forward paraphrase generates the imminence reading. Mark 13:30: “all these things” traced through 13:4, 23, 29 fixes its referent as the destruction of Jerusalem and preceding tribulation — not the parousia of 13:24–27.

Reductio

Mark 13:32 seals it: Jesus cannot have been predicting a within-the-generation return when he disclaims knowing the day even for himself.

“How could Jesus have been predicting that his return would occur before this generation is dead when he himself says that even the Son does not know?” The conclusion is modest by design: imminence was never securely predicted — an undercutting, not a rebutting, defeater.

Six of the nine sessions are catechetical exposition or pastoral application to believers (Parts 1, 7, 8, 14, and the pastoral portions of 13), not argued apologetics. The genuinely adversarial material is concentrated in Parts 9–13. A doctrinal node with a strong apologetic core — not a debate transcript.

Coverage caveat

Lennox and Craig are not arguing about the same thing. Craig defends the NT against a failed near-term prediction; Lennox argues for reading Revelation forward. They share only the broad theme of Christ's return. No convergence to claim, no disagreement to adjudicate.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

Mark 13:30 is a failed prediction of an imminent return

“This generation will not pass away before all these things take place” fixes the parousia within the first-century hearers' lifetime. It did not happen. The gospels record a falsified prophecy.

Reimarus, Schweitzer, Weiss; popular skepticism

Strongest response

“All these things” = AD 70, not the parousia

Traced through Mark's own earlier usage (13:4, 23, 29), “all these things” denotes the destruction of Jerusalem and the preceding tribulation — events prior to the return. The juxtaposition with the Son-of-Man passage (13:24–27) manufactures the imminence misimpression.

Craig lexical-trace method, Parts 10–11

Objection 2

“Some standing here will not taste death”

Mark 8:38–9:1 // Matt 16:28 has Jesus telling bystanders they will live to see the Son of Man come in his kingdom — another within-the-lifetime prediction.

Critical scholarship; skeptics

Strongest response

Perfect tense points backward — to the resurrection

Mark's wording (“the kingdom of God has come with power”) is perfect tense, looking back; in Mark's surrounding passion-prediction context the natural referent is the resurrection. Matthew's forward-looking paraphrase is what creates the imminence reading.

Craig perfect-tense pivot, Part 11

Objection 3

Preterism already solves the delay

If all the predictions — including the Son of Man's coming — were fulfilled in AD 70 with the destruction of Jerusalem, there is no delay to explain. The problem dissolves entirely.

Preterist interpreters

Strongest response

Conceded a strength, rejected as implausible

Craig grants it “certainly… a strength” that it “completely solves the problem,” but rejects it as an implausible reading of the text. (See gaps — his own AD-70 reading of Mark 13:30 sits in unacknowledged tension with this rejection.)

Craig Part 10

Objection 4

Maybe the prediction was provisional, later revised

Perhaps Jesus genuinely predicted a near return, but — like Jonah's Nineveh oracle or Isaiah's word to Hezekiah — it was a conditional prophecy that circumstances later revised.

Sympathetic / liberal theology

Strongest response

Disanalogy — nothing changed

Those OT prophecies were altered because circumstances changed (Nineveh repented; Hezekiah prayed). In Christ's case nothing changed — the predicted signs proceeded just as given — so the revision model has no purchase.

Craig Part 10

Objection 5

Jesus claimed to know the timing of his return

If Jesus issued time-bound predictions of his coming, he was presuming to know when it would occur — and was wrong.

Implicit in the failed-prophecy charge

Strongest response

Mark 13:32 reductio

Jesus explicitly disclaims knowledge of the day or hour — “not even… the Son.” It is incoherent to read him as simultaneously predicting a within-the-generation return whose timing he says he does not know.

Craig reductio, Part 11

Objection 6

A sudden world-ending return is incredible

Everything has run uniformly under natural law since the beginning of creation. “Where is the promise of his coming?” A literal, imminent cosmic destruction is just too otherworldly to believe.

2 Peter 3 “scoffers”; modern uniformitarianism

Strongest response

Hostile-witness parity from physical eschatology

Secular cosmology itself entertains an unpredictable, imminent, total cosmic destruction — false-vacuum decay, which “for all we know could be realized tomorrow.” Craig reads Adams & Laughlin aloud so the apocalyptic register comes from physicists. The theological forecast is “quite on a par.”

Craig parity argument, Part 13

Objection 7

The disjunction is not exhaustive

“Rejecting rapture + preterism leaves the classical view” ignores historicism, idealism, partial preterism, and the competing millennial schemes. The elimination only feels conclusive.

Aggregator / careful student

Partially met

Clean but not formally closed

Craig's elimination is rhetorically tidy but not a closed disjunction within the segment. He engages some alternatives elsewhere in the series, but not by name here, and the millennial options are deferred entirely.

Aggregation gap

Objection 8

“Sudden” conflicts with the Olivet signs

If the discourse catalogues preceding signs — wars, the abomination of desolation, cosmic disturbances — that would seem to telegraph the approach. So how can the return also be an unforeseeable “thief in the night”?

Standard skeptical / exegetical

Gap — largely unaddressed

Tension between signs and unpredictability

Craig stresses unpredictability without reconciling it against the discourse's own catalogue of preceding signs. A natural objection from a careful listener that the corpus does not resolve.

Aggregation gap

Objection 9

Pinning Revelation to current technology has failed before

Reading the book's symbols onto specific present-day technologies has repeatedly discredited it. Why should the AI-age reading be any different?

Self-raised by Lennox

Strongest response

“Perfectly fair” — read carefully, don't over-specify

Lennox grants the worry: “people trying to pin everything down” has “really destroyed taking the book… seriously.” The discipline is to read carefully while noting that some scenarios — universal economic control under one leader — are newly conceivable.

Lennox ICMDA webinar

Persistent gaps in the corpus

Gap 1 — most notable

An unacknowledged partial-preterist split

Craig reads Mark 13:30 / “all these things” as fulfilled in the AD 70 destruction of Jerusalem — which is the partial-preterist exegesis — while in Part 10 rejecting preterism as “implausible.”

Internal tension across Parts 10–11

Owed and not given

He splits the discourse without naming the split

A near-preterist reading of the generation saying sits alongside a futurist reading of the parousia (13:24–27), with no defence against the charge of being ad hoc. He owes an account of why “all these things” (13:30) excludes the Son-of-Man coming that immediately precedes it — the very partitioning a critic contests.

Flag for Q-12.2.3

Gap 2

The skeptic's strongest reading is not met head-on

On the standard reading, “this generation” (hē genea hautē) means Jesus' contemporaries and “all these things” gathers up everything in the discourse, parousia included.

Critical exegesis

Undercutting, not rebutting

Craig establishes a plausible alternative — consistent with his stated modest aim, “we can't know for sure” — but does not refute the imminence reading on its own terms. The defeater undercuts; it does not rebut.

Aggregation gap

Gap 3

The delay-parables may be post-Easter creations

The standard form-critical reply (Schweitzer / Weiss): the delay-parables are themselves later community products — the church retrojecting delay-themes to cope with the non-return. On that reading they are evidence of the problem, not its solution.

Schweitzer, Weiss; form criticism

Gap — not engaged

Craig takes the four parables as authentic dominical teaching without addressing the retrojection thesis — the single most obvious gap given his audience-facing apologetic aim in Part 12.

Aggregation gap

Gap 4

Contextual ambiguity is applied asymmetrically

Parts 10–11 warn that editorial placement manufactures meaning, so an imminence impression is untrustworthy. Part 12 then reads four parables straight from their (largely Matthean) contexts as transparently teaching delay.

Method internal to Craig's own argument

Gap — unexplained asymmetry

If placement can fake imminence, in principle it can fake delay too. Craig does not explain why the delay-reading is contextually secure while the imminence-reading is not.

Aggregation gap

Gap 5

Conceding redactional freedom is double-edged

Craig leans hard on the evangelists “moving sayings around” and Matthew “paraphrasing Mark's words in a way that gives them a very different meaning.”

§14 reliability / inerrancy

Gap — left for §14

If Matthew freely alters Mark's tense, an inerrancy-minded listener — and, oppositely, a skeptic — can press how secure any dominical wording then is. The reliability implication is not addressed.

Flag for §14

Gap 6

The parity argument is a defence, not evidence

It shows a cosmic end is not incredible, not that the end will be Christ's return rather than a blind quantum event. And vacuum decay is offered as “the real possibility” without flagging that it is speculative and model-dependent.

Informed skeptic

Partly self-flagged

Craig himself limits it to “a much more modest point.” But the evidential gap from “physics permits an apocalypse” to “the apocalypse is the parousia” is left open, and the cosmology's contested status goes unmentioned.

Aggregation gap

Gap 7

Single resurrection / judgment vs staggered schemes

Craig presents one general resurrection and one judgment “of all people” at the parousia, without flagging that dispensational and premillennial schemes posit staggered resurrections (Rev 20) and distinct judgments (bema vs great white throne).

Dispensational / premillennial readers

Gap — tension not noted

A reader from those traditions would expect the divergence acknowledged. Because the sessions are catechetical, the alternative schemes are simply not in view (Q-12.2.2, Q-12.2.4).
