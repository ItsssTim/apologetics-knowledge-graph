The question

In a live cross-examination, which manoeuvres legitimately advance the argument, and which are merely rhetorical evasions dressed up as engagement?

This is a meta-level node. It is not about any one apologetic claim but about the conduct of the cross-examination itself — the round-by-round, question-and-answer phase where debaters press each other directly.

The recurring fault-line is the dialectic-versus-rhetoric tension: a move can be substantively sound yet rhetorically losing, or rhetorically devastating yet substantively empty. The experts disagree — sometimes within a single exchange — about where legitimacy actually lies.

The convergent structure — tactical streams, not a syllogism

There is no shared conclusion here — there is a shared evaluative criterion: a tactic is legitimate to the degree it tracks the actual strength of evidence rather than merely shaping audience perception. Four distinct tactics, each independently evaluated.

Stream A · Conspiracy-theorist frame

  • Brand the opponent unfalsifiable
  • “An answer for everything”
  • McLatchie vs Gleason
  • Turns on far-fetchedness, not on having answers
  • Criterion never operationalised

Stream B · Steelmanning

  • Restate opponent at his strongest
  • McLatchie genuine vs Dillahunty pseudo
  • Analysed by McGrew + Porter
  • Go-first-go-short rhetorical trap
  • Dialectic win, rhetorical loss

Stream C · Falsification reversal

  • “What would convince you you’re wrong?”
  • Meyer vs Ward
  • Resist the single-silver-bullet frame
  • Then commit publicly anyway
  • “I will resign the Discovery Institute”

A fourth stream — exposing misquotation (Craig vs Shabir Ally) — checks the primary source between rounds and surfaces the misreading publicly. The unifying lesson, stated by McGrew, is that the dialectical winner and the rhetorical winner are frequently different people, and legitimacy must be judged on the dialectical axis even when the rhetorical axis is what moves the room.

How each expert deploys it

Jonathan McLatchie2 transcripts
The dominant voice — conspiracy-frame + genuine steelman
Conspiracy-theorist frame (offensive, vs Gleason)
  • The single most distinctive named tactic in the node — applied ~5 times across the cross-examination.
  • Structure: conspiracy theorists rationalise away any contrary evidence → Gleason does this to every textual datum (Alexander & Rufus, Pauline material, Pastorals, Ignatius, “rulers of this age”) → a theory that absorbs anything is unfalsifiable.
  • Signature counter-distinction: the charge is not “having answers” but having answers far-fetched in proportion to the strength of contrary evidence.
Steelmanning done right (defensive, vs Dillahunty)
  • His steelman actually improves Dillahunty’s case — states the strongest form (miracle = least probable explanation; gospels are anonymous hearsay; claims are not evidence).
  • Contrast: Dillahunty “just said Jonathan is convinced” without saying what convinced him — preserving the warm-fuzzy impression.
Honest concession

McLatchie accepts the tu-quoque: a Carrier mythicist “almost has an answer to objections, they’re just very far-fetched.” But he never specifies how the audience is to tell “far-fetched” from “minority but legitimate” — the frame rests on audience intuition, not a stated criterion.

Stephen Meyer1 transcript
Falsification reversal — resist the frame, then commit
Two-stage response (vs Peter Ward)
  • Ward asks the classic Socratic question — “what set of data would totally convince you you’re absolutely wrong?” — and answers his own (a human skull in a T-rex’s jaws).
  • Stage 1 — principled resistance: “I don’t think theories are tested by one silver bullet experiment… I wouldn’t accept the way you framed your question.”
  • Stage 2 — concrete commitment anyway: if RNA-world simulations get RNA to self-assemble and solve the sequencing problem without intelligent design, “I will resign the Discovery Institute.”
  • Lesson: you can reject a question’s epistemology and still offer a credibility-establishing commitment — refusing the frame without appearing evasive.
William Lane Craig1 transcript
Exposing misquotation — the between-rounds source check
Distinctive move (vs Shabir Ally)
  • Ally claimed Raymond Brown held there was only one original resurrection appearance.
  • Craig “knew Brown was too intelligent a scholar” — went to Brown’s commentary on John between debates and surfaced the real context: Brown affirmed multiple appearances; only the appearances to the Twelve trace to one tradition.
  • Turns the misquotation into evidence for his own view: even Ally’s chosen scholar grants recipients (Paul, James, the 500, Mary Magdalene) outside the official witnesses — “so that actually proves my point.”
Not a clean victory

In his closing, Ally re-reads Brown’s footnote 170 more fully and argues it supports him (Brown sets the minor appearances aside as not formative for resurrection faith). A paradigm of the debate-table source-dispute the audience cannot resolve in real time — the Q-21.5.2 problem.

Timothy McGrew + Richard Porteranalytical commentary
The governing criterion — dialectic ≠ rhetoric
Verdict on the steelman exchange
  • Steelmanning means “making your opponent’s argument stronger… to the best of your ability.” Dillahunty’s was a tendentious short summary, not a steelman.
  • The trap: by going first and going short (10–15 seconds), Dillahunty ensured any real steelman from McLatchie would run long and lose the room.
  • The most quotable line in the node: McLatchie “did everything dialectical but lost the rhetorical moment; Matt did nothing dialectical but caught the rhetorical moment.”

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

“Having an answer to every objection makes you a conspiracy theorist? Then so are you.”

Reciprocity / tu-quoque: if I pose questions and you have answers for all of them, by your own logic that makes you a conspiracy theorist too.

John Gleason (Godless Engineer), vs McLatchie

Partial response

The charge is far-fetchedness, not having answers

McLatchie: the issue is having answers far-fetched in proportion to the strength of the contrary evidence — he concedes he holds answers too, just non-far-fetched ones. But the far-fetched / legitimate line is never operationalised, so the force rests on audience intuition.

McLatchie counter-distinctionCriterion left unstated

Objection 2

Recognising literary devices is standard analysis, not conspiracy thinking

Using a literary device to read a gospel that is non-historical and copying earlier works is normal literary scholarship — not a “conspiracy like idea.”

John Gleason, vs McLatchie

Contested

Reinterpreting a plain name-drop is the tell

McLatchie: when a name the original audience plainly knew (Alexander, Rufus) is recast as a “literary device” specifically to save the theory, that is the diagnostic move. But the corpus never settles when a literary-device reading is legitimate versus ad hoc.

McLatchie name-drop testLine not settled

Objection 3

“You defend intelligent design — also a crank idea. Glass houses.”

McLatchie defends ID, which many treat as a conspiracy theory or crank position; he should not be throwing stones from a glass house.

John Gleason, vs McLatchie

Gap — unanswered in transcript

McLatchie does not rebut the ID parity

No disanalogy between ID and mythicism is offered; he redirects to the far-fetchedness distinction. A node on cross-examination legitimacy ought to be able to say why (if at all) the ID case differs — it does not.

Objection 4

“I didn’t hold the position you steelmanned — I only asked if there’s evidence.”

Dillahunty disowns the steelmanned view: “I didn’t talk about miracles or how unlikely they were; all I talked about is, is there evidence for the resurrection.”

Matt Dillahunty (via debate review)

Strongest response

He has stated exactly this in other debates

McLatchie / McGrew: Dillahunty has said precisely this (a miracle is by definition the least probable explanation) in the Licona debates and implied it throughout; the retreat is a rhetorical move, not a substantive correction.

McLatchie prior-debate recordMcGrew dialectical read

Objection 5

“Name a single datum that would falsify you.”

The Socratic falsification demand: a real scientific theory must specify what observation would totally convince you you are wrong — name it.

Peter Ward, vs Meyer

Strongest response

Reject the single-silver-bullet frame, then commit

Meyer: theories are tested by preponderance, not one experiment — so he rejects the framing. Then he supplies a concrete public commitment: RNA self-assembly without design → resign the Discovery Institute.

Meyer resist-then-commit

Objection 6

“Your own cited scholar (Brown) actually supports my reading.”

In closing, Ally re-reads Brown’s footnote 170 (p.101 of The Virginal Conception) more fully, arguing Brown sets the minor appearances aside as not formative for resurrection faith — supporting Ally, not Craig.

Shabir Ally (closing), vs Craig

Gap — irresolvable at the table

The audience cannot adjudicate live

Craig’s between-rounds context check shows Brown affirms multiple appearances. But duelling readings of the same footnote cannot be settled in real time, and no expert offers a tactic for handling this honestly in the moment beyond “check it yourself afterwards.”
