The question

Do objective moral values and duties exist — and if they do, what grounds them?

The moral argument is, by the testimony of its most rigorous defender, the single most effective theistic argument in practice. Craig ranks the Kalam as his personal favourite but concedes the moral argument is “far more effective,” because “you can shut your eyes to the evidence of contemporary cosmology — but every day you get up, you answer by how you live the question: do you think other people have intrinsic and objective moral worth?”

Its force is that it cannot be evaded by abstraction. Cosmology can be deferred to specialists; moral commitment is enacted at every waking moment — in the lived conviction that the torture of a child for fun is really wrong, not merely unfashionable.

The convergent argument — Craig’s canonical two-premise form

Premise 1

If God does not exist, objective moral values and duties do not exist.

“Many theists and atheists alike concur.” Craig wins this premise by hostile-witness stacking — getting it conceded from the mouths of named atheists (Ruse, Mackie, Marks, Nietzsche, Russell, Sartre) so that denial looks philosophically eccentric. Objective = “valid and binding independently of whether anybody believes them or not.”

Premise 2

Objective moral values and duties do exist.

Apprehended in moral experience — “there’s no more reason to deny the objective reality of moral values than the objective reality of the physical world.” Supported by clear cases (torturing a child for fun) and by atheist moral realists themselves (Louise Antony, Peter Cave). Note: objective ≠ absolute — some killing may be justified; the claim is only belief-independence.

Conclusion

Therefore God exists.

“It follows logically and inescapably.” A deductive syllogism — if both premises hold, the conclusion is forced. The conclusion is modest: it establishes a transcendent moral ground, not yet the Christian God. The resurrection is a separate step.

The distinction the whole argument turns on

This is about moral ontology (what makes moral facts real), not moral epistemology (whether atheists can recognise or obey them). The argument concedes freely that atheists can be good and can know right from wrong — that is an epistemological observation and leaves the grounding question untouched. Almost every popular rebuttal (“atheists can be moral,” “I don’t need God to be good,” “name a moral act I can’t do”) commits this one conflation.

William Lane Craig 10 transcripts
The architect — the two-premise syllogism
Signature delivery
  • States the syllogism, defines “objective” as belief-independent binding.
  • Wins P1 from hostile witnesses: Ruse (“ethics is illusory”), Mackie, Joel Marks (“no longer in the business of deriving an ought from an is”), plus Nietzsche, Russell, Sartre.
  • Wins P2 from atheist moral realists: Antony (“premises less obvious than objective values themselves”), Cave (“torturing a child for fun is wrong, full stop”).

  • Argument-from-evil-for-God reversal + three reasons against moral Platonism + the Harris possible-worlds self-refutation.

Cliffe Knechtle 14 transcripts (often with Stuart)
Folk-level — “a law above the law”
Signature deployment
  • Through-line: matter cannot tell right from wrong, so morality needs “a mind prior to the human mind.”
  • The Nuremberg / Robert Jackson parable — his single most-deployed illustration: the cultural “just following orders” defence is defeated only by “a law above culture.”
  • MLK’s Letter from Birmingham Jail — the same move: “a law above the law of the great state of Alabama.”
  • Four-options framing (powerful / culture / individual / God); the cheated-on relativist who cries “that’s not fair”; FGM and honour-killing test cases; racism as objective evil via the imago Dei.
  • Third-horn Euthyphro: goodness “flows from the character of God,” neither arbitrary decree nor independent standard.

John Lennox 5 transcripts

  • The “rebel against your genes” trap: if we are nothing but our DNA, “what part of us can rebel against it?”

Jonathan McLatchie 1 transcript
Value-based argument — the stroke-victim reductio
Distinctive moves
  • Distinguishes pragmatic goodness (utility) from moral goodness (intrinsic virtue) — the argument concerns the latter.
  • Objectivity test: the Holocaust would have been wrong “whether anyone believes it or not — even if Hitler had won and eliminated everyone who thought it immoral.”
  • The stroke-victim reductio: if moral worth is grounded in cognitive capacity, a stroke victim loses moral value — which no one will accept. So cognition cannot ground worth; intrinsic value points to a Creator.
Preston Perry 1 transcript
Popular Socratic — “where do you get your moral standard?”
Distinctive move
  • Asks one hinge question — roughly six times, refusing every topic-pivot (Catholic abuse, “which Bible”) — until it is answered: “What do you get your moral status from?”
  • The adjudication problem: a self-grounded standard cannot adjudicate between itself and a rapist’s — “what is your argument against me?”
  • The imago Dei turn: “when you said murder is wrong, that’s evidence that you were created in the image of God” — her moral intuition is itself evidence for the God she denies.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

The Euthyphro dilemma

Is something good because God commands it (then goodness is arbitrary), or does God command it because it is good (then goodness exists independently of God)? Either way, God is not the ground of morality.

Julian Baggini, Eric Steinhart; the standard counter

Strongest response

The third horn — God’s good character

Goodness is neither arbitrary decree nor an external standard: it flows from God’s necessary character. “There is a good God who has a good character”; “an eternal value that flows from the character of God.”

Knechtle third-horn character replyCraig divine-nature account on record

Objection 2

“Atheists can be moral / I don’t need God to be good”

Plenty of atheists live decent moral lives and condemn atrocity. To say morality needs God is an insult to atheists everywhere — “it casts a slur on atheists.”

Hitchens, Atkins, Sinnott-Armstrong, Newdow, Dawkins

Strongest response

Ontology, not epistemology

Conceded entirely — and irrelevant. “I would never think of saying atheists can’t live good moral lives. What I’m saying is that there is no transcendent foundation for the values we both want to affirm” (Craig). “Of course atheists can be good — but can we find foundations?” (Lennox).

Craig ontology/epistemology distinctionLennox good-without-ground

Objection 3

Morality is grounded in evolution

Morality is a Darwinian adaptation — reciprocal altruism in small kin-groups plus a shifting “moral Zeitgeist.” We are social primates with an evolved moral sense; no God required.

Ruse & Darwin (as hostile witnesses), Shermer, Atkins, Dawkins, Smalley

Strongest response

Explains origin, not obligation

A causal story for moral belief does nothing to make the contents binding (Hume’s is/ought gap). “You can get moral feelings from evolution, but you cannot get to obligations, duties and the shoulds” (Knechtle). A lion kills but does not murder; rewind the film and a different creature emerges — calling our values binding is “speciesism” (Craig).

Craig Darwin / lion-zebra reductioLennox any morality from animal behaviourKnechtle feelings vs duties

Objection 4

Moral realism without God — atheistic Platonism

Objective moral facts can exist as part of the rational fabric of reality — brute, necessary, mind-independent — just like mathematical and logical truths. No “who” is required; adding “God” to the form of the good adds nothing.

Erik Steinhart (axiarchism), Arif Ahmed, Wielenberg; gestured by Law

Partial response — under-engaged

Three reasons against moral Platonism

Craig: (i) unintelligibility — “I have no inkling what it means to say Justice just exists”; (ii) abstract values generate no duty; (iii) it is an “incredible coincidence” that evolution threw up creatures conforming to a causally unconnected abstract realm. Stuart Knechtle: “you can’t have a relationship with a mathematical law.” But the robust non-natural realist alternative is repeatedly collapsed into “Platonism” and not engaged on its own terms.

Craig three reasonsWielenberg / Ahmed under-engaged

Objection 5

Cultural relativism

Different societies hold different values; there is no neutral vantage from which to call one culture’s morality “superior.” The South Sea Islander’s values are no more false than the European’s.

Newdow, Richardson; Sudanese honour-killing cases

Strongest response

Test-case reductio + “a law above culture”

Societies endorse atrocity — apartheid, the Dred Scott 3/5 clause, FGM, Nuremberg. The relativist cannot condemn them without a transcultural standard. “If you’re a relativist, the question’s not difficult — just murder the kid. The only reason it’s difficult is because you know that child has value” (Cliffe vs Newdow).

Knechtle Nuremberg / FGM test casesCraig South Sea Islanders

Objection 6

“Well-being is the foundation” (Sam Harris)

Moral goodness just is the well-being of conscious creatures; science can map the “moral landscape” of flourishing, closing the is/ought gap. Rape is wrong because it harms with no compensating benefit.

Sam Harris; Sinnott-Armstrong (harm-based); Atkins (social stability)

Strongest response

Harris’s possible-worlds self-refutation

Harris concedes in his own footnotes that the peaks of well-being and the peaks of moral goodness could come apart. “On identity theory, if it’s possible that two things are not identical, then they’re not identical — this completely undermines his moral theory” (Craig). And “harm” grounds nothing on naturalism: animals harm without immorality.

Craig identity-theory refutationLennox Harris hasn’t closed the is/ought gap

Objection 7

Theistic morality is itself subjective

If morality must be backed by an authority, it is just God’s opinion — no more objective than mine. And divine commands are fickle: “one minute gods say don’t kill, the next they say kill everyone in this town” (citing Craig’s own Canaanite-genocide defence).

Andrew Copson, Randolph Richardson

Partial response + dialectical gap

Necessary character — but the Canaanite case went unanswered

Grounded in God’s necessary character, not arbitrary will (the same third-horn move). But when Copson cited Craig’s own published defence of the Canaanite conquest directly, Craig did not return to it in his summary — reframing instead to formal moral ontology.

Knechtle / Craig character not willCanaanite citation unaddressed

Objection 8

Moral anti-realism is the majority view

A large proportion of professional meta-ethicists deny objective moral values altogether; many who accept them deny that God is the best explanation. So P2 (or the God-link) is far from secure.

Klemens Kappel

Strongest response

Authority cuts both ways; controversy ≠ uncertainty

Craig answers with his own long list of atheist P1-affirmers, and with Antony’s line that any argument for moral skepticism rests on premises less obvious than objective values themselves. His sharper rejoinder — anti-realists are “the moral equivalent of a blind person” — is rhetorically risky.

Craig hostile-witness list + Antony

Objection 9

“With God, all things are thinkable” — religion enables atrocity

Reverses Dostoevsky: it is not that without God all is permitted, but that with God any crime becomes thinkable when believers are convinced it is his will — apartheid, suicide bombing, the Canaanite conquest. Submitting moral judgement to God is “servile” and degrades human dignity.

Christopher Hitchens

Talking past each other

Treated as a category error, not resolved on the merits

Craig insists the argument is about ontological grounding, not the behavioural record of religion — Hitchens persistently answers the epistemological/behavioural question Craig did not ask. Hitchens’s counter-genealogy (apartheid as a Dutch Reformed creation) is contested but never adjudicated.

Craig ontology reframeBehavioural record not adjudicated

Objections not adequately addressed

The honest weak points where the popular moral-argument corpus under-delivers.

Gap 1

Atheistic moral realism (Wielenberg, Enoch, Shafer-Landau, Parfit) is under-engaged.

The most serious gap. The genuinely live contemporary alternative — robust non-natural, non-theistic moral realism — is repeatedly collapsed into “moral Platonism” (McLatchie explicitly) or not addressed at all (Ahmed’s “brute moral facts in the fabric of the world” goes unanswered; Steinhart’s axiarchism exposes that the “relation requires a person” claim is asserted, not argued).

Gap 2

The third-horn Euthyphro defence is often compressed or absent.

Where it appears it is asserted — “goodness flows from God’s character” — without engaging the natural follow-up Steinhart pressed: why is God’s character not itself an arbitrary brute fact, no better than a brute Platonic moral fact? Craig’s fuller divine-nature account simply does not surface, because few opponents pressed it hard.

Gap 3

The Canaanite-genocide problem went unanswered.

Copson raised it directly, quoting Craig’s own published defence. Craig never returned to it in that debate — a real dialectical gap.

Gap 4

Law’s combinational reply is restated rather than engaged.

Law argues the massive evidential weight of evil should drive one to reject P2 (objective values) rather than affirm the conclusion. Craig restates his syllogism rather than meeting this strongest version of the reply.

Gap 5

Baggini’s Humean sentimentalism / “false dichotomy” charge is left hanging.

Baggini argues that “arbitrary vs objective” omits a middle position grounded in suffering and compassion — “it’s not arbitrary that rape is awful, because it creates suffering.” Knechtle reverts to his imago-Dei reply rather than engaging it.

Gap 6

At folk level, the is/ought gap is asserted more than defended.

Knechtle and Perry lean on test-case reductio rather than meta-ethics, leaving sophisticated naturalist accounts (kin selection, contractualism) engaged only as strawmen.
