The question

Are the central Christian holy days — Easter, Christmas and Sunday worship — repackaged pagan festivals?

The allegation comes in three recurring forms, pressed hardest by Hebrew Roots, KJV-only and Sabbatarian streams and echoed by sceptics who use it against Christianity wholesale: that Easter is named for the goddess Ishtar (or Ostara) and its eggs and bunnies are fertility symbols; that Christmas was placed on 25 December to co-opt Saturnalia, Sol Invictus or the birthday of Mithras; and that Sunday worship is sun-god worship that Constantine imposed by changing the Sabbath.

The unifying answer is that all three rest on the association fallacy — a conclusion drawn from surface resemblance (a similar-sounding name, an overlapping date, a shared symbol) where the linguistic, historical and causal roots never actually cross. When the primary sources are dated and read, the borrowing — where it exists at all — runs the other way: the Christian practice is earlier, and the pagan parallel is later, mundane, or invented.

The convergent case — three strands, one fallacy

This is not a deductive syllogism. It is a historical-evidential cumulative case in which each holy day is a separate strand, defended by the same method: name the fallacy, date the sources, supply a mundane positive explanation. The spine running through all three is the association fallacy — correlation is not derivation — a tool Huff names out loud.

Strand A · Easter

  • Allegation: name from Ishtar / Ostara
  • Eggs & bunny = pagan fertility
  • Ishtar link is sound-only
  • Name from month Ēosturmōnaþ
  • Bunny 1572; eggs 13th c.

Strand B · Christmas

  • Allegation: 25 Dec = Saturnalia / Sol Invictus
  • Pagan dates are later
  • Christians fixed 25 Dec first
  • Saturnalia falls on 17 Dec
  • Pagans moved to compete

Strand C · Sunday

  • Allegation: sun-god worship via Constantine
  • Sunday is in the NT itself
  • Justin Martyr, mid-2nd c.
  • Constantine decriminalised it
  • “Every weekend is Easter”
The fallacy

Surface resemblance is not derivation.

The spine. A similar-sounding name (Easter / Ishtar), an overlapping date (25 December), or a shared symbol (an egg) is treated as proof of pagan origin. Huff names this the association fallacy: “a conclusion drawn based on the similar sounding words but in reality … an irrelevant association as the linguistic cultural and etymological roots don’t cross paths.”

The engine

Date the source — and the borrowing reverses.

The decisive move. In every strand the pagan-origins claim collapses once the Christian attestation is shown to predate the alleged pagan borrowing. Africanus fixes 25 December before any Sol Invictus link; Justin Martyr describes Sunday worship before Constantine is born; the Easter name comes from a month, not a goddess.

Conclusion

The holy days are not pagan in origin; the parallels are later, mundane, or invented — and the only fair critique is commercialisation, not paganism.

Positive account, every time. The bunny is a springtime hare; the eggs are Lenten preservation; the Christmas date is an internal “died-on-the-day-conceived” calculation; Sunday is the resurrection. Huff concedes the secularisation lament while denying the pagan-origin charge.

How each expert deploys it

Wes Huff 5 transcripts · all 3 strands
The etymological / source-dating debunker — the sole carrier of the full case
Method, repeated across every strand
  • Names the fallacy. The one expert who uses the technical term “association fallacy” out loud — making his Easter clip the canonical exhibit for Q-19.6.6.
  • Makes it sticky. Easter and Ishtar “have about as much to do with each other as steven seagal has to do with the beach seagull.” A homophone gag that disarms a “sounds-like” argument.
  • Dates the source — the real engine. Easter: only Germanic; elsewhere Pascha, from Passover; Ostara has one late line in Bede. Christmas: Sol Invictus link only mid-4th c., Africanus fixed 25 Dec late 2nd / early 3rd c. Sunday: Justin Martyr, mid-2nd c., read aloud on camera.
  • Supplies a mundane positive story. The bunny is a springtime hare (can’t breed with rabbits); the eggs are Lenten preservation plus Edward I; the date is the “died-on-the-day-conceived” calculation; Sunday is the resurrection.
  • Reverses the dependence. With Thomas Talley: pagans likely “moved their celebrations to december 25th to compete with the christians.” Constantine “basically made legal without repercussion” an existing practice.
Distinguish-and-concede

“There’s definitely grounds to lament the commercialization and secularization of these holidays … i can certainly understand that even if i don’t necessarily agree.” He grants the cultural critique while denying the historical one — and on Christmas opens by admitting he once held the pagan-origins view himself, the “I was wrong too” credibility move for a fringe audience.

John Lennox 1 transcript · all 3 at once
The direction-of-dependence framer — principle, not source-work
Distinctive moves
  • Christianity’s root is anti-pagan Judaism. “Judaism in the old testament was a protest against paganism … it didn’t arise at all out of pagan religion quite the opposite.” Abraham was “a pagan idolater to begin with” — called out of paganism.
  • Direction of dependence (citing Oxford’s Werner Jaeger): “the pagan gods are descended from the universe the biblical god created the universe.” A categorical, not cosmetic, difference.
  • Dead etymology is not live theology. “Today is monday that’s moon day … but these associations have dropped.” Eating cereal is not worshipping Ceres; a Christmas tree is not a pagan ritual to the person decorating it.
  • Science buried only the gap-god. “You don’t need a god for lightning — but the god who created a world in which lightning exists, that’s a very different matter.”
Role in the case

Lennox supplies no source-dating; his contribution is the frame Huff’s evidence sits inside — that the borrowing thesis runs the genealogy backwards, and that an etymological fossil is not evidence of worship. On its own it would not move someone committed to the specific etymological claims.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1 · Easter

“Easter” comes from Ishtar

The Christian festival is named for the Babylonian fertility goddess Ishtar; the near-identical sound proves the derivation.

Hebrew Roots, internet folk-etymology

Strongest response

Association fallacy — the resemblance is sound-only

The “linguistic cultural and etymological roots don’t cross paths.” The two have as much to do with each other “as steven seagal has to do with the beach seagull.” Outside Germanic languages the festival is Pascha, from Passover.

Huff association-fallacy anchor

Objection 2 · Easter

“Easter” comes from the goddess Ostara

The Anglo-Saxon spring goddess Ostara gave the festival its name, as recorded by the Venerable Bede.

Folk-etymology citing Bede

Strongest response

One late source; the name is really a month

Bede (7th c.) is the only source, with no corroboration “christian or pagan”; many scholars think he invented or misremembered her. “Ostara” ties to Germanic ostaro, “springtime.” The name comes from the month Ēosturmōnaþ, the way “Fourth of July” names Independence Day.

Huff date-the-source + parity

Objection 3 · Easter

The Easter bunny is a pagan fertility symbol

Rabbits breed prolifically, so the Easter bunny is an obvious survival of a pagan fertility cult.

Hebrew Roots, citing Jacob Grimm

Strongest response

First attested 1572 — and it was a hare

The first official Easter bunny is 1572, long after paganism’s European foothold; it was originally a hare, which “can’t even breed with rabbits.” The pagan link is Jacob Grimm’s evidence-free 19th-c. aside. Rabbits simply “appear more numerous in springtime.”

Huff source-dating + positive explanation

Objection 4 · Easter

Easter eggs are pagan

Eggs appear in pagan ritual; their place in Easter proves a pagan origin.

Hebrew Roots

Strongest response

Universal food + Lenten preservation

Eggs are a near-universal food and “feature no more prominently than meat” in pagan ritual. The earliest Easter eggs are Edward I (late 13th c.); the medieval prevalence comes from Lenten fasting — eggs were hard-boiled to preserve them through the 40-day fast.

Huff documented-history anchor

Objection 5 · Christmas

25 December was copied from Sol Invictus / Saturnalia

Christians scheduled Jesus’s birth on an existing pagan festival date to ease conversion.

Sceptics; mythicist-adjacent

Strongest response

The pagan dates are later, or the wrong day

Sol Invictus is tied to 25 December only from the mid-4th c.; Christians (Africanus) fixed it late 2nd / early 3rd c. Saturnalia falls on 17 December (Macrobius). It is “even more probable that the pagans moved their celebrations … to compete” (Talley, Aurelian).

Huff date-the-source + causal reversal

Objection 6 · Christmas

Other dying-god birthdays fall on the solstice

Tammuz, Horus, Mithra, Attis and Dionysus were all born on or near the winter solstice — Christmas is just another solstice myth.

Mythicist

Strongest response

Those attestations are too late

The solstice-birth dates for those deities “come well after the fourth century” — long after Christians had already identified 25 December. The chronology rules them out as the source rather than the borrower.

Huff chronological priority

Objection 7 · Christmas

Shepherds in the fields rule out a December birth

Luke has shepherds grazing flocks at night, which only happened March–November — so a 25 December birth is impossible and the date must be borrowed.

Internal Christian / sceptic

Strongest response

Unlikely, not impossible — and beside the point

Winter grazing is “unlikely although not impossible.” Ultimately no one knows the real birth date — which is exactly what we expect if 25 December is a theological calculation rather than a preserved memory, not a pagan import.

Huff concede-and-redirect

Objection 8 · Sunday

Constantine changed the Sabbath from Saturday to Sunday

The emperor Constantine moved Christian worship from the biblical Saturday Sabbath to Sunday in the 4th century.

Sabbatarian, Seventh-day, Hebrew Roots

Strongest response

He decriminalised it; he did not change it

Sunday worship predates Constantine by centuries — Justin Martyr (mid-2nd c., read aloud), Revelation’s “day of the Lord.” Constantine only stopped Sunday legal proceedings so Christians could worship “without repercussion.” There are recorded arrests on Sundays before this.

Huff pre-Constantinian primary source

Objection 9 · Sunday

Sunday worship is really sun-god worship (the dies Solis edict)

Constantine’s own 321 civil edict commands rest on “the venerable day of the Sun” — the day is named for the sun-god, so the practice is solar in origin.

Sabbatarian

Gap — only indirectly answered

Resurrection rationale established; the edict wording is not addressed

Huff anchors Sunday in the resurrection (“every weekend is easter weekend”) and in pre-Constantinian practice, which defeats the “Constantine changed it” claim. But he never quotes or defuses Constantine’s dies Solis edict — the specific solar wording Sabbatarians cite.

Aggregation gap

Objection 10 · General

Christianity is just repackaged paganism

The faith as a whole is a syncretism of the pagan religions around it — the holy days are simply the most visible evidence.

Sceptic / mythicist

Strongest response

The root is anti-pagan Judaism; dependence runs the other way

Christianity grows from Judaism, which was “a protest against paganism.” Jaeger’s direction-of-dependence: “the pagan gods are descended from the universe the biblical god created the universe.” A categorical break, not a borrowing.

Lennox direction-of-dependence frame

Objection 11 · Easter

Ishtar herself was a dying-and-rising deity (Akitu / Tammuz)

Beyond the name, the substance of Easter — death and resurrection — mirrors the Ishtar / Tammuz resurrection myth of the Babylonian Akitu festival.

Mythicist (the strong form)

Gap — dismissed by analogy, not engaged

The resurrection-myth claim is never met on its own terms

Huff defeats the name link by etymology and the Seagal/seagull gag, but never engages the substantive Akitu / Tammuz dying-and-rising allegation. This is the largest gap and overlaps directly with Q-3.4.6 (Christ-myth / pagan parallels).
