Divine foreknowledge and the “bare foreknowledge” problem

If God already knows the whole free future, can that knowledge do any work — or does simple foreknowledge arrive too late to ground providence? The diagnosis that Molinism is built to cure. A William Lane Craig –only node.

The question

Does God exhaustively foreknow the free future — and if so, can that foreknowledge ground providence, or is it a “fifth wheel” that arrives too late?

Two sub-questions sit inside one node. The first is whether God foreknows contingent free acts — Craig says yes, and that the alleged conflict with freedom rests on a fallacy. The second is harder: granting exhaustive foreknowledge, what can it actually do? If God has already issued the creative decree, then learning the future “afterwards” by simple foreknowledge is explanatorily idle. He cannot plan around it, because it is settled only once everything else is. That is the bare-foreknowledge, or “fifth wheel,” problem.

The node is strategically load-bearing because it is the problem Molinism is built to solve. Middle knowledge inserts a third moment of divine knowledge — what each free creature would do in any circumstance — logically prior to the creative decree, so providence can be planned with the free future in view without overriding it. Read this page as the diagnosis and Q-7.4.2 as the cure.

The convergent case — three argument threads, not one syllogism

This is a WLC-only node, so “convergent” means the three distinct threads Craig runs across four transcripts, not a consensus across experts. They stack: foreknowledge is real, it is compatible with freedom — but bare foreknowledge is explanatorily inert, which is precisely why a richer account is needed. Throughout the Defenders lectures Craig is steel-manning the Calvinist; his own Molinist resolution is deliberately withheld for later instalments.

Thread 1 · Foreknowledge is real & free

  • The NT pro· vocabulary: prognosis, proginosko, proorizo, promartyromai
  • Fulfilled contingent prophecy — Peter’s threefold denial, uninferrable from present causes
  • The freedom worry is fatalism — an ancient Greek error the Fathers rebutted
  • Do not slide from certainty to necessity
  • The infallible-barometer analogy: it tracks, it does not cause

Thread 2 · “Foreknew” = “foreloved”

  • Hebrew “to know” = favour / love / choose, not data-gathering
  • Genesis 18:19 — “I have known him” = “I picked him out”
  • Psalm 1:6 — God knows the way of the wicked too, so “knows” must mean favours
  • Jeremiah 1:5 — paired with “consecrated” and “appointed”
  • Rom 8:29 “foreknew” = an active bestowal of love (citing Charles Horn)

Thread 3 · Bare foreknowledge is a fifth wheel

  • Naive reading: God foresees free belief, then ratifies it
  • But they will believe anyway — “before” God predestines them
  • So the predestining act does no work — a “superfluous exercise”
  • Calvinist fix: relocate causality into the bestowal of faith itself
  • Molinist fix (withheld here): knowledge logically prior to the decree → Q-7.4.2

Structural tell — Craig is steel-manning, not asserting

Read in isolation these lectures look pro-Calvinist. Craig is voicing the Calvinist objection to a naive Arminian reading, building it at full strength before his own rebuttal. His Molinist answer — that God can foreknow how a libertarian-free agent would respond to grace, which blocks the fifth-wheel charge without making faith unilateral — never surfaces here. Objections handled — cross-expert master list

A single-expert node: “source” is the position Craig is answering; “response” is his own, or the Calvinist’s where he is steel-manning.

Objection 1

Foreknowledge destroys freedom (theological fatalism)

If God infallibly foreknows that you will do X, then you must do X — you are not free to do otherwise. Certainty of the outcome looks like necessity of the outcome.

Ancient Greek fatalism; modern incompatibilists

Strongest response

The fallacious slide from certainty to necessity

The argument equivocates: X is certain on God’s foreknowledge, but it is not thereby necessary. “X will happen but it could fail to happen — but if it were to fail to happen, then God would have had different foreknowledge instead.” The barometer tracks the weather without causing it.

Craig certainty ≠ necessity + barometer analogyCraig fatalism is an old Greek error the Fathers rebutted

Objection 2

Simple foreknowledge of foreseen faith already grounds predestination

God looks into the future, sees who would freely believe, and predestines them on that basis. No richer account is needed — Romans 8:29 means exactly that.

Naive Arminian reading of Rom 8:29

Strongest response

Then predestination is a fifth wheel

If they will believe anyway, the predestining act does no work — “they will believe in Christ even ‘before’ God predestines them.” To capture genuine sovereign election the causality must be relocated into God’s own act of bestowing saving faith.

Craig steel-manning the Calvinist reductio

Objection 3

“Foreknew” just means informational advance-knowledge

In Romans 8:29 “those whom he foreknew” means simply those whose future faith God knew in advance — a passive acquisition of information, nothing more.

The thin lexical reading

Strongest response

Hebrew “know” means favour / love / choose

“Foreknew” = “foreloved.” Psalm 1:6 settles it: God “knows the way of the wicked” too, so mere awareness cannot be what distinguishes the righteous — the word must carry relational favour. Gen 18:19 and Jer 1:5 confirm the election sense.

Craig citing Charles Horn — OT word-study

Objection 4

There is no exhaustive foreknowledge of free acts to explain

Genuinely free future acts are not there to be known in advance; an open future cannot be exhaustively foreknown, so the whole problem is misframed.

Open-theist / process-adjacent denial

Strongest response

Scripture plus uninferrable prophecy

The Bible’s pro· vocabulary, together with abundant fulfilled prophecies of “highly contingent” events — Peter denying Jesus three times before the cock crowed twice — establishes exhaustive foreknowledge of free acts that “couldn’t have been inferred from present causes.”

Craig Rutgers Q&A

Objection 5

The fifth-wheel reductio has a Molinist answer — so why not give it?

Middle knowledge would let God foreknow how a libertarian-free agent would respond to grace, logically prior to the creative decree — defeating the fifth-wheel charge without making faith a unilateral gift. The reader is left with the problem and not the cure.
