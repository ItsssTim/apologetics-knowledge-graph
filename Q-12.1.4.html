The question

What does the doctrine actually say hell is — its nature, its duration, and the form of the unbeliever’s final state?

This is the doctrinal node — “what hell is” — and it is deliberately distinct from the apologetic nodes that defend the doctrine. Whether hell is just lives at Q-2.5.1; whether its severity is proportionate lives at Q-2.5.5; the three-way debate between eternal conscious torment, annihilationism, and universalism lives at Q-2.5.2 / Q-12.1.5. This page asks the prior question those debates presuppose: on the view the experts hold, what is the final state like?

Two answers converge from opposite registers. Craig supplies the systematic-theology content — a two-stage scheme (hades now → gehenna after the general resurrection) terminating in eternal conscious punishment, grounded in the Matthew 25:46 parallelism. Knechtle supplies the pastoral framing — hell as self-chosen separation from God, deliberately under-pictured in Scripture, against which we should resist importing Dante’s imagery.

Honest density note

The corpus states eternal conscious torment as doctrine but largely defers the argument for it. Neither expert engages the annihilationism debate on its merits, and the moral defence is explicitly held over (WLC: “in the final two lessons”). This page captures the doctrinal content and points to where each defence actually lives, rather than manufacturing arguments the sources do not make.

The doctrinal structure — convergent and complementary

Not a syllogism (cf. Q-1.1.1 Kalam) but a cumulative doctrinal case plus a pastoral reframe. The two experts work different strands of one picture: Craig answers what happens and how long; Knechtle answers what it is and why anyone ends there.

Strand A · Craig — the scheme

  • Two stages: hades now → gehenna after the general resurrection
  • At Christ’s return all the dead are raised and judged
  • The unsaved pass “from hades into gehenna”
  • Hades ≠ hell — hell is the final state

Strand B · Craig — the duration

  • Matthew 25:46 parallelism settles it
  • “Eternal punishment” yoked to “eternal life”
  • The punishment is as enduring as the life
  • Eternal conscious punishment — not annihilation, not universalism

Strand C · Knechtle — the nature

  • Hell is fundamentally separation from God
  • All good gifts come from God → separation = absence of all joy
  • The ratified result of one’s own refusal to repent
  • Scripture is “sufficiently vague”; gehenna is the controlling image; resist Dante

Craig's cumulative case — resurrection to eternal hell

Craig’s Defenders 3 lecture builds the final state as a chain of prooftexts driving to one doctrinal conclusion. The one genuinely argumentative stroke is the parallelism at Step 4; everything else is exposition.

Step 1 · John 5:25-29

At Christ’s coming, all the dead are raised — the good to a resurrection of life, those who did evil to a resurrection of judgment.

The dead, “whether in hades or in paradise,” are raised, the soul reunited with a body, and then judged before the judgment seat of Christ. The intermediate state (Q-12.1.1) is not the final state.

Step 2 · 2 Cor 5:10 / 1 Cor 4:5

Judgment falls at the parousia, not at the moment of death.

Timing claim. Craig presses this against any “judged immediately at death” view: “do not pronounce judgment before the time, before the Lord comes.” The judgment “will take place not when you die — it will be at the time of christ’s return when we are raised from death.”

Step 3 · 1 Cor 3:12-15

Believers face a works judgment — reward or loss — but the believer is still saved.

Preserves grace. The Christian whose work is burned up “will suffer loss, though he himself will be saved, but only as through fire” — they get in “only as it were with the smell of smoke on their garments.” This distinction answers the worry that the universal judgment seat sweeps the saved into condemnation. Reward language corroborated at 2 Tim 4:8.

Step 4 · Matt 25:31-46 — the load-bearing inference

The unsaved are sentenced to eternal punishment — by the grammatical parallelism with eternal life.

The symmetry argument. “Just as the saved … will have eternal life, so the unrighteous dead will be raised and will be given eternal punishment.” The duration of the punishment is read off the duration of the life it is paired with in the same sentence. This is the standard exegetical defence of eternal conscious torment against annihilationism — deployed here without naming the opponent.

Conclusion · the two-stage fate

The unrighteous pass from hades into gehenna — the final state called hell — in everlasting punishment and separation from God.

Corroborated from Romans 2:6-11 (wrath for the wicked, eternal life for the good, “God shows no partiality”). Craig states the severe doctrine plainly and withholds the pastoral and apologetic softening for the closing lessons.

How each expert frames it

William Lane Craig 1 transcript · dominant voice
The systematic-theology spine
Signature deployment
  • Doctrinal exposition, not polemic — Defenders 3, Doctrine of the Last Things Part 20.
  • Two-stage scheme: hades (the conscious intermediate state) now → gehenna / hell as the final state after the general resurrection.
  • Judgment at the parousia, not at death — pressed via 2 Cor 5:10 and 1 Cor 4:5.
  • Believers’-works judgment (1 Cor 3:12-15): reward vs loss, “but he himself will be saved” — carefully preserves salvation by grace.
  • Matthew 25:46 parallelism — the one argumentative move: punishment as enduring as the life it is yoked to.
  • Vivid teaching figure: the minimally-rewarded believer saved “with the smell of smoke on their garments.”
Unaddressed tension

WLC twice couples punishment with “death” — “eternal punishment and death” — which is exactly the language an annihilationist would cite as supporting their reading. He does not notice or address the tension with his own anti-annihilationist parallelism.

Cliffe Knechtle 2 transcripts
The pastoral reframe
Signature deployment
  • Hell is separation, not a spatial location — “you chose to live your life separate from me, you’ll spend eternity separate from me.” No party in hell, because all the gifts that make a party are from God.
  • “Be very careful about people who give you a photograph of hell” — his signature move. Jesus uses metaphor pervasively (light, door, living water).
  • Fire and outer darkness can’t literally co-exist — the incompatibility signals metaphor.
  • Gehenna as the controlling image — the Jerusalem garbage dump: refuse, gone, not coming back. The concrete picture left in place of Dante’s.
  • Hell respects free will — “he’s not going to force his forgiveness on me. I have to repent.”
  • The cross bore the penalty (street clip) — Christ “took the hell that I deserve … in his body” (hinge to Q-8.2.3).
Pastoral humility as tactic

Pressed on the hardest case, Knechtle retreats to “the Bible’s sufficiently vague about it that I really don’t know,” and explicitly holds even annihilationism open as a live option — where Craig’s exegesis excludes it by implication, Knechtle declines to close the door.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

The Hitler-vs-Sarah disparity

How can a good person who never heard of Christ — “Sarah who’s helping homeless people, giving half her money away” — end up “in the same spot” as Hitler and Genghis Khan?

George Janko, to Knechtle

Response (partial)

Deny “same spot”; gesture at degrees

Knechtle: hell is separation, not a location, so “same spot” mis-describes it. Then gestures at possible degrees of hell (Paul’s striving for a heavenly prize implying levels), then concedes openness on the details.

Knechtle separation reframe + degrees hint

Objection 2

Fire AND outer darkness — the images are incompatible

How can hell be both fire and outer darkness at the same time? The picture is incoherent on a literal reading.

Implicit in literalist readings

Strongest response

The incompatibility is the point

Knechtle: the clash of images signals that Jesus is speaking metaphorically, as he does throughout — “I am the light of the world” (not a 100-watt bulb), “I am the door” (not two pieces of plywood).

Knechtle metaphor diagnosis

Objection 3

“You’re just quibbling”

Calling the fire metaphorical is an ad hoc dodge — you’re just getting out of a difficult passage.

Anticipated by Knechtle

Strongest response

Not ad hoc — Jesus’ systematic method

Knechtle: “wait a second — Jesus uses metaphor all the time.” Light, door, living water, born again. A consistent hermeneutic, not a convenient exception for the hard text.

Knechtle internal-critique appeal

Objection 4

Why not annihilation?

“I pray that whoever goes to hell gets burnt up and then no longer exists.” Why eternal conscious punishment rather than final destruction?

George Janko, to Knechtle

Split response

Knechtle holds it open; Craig excludes by implication

Knechtle treats annihilation as a live option within Bible-vague terrain (“might be annihilated”). Craig excludes it via the Matt 25:46 parallelism (punishment as enduring as the life) — but never names or argues against annihilationism. The debate proper belongs at Q-12.1.5.

Knechtle live optionCraig excluded by parallelism

Objection 5

Aren’t believers condemned too?

If all the dead face the same judgment seat, does the universal judgment sweep the saved into condemnation alongside the unsaved?

Implicit clarification (Craig)

Strongest response

Reward-vs-loss ≠ condemnation

Craig (1 Cor 3:12-15): the believers’-works judgment yields reward or loss, “but he himself will be saved.” It is categorically distinct from the condemnation of the unsaved — salvation by grace is preserved.

Craig believers’-judgment distinction

Persistent gaps in the corpus

Gap 1

The annihilationism debate is stated, not argued

Craig asserts eternal conscious punishment and excludes annihilationism by the parallelism, but never states the annihilationist reading of these very texts and never rebuts it. His own twice-repeated “punishment and death” phrasing is exactly what an annihilationist would cite in support.

Defence lives at Q-2.5.2 / Q-12.1.5
