Imputed vs infused righteousness

The doctrinal fault line of the Reformation, turning on a single preposition — is justifying righteousness credited to the believer or infused in the believer? Two experts treat it in five substantive extractions, dominated by William Lane Craig across four consecutive lessons.

The question

When God justifies a sinner, does he credit them with a righteousness from outside, or make them inwardly righteous?

This is the doctrinal fault line of the sixteenth-century Reformation, and it turns on a single preposition. The Reformers (Luther) held that righteousness is extrinsic — the alien righteousness of Christ, legally credited, received complete and once-for-all by faith. The Council of Trent held it is intrinsic — God actually makes the believer morally righteous, infusing a real quality that is then increased over time through the sacraments and obedience.

The same word, justification, names two incompatible doctrines: on one the believer’s standing changes (a verdict); on the other the believer’s being changes (a transformation). The corpus also folds in a second, intra-Protestant dispute — whether Christ’s righteousness is imputed to us at all, or only a divine pardon — where Craig defends classical dual imputation against the Evangelical scholar Robert Gundry.

The convergent case — a binary on three axes, not a syllogism

Unlike the Kalam (a deductive syllogism), this is a comparative-definition case. Craig’s whole pedagogy is the paired binary — he hammers three opposed axes until the contrast is unmissable, then mounts the positive biblical argument. McLatchie supplies the same forensic pole from the pulpit, exegetically rather than historically.

Axis 1 · Location

  • Extrinsic vs intrinsic
  • Christ’s righteousness, credited
  • “alien” — outside me
  • vs “my righteousness”
  • God produces vs God credits

Axis 2 · Mechanism

  • Imputed vs infused
  • legally reckoned to my account
  • vs imparted / poured in
  • a verdict vs a moral quality
  • declared righteous vs made righteous

Axis 3 · Time

  • Event vs process
  • complete, once-for-all by faith
  • vs initial infusion, then increased
  • justification done; sanctification grows
  • vs “more and more justified”
Reformation pole

Righteousness is extrinsic, imputed, and complete — Christ’s, credited to the believer’s account by faith.

Both experts hold this. Craig: “the righteousness of Christ that is imputed to you … not your righteousness that God produces.” McLatchie reads it off the Old Testament types — Joshua re-clothed in the high priest’s garments (Zechariah 3), the plate “holy to the Lord” (Exodus 28) — and seals it with forensic vocabulary: believers are “legally acquitted … and forensically declared righteous.”

Catholic pole (Trent)

Righteousness is intrinsic, infused, and growing — God actually makes the believer righteous, increasing it via the sacraments.

As mapped by Craig, reading Trent aloud. “God actually makes me righteous; it is my righteousness that I come to possess.” Justification is “both an event and a process”: an initial infusion, then “more and more righteousness over time.” Note the asymmetry — the corpus gives this pole decrees, not its biblical case.

Dual imputation

Our sin is imputed to the sinless Christ; his (God’s) righteousness is imputed to us — a two-directional exchange.

Craig’s positive argument (vs Gundry). Grounded not in Romans 4 but in Philippians 3:8-9 (“a righteousness from God that depends on faith”) and 2 Corinthians 5:21 (sin imputed to Christ; “so that in him we might become the righteousness of God”). McLatchie reaches the same verse from the cross.

Conclusion

Same word, two doctrines: an alien, completed, credited righteousness (the Reformers) vs a real, growing, infused one (Rome).

Craig maps the divide with conceded fairness but, in the comparative lesson, does not adjudicate it. The Protestant case against infusion is asserted (the marriage analogy; the faith/works contrast) rather than fully prosecuted — mark the density accordingly.

How each expert deploys it

William Lane Craig 4 transcripts
The cartographer of the divide — Defenders Parts 9, 12, 13, 16
Signature moves
  • The core binary (Part 12, canonical anchor): Luther vs Trent on three axes, in a memorable triple cadence — “intrinsic to me … my righteousness … I come to possess” vs “extrinsic … righteousness of Christ … credited to his account.” Reads Trent’s decrees and canons aloud.
  • Marriage analogy (Part 13): a couple “may feel exactly the same … but their status has now changed” — status without internal quality, modelling imputation against infusion.
  • Turretin circularity correction (Part 9): grounding justification in mystical union is viciously circular, since only the justified are in union. Justification is explanatorily prior — explanatory priority is not temporal priority (the bankruptcy / court-order analogy).
  • Dual-imputation defence (Part 16): concede Gundry’s Romans 4 point, then relocate the doctrine to Philippians 3 and 2 Corinthians 5; collapse the “God vs Christ” wedge with a high-Christology lemma (Acts 20:28 “blood of God”).
Striking candour

Mid-series, Craig publicly corrects his own teaching order: “were I to teach this section over again I would have discussed mystical union after … regeneration and justification rather than before.” Privileging explanatory rigour over received structure — the most apologetically interesting moment in the cluster.

Fair concession (steelman)

Trent’s language of justification as a “translation … to the state of grace and of adoption” is something Protestants “could … affirm … so long as one is talking about a change of legal status.” The disagreement is isolated to what justification consists in.

Jonathan McLatchie 1 transcript
The forensic pole from the pulpit — Hebrews 4–5 sermon
Distinctive moves
  • Forensic, not ontic, imputation: at the cross “Christ … was forensically imputed with our iniquity, no in the sense that he somehow became corrupted in his own nature” — the clause pre-empts the “PSA makes Christ a sinner” objection.
  • The perfect record substituted: “Christ spent every single moment of his life perfect before God” yet “stood in our … place condemned on our behalf.”
  • Zechariah 3 / Joshua: filthy garments (the nation’s sins) removed, Joshua re-clothed in the high priest’s garments; the angel claims the divine prerogative of removing sin.
  • Exodus 28 high-priestly garments = imputed righteousness: the plate reads “holy to the Lord” though the priest “wasn’t really holy… he’s treated as though he is.”
  • 2 Corinthians 5:21 as the New Testament hinge (the same verse Craig makes load-bearing); a Genesis 3 animal-skin typology brackets the close. Closes on the forensic verdict: “legally acquitted … and forensically declared righteous.”

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

Penal substitution makes Christ literally a sinner

If our iniquity is imputed to Christ, then Christ becomes corrupt or sinful in his own nature — an intolerable conclusion.

Standard anti-PSA objection (pre-empted, not named)

Strongest response

The imputation is forensic, not ontic

God reckoned our guilt to Christ “no in the sense that he somehow became corrupted in his own nature” but in the sense that, looking on the cross, God saw all our lawlessness laid on the sinless one. Legal transfer, not moral contamination.

McLatchie forensic-not-ontic clause

Objection 2

Mystical union is the foundation of justification

Our sins are imputed to Christ and his righteousness to us in virtue of our union with Christ — so union is “the cause and foundation” of justification, and is explanatorily prior.

Francis Turretin (an internal, Reformed critique)

Strongest response

Viciously circular; reverse the order

Only the regenerate-and-justified are in union with Christ. So Turretin needs union to be prior to justification and justification prior to union — a vicious circle. Justification is explanatorily prior; being “in Christ” is a legal status received in virtue of God’s declaring us righteous.

Craig internal critique + self-correction

Objection 3

Union and justification are simultaneous, so neither is “prior”

If union and justification happen at the same instant, why insist justification grounds union rather than the reverse?

Implicit rejoinder to Craig’s own correction

Strongest response

Explanatory priority is not temporal priority

A company’s bankruptcy is a legal status conferred by a court order; the order is explanatorily prior to the bankruptcy even though they are simultaneous. “The court does not issue its declaration in virtue of your being in bankruptcy.” So too with justification and union.

Craig bankruptcy / court-order analogy

Objection 4

No imputation of Christ’s righteousness — God reckons our own faith

Romans 4:2-5: God counts Abraham’s faith as righteousness. There is no imputation of Christ’s righteousness to you at all — only a declaration of pardon.

Robert Gundry (intra-Evangelical)

Strongest response

Wrong text; and it ironises faith into a work

(a) Faith-reckoning is itself a “legal fiction” (the Mostyn v. Fabrigas “Minorca deemed part of London” precedent). (b) Paul’s many “righteousness through faith” texts govern the Romans 4 quotation, not vice versa. (c) Reckoning faith itself as righteousness makes faith a meritorious work — the thing Paul denies.

Craig relocate to Phil 3 / 2 Cor 5

Objection 5

Paul always opposes faith to works, so faith is never a meritorious work

Gundry’s defence: precisely because Paul contrasts faith and works, faith-reckoning cannot be turning faith into a work.

Robert Gundry

Strongest response

“But surely that’s exactly the point”

The faith/works contrast only coheres if the righteousness we have in Christ is extrinsic to us. If righteousness were my own quality, faith would collapse into a work. The contrast itself presupposes an imputation view.

Craig turns the contrast back

Objection 6

2 Cor 5:21 says “righteousness of God,” not “of Christ”

Paul could easily have written “the righteousness of Christ” but wrote “the righteousness of God” — so the verse is not about imputing Christ’s righteousness.

Robert Gundry

Strongest response

A trivial distinction, given Christ’s deity

Since Paul held Christ to be God, “the righteousness of God” just is the righteousness properly Christ’s — no more troubling than Acts 20:28’s “the blood of God,” plainly Christ’s blood since the Father has no body.

Craig high-Christology lemma + Acts 20:28 parity

Objection 7

The Tridentine biblical case — James 2, grace-wrought merit, unmerited initial grace

“Faith without works is dead” (James 2); James 2:23 quotes the same Abraham text to a works-vindicating end; Trent insists the initial grace of justification is unmerited (ch. 8) and that meritorious works are themselves grace-wrought (ch. 16); the 1999 Joint Declaration narrows the gap.

Council of Trent; James 2; Catholic theology

Gap — largely unanswered

The corpus gives Rome decrees, not Scripture

The Protestant view is given the biblical texts (Romans 4, Philippians 3, 2 Corinthians 5, Zechariah 3, Exodus 28); the Catholic view is given conciliar canons read aloud. James 2 is named as a tension but its exegesis is deferred; congruent/condign merit and the Joint Declaration go unengaged. The single most important asymmetry in this node.

Aggregation gap

Persistent gaps in the corpus

This node carries an unusually lopsided evidential record. Beyond the headline asymmetry above, five further flanks are unengaged.

Mapped, not adjudicated

  • Part 12 lays both views side by side fairly — but does not there argue the infusion view is wrong.
  • The Romans 4 “wages … as his due” rebuttal WLC had in hand is deferred or not supplied.
  • Comparative definition, not refutation. Mark density.

Forensic model assumed

  • The Turretin circularity only bites given a forensic model of justification.
  • A participationist (Catholic, Orthodox, Finnish-Lutheran, apocalyptic-Pauline) reading denies union must presuppose a prior verdict.
  • WLC does not engage that flank — bears on Q-9.1.2, Q-9.1.3.

Charge never turned back

  • “Legal fiction” is levelled at Gundry’s faith-reckoning.
  • The identical Tridentine charge — imputation declares righteous someone internally unchanged (the “snow-covered dunghill”) — is gestured at (marriage analogy) but not re-mounted.
  • And John 15’s branches-in-the-vine sit in unnoticed tension with “impossible for an unjustified person to be in Christ.”
