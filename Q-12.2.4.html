The question

Will Christ return invisibly and secretly to remove believers before the great tribulation — or is the “rapture” of 1 Thessalonians 4 simply the one visible Second Coming itself?

This is the central question of the dispensational pretribulational view — popularised by the Scofield Reference Bible, Dallas Theological Seminary, Hal Lindsey’s The Late Great Planet Earth, and Tim LaHaye’s Left Behind, and held, often unreflectively, across much of evangelicalism. If the pretrib reading stands, the parousia is staged: an invisible coming for the saints, then a later visible coming with them. If it falls, there is one return, after the tribulation, at the resurrection of the dead.

This is a doctrinal node, not a debate node. The entire treatment comes from one expert, William Lane Craig, across the three-part Defenders 3 “Rapture Interpretation” series (2021). There is no cross-expert convergence to report and no hostile interlocutor in the room — the opponent is a view (Darbyism), engaged exegetically on shared biblical-authority grounds.

The argument structure — a cumulative internal critique

Not a deductive syllogism but a cumulative internal critique: WLC stacks four converging streams, each already accepted by the dispensationalist as authoritative, so the texts interpret one another and leave no room for a separate prior event. The two lexical streams — apantesis in 1 Thess 4 and episynagōgē shared between Mark 13:27 and 2 Thess 2:1 — are the decisive levers.

How William Lane Craig builds it — the three-part series

WLC · Part 2 2021-03-29
The view defined — origins, Olivet, and the apantesis lever
Movements
  • Historical novelty. “Not the historic position” — traces to John Darby (1827), spread by Scofield, Dallas, Lindsey, LaHaye.
  • The “18 centuries” framing. The view requires that the church erred for eighteen centuries about a single, un-preceded return.
  • Olivet Discourse (Mark 13). The gathering is visible, occurs after the tribulation, and the elect are not spared it.
  • The apantesis lexical lever. The word denotes going out to welcome a returning dignitary back — so the saints escort Christ to earth, not away from it.
  • Paul builds on Jesus. No reason to read 1 Thess 4 as a distinct event from the Second Coming.
WLC · Part 3 2021-04-05
The keystone — one resurrection, same vocabulary
Movements
  • 1 Thess 4 = the final resurrection. The trumpet-descent is when the dead rise, “not a secret snatching of the elect out of the world.”
  • Both rise together (John 5:25-29). Righteous and unrighteous dead — resurrection of life and resurrection of judgment.
  • Same language in 1 Cor 15:51-55. “The dead will be raised imperishable.”
  • Death is destroyed (1 Cor 15:22-26). “No more death after this event” — so no further death-and-resurrection sequence can follow.
  • 2 Thess 2 = same parousia, sign-preceded. Comes after the man of lawlessness — so not signless or secret.
  • The episynagōgē clincher. Jesus’ gathering-verb (Mark 13:27) is Paul’s noun (2 Thess 2:1) — one and the same gathering.
WLC’s own signal

He says the 2 Thess 2 data “cements this understanding” — flagging Part 3 as the keystone of the three-part case. Method: convergence by vocabulary and event-identity.

WLC · Part 4 2021-04-13
The “day of the Lord” — and 2 Thess 2 refuted
Movements
  • Christological reading. “The day of the Lord” is the day of Christ’s return; the genitive gives it away — “the day of our Lord Jesus Christ” (1 Cor 1:8).
  • “Like a thief in the night” — Paul (1 Thess 5) echoes Jesus’ Olivet phrasing about the visible coming (Matt 24:42-44).
  • The left-behind reading rejected. “There just isn’t anything in this passage” to suggest the Thessalonians feared they had missed the rapture.
  • The empty-graves reductio. On that reading “the graves should be empty and Paul would be gone and not writing to them.”
  • Honesty as tactic. He concedes reconstructing Paul’s opponents is “conjecture,” then shows his thesis survives every reconstruction — Gnostic “resurrection past” or imminence (enistēmi).
Conclusion of the series

“We don’t know what his opponents taught, but we do know what Paul taught… Christ’s return is neither past nor imminent; it still lies sometime in the future.”

The position being refuted — dispensational pretribulationism

The whole node is an argument against a view rather than between experts. This is the case as WLC states it before dismantling it — the “objection” the cumulative argument exists to answer.

The view

A two-stage return: an invisible coming for the saints, then a visible coming with them

“There is going to be an invisible return of Christ to snatch away believers before the final visible return of Christ to establish his kingdom” — to rescue Christians from the great tribulation Jesus predicted. Proof-text: 1 Thess 4:15-17, “caught up… to meet the Lord in the air.”

Darby (1827) · Scofield · Dallas Theological Seminary · Lindsey · Left Behind

WLC’s verdict

Exegetically unsupported and historically novel

No hint in the Olivet Discourse; the gathering is visible and post-tribulational; 1 Thess 4 is the same event as Mark 13:27, not a distinct prior one; 2 Thess 2 uses identical vocabulary and is sign-preceded. And the view is recent — Darby 1827, not the historic church.

WLC three-part internal critique

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

A single-expert doctrinal node, so this records the rejoinders WLC raises and answers within his own case — and the standard dispensational rejoinders he does not engage, gap-flagged in red.

Objection 1

“Meeting the Lord in the air” proves believers are taken away to heaven

1 Thess 4:17 says the living are “caught up… to meet the Lord in the air.” Surely that is the saints being removed upward, out of the world?

The natural rapture rejoinder (WLC raises it himself)

Strongest response

The apantesis direction-of-travel argument

In Greek usage apantesis denotes a delegation going out to welcome a returning dignitary back to his city. So the saints rise to escort Christ back to earth, not to be evacuated from it. The word fixes the direction against the pretrib reading.

WLC Part 2 lexical lever

Objection 2

2 Thess 2 — the Thessalonians feared the rapture had occurred and they were left behind

Paul writes to calm a church alarmed that “the day of the Lord has come”; on the pretrib reading they feared the secret rapture had happened and they had missed it.

Proponents of the rapture doctrine

Strongest response

Nothing in the text — and the empty-graves reductio

“There just isn’t anything in this passage to suggest” a left-behind scenario. And on that reading “the graves should be empty and Paul would be gone and not writing to them” — yet he is alive and writing. The fear concerns the visible return, which has not come.

WLC Part 4 reductio ad absurdum

Objection 3

“The resurrection is past already” — a spiritualised eschatology

Is there NT precedent for claiming the resurrection / second coming had already happened? Yes — Hymenaeus and Philetus, “who have swerved from the truth by holding that the resurrection is past already.”

2 Tim 2:17-18; cf. the Corinthian resurrection denial

Strongest response

Cannot be literal — so it is a Gnostic spiritualising

“The graves would be empty if the resurrection were passed already — there wouldn’t be any corpses.” So it must be a spiritualising error. Against it Paul insists the bodily resurrection is future and the day of the Lord has not come.

WLC Part 4 physical-resurrection insistence

Objection 4

The secret rapture itself — a signless, invisible removal before the tribulation

The core dispensational claim: a preliminary, invisible coming snatches believers out before the tribulation, distinct from the later glorious return.

Darby / dispensationalism

Strongest response

One event — the visible, post-tribulational resurrection

1 Thess 4 is the end-time resurrection and the destruction of death (the last enemy); since death is finally destroyed there, it cannot be a mere preliminary stage. 2 Thess 2 confirms the same parousia is sign-preceded, so not signless.

WLC Part 3 single-resurrection + same-vocabulary

Objection 5

The two-stage parousia — one complex event with distinguishable phases

Sophisticated pretribulationists grant the shared vocabulary but hold the parousia is a single complex event with distinguishable phases; the words parousia / apokalypsis / epiphaneia do not collapse them into one moment.

Sophisticated dispensationalism

Gap — not engaged

“Same word ⇒ same event” is asserted, not defended

WLC surveys exactly these three words yet never argumentatively rules out the staged reading — he asserts event-identity. It is precisely the premise a sophisticated pretribulationist concedes while keeping a staged parousia. The corpus’s most conspicuous gap.

Aggregation gap

Objection 6

Revelation 20 / the “first resurrection” — two resurrections, not one

There are two resurrections separated by the millennium: the righteous at the return, the unrighteous after the thousand years (Rev 20:4-6, the “first resurrection”). WLC’s single-resurrection premise contradicts this directly.

Premillennialism / dispensationalism

Gap — not engaged

The obvious counter-text is never named

His whole case turns on righteous and unrighteous rising together (John 5:28-29) with death destroyed at one moment — yet he never engages Rev 20:4-6. Adjacent: premil mortality during an earthly millennium is ruled out by “no more death after this event,” undefended against the premil reading.

Aggregation gap

Objection 7

The 2 Thess 2 “restrainer”

“He who now restrains” (2 Thess 2:6-7) is read by pretribulationists as the Spirit or the church, removed at the rapture — a standard proof-text for a pre-tribulational removal.

Standard pretrib proof-text

Gap — not engaged

Quoted in Part 3, never addressed in Part 4

WLC quotes 2 Thess 2:3-8 but never engages the restrainer, despite Part 4 being the conclusion of the rapture case. Conspicuous, given how central the restrainer is to the dispensational reading of this very chapter.

Aggregation gap

Objection 8

Imminence — the rapture is signless and could occur at any moment

The central pretrib motive: the rapture must be signless and imminent, unlike the sign-preceded visible return. The two are distinguished precisely by imminence.

Dispensational imminence doctrine

Gap — not engaged head-on

Framed as a Thessalonian error, not the modern argument

His “neither past nor imminent; a lot has to happen first” cuts against it, but he treats imminence as a possible Thessalonian error (enistēmi) rather than meeting the modern signless-rapture argument on its own terms.

Aggregation gap

Objection 9

Genetic fallacy — recency does not entail falsehood

A dispensationalist can reply that novelty of systematic articulation (Darby 1827) does not entail novelty of the doctrine; recency is logically irrelevant to truth. The “18 centuries” point proves nothing on its own.

A reply available in principle to dispensationalism

Gap — not pre-empted

Origins point left logically exposed

WLC treats the Darby / “18 centuries” observation as evidentially weighty without isolating it from the exegetical case. In fairness, the exegetical premises carry the real argumentative load — but the historical point is never insulated from the truth question.
