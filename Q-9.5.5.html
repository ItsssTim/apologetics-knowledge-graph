How can a believer have assurance of salvation?

Not whether salvation is by grace, but how a believer knows the faith they profess is real — and how assurance is told apart from presumption. Three experts converge on a single test: the regenerate life changes.

The question

On what basis can a Christian be confident of being saved — and how is that assurance distinguished from mere presumption?

This sits at the join of soteriology and lived experience. It is not “is salvation by grace?” — that is §9.1 — but rather: given that salvation is by grace through faith, how does a believer know the faith they profess is the real thing?

The danger runs both ways. Too little assurance produces the anxious Christian forever re-checking their standing; too much produces the presumptuous professor who “prayed a prayer” decades ago and “lived like the devil ever since” yet rests easy. The experts converge on one diagnostic: the regenerate life changes, and that change is the evidence on which assurance rests. The classic text is 1 John 3:6–9, read through the Greek present tense — the one born of God does not practise sin, though he still stumbles and falls.

The cumulative case — evidence streams, not a syllogism

This is not a deductive proof of one’s own salvation — no such proof exists from the inside. It is an abductive, criterion-based case: the believer infers genuine regeneration from converging marks. The streams interlock — the text sets the rule, the fruit supplies the criterion, the Spirit supplies the engine.

Stream A · The text

  • 1 John 1:8 — no sinlessness this side of glory
  • 1 John 3:6–9 — no practice of sin
  • Habitual-present / continuous verb aspect
  • 1 John 1:9 — confession & cleansing when we fall
  • Sets the exegetical rule (Craig)

Stream B · The fruit

  • Sincere faith vs insincere faith
  • Works as fruit, not root (not currency)
  • Tree known by its fruit (Matt 7:16–20)
  • Thief on the cross — obedience follows, never earns
  • Supplies the criterion (Knechtle, Perry)

Stream C · The witness

  • “God’s nature abides in him”
  • Spirit-empowered change (Mentos-and-soda)
  • Works as overflow, not exertion
  • New birth “works itself out” in a changed life
  • Supplies the engine (Craig, Perry)

How each expert deploys it

William Lane Craig1 transcript
The habitual-present argument & the assurance corollary
Signature delivery
  • The apparent contradiction: John says both that claiming sinlessness is self-deception (1 John 1:8) and that “no one born of God commits sin” (3:9).
  • The grammatical key: the Greek present tense signals continuous aspect — “no regenerate christian can live a lifestyle of sin.” The claim is about ongoing practice, not isolated acts (the habitual present).
  • The engine: what is excluded is the person who “just goes on repeatedly committing sin,” because “god’s nature abides in him.”
  • The assurance corollary: a true new birth “ought to work itself out in some difference in his life.” The childhood altar-call responder who “lived like the devil ever since” has “no right” to assurance.
Perfectionism guard

Craig will not swing to the opposite error. The regenerate still “stumble and fall,” and when they do they confess and “claim god’s forgiveness and cleansing” (1 John 1:9). What is excluded is settled practice, not any sin. His tag: “keep short accounts with god.”

Cliffe Knechtle3 transcripts
Sincere vs insincere faith — works as fruit, not root
Signature deployment
  • Works are fruit, not currency: “the real issue is … is your faith genuine sincere or is it insincere — and sincere faith will be shown in the way I seek to obey Jesus.”
  • The thief on the cross — pure trust, no works; the “would he have got baptised had he survived?” thought-experiment inverts the works→faith order.
  • Matthew 7:21–23 turned against works-righteousness: the rejected came “with a list of what they did.” The error was a credential, not too few works. Internal critique.
  • The slap-test (James, not Luther): insincere faith “shows itself in no change of Lifestyle”; sincere faith in “a radical change of Lifestyle.”
  • Confessional self-implication: turns the blade on himself (“I am a sinner,” “I’m an arrogant son of a gun”) — ongoing confession strengthens a credible profession.
Preston Perry1 transcript
Spirit-empowered fruit — the Mentos-and-soda engine
Distinctive moves
  • The Mentos-soda analogy: when the Spirit comes in, “the soda just pours out on its own” — good works are “not by my own doing” but “the Holy Spirit empowering me.”
  • Fruit as evidence, not cause: “If the person is not producing good works, he’s not a Christian” — yet “Jesus came and did the work for us.” Absence falsifies; presence never earns.
  • Manual pouring vs chemical change: the works-righteous “manually pour” works out; the regenerate’s works pour out by the Spirit’s pressure from within.
  • Backs it with Philippians 3 (skubalon) and Isaiah 64 (filthy rags) to deny meritorious works.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

Doesn’t the new birth then demand sinless perfection?

1 John 3:9 says “no one born of God commits sin.” Read flatly, that makes assurance impossible — every Christian still sins, so by this test no one qualifies.

The plain-reading worry; perfectionist holiness traditions

Strongest response

The present tense excludes settled practice, not every fall

1 John 1:8 rules out perfectionism outright. The continuous aspect of the verbs in 3:6–9 excludes a lifestyle of sin, not isolated acts. The regenerate “stumble and fall,” then confess and are “forgiven and cleansed” (1 John 1:9).

Craig habitual-present harmonisation

Objection 2

“Then you can just sin and ask forgiveness”

If salvation is by grace and works are only fruit, the believer can do whatever they want and simply ask forgiveness later — the easy-grace charge.

An outspoken atheist’s version, reported by Stuart Knechtle

Strongest response

Genuine faith produces obedience; insincere faith never saved

“Sincere faith will be shown in the way I seek to obey Jesus.” A faith that seeks no obedience at all is insincere faith — and insincere faith did not save in the first place. The objection assumes a real faith that produces nothing, which the criterion denies.

Knechtle sincere-vs-insincere faith

Objection 3

Matthew 7:21–23 — people who did mighty works are still rejected

Jesus turns away those who prophesied, cast out demons and did many works in his name. If that terrifying group is rejected, how can anyone have assurance?

The works-anxious believer; raised as a terror-text

Strongest response

The verse cuts against works-righteousness

The rejected came “with a list of what they did” — presenting works as their credential instead of trusting Christ. “I never knew you” signals an absent relationship, not insufficient works. The text most feared by the works-anxious is wielded against works-righteousness itself.

Knechtle internal critique

Objection 4

“Faith without works is dead” (James 2)

James says faith without works is dead and that we are justified by works — so works belong inside the gospel, not merely downstream of it. (The Luther-vs-James worry.)

LDS elder (Perry’s interlocutor); the classic James objection

Strongest response

James distinguishes living faith from dead faith

Knechtle: James separates sincere faith (radical change of lifestyle) from insincere faith (no change) — not salvation-by-works. Perry: works are evidence-of-regeneration (“tree by its fruit”), not cause-of-acceptance; Philippians 3 skubalon and Isaiah 64 filthy rags deny meritorious works.

Knechtle James-not-Luther readingPerry fruit-not-cause

Objection 5

Works-conditioned grace — “grace after all we can do”

2 Nephi 25:23 has grace operate “after all we can do,” and Mormon 9/10:32 conditions sufficient grace on first denying “all ungodliness” — so works precede grace.

LDS canon

Strongest response

Any addition to the finished work is anathema

Ephesians 2:8–9 (“not of works, lest any man boast”) plus Galatians 1:8–9 (a curse on any addition to Christ’s finished work). Spirit-empowered works — the Mentos-soda overflow — are the result of grace, never its precondition.

Perry internal critique of LDS canon

Objection 6

Repentance and faith are themselves “works”

Exercising repentance and faith is something the believer does — so even on the grace view, a human work is a precondition of salvation. The parity move.

LDS missionary

Strongest response

Faith apprehends the gift; it does not earn it

Repentance and faith receive the gift rather than purchasing it. The soda-bottle (manual pouring) versus Mentos (chemical change) contrast marks the difference between human exertion and Spirit-driven reception.

Perry apprehending-vs-earning

Persistent gaps in the corpus

Gap 1

Assurance vs the real possibility of apostasy (§9.4)

If a true believer can fall away (the live possibility at Q-9.4.1), then a present absence of fruit is ambiguous: it might mean the profession was always spurious, or that a genuine believer has lapsed and may yet be restored. How should the anxious believer read a bad season?

Arminian and Free-Grace readers press exactly here

Gap — unreconciled (headline)

The corpus gives a criterion but not a way to read a lapse

Craig’s “no right to assurance” corollary never distinguishes the never-regenerate professor from the regenerate-but-fallen believer. The changed-life test answers the presumptuous professor but leaves the genuinely struggling believer without guidance — precisely where assurance bites hardest pastorally.

Aggregation gap

Gap 2

The habitual-present reading is contested, not plain

The continuous-aspect (“does not practise sin”) solution to 1 John 3:6–9 is one reading among several — some scholars read the passage perfectionally, others eschatologically. The entire assurance argument rests on it.

Greek-aspect and Johannine scholarship

Gap — presented as obvious

No grammarian or commentary is named

Craig presents the habitual present as the plain meaning without flagging the dispute. Acceptable for a lay class, but the aggregator should record that this is one reading, not the only one — and that the load-bearing exegesis is undefended against the alternatives.

Aggregation gap

Gap 3

The internal witness of the Spirit as a direct ground

Romans 8:16 — “the Spirit himself bears witness with our spirit that we are children of God” — is treated by much of the tradition as an immediate, self-authenticating ground of assurance, distinct from the evidential fruit-inference.

Romans 8:16; the witness-of-the-Spirit tradition

Gap — undertreated

The corpus runs almost entirely on the evidential route

Perry’s Mentos-soda gestures at the Spirit, but as the engine of changed behaviour (an indirect ground), not as the direct inner testimony. The corpus is heavily weighted toward fruit-inference and light on the self-authenticating witness.

Aggregation gap

Gap 4

James 2 never gets its full Protestant exegesis

Both Knechtle and Perry gesture at “faith without works is dead,” but neither develops the living-faith vs dead-faith distinction in detail, and Knechtle’s flat disagreement with Luther is asserted rather than defended.

James 2:14–26; the Paul-James harmonisation

Gap — gestured, not developed

The reconciliation is named, not argued

A full treatment would distinguish James’s “justify” (vindicate / show to be righteous) from Paul’s forensic “justify,” and exegete the Abraham example both authors share. The popular corpus asserts the harmony without doing the work.
