The question

What makes a hominin a human being rather than merely a member of genus Homo — and how far back can we date the first beings who clear that bar?

This is the empirical hinge of the historical-Adam project. Q-13.4.1 argues that Adam was real; this node supplies the identification criteria that let palaeoanthropology recognise a human when it finds one, and the dating bounds within which Adam must fall.

It sets the floor and the ceiling for human origins. Read generously, the criteria reach back to Homo erectus (~1 million years); at the latest they cannot be denied past the European cave-art explosion (~30,000–50,000 years). Craig places Adam near the early edge — Homo heidelbergensis, ~750,000 years ago — so that Neanderthals, Denisovans and Homo antecessor fall inside the human family rather than outside it.

The case — two arms, one conclusion

A single expert across two registers. A criteriological arm (the Defenders lecture) establishes what counts as human; a cumulative-case arm (the Capturing Christianity dialogue) uses it to date when humans appear and which species Adam belongs to. The third column brackets the dating bounds that fall out.

Arm 1 · The criteria

  • Genus Homo ≠ human
  • Necessary: anatomical similarity to us
  • Marker 1 — abstract thinking
  • Marker 2 — planning depth
  • Marker 3 — behavioural / economic innovation
  • Marker 4 — symbolic behaviour
  • Standards “universally recognized” (McBrearty & Brooks)

Arm 2 · Dating & locus

  • Neanderthal cognition comparable to ours
  • Manufactured cord → near-linguistic ability
  • Hyoid bone indistinguishable from ours
  • … and from Heidelberg man’s
  • H. heidelbergensis = last common ancestor
  • Of sapiens, Neanderthals, Denisovans, antecessor
  • So Adam ~750,000 years ago or earlier

Bounds · The window

  • Earliest: Homo erectus, ~1 Mya
  • Latest: Chauvet / Lascaux cave art
  • ~30,000–50,000 years ago
  • Craig’s locus: heidelbergensis
  • ~750,000 years ago or earlier
  • Pushed early to hold Neanderthals
  • inside the human family

How the case is built

William Lane Craig2 transcripts · sole voice
Arm 1 — the criteria (Defenders lecture, 2020)
Structural moves
  • Taxonomy is not humanity. Early Homo (habilis, erectus, rudolfensis) had brains “hardly larger than those of the Australopithecines” — against sapiens at more than twice the volume. Being classed Homo is not sufficient.
  • A necessary condition: anatomical similarity. Allows a range (modern vs archaic sapiens) but excludes the Australopithecines (~460 cm³ brains). This stops the argument proving too much.
  • The four jointly-sufficient markers (McBrearty & Brooks): abstract thinking, planning depth, behavioural / economic / technological innovation, and symbolic behaviour.
  • Person ≠ human person. A rational alien or chimpanzee would be a person — but not a human person. The anatomical clause does the distinguishing work.
  • Reductio backstop. Denying humanity to qualifying past beings would license denying it to living people too — “morally unconscionable.”
Borrowed yardstick

The four markers are not Craig’s; they are mainstream secular palaeoanthropology, deployed as a quasi-hostile-witness standard. McBrearty & Brooks’ own claim that the criteria “are universally recognized and are frequently repeated in the literature” pre-empts the charge of theological gerrymandering.

William Lane Craigsame voice · dialogue register
Arm 2 — dating & locus (Capturing Christianity dialogue)
Application moves
  • Neanderthal cognition clears the bar. The evidence is “so persuasive and powerful” that their capacity is “comparable to modern humans.”
  • The manufactured cord. Inner-bark fibres twisted clockwise into strings, three strings twisted counter-clockwise into a cord — an operation involving “mathematical computational knowledge” that “approaches linguistic ability.”
  • Heidelberg man as Adam. H. heidelbergensis is “widely regarded as the last common ancestor of homo sapiens and neanderthals” — and, on recent Spanish fossil work, of Denisovans and antecessor — placing Adam “around 750,000 years ago or earlier.”
  • The moral-parity engine. Excluding Neanderthals “treats them as being less than human”; he pushes Adam back “to incorporate them into the human family in the same way that you would want to incorporate indians and chinese.”

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

Behaviour alone over-counts — a rational alien or chimpanzee would meet the markers

If the four behavioural markers are sufficient for humanity, then any self-conscious, rational being — an extra-terrestrial, even a chimpanzee — would have to be counted human.

Anticipated by Craig (Defenders lecture)

Strongest response

The anatomical clause is necessary — person vs human person

Anatomical similarity is a necessary condition working jointly with the markers. Such a being would be a person but not a human person. The two conditions together exclude the over-count.

Craig jointly-sufficient-conditions analysis

Objection 2

Neanderthals had a higher, smaller larynx — perhaps they could not speak

Their larynx sat higher in the throat than ours, so some researchers claim they lacked our phonetic range and therefore language — failing the symbolic-behaviour marker.

Some palaeoanthropology researchers (cited in dialogue)

Strongest response

The same researchers grant language — and the hyoid matches

The very researchers who note the laryngeal difference say Neanderthals probably did have language. The hyoid bone, particularly important for speech, is indistinguishable from modern humans’ — and from Heidelberg man’s.

Craig turns cited researchers into witnesses

Objection 3

Some scientists treat Neanderthals as not fully behaviourally modern

The behavioural-modernity record for Neanderthals is read by some as patchy; one cannot simply assume they cleared the four-marker bar.

Joshua Swamidass (in dialogue)

Strongest response

Adjudicate case-by-case — but the capacity evidence is decisive

Craig grants that the evidence must be weighed case-by-case, but holds the cognitive-capacity evidence is sufficient: it is “so persuasive and powerful” that comparable cognition is the best explanation.

Craig inference to best explanation

Objection 4

Genesis presents adam in a Neolithic, agricultural setting

The text seems to locate Adam among farming and the rise of agriculture — a Neolithic frame — not ~750,000 years ago. How important is it to keep Adam in that setting versus at the very beginning of beings with a human mind?

Joshua Swamidass (the chief difficulty he presses)

Gap — not engaged here

Left hanging in this exchange

This is the strongest live objection to the ancient-Adam locus, and Craig does not answer it in this dialogue. The Neolithic-setting problem is surfaced by the interlocutor and deferred rather than met.

Aggregation gap

Objection 5

“Behavioural modernity” is itself a contested, shifting category

A substantial school argues modernity emerged gradually and mosaically, not as a unified package — which blurs a clean “four markers, jointly sufficient” threshold and the sharp dating bounds that depend on it.

Mainstream palaeoanthropology (gradualist view)

Gap — largely unaddressed

Touched only obliquely

Craig does not engage the gradualist critique on this node. The companion lecture’s emphasis on the deep antiquity of symbolic behaviour speaks to it indirectly, but the “package” assumption is never defended head-on.

Aggregation gap

Objection 6

A single expert, never independently stress-tested

Every treatment is Craig’s. Whether the four markers actually track the imago Dei — or merely cultural complexity — is assumed, not argued by a second apologetics voice.

Gap — corpus thinness

No second voice on this node

The theological adequacy of the criteria, and the heavy weight placed on a single recent cord study, go unchecked by any independent expert in the corpus. Flagged honestly rather than smoothed over.
