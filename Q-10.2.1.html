The person and work of the Holy Spirit

A doctrinal gap-fill for the near-empty §10 branch — William Lane Craig’s 2016 Defenders unit on the Spirit’s eight ministries, the Old- versus New-Covenant relocation of the temple, indwelling versus filling, and why so few Christians are filled. Constructive teaching, not adversarial debate.

The question

Who is the Holy Spirit, and what does he do — from the creation of the universe to the daily empowerment of the believer?

This is a doctrinal gap-fill rather than an adversarial battleground. §10 Pneumatology was a near-empty branch; the corpus here is overwhelmingly constructive teaching. The governing source is William Lane Craig’s 2016 Defenders Holy-Spirit unit — a systematic, note-taking-friendly Sunday-school exposition for a believing lay audience, with a near-identical 2023 re-teaching of its catalogue.

The unit answers four connected questions: the catalogue of the Spirit’s works (Part 2), the covenant contrast in his mode of presence (Part 4), the difference between being indwelt and being filled (Part 5), and why so few are filled, and how to be filled (Part 6). The “argument” is a cumulative catalogue and a redemptive-historical contrast supported almost entirely by proof-texts; the deepest tensions are internal ones the teacher leaves open.

The convergent core — four connected theses, not a syllogism

There is no multi-expert convergence to tabulate; the spine is WLC’s single systematic exposition delivered across four lessons. It reads best as four theses, the later three building on the catalogue. The method is proof-text catalogue and parable, not deduction.

T1 · The eight ministries

  • Creation of the universe (Gen 1:2–3)
  • Divine revelation (1 Cor 2:9–13)
  • Inspiration of Scripture (2 Pet 1:20–21)
  • Conception of Christ (Luke 1:35)
  • Regeneration / new birth (John 3:5–7)
  • Indwelling & baptism (Rom 8:9; 1 Cor 12:13)
  • Assurance of salvation (Rom 8:14–16)
  • Enablement for living (Gal 5:16–25)

T2 · The covenant contrast

  • Timeline divides at the cross
  • OT locus: the temple / Holy of Holies
  • OT: temporary task-anointings (prophecy)
  • Spirit withdraws under judgment (Ezek 9, 11)
  • NT locus: the believer’s own body
  • “We are the temple of the Holy Spirit”
  • Transition mechanism: baptism in the Spirit

T3 · Indwelling ≠ filling

  • All Christians indwelt; not all filled
  • Natural man — unregenerate, no Spirit
  • Spiritual man — regenerate, Spirit-empowered
  • Carnal man — saved but in the flesh
  • Filled = controlled & empowered
  • Corinth: most gifted, yet most carnal
  • Evidence = fruit, not charismatic gifts

T4 · Why few are filled, and how

  • Diagnosis 1: lack of total commitment (sower)
  • Diagnosis 2: reliance on self-effort (vine)
  • “Apart from me you can do nothing”
  • Repent — confess known sin (1 John 1:9)
  • Believe / yield — a living sacrifice (Rom 12)
  • Walk daily — grieve not, quench not
  • “A living sacrifice tends to crawl off the altar”

How each voice treats it

William Lane Craig 5 transcripts · dominant
The systematic anchor — Defenders proof-text catalogue
Part 2 · the eight ministries
  • Calls the Spirit’s works “a rather startlingly long list”; numbers eight for note-taking lay students.
  • Soteriological core (Ministries 5–8) gets the fullest glosses; cosmological / bibliological items (1–3) stated briefly.
  • Ministry 6 — the criterion of genuine faith: “anyone who does not have the indwelling Holy Spirit is not a Christian, he is unregenerate.”
  • Ministry 7 — assurance: the Spirit’s inner witness is “the source of your assurance” (kept devotional; the apologetic payoff is left on the table).
Part 4 · the covenant contrast
  • Literally draws a timeline divided at the cross; contrasts the Spirit’s locus — Holy of Holies versus the believer’s body.
  • Traces the Spirit’s withdrawal under judgment (Ezek 9:3 → 11:23) before the Babylonian conquest.
  • Maps the geography of Acts 1:8 (Jerusalem → Judea → Samaria → ends of the earth) onto Acts 2 / 8 / 10–11 / 19.
Part 5 · indwelling versus filling
  • Reduces 1 Cor 2:14–3:3 to a clean three-cell grid: natural / spiritual / carnal man.
  • “Filled” = controlled and empowered — modelled on “filled with rage / filled with jealousy.”
  • Corinth counter-example: the most charismatically gifted NT church was simultaneously the most carnal — so gifts are no index of spirituality.
Part 6 · why few are filled, and how
  • Hangs the whole diagnosis on two parables: the sower (commitment) and the vine and branches (self-effort).
  • Prescription: repent (1 John 1:9), believe / yield (Rom 12, “a commitment of the will”), walk daily — distinguishing grieving the Spirit (through sin) from quenching him (suppressing his leading).
  • The signature move: reads aloud a real ex-minister’s deconversion testimony, framing it diagnostically as “a perfect description of the carnal Christian Life lived in the power of the flesh.”
Candid concession · the unresolved seam

On the apostate testimony he reads aloud: “to all appearances [it] is of a person who has committed apostasy” — yet he explicitly declines to say whether that person was ever genuinely saved: “I don’t think we know when a person has crossed that line ... that’s a question we’ll take up later.” Assurance (Ministry 7) is never reconciled with perseverance.

Cliffe & Stuart Knechtle 1 transcript · peripheral
Pastoral fragment — a boundary case, mostly Q-10.1.1
What it actually treats
  • A single Clemson Q&A, double-tagged Q-10.1.1 / Q-10.2.1: primarily the unforgivable sin and habitual sin, touching the Spirit only obliquely.
  • Stuart: the unforgivable sin is “not a single act but a lifestyle” — unrepentant pattern, not a single fit of rage, is the danger.
  • Cliffe: forgiveness for sincere confession is genuine (1 John 1:9); persistent post-confession guilt signals perfectionism-as-idol, anchored in Romans 7.
  • Stuart’s positive method: fight habitual sin “by finding God more attractive than the sin itself.”
Boundary note

This fragment overlaps §9 soteriology and the unforgivable-sin node far more than the doctrine of the Spirit. The genuinely cross-expert pneumatology node is Q-10.1.1 (the Spirit’s personhood & deity), which pulls three experts; this node is deliberately the WLC systematic gap-fill.

The honest density & coverage note

This node is not a cross-expert convergence and should not be read as one. No consensus is manufactured here because there is none in the corpus.

Caveat 1 · coverage

Expert count is 2, WLC-dominant — effectively 1

William Lane Craig supplies five of the six treatments, all from one 2016 Defenders unit (Parts 2, 4, 5, 6) plus a 2023 re-teaching. The Knechtle exchange is a single peripheral fragment that belongs more to Q-10.1.1 and §9.

Caveat 2 · density

Medium, teaching-grade — not debate-grade

Doctrinally complete as a catalogue, a contrast, and a distinction, with clean single-speaker transcripts. But it is exposition to a sympathetic class, not argued defence — so the “objections” below are mostly internal tensions and unflagged exegetical disputes, not challenges WLC defeats.

Caveat 3 WLC reads OT/NT “spirit” texts through a settled Trinitarian lens and rarely steelmans the Unitarian / JW counter-reading — his one candid concession (adjacent Part 3) is that the OT data yields a distinct person only in retrospect, through the lens of the later church. See Q-10.1.1.

Objections & tensions — cross-source master list

Because this is exposition, most entries are internal tensions or unflagged disputes rather than objections WLC defeats. That status is preserved honestly below.

Objection 1

Baptism in the Spirit is a second blessing subsequent to conversion

The Pentecostal / charismatic view: Spirit-baptism is a distinct, later experience from regeneration, often evidenced by tongues.

Pentecostal / charismatic theology

Response

Implicitly rejected, never named

WLC’s framing makes indwelling / baptism universal and at-conversion (“anyone who does not have the indwelling Holy Spirit is not a Christian”), and the Corinth counter-example severs gifts from spirituality. But he never names or rebuts the position directly.

WLC Parts 2 & 5 — framing only

Objection 2

Surely the charismatically gifted are the most spiritual?

If a believer manifests tongues, prophecy and miracles abundantly, that must be the mark of a Spirit-filled life.

Implicit charismatic inference

Strongest response

The Corinth counter-example

The NT church with the most abundant gifts was simultaneously the most carnal — drunkenness at communion, incest, strife. So gifts are no index of spirituality; the true evidence is the fruit of the Spirit (Galatians 5).

WLC Part 5 — load-bearing premise

Objection 3

Is Ezekiel 11:23’s glory “standing” an anthropomorphism?

A questioner asks whether the glory of the Lord “standing” on the Mount of Olives implies a human figure.

In-class questioner (Part 4)

Response

“Stand” has a wide range

WLC: “stood” need not mean standing on two legs; he takes the glory to be a visible luminescence “the way the cloud or the pillar of fire” would be visible, not a human form — which left the temple, went to the mount, then ascended. The unit’s one genuine Q&A objection.

WLC Part 4 Q&A

Objection 4

Do severed branches (John 15:6) mean a believer can lose salvation?

“Taylor” presses: are the unfruitful branches nominal or genuinely saved Christians — and being “cast off, withered, burned” raises apostasy.

In-class questioner “Taylor” (Part 6)

Answered only partially

Genuinely saved — but apostasy deferred

WLC means genuinely saved Christians (1 Cor 3:15, “he himself will be saved” though his works burn), but on apostasy he explicitly declines to commit: “I don’t think we know when a person has crossed that line ... that’s a question we’ll take up later.”

WLC Part 6 — deferred by designfeeds §9.4

Objection 5

The “carnal Christian” as a stable third category is contested

Reformed / Lordship interpreters deny a permanent “carnal Christian” class — no fruit may indicate no faith — and read 1 Corinthians 3 as immaturity, not a settled type.

Reformed / Lordship-salvation interpreters

Gap — largely unaddressed

Presented as straightforward Pauline teaching

WLC offers the natural / spiritual / carnal taxonomy without naming the dispute. The Lordship-vs-free-grace controversy is latent throughout and surfaces only obliquely in the Part-6 apostasy Q&A.

Aggregation gap

Objection 6

The OT also speaks of the Spirit remaining on figures

If the Spirit “remained” on David, or “remained on them” (Numbers 11), is the OT-temporary / NT-permanent contrast really clean? Were OT saints indwelt soteriologically?

Standard counter-data to T2

Gap — asserted, not defended

Hedged with “it seems that”

WLC concedes obliquely (church-establishment Q&A) that OT believers “didn’t have the Holy Spirit as a permanent possession,” but does not engage the remaining-Spirit texts or the soteriological question. Acceptable in a class; still asserted, not argued.

Aggregation gap

Objection 7

Is the Spirit a divine person, or God’s impersonal active power?

The Unitarian / Jehovah’s-Witness reading takes “the spirit of God” as God’s impersonal force, not a distinct person — the whole catalogue’s personhood is the contested premise.

Unitarian / Jehovah’s Witness

Gap — not steelmanned here

Read through a settled Trinitarian lens

WLC’s only candid moment (adjacent Part 3) is that the OT data yields a distinct person only in retrospect, through the lens of the later church. The substantive three-expert rebuttal lives at Q-10.1.1, not here.

Handled at Q-10.1.1

Objection 8

Could the Spirit “withdraw” from a believer the way he left the temple?

WLC narrates the Spirit’s withdrawal from the physical temple under judgment (Ezekiel). If the believer is now the temple, could the same befall a believer-temple?

Internal tension (Part 4)

Gap — unprobed

Never connected to permanence / perseverance

The contrast between the Spirit’s withdrawal from the physical temple and the permanence claimed for New-Covenant indwelling is not probed — it feeds straight into the perseverance question WLC defers.

Aggregation gap

Persistent gaps in the corpus

The genuine soft spots — questions WLC’s own exposition raises but does not resolve. Stated honestly, not papered over.

Gap 1

Assurance versus perseverance, left in unresolved tension.

The indwelling Spirit is both the permanent mark of genuine faith (Ministry 6) and the subjective source of assurance (Ministry 7) — yet whether that indwelling can be forfeited is repeatedly and explicitly deferred. Taylor presses exactly this; WLC declines. The central unresolved seam of the whole unit.

Gap 2

No criterion to tell a “carnal Christian” from an apostate.

For a diagnostic lesson, WLC offers no test by which a hearer could know whether they are merely carnal (saved, will return) or genuinely lost — by his own admission, “we don’t know when a person has crossed that line.” The practical question (am I carnal, or lost?) is left open.

Gap 3

Old-Covenant indwelling is asserted, not defended.

The OT-temporary / NT-permanent contrast does not engage the counter-evidence that the OT speaks of the Spirit remaining on certain figures, nor whether OT saints were indwelt soteriologically.

Gap 4

The major doctrinal disputes go unnamed.

The Pentecostal-vs-Reformed baptism dispute and the Lordship-vs-free-grace controversy both sit directly under WLC’s framing, yet neither is named or rebutted — his at-conversion, free-grace-leaning readings are simply assumed.

Gap 5

The apologetic application of the inner witness is never made.

Ministry 7 is the doctrinal seed of WLC’s double-warrant epistemology, but in this unit it stays purely devotional; the knowing-vs-showing apologetic payoff (Q-10.2.3) is left on the table. The Unitarian / JW personhood counter-reading is likewise not steelmanned here (handled at Q-10.1.1).
