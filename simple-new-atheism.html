Loud, popular, and worth weighing

What is the New Atheism, and does it hold up?

For a few years the bestseller lists belonged to Dawkins, Hitchens, Harris and Dennett, and the message was blunt: faith is blind, religion poisons everything, science has explained God away. It landed hard. The fair question is not whether it was confident, but whether the confidence was earned. Take the moves one at a time and the strongest answers are still standing.

First / The phenomenon
Step 1 of 4
A moment, not a discovery

Four books in four years, one big swing.

The New Atheism is a movement with a date stamp. Between 2004 and 2007 a run of bestsellers opened a fresh public argument about God.

The four
Richard Dawkins, Daniel Dennett, Sam Harris and the late Christopher Hitchens. Different fields, different styles, but a shared aim: to make disbelief not just respectable but obvious.
The tone
What set them apart was attitude. Belief in God was not treated as a mistake to be discussed but as a delusion to be exposed, ideally with a sharp line and a knowing laugh.
Three claims
Strip away the wit and three charges carry the weight. Belief is irrational. Religion is dangerous. Science has made God unnecessary. The rest of this card takes each one in turn.
The theory you will hear
These are the smartest objections to God ever assembled. They are forcefully put, which is not the same thing. The confidence with which the claims are made far outstrips the arguments actually presented. A bestseller proves an audience, not a case.
✓
Knowing what the New Atheism is helps you see what it is not: a body of new evidence. It is a style of attack on old questions.
Second / The charges
Step 2 of 4
Faith is blind, religion poisons

Two charges that only work if you redefine the words.

The first two claims feel devastating until you ask what they assume. Both lean on a definition the believer never accepted.

Blind faith
Faith is recast as belief without evidence, even against the evidence. From there it is easy to call it irrational, and even to call teaching it to children a kind of abuse. But that is not what faith has historically meant. Trust grounded in reasons is not the same as belief in spite of them.
Poisons everything
Religion is named the root of human harm, so sidelining it becomes the cure. The analysis is strikingly simple for so large a claim. It tallies faith's failures while quietly crediting none of its hospitals, abolitionists or charities, and waves away the body count of the avowedly atheist regimes of the last century.
All the same
A flat definition of faith lets every religion be treated as equally guilty and equally mistaken, which spares the critic the work of engaging any one of them on its merits.
The theory you will hear
Faith just means believing things without good reason. If that were the definition, the charge would stick. It is not. Define faith as the New Atheist needs it to be and the argument is won before it starts. Define it as reasoned trust, the way believers actually use the word, and the charge has nothing to land on.
✓
Both charges are powered by a loaded definition. Hand it back and the strong version of each claim loses most of its force.
Third / The science
Step 3 of 4
The leap that is never argued

Science explains. That is not the same as explains away.

The flagship claim is that science, above all evolution, has made God redundant. The gap in it is small to state and impossible to close.

The slide
There is no straight road from science explains how something works to science has explained God away. That move is not a scientific finding. It is a philosophical step, and a philosophical step has to be argued, not smuggled in as if the lab had settled it.
Wrong tool
Describing the mechanism of a thing does not tell you why there is anything to describe, or why the universe is the kind of place that yields to reason at all. Those are not questions a microscope was built to answer.
Cuts both ways
Modern observational science arguably supplies some of the strongest reasons for belief in God: a universe with a beginning, finely balanced for life, intelligible from top to bottom. The same evidence the New Atheist claims can be read the other way. (Romans 1:20)
The theory you will hear
Evolution removed the last good reason to believe in God. Evolution explains the development of life. It says nothing about why there is a universe with laws that make life possible. Treating a how question as if it had closed a why question is the unstated leap, and it is doing all the work.
✓
Science explaining a process leaves the deepest questions open. The jump to God is unnecessary is philosophy wearing a lab coat. (Colossians 2:8)
Fourth / The verdict
Step 4 of 4
Why none of it is actually new

The packaging was new. The arguments were not.

There is a last charge, a flat contempt for revelation, and then a question worth answering honestly. If the case is weak, why did it land so hard?

Old doubts
Miracles are dismissed as unscientific, the existence of Jesus is floated as debatable, the Gospels are waved off as legends arbitrarily picked and corrupted. Every one of these doubts is centuries old. Sceptics raised them long before, and careful answers were given long before.
Why it spread
What was genuinely new was the moment. A particular political and cultural climate, plus the effective use of ridicule, the internet and social media, carried the message far among the cynical and the disaffected. Reach is not the same as rigour.
So respond
That is exactly why the weak case still matters. The intellectual argument leaves a lot to be desired, but its cultural impact is real, and Christians who care about the disaffected need to be ready with a calm, reasoned reply. (1 Peter 3:15)
The theory you will hear
If millions found it convincing, it must be onto something. Popularity tracks delivery, not truth. None of the core tenets is unique to the New Atheism, and each had been answered before it was repackaged. What made it spread was timing and tone, which is a reason to engage it, not to be cowed by it.
✓
Take the New Atheism seriously as a cultural force and lightly as an argument. The strongest reasons for belief in God are untouched by it.

So does the New Atheism hold up? As a cultural wave it was powerful and it deserves a serious reply. As an argument it is mostly old objections in confident new clothes, resting on definitions it sets for itself and a leap from science it never makes good. Weigh the moves one by one and the strongest reasons for belief in God remain standing. The right response is not to flinch, and not to mock back. It is to answer, plainly and well.
