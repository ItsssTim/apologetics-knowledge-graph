The question

When a person dies, what becomes of them before the resurrection — and does Scripture teach a conscious post-mortem existence at all?

This is the doctrine of the intermediate state: the condition of the human person in the gap between bodily death and the resurrection at Christ’s return. The historic Christian answer is that the soul outlives the body — the saved consciously and blissfully with Christ, the unsaved consciously tormented in hades — each awaiting a resurrection. The contested alternatives are soul sleep (the dead are unconscious until raised) and Christian physicalism (there is no immaterial soul to survive, so any “intermediate state” is a divine re-creation rather than a continuing consciousness).

This is a single-voice node. The entire treatment is Craig’s, drawn from three consecutive lessons of his Defenders 3 series on the Last Things (Parts 17, 19 and 20, 2021). There is no cross-expert convergence to report — there is one sustained, internally-structured case. The value of this page is depth and honest gap-flagging, not triangulation across voices.

The convergent case — three biblical-theological streams, not a syllogism

Craig’s case is not deductive. It is a cumulative biblical-theological survey under the controlling principle of progressive revelation: the doctrine is unfolded gradually across the canon — from a “gloomy” and undifferentiated Old Testament sheol, through late glimmers of resurrection hope, to a fully differentiated New Testament picture of conscious bliss and conscious torment. The three lessons supply three streams that converge on one doctrine.

Stream A · The OT trajectory

  • Progressive revelation as the key
  • Sheol — undifferentiated underworld
  • “Gloomy”: Isa 38, Job 7, Isa 14
  • Glimmers of hope (Ps 73)
  • Explicit resurrection: Isa 26:19; Dan 12:2

Stream B · The saved soul

  • Soul “outlives the body”
  • Conscious, blissful, with Christ
  • Phil 1:21–24 — death is gain
  • 2 Cor 5 — “further clothed,” not naked
  • Luke 16 + Luke 23:43 — paradise

Stream C · The unrighteous soul

  • Conscious torment in hades
  • LXX: sheol = hades
  • Re-read Ps 6:5; Isa 38:18
  • Separated from Christ, awaiting resurrection
  • Hades ≠ hell
Principle

Progressive revelation makes the “mixed” canonical picture coherent.

The controlling key. God “has not given to humankind all of his truth ... at once but has revealed it gradually over time.” Craig illustrates with two non-eschatological cases — the Trinity (not evident in the OT) and the inclusion of the Gentiles (the “mystery hidden for ages”) — before applying it to immortality. The OT’s thin afterlife is expected developmental immaturity, not an embarrassment.

Mirror

The NT differentiates the interim into two conscious states, each awaiting a resurrection.

How the streams combine. The undifferentiated OT sheol resolves, under fuller revelation, into a deliberate symmetry: the saved in “conscious blissful communion with Christ” (Stream B) and the unsaved in “conscious torment ... separation from Christ” in hades (Stream C). The LXX sheol = hades equivalence licenses re-reading the “gloomy” OT texts as describing the unrighteous dead most accurately.

Conclusion

At death the soul outlives the body in a conscious disembodied state — bliss or torment — until Christ returns and the body is raised.

The de facto anti-soul-sleep verdict. The soul is “not extinguished” and does not “go into a state of unconsciousness.” The disembodied interim is real but sub-optimal — better than mortal embodiment, inferior to the resurrection body — hence Paul’s “catch-22.” Hades is the intermediate state; hell (gehenna) is the final one.

How the single voice deploys it

William Lane Craig3 transcripts
The sole voice — Defenders 3, Last Things Parts 17 / 19 / 20
Movement 1 — the OT development (Part 17)
  • Opens not with the afterlife but with the hermeneutical principle: progressive revelation, worked through the Trinity and the Gentile “mystery” before immortality.
  • Lays out the sheol data — Isaiah 38 (Hezekiah), Job 7, Isaiah 14 — and calls the picture “gloomy.”
  • Lexical sub-argument: sheol is not merely “the grave.” The dead are conscious shades who greet new arrivals (Isaiah 14 taunt over the king of Babylon).
  • Concedes “glimmers of hope” (Psalm 73), then the two explicit late resurrection texts — Isaiah 26:19; Daniel 12:2 — and the inter-testamental flourishing (Pharisee / Sadducee divide).
Movement 2 — the saved soul (Part 19)
  • Philippians 1:21–24 — death is gain: “to depart and be with Christ ... is far better.”
  • 2 Corinthians 5:1–10, the centrepiece — “earthly tent” vs “building from God”; the “nakedness” of disembodiment; the decisive verb ependysasthai, glossed with the “sweater over a shirt” image (further clothed, not unclothed).
  • The “three states + catch-22” schema — mortal embodiment (worst), disembodied bliss (middle), resurrection embodiment (best): “to get to the best state you’ve got to keep on living in the worst state.”
  • Luke 16 (rich man and Lazarus) — used only after the explicit fence “we must not press parables for doctrinal precision”; and Luke 23:43 (the thief) — “today you will be with me in paradise.”
Movement 3 — the unrighteous soul (Part 20)
  • The mirror image: the unsaved “imprisoned” in hades — conscious torment until the resurrection.
  • Lexical bridge: hades is the LXX’s Greek for the Hebrew sheol — so the “gloomy” OT texts (Psalm 6:5; Isaiah 38:18) “describe most accurately the state of the unrighteous dead.”
  • The load-bearing distinction: hades is not hell. Hades is the disembodied intermediate state; hell (gehenna) is the final state after the resurrection.
Characteristic restraint — he does not over-read

Craig calls the OT picture “gloomy” and “mixed,” does not claim the OT clearly teaches conscious bliss in sheol, and concedes the disembodied interim is “not the best state.” This refusal to over-claim is the most distinctive feature of the treatment — and, paradoxically, the source of the corpus’s central gap: he builds the positive case so carefully that he never turns to rebut the counter-texts.

Self-flagged speculation — the NDE resolution

Craig introduces a near-death-experience “mental projection” resolution but explicitly labels it “just a speculation of course.” Not a gap so much as a self-acknowledged limit — but it should not be treated as a load-bearing doctrinal claim.

Objections handled — cross-source master list

Because this is a single voice, the “source” column tracks which lesson engages the objection; all responses are Craig’s.

Objection 1

“Sheol just means the grave / death”

So the Old Testament has no real concept of post-mortem existence at all — only the cessation of life and burial.

Reductive / minimalist reading · Part 17

Strongest response

The dead are conscious shades, not merely buried

Craig rejects the reductive reading on textual grounds: the dead are pictured as shades who greet new arrivals (the Isaiah 14 taunt over the king of Babylon as he descends to sheol). “I don’t think that we can agree with those who say that sheol simply means the grave or death.”

Craig Part 17 — lexical sub-argument

Objection 2

The OT has no hopeful afterlife — so the biblical hope is a late invention

If the dominant OT picture is a gloomy, undifferentiated underworld, the later resurrection hope looks inconsistent or bolted-on — evidence the doctrine was invented late.

Implicit critical objection · Part 17

Strongest response

Progressive revelation — absence is expected, not embarrassing

The thin early doctrine is expected developmental immaturity, because God “reveals it gradually over time.” A hermeneutical defusing rather than an argued rebuttal — the same frame that handles the OT’s implicit Trinity and the late inclusion of the Gentiles.

Craig Part 17 — the controlling principle

Objection 3

Soul sleep — the dead are unconscious until the resurrection

The “sleep” language (Daniel 12:2; the NT’s “fallen asleep”) implies the dead are not conscious; they await the resurrection in oblivion, not bliss.

Implicit / structural · Part 19

Response — positive construction only

The whole thesis is its denial

Not named, but answered by construction: the soul is “not ... in a state of unconsciousness.” The 2 Cor 5 “further clothed, not unclothed” reading and the Phil 1 “to depart and be with Christ ... far better” both presuppose conscious presence at death. But the soul-sleep prooftexts are never individually rebutted — see Gap 1.

Craig Part 19 — de facto refutationCounter-texts unengaged

Objection 4

Isn’t the disembodied interim itself sub-optimal — “naked”?

If the resurrection body is the goal, a disembodied soul is a diminished, “naked” state — which seems to undercut the claim that death is “gain.”

Internal tension in the NT data · Part 19

Strongest response

Conceded, not papered over — the “catch-22”

It is “not the best state” — the worst-ranked relative to the resurrection body in one sense. But it is better than embodied mortal life: “you’d rather die and go to be with the Lord ... than the worst state.” A notable instance of Craig resisting an over-rosy reading.

Craig Part 19 — three-states ranking

Objection 5

You may have conflated hades and hell

The temporary torment of the disembodied dead is treated as if it were the final punishment — collapsing two distinct doctrines into one.

Common doctrinal confusion · Part 20

Strongest response

Hades ≠ hell — a terminological fix

Reinforced by the LXX bridge: hades is the intermediate state of disembodied unrighteous souls; hell (gehenna) is the final state after the resurrection. “This is not hell ... hades is this intermediate state ... as they await resurrection.” A “here is the fix” teaching correction.

Craig Part 20 — the load-bearing distinction

Persistent gaps in the corpus

These are the objections the single-voice corpus does not adequately answer. Several follow directly from Craig’s positive-case-only method, and are flagged honestly rather than minimised.

Gap 1

The classic soul-sleep prooftexts are never engaged.

The central gap. Craig builds the positive case for conscious presence but never takes up Ecclesiastes 9:5 (“the dead know nothing”), Psalm 6:5 / 115:17, Daniel 12:2’s “sleep ... awake” language, or the NT’s own “fallen asleep” idiom — the very texts soul-sleep advocates rely on. He even uses the “fallen asleep” language himself without explaining how it coheres. The position is asserted-and-supported but the counter-texts are left unrebutted; this page is the de facto anti-soul-sleep case, yet it never argues against soul sleep on its own ground. See Q-12.1.2.

Gap 2

The moral objection to hell is entirely absent — despite hell being the topic of Part 20.

Craig establishes the doctrine (conscious torment now, eternal punishment after the resurrection) but offers no theodicy: no proportionality defence, no free-will / self-exclusion account, no engagement with annihilationism as an objection. He explicitly defers all evaluative reflection (“in the final two lessons ... I’ll share some final thoughts”). An aggregator coming here for WLC’s defence of hell will not find it. See Q-12.1.4 / Q-2.5.1 — this node does not satisfy them.

Gap 3

Substance dualism is presupposed, not defended.

The whole argument assumes a body / soul dualism in which “the soul stripped of the body continues to exist.” Christian physicalism — the view that there is no immaterial soul to survive, so any “intermediate state” would be a re-creation rather than a continuity — is never engaged. The anthropological premise is load-bearing and undefended. See Q-13.x (substance dualism / the soul).

Gap 4

The resurrection-as-cultural-borrowing objection is absent.

Craig dates the explicit resurrection texts late (“late in the development of the old testament in Isaiah and Daniel”) and notes inter-testamental flourishing, but never engages the history-of-religions claim that resurrection belief was imported (e.g. from Persian / Zoroastrian sources during or after the exile). An aggregator tracking that objection should note it is simply not raised.

Gap 5

The Part 17 / Part 20 sheol tension is left unresolved.

In Part 17 the gloomy sheol texts describe the undifferentiated realm of all the dead, “no discrimination between good and evil.” In Part 20 the same texts “describe most accurately the state of the unrighteous dead.” Whether sheol / hades houses only the unrighteous, or also (pre-resurrection) the righteous in a separate compartment (“Abraham’s bosom”), is under-specified — and the Luke 16 picture, with the tormented rich man and the comforted Lazarus within view of each other, complicates a clean reading. Craig does not reconcile the two lessons.

Gap 6

The near-death-experience resolution is self-flagged as speculation.

Craig introduces an NDE “mental projection” resolution but labels it “just a speculation of course.” This is therefore not a gap so much as a self-acknowledged limit — logged here so a renderer does not mistake it for a load-bearing doctrinal claim.
