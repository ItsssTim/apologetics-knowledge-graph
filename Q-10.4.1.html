Spirit-baptism: at conversion, or a second blessing?

An in-house Christian dispute. William Lane Craig argues, from the Pentecostals’ own proof-texts, that baptism in the Spirit is the initiating work given to every believer at conversion — not a later, fuller empowerment.

The question

Is baptism in the Holy Spirit an initiatory work given to every believer at conversion, or a distinct “second work of grace” received afterwards?

This is an in-house Christian dispute, not a believer-versus-sceptic argument. The Pentecostal and charismatic tradition holds that Spirit-baptism is a second blessing subsequent to regeneration: the convert is indwelt at conversion but must seek a later, fuller baptism — often evidenced by tongues — to enter the deeper Spirit-filled walk. The Reformed-leaning view Craig defends holds that Spirit-baptism is the initiating act: the moment a person is regenerated, indwelt, and incorporated into the body of Christ. Every believer therefore already has the Spirit.

What rides on it is the doctrine of the ordinary Christian life — whether a two-tier Christianity exists, and whether tongues are the diagnostic of Spirit-fulness. Craig answers no on both counts, while carefully reserving a legitimate home for the Pentecostal experience under a different heading: the filling of the Spirit (Q-10.4.2).

The convergent argument — an internal critique built as a cumulative case

This is not a deductive syllogism. Craig states the Pentecostal position fairly, declares it mistaken, then refutes it from the very texts the charismatic reading rests on. Because only one expert treats the node, the streams below are his own, surveyed across four transcripts — not pooled from rival voices.

Stream C · The criterion

  • Rom 8:9–10 — no Spirit, not his
  • 1 Cor 3:16 — indwelt temple
  • No Spirit-less tier of believers
  • Fruit, not gifts, is the evidence
Opponent

The charismatic thesis: Spirit-baptism is a second work of grace.

Stated fairly first. Pentecostals and charismatics hold that the convert is indwelt but not yet baptised, and needs a subsequent baptism — often with tongues — to enter a “Fuller and deeper experience of the Holy Spirit.” Craig restates this in their own terms before answering it.

Premise 1

1 Cor 12:13 makes Spirit-baptism universal and initiatory.

The word doing the work is “all.” “By one Spirit we were all baptised into one body” describes the church’s universal experience — “the initiating act by means of which we are placed into the body of Christ.” If all are baptised, it cannot be a second tier some attain and others miss.

Premise 2

Every relevant Acts narrative describes an initial, not a second, experience.

Refuting the opponent from the opponent’s own texts. Charismatics appeal to Acts; Craig answers from Acts. Across Pentecost, the Samaritans, Cornelius, and the Ephesian disciples, each case is “clearly an initial experience of the Holy Spirit … and not a second experience.” Water-baptism’s relation to Spirit-baptism varies — sometimes preceding, sometimes following — but the Spirit-baptism itself is always the person’s first reception.

Premise 3

The indwelling Spirit is the criterion of being a Christian.

No Spirit-less believers. “It is the presence of the holy spirit that makes a difference whether a person is a Christian or not” (Rom 8:9–10; 1 Cor 3:16). If every Christian has the Spirit, there is no converted-but-un-baptised tier waiting on a second blessing.

Conclusion

Spirit-baptism happens once, at conversion — so the “second work of grace” framing is mistaken.

Concede the experience, deny the category. Through Spirit-baptism a person is placed into the body of Christ, regenerated, born again, and indwelt — the Spirit being “the permanent possession of every believer.” The Pentecostal experience of a deeper walk is real and good, but it is the filling of the Spirit (repeatable, commanded, Eph 5:18), not a second baptism.

How the one expert deploys it

There is no cross-expert consensus to report; the density is depth-in-one-voice, not breadth. Read the four extractions as one developing argument at three depths, not four independent witnesses.

Dogmatic location — record precisely

Spirit-baptism at conversion, universal, anti-second-blessing (Reformed-leaning). On the gifts he is non-cessationist — he declines a cessationist verdict here; his quarrel is with the second-blessing framing and with tongues-as-evidence, not the continuance of the gifts. Evidence of Spirit-fulness: fruit, not gifts. Do not conscript him into the cessationist camp.

Asymmetric charity (concession)

Craig is markedly less charitable to the strongest opposing datum than to the weaker ones. The Acts 8 Samaritan case he grants is “peculiar,” then defers “in the interest of time.” When a class member (Steve) presses further complications, he accepts them as a “very nice Segway” into the filling material rather than a defeater — conceding the thesis needs the indwelling/filling refinement without retracting it.

Objections handled — the Pentecostal case

A single-voice node: “source” names the position raising the objection — largely Pentecostal/charismatic — and, where relevant, the in-class questioner. The response is Craig throughout.

Objection 1

Spirit-baptism is a second work of grace, subsequent to conversion

The convert is indwelt but not yet baptised; a later, fuller baptism — a “deeper experience,” often with tongues — brings them into the Spirit-filled walk.

Pentecostal / charismatic tradition

Strongest response

1 Cor 12:13 makes it universal and initiatory

“By one Spirit we were all baptised into one body” — the initiating act that places a believer into the body of Christ. If all are baptised, it cannot be a second tier some attain and others miss.

Craig decisive proof-text

Objection 2

The Acts narratives show Spirit-baptism as a subsequent experience

Charismatics point to Acts, where the Spirit appears to come on people who are already disciples — evidence of a second, post-conversion reception.

Charismatic appeal to Acts

Strongest response

Every case is an initial reception

A close reading of all four narratives — Pentecost, the Samaritans, Cornelius, the Ephesian disciples — shows in each “clearly an initial experience of the Holy Spirit … and not a second experience.”

Craig case-by-case Acts survey

Objection 3

Water-baptism and Spirit-baptism don’t line up consistently in Acts

Sometimes the Spirit comes before water-baptism, sometimes after — the inconsistency suggests Spirit-baptism is a separate, later event.

Implicit in the Acts data

Strongest response

The variance doesn’t rescue the second-blessing reading

Granted — the relation to water-baptism varies, “sometimes preceding it sometimes actually coming after.” But in every case the Spirit-baptism is still the person’s initial act of the Spirit, so the variance changes nothing for the thesis.

Craig concedes the data, holds the point

Objection 4

Tongues are the evidence of Spirit-baptism

If you have not spoken in tongues, you have not received the baptism — tongues are the diagnostic sign of the second blessing.

Classical Pentecostalism

Strongest response

Fruit, not gifts, is the evidence

The mark of being Spirit-filled is the fruit of the Spirit, not the gifts; so the absence of tongues shows nothing. Note: Craig does not therefore declare the gifts ceased — he is non-cessationist.

Craig from the wider Holy-Spirit unit

Objection 5

“There’s more to it than that — you can’t be too simple”

A class member grants Craig is not wrong but presses that the account is too tidy: there is a difference between being a Christian and walking in / being filled by the Spirit.

Steve, in-class questioner (Part 5)

Strongest response

The indwelling / filling refinement

Craig accepts the point as a “very nice Segway,” distinguishing being a Christian (indwelt, once) from being filled (repeatable) — which is exactly where the genuine Pentecostal experience finds its home.

Craig pivots to the filling (Q-10.4.2)

Objection 6

The Samaritans (Acts 8) received the Spirit only after belief and baptism

The Samaritans believe and are water-baptised, yet the Spirit comes only later — when the Jerusalem apostles, Peter and John, lay hands on them. A clear temporal gap between conversion and Spirit-reception. This is precisely the data Pentecostals press.

The strongest charismatic counter-datum; raised in class by Steve

Gap — granted, not answered

Conceded as “peculiar,” then deferred

Craig calls it “this peculiar case with the Samaritans” and admits “they hadn’t received him yet until they came down and laid hands on them” — then sets it aside “in the interest of time.” It is never reconciled with the “Spirit-baptism = conversion” thesis. The corpus’s strongest opposing datum stands conceded rather than rebutted.
