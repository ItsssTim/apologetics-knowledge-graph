Forgiving the unforgivable

How could you forgive someone like Hitler?

Christianity tells you to forgive, even to love your enemies, and to a wartime reader, or anyone deeply wronged, that can sound not just hard but obscene. Forgive the man who ordered the camps? The abuser? It seems like a betrayal of every victim. Almost all of the horror, though, comes from a misunderstanding of what forgiveness actually is.

First / It isn't excusing
Step 1 of 4
What forgiveness is not

Forgiving is not the same as excusing.

The first and biggest confusion is to hear the word forgive and think it means pretend it was not so bad. It means very nearly the opposite of that.

Not excusing
As C.S. Lewis insisted, forgiving and excusing are almost opposites. To excuse is to say there was nothing really to forgive, it was understandable, it did not truly count. To forgive is to look at something that very much did count, name it as the genuine wrong it was, and release it anyway. You cannot forgive what you are busy excusing.
Name the wrong
So forgiveness never requires you to call evil something less than evil. Quite the reverse: it begins by calling it exactly what it is. Forgiving the architect of a genocide would not mean deciding the genocide was a smaller thing than you thought. It would mean facing its full horror, and still refusing to be ruled by hatred of it.
Not minimising
This is why forgiveness is the furthest thing from minimising what happened. The people most tempted to shrink an atrocity, to say it was not really so bad, are usually the ones avoiding it. Forgiveness does not look away. It looks the wrong dead in the eye, and that honesty is the very thing that makes it cost so much.
The theory you'll hear
Forgiving evil just lets monsters off the hook. Only if forgiveness meant excusing, and it does not. As Lewis insisted, you can only forgive what you first name as a real and terrible wrong; forgiveness looks evil full in the face, calls it evil, and releases its grip on you anyway. Excusing says there was nothing to forgive. Forgiveness says there was, and refuses to be owned by it.
✓
Confidence: forgiveness is not excusing, minimising, or pretending the wrong did not matter. It begins by naming evil as evil. That is why it is serious and costly, not soft, and why it can only ever be offered for something genuinely wrong.
Second / It isn't cancelling justice
Step 2 of 4
Doesn't it cheat the victims?

It isn't the cancelling of justice.

The next fear is that to forgive is to wave evil through, to tell the victims their suffering will go unanswered. But forgiveness and justice are not opposites.

Keeps justice
To forgive a person is to give up your own claim to personal revenge, and the bitterness that eats you. It is not to declare the act fine, or to demand that courts look away. You can forgive someone and still want them tried, still want the truth told, still want the harm stopped. Releasing your hatred is a different act from abolishing justice.
Not vengeance
What forgiveness lays down is the sword in your own hand, the drive to make them pay, personally, with your own bitterness as the weapon. That drive can feel like justice, but is usually pain looking for somewhere to go. Letting it go is not letting them win. It is refusing to let what they did go on shaping who you become.
A final Judge
And here is what makes it possible. Miroslav Volf, who lived through the wars in the Balkans, noticed that people could only lay down the cycle of revenge once they trusted that a just God would finally set things right (Romans 12:19). You can release your own claim precisely because you believe evil will not, in the end, get away with it. Justice secured is what frees you to stop taking it yourself.
The theory you'll hear
Forgiving the wicked is an insult to their victims. It would be, if forgiveness meant the wrong no longer mattered or that no justice would be done. But to forgive is to surrender your own right to vengeance, not to cancel accountability. The Christian can do this precisely because they trust a final Judge who will not let evil have the last word, which is what lets a wronged person lay down the sword without ever pretending they were not wronged.
✓
Confidence: forgiveness releases your personal claim to revenge and bitterness; it does not abolish justice or accountability. Christians can let go of vengeance because they trust a final Judge, so the wrong is not ignored, only handed to the One who will set it right.
Third / It frees you
Step 3 of 4
Who is forgiveness for?

It frees the one who forgives.

There is a reason forgiveness is urged so strongly on the wronged rather than the wrongdoer. It is, in large part, for them.

The poison
Refusing to forgive is often described as drinking poison and hoping the other person dies. The hatred you nurse rarely touches the one who hurt you; it corrodes you instead, and ties you to them more tightly than ever. Unforgiveness keeps the wrongdoer living rent-free in your head, the wound reopened daily by your own hand.
Breaks the cycle
Forgiveness is the refusal to let the evil done to you keep producing more evil through you. Hurt people hurt people, and revenge simply passes the wound along to the next victim. Volf saw it in the Balkans: the cycle of vengeance only ever stops when someone absorbs the wrong instead of passing it on. Forgiveness is where the chain finally breaks.
Not weakness
None of this makes forgiveness weak, or easy. It is among the costliest things a person can do, because someone always absorbs the wrong, and in forgiving you choose to bear it rather than repay it. And it is rarely a single clean moment. It is usually slow, walked through real grief and relapse, far more a road than a switch. Strength, not surrender.
The theory you'll hear
Refusing to forgive is the only strong, self-respecting response. It can feel that way, but unforgiveness mostly imprisons the one who holds it, chaining them to the person who hurt them. Forgiveness is not weakness and it is not forgetting; it is the refusal to let the harm done to you keep producing harm through you. As those who have watched cycles of revenge up close will tell you, it is the one thing that ever breaks them.
✓
Confidence: forgiveness frees the forgiver from the corrosive grip of hatred and breaks the cycle of revenge that otherwise passes the wound on. It is not weakness or forgetting, but the costly strength of bearing a wrong rather than repaying it, and usually slow.
Fourth / Not alone, not unlimited
Step 4 of 4
But how is it even possible?

You're not asked to do it alone, or first.

Even granting all of that, forgiving great evil can feel simply beyond human strength. The Christian answer is that you are not asked to summon it from nowhere.

Forgiven first
A Christian forgives as someone who has first been forgiven, at enormous cost, by a God who, from the cross, asked forgiveness for the very men killing him (Luke 23:34). It is not a feat of willpower you generate to earn God's approval. It is a response, flowing out of having received mercy you did not deserve, and then extending the same mercy to others (Colossians 3:13).
Not by willpower
And it comes with help. Christianity does not say grit your teeth and feel warmly towards your enemy by sheer effort. As Lewis pointed out, you already manage to love one deeply flawed person without approving of all they do: yourself. Loving an enemy means willing their good and their repair, not feeling fond of them, and it is the slow work of grace, not a switch you flip.
The limits
It also has honest limits worth saying plainly. Forgiveness does not demand instant feelings, or reconciliation with someone unrepentant, or returning to a person who will harm you again. You can forgive an abuser and never go back. Forgiveness releases the debt and the bitterness. Reconciliation is a further thing, and it takes two.
The theory you'll hear
Easy for Christians to preach forgiveness when they weren't the ones who suffered. A fair and serious point, and Christianity does not hand this down lightly or quickly. It does not require instant feelings, reconciliation with an unrepentant abuser, or staying in harm's way. It asks only that you not be finally mastered by hatred, and it points to a God who forgave his own killers and who supplies the grace to do, slowly, what no one could manage alone.
✓
Confidence: a Christian forgives in response to being forgiven first, in the strength of grace rather than raw willpower. And it has honest limits: it never requires instant feelings, reconciliation without repentance, or returning to danger. Releasing bitterness is not the same as restoring the relationship.

So how could you forgive someone like Hitler? Not by deciding the evil was small, and not by letting it go unanswered. Forgiveness names the wrong in full, hands the justice to a God who will not let it slide, and then refuses to let the hatred keep ruling the one who was wronged. It is not weakness; it is the hardest and strongest thing a person can do, and a Christian does it not by sheer grit but as someone already forgiven at the cross. It does not mean feeling fond of evil, or reconciling with the unrepentant, or walking back into harm. It means you will not, in the end, let what was done to you have the final word.
