The question

Do Isaiah’s messianic texts — above all the suffering servant of Isaiah 52:13–53:12, and the child of Isaiah 9:6–7 and 7:14 — genuinely point to Jesus, or are they Christian re-readings of passages that, in context, mean something else?

The stakes run both ways. If Isaiah 53 most naturally reads as collective Israel, and Isaiah 7:14 was a near-term sign to King Ahaz, then the New Testament writers stand accused of retrofitting prophecy from a Greek mistranslation — the charge Alex O’Connor, Aron Ra, and the TenakTalk anti-missionaries press hardest. If, instead, the servant is an individual who is exalted in language Isaiah reserves for Yahweh alone, who makes priestly intercession that Isaiah says only Yahweh can make, and who is named “Mighty God,” then Isaiah supplies the Hebrew Bible’s own portrait of a divine, suffering, atoning Messiah.

This is an expert deep-dive dominated by one voice. Jonathan McLatchie carries four of the six substantive extractions and supplies the controlling method. Cliffe Knechtle contributes the popular Isaiah 7:14 almah/parthenos exchange; John Meade (a Septuagint specialist, with Wes Huff) contributes a textual-transmission case for Isaiah 9:6 unmatched elsewhere in the corpus. The page is structured around that asymmetry, not a symmetric panel.

The cumulative case — convergent evidence streams

Not a single proof-text but an abductive cumulative case. Several independent features of Isaiah’s text, each contestable alone, converge on an individual divine-and-suffering Messiah. McLatchie’s signature is intertextual chaining — reading a title against its other uses across the same book, so Isaiah interprets Isaiah.

Stream A · Divine titles

  • Isa 9:6 “Mighty God” (El Gibbor) = Yahweh in Isa 10:21
  • “Wonderful Counsellor” = Yahweh in Isa 28:29
  • Hebrew pele (“wonder”) describes God’s own acts

Stream B · Exaltation grammar

  • Isa 52:13 “high and lifted up” = Yahweh in Isa 6:1, 33:5/10, 57:15
  • Isa 2: “the LORD alone shall be exalted in that day”
  • Servant exalted after that day → servant is Yahweh

Stream C · Priestly / atoning

  • Isa 53 “sprinkle many nations” = priestly rite (Lev 4)
  • The priest’s blood is his own blood
  • Isa 59:16: only Yahweh worthy to intercede

Stream D · Servant ≠ nation

  • Isa 49 servant titled “Israel” yet redeems/gathers Israel
  • Isa 42 faithful servant vs blind/deaf servant Israel
  • zerah used non-literally (Isa 1:4, 14:20, 57:3-4)

Stream E · Pauline reception

  • Phil 2 applies Isa 45:23 (Yahweh’s every-knee oath) to Christ
  • Joel 2:32 / Rom 10 — same Yahweh-text pattern
  • Presupposes Christ is Yahweh

Stream F · Textual transmission

  • LXX “messenger of great counsel” = paraphrase, ~150 yrs pre-Jesus
  • Aquila / Symmachus / Theodotion restore literal Hebrew titles
  • Eusebius reasons from the Hebrew to the son’s divinity

The argument structure

The case is abductive and cumulative, not deductive: the conclusion is the best explanation of the convergence. Most live dispute is over P2–P4 (individual or nation?) and P5 (the almah lexical question).

P1

Isaiah 9:6 names the messianic child by titles Isaiah elsewhere applies to Yahweh himself.

“Mighty God” (El Gibbor) is Yahweh’s own title one chapter later in Isaiah 10:21; “Wonderful Counsellor” is used of Yahweh in Isaiah 28:29 (McLatchie). Meade adds that the Hebrew pele (“wonder”) describes God’s own acts — the Exodus plagues.

P2

Isaiah 52:13’s servant is exalted with grammar Isaiah reserves for Yahweh alone.

“High and lifted up… exalted” recurs of Yahweh in Isaiah 6:1, 33:5/10, 57:15; Isaiah 2 says “the LORD alone shall be exalted in that day” — and the servant’s exaltation comes after that day. Therefore the servant is Yahweh (McLatchie, in both the debate and the sermon).

P3

Isaiah 53’s servant performs a priestly, atoning act Isaiah says only Yahweh can perform.

The servant “sprinkles many nations” — sprinkling is the priest’s blood-rite of Leviticus 4:6 — and here the priest’s blood is his own. Isaiah 59:16 says no one but Yahweh was worthy to intercede; so the intercessor must be a divine person (McLatchie sermon).

P4

The servant is an individual distinguished from national Israel, even when given Israel’s name.

Isaiah 49’s servant is titled “Israel” yet “brings Jacob back” — he is “greater Israel,” not the nation; Isaiah 42 contrasts the faithful servant with the blind/deaf servant Israel (McLatchie).

P5

The objections from “offspring” (zerah) and a near-term sign do not hold.

Zerah is used non-literally in Isaiah 1:4, 14:20, 57:3-4 (seed of evildoers / adulterer / falsehood), so the servant’s “seed” need not be biological. Isaiah 7:14’s almah is rendered parthenos (“virgin”) in the pre-Christian Septuagint, Matthew’s source (McLatchie internal critique; Knechtle on the LXX).

P6

The earliest reception confirms the divine-Messiah reading from the Hebrew, not merely from the Greek.

The Septuagint of Isaiah 9 is an interpretive paraphrase made ~150 years before Jesus (not Christian polemic); Jewish revisers (Aquila, Symmachus, Theodotion) restored the literal titles; Codex Sinaiticus marginalia, the Syro-Hexapla, and Eusebius all reason from the Hebrew text to the son’s divinity — centuries before Jerome (Meade).

∴ C

Isaiah portrays an individual, divine, suffering, atoning Messiah — and the New Testament’s application of these texts to Jesus follows the Hebrew, the earliest Jewish-Greek reception, and Isaiah’s own internal usage.

The cumulative force of P1–P6, sealed by Paul’s application of Yahweh’s Isaiah 45:23 oath to Christ in Philippians 2 (McLatchie). The conclusion is the best explanation of the convergence, not a syllogistic necessity.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

The suffering servant is collective Israel personified

Isaiah 41 explicitly calls Israel “my servant.” Context throughout Isaiah makes the suffering servant a personification of the suffering and ultimate redemption of the nation — not an individual Messiah.

Bashir Vania (Muslim); TenakTalk Jewish anti-missionaries; the standard Jewish reading

Strongest response

Isaiah distinguishes the faithful servant from the nation

Isaiah 42 contrasts the faithful servant with the blind/deaf servant Israel; Isaiah 49’s servant is titled “Israel” yet redeems and gathers the nation, so he is distinguished from it (“greater Israel”). Individuated priestly intercession (Isa 53) and Yahweh-only exaltation (Isa 52:13) do not fit a nation.

McLatchie servant-vs-nation distinction

Objection 2

Isaiah 7:14 almah means “young woman,” not “virgin”

Matthew built a virgin birth on a Greek mistranslation: the Hebrew almah meant “unmarried maiden,” but the NT writers read the Greek parthenos and created a fulfilment the Hebrew never asserted.

Aron Ra, Alex O’Connor (atheist)

Strongest response

Matthew quotes the pre-Christian Jewish Greek

Knechtle: the Septuagint — the Greek Old Testament used by first-century Jews — already renders almah as parthenos (“virgin”). Matthew follows that existing Jewish translation, not a fresh error.

Knechtle Septuagint defence

Objection 3

Isaiah 7 was a near-term sign to King Ahaz

The prophecy concerns an 8th-century child born in Ahaz’s own day — “when that child is old enough to choose honey over curds, you’ll know your enemies are no threat.” Isaiah himself fathers a child later in the chapter. It is about a kid who lived 700 years before Jesus.

Aron Ra (atheist)

Gap — largely unanswered

No contextual or dual-fulfilment reply offered

Knechtle stays on the lexical almah/parthenos point and never engages the near-term-sign argument; no expert articulates a dual (near + far) fulfilment hermeneutic, and McLatchie defers on the verse entirely against O’Connor.

Aggregation gap

Objection 4

The servant has human qualities — so cannot be divine

The suffering servant fears God, follows the law, and has offspring (zerah). None of these fit God; the servant is therefore a human (or the nation), not a divine Messiah.

Bashir Vania (Muslim)

Strongest response

Zerah is used non-literally by Isaiah himself

McLatchie: Isaiah uses zerah non-literally in 1:4, 14:20, 57:3-4 (seed of evildoers / adulterer / falsehood), so the servant’s “seed” need not be biological — he can be “father” of those who hope in him. Vania concedes Isaiah 14:20 but holds the broader point.

McLatchie internal-critique on Isaiah’s usage

Objection 5

The servant might be Jeremiah (or another prophet)

Jeremiah 1:5 (“I formed you in the womb”) parallels Isaiah 49:5; the servant could be Jeremiah, Ezekiel, or any suffering human prophet rather than the Messiah.

Bashir Vania (Muslim)

Strongest response

Your disqualifier disqualifies your own candidate

McLatchie tu quoque: if “having seed” disqualifies Jesus from being the servant, then Jeremiah 16:1-2 — where Jeremiah is commanded not to take a wife or have sons — disqualifies Jeremiah just as much. The objection is “rather inconsistent.”

McLatchie parity / tu quoque

Objection 6

“Mighty God” / “Everlasting Father” are merely honorific names

These are “godly names” that righteous people can bear (Elijah = El + Yah). And two “Everlasting Fathers” create a Trinity problem — the title cannot prove the child is God.

Bashir Vania (Muslim)

Strongest response

“Father of eternity” connotes creator; El means God

McLatchie: “Father of eternity” carries connotations of creator of the universe, not a second Trinitarian person; the Messiah is “father” of his spiritual seed. Meade independently: Hebrew pele and el describe God himself, and Eusebius argued el “almost always means God.”

McLatchie Hebrew sense of the titlesMeade Eusebius on el

Objection 7

God does not accept a ransom for the wicked (Psalm 55)

Scripture says God accepts no ransom for the wicked; Jesus was not wicked, so cannot be ransomed. Penal substitution contradicts the Old Testament theology of personal responsibility.

Bashir Vania (Muslim)

Gap — unanswered

No response developed within the penal-substitution frame

McLatchie did not engage Vania’s Psalm 55 / “you cannot ransom Jesus, he was not wicked” critique in any depth — a real hole in the atonement leg of the Isaiah 53 case.

Aggregation gap

Objection 8

Matthew’s Hosea 11:1 use attributes Israel’s idolatry to Jesus

“Out of Egypt I called my son” (Hosea 11:1) refers in context to idolatrous Israel. Applying it to Jesus would attribute idolatry to him — Matthew ignored the text and seized on one word, “Egypt.”

Bashir Vania (Muslim)

Strongest response

Jesus as the new Israel who succeeds where the nation failed

McLatchie: Matthew presents Jesus as the greater Israel — flight into Egypt as an Exodus-reversal, then Deuteronomy 8 quoted in the wilderness temptation. The typology is vocational fulfilment, not a transfer of guilt.

McLatchie greater-Israel typology

Objection 9

Paul “replaces” Hashem’s name with “Jesus”

Philippians 2 substitutes the divine name with “Jesus,” violating the Tanakh’s prohibition on adding to or taking from scripture. Changing Hashem to Jesus Christ is changing the Hebrew Bible.

TenakTalk (Jewish anti-missionary)

Strongest response

Paul applies a Yahweh-text to Christ, not substitutes a name

McLatchie: Paul applies Yahweh’s Isaiah 45:23 every-knee oath (and Joel 2:32 in Romans 10) to Christ — which only makes sense if Christ is Yahweh. “Jesus” is an additional name attached to the human nature 2,000 years ago; the eternal person remains Yahweh.

McLatchie application not replacement

Objection 10

“The name above every name” means reputation, not deity

The name given to Jesus in Philippians 2 is probably about the reputation or authority he has — not an identification with the divine name.

TenakTalk (William)

Strongest response

The Isaiah 45:23 citation fixes the referent

Vladimir / McLatchie: “name” can connote authority (“stop in the name of the Queen”), but the explicit citation of Isaiah 45:23 determines the referent — the divine name, and the authority Christ exercised before the Incarnation.

McLatchie / Vladimir context fixes reference

Objection 11

Early Christians cared only about the Septuagint, never the Hebrew

The church relied solely on the Greek translation; the Hebrew text and canon never factored into Christian thought until Jerome “went back to the Hebrew” over a thousand years later.

Popular skeptical narrative

Strongest response

The Hebrew titles were live in the church for centuries

Meade: Codex Sinaiticus marginalia, the Syro-Hexapla note, and Eusebius’s commentary all show Christians actively engaging the Hebrew titles — Eusebius reasoning from the Hebrew to the son’s divinity decades before Jerome, who was “the last link in a chain,” not an innovator.

Meade transmission history

Persistent gaps in the corpus

Honest assessment: the node is strong on the servant’s divine identity and on textual transmission, but thin on several standard Jewish counter-arguments and on Isaiah 7:14 in context.

Gap 1

Isaiah 7:14 in context (the near-term-sign argument) is essentially unanswered.

Aron Ra’s strongest point — a sign to Ahaz about a child of his own day — gets no contextual reply. Nobody articulates a dual-fulfilment (near + far) hermeneutic, and McLatchie punts on the verse against O’Connor.

Gap 2

Pre-Christian Jewish messianic reception of Isaiah 7:14 / the Dead Sea Scrolls is not deployed.

The material that would directly rebut “Christians invented this from the Greek” is flagged as available in Q-3.6.16 but never brought into this node.

Gap 3

The Hezekiah-as-fulfilment Jewish counter-reading is never engaged.

The traditional rabbinic candidate for the Isaiah 7:14 child (Q-3.6.19) goes unaddressed.

Gap 4

The Psalm 55 ransom objection to penal substitution is left unanswered.

Vania’s “God does not accept a ransom for the wicked” press is a real hole in the atonement leg of the Isaiah 53 reading.

Gap 5

The strongest rabbinic collective-Israel exegesis is not met on its own terms.

Rashi’s reading of Isaiah 53 and the grammatical “stricken for the transgression of my people” (53:8) are answered only at the level the debate opponent (Vania) pressed them.

Gap 6

The explicit substitution verses (Isaiah 53:9-10) are underused.

The most direct individual-guilt-bearing verses are oddly secondary; McLatchie leans on 52:13’s exaltation grammar and the sprinkling/intercession argument instead.
