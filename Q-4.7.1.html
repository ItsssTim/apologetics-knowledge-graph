What does it mean for the Bible to be inspired?

Not “is the text reliable?” but “what is being claimed when Christians say God wrote this?” The answer is confluence — an author who writes himself into his own play.

The question

If the Bible was written by human beings — with their own personalities, errors, languages and centuries of scribal copying — in what sense is it the “inspired” word of God?

The skeptic’s move is almost always a category error made vivid. Bart Ehrman says we cannot have an inspired Bible if we do not possess the inspired originals. Jon Steingard says inerrancy is a presupposition smuggled in, not a finding. Keith Ward says the gospels are “lovingly treasured memories,” not transcripts, so inerrancy in the “exact words” sense is simply false.

The convergent Christian answer is confluence (concursive, or verbal-plenary inspiration): God breathes out the words (theopneustos, 2 Tim 3:16) while genuinely human authors write in their own styles, “carried along by the Holy Spirit” (2 Pet 1:21). Human authorship is not a defeater but a feature — on the strongest framing, it is the very mechanism of the incarnation.

The convergent argument — evidence streams, not a syllogism

This is a cumulative case assembled from distinct streams. No single expert runs all of them; together they form the corpus’s answer to “what does inspiration mean?” The case is abductive — each stream defuses a different way the “human book” objection is pressed, and the confluence model is offered as the best explanation of the very data the skeptic cites against it.

The mechanism

  • Theopneustos — “breathed out,” not in (2 Tim 3:16)
  • Confluence: Romans authored by Paul and the Spirit (2 Pet 1:21)
  • Dictation (Decalogue, oracles) is the outlier, not the model
  • Not Islamic stenography — authors keep their personalities
  • Author-in-the-play: God wrote himself in as Christ

The boundary

  • Inspiration is a property of the text, never of the ink
  • Material vs immaterial — Ehrman’s category error
  • Inspiration is a finite event at composition
  • Transmission is separate and ungoverned
  • “Verbal plenary inspiration” ≠ “verbal plenary preservation”

The warrant

  • Don’t start with inspiration — start with historicity
  • Meet Christ; then revise upward (bottom-up)
  • Unity: “intricate harmonies” across ~40 authors
  • One divine Mind best explains cross-century interlocking
  • “Literal” is inadequate — more than a book, not less

Expert framings

Wes Huff4 transcripts
Dominant voice — etymology, material/immaterial, inspiration ≠ preservation
Distinctive moves
  • Etymology as apologetic. Theopneustos (2 Tim 3:16) means “breathed out”; the English “inspiration” (Latin inspiratus, “breathe in”) points the wrong way
  • Confluence, not dictation. “Who’s the author of Romans — Paul or the Holy Spirit? Theologically it’s both.” Decalogue / Isaiah 38 oracle are outliers
  • Material vs immaterial. Ehrman’s whole case rests on ascribing inspiration to ink and papyrus; Warfield (1886) defined “text” as immaterial for exactly this reason
  • Self-implicating pastoral diagnosis. The church’s own “inspired autographs” talk gave Ehrman his fertile ground; “inerrancy is a property of God’s words, not of the ink”
  • Inspiration ≠ preservation (with Tim Mitchell) — a poor copy of a single gospel may still transform a life
Humility signature

Conceding the question “why didn’t God preserve it more tidily?” is “a bit of a silly question … because that is how God did it.”

Stuart Knechtle1 transcript
The author-in-the-play analogy (title anchor)
Distinctive moves
  • Shakespeare-as-Hamlet. “The most beautiful picture I’ve seen of what God has done” — the analogy models incarnation (author enters his own story) and inspiration (works through characters with real agency) at once
  • Confluence with accountability. God-breathed words “does not mean he was picking up the apostles’ hands … there was also accountability within the earliest Christian community”
  • Love-letter conclusion. Human authorship is the feature: God is “using us in the process … the ultimate love letter to humanity”
Cliffe Knechtle2 transcripts
The bottom-up methodological reframe
Distinctive moves
  • Refuses to start with inerrancy. “There’s absolutely no way … to show that any book is inspired by God — that’s a dead end”
  • Start with historicity. “Maybe they contain errors, but if they’re historically reliable I can get an accurate picture of Jesus”
  • Faith in Christ, not the Bible — then, “as a result of experiencing his trustworthiness,” revise the view of Scripture upward
  • Against Ward: concedes authorial personality is real and evidential (variation defeats collusion); contests only wooden literalism (“I am the door” is not two pieces of plywood)
Accommodation caveat

In the Ward dialogue Knechtle never actually defends high-view inerrancy — he agrees personality-variation is real and only resists the caricature of fundamentalism. A clean in-house-dispute posture.

  • Divine plurality stack: Zechariah 2 (“Yahweh sent by Yahweh”) → Proverbs 30:4 → Isaiah 63 (three persons) → Exodus 23 + Malachi 3 + Judges 2 (Angel of the LORD)

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

We don’t have the inspired originals, only error-ridden copies — so inspiration is meaningless

If God inspired the actual words but we possess only scribal copies that differ from one another, the doctrine has nothing to attach to.

Bart Ehrman, Misquoting Jesus

Strongest response

The material / immaterial distinction

Inspiration is a property of the immaterial text, never of the ink, papyrus or autograph. “It has never been Evangelical doctrine that any material was ever inspired.” Warfield’s 1886 handbook opens by defining “text” as immaterial for exactly this reason.

Huff Warfield groundingGurry original/copying distinction

Objection 2

If God wanted us to have his words, he would have miraculously preserved them

He inspired them miraculously; the failure to preserve them the same way implies he never bothered to inspire them at all.

Bart Ehrman

Strongest response

The demand is anthropocentric

“Will it suffice that God wanted some people to have some of his words for some of the time?” No pre-printing-press Christian could meet Ehrman’s condition. “A poor translation of a poor manuscript of a single gospel may still be a powerful document for spiritual transformation.”

Huff anthropocentric rebuttalGurry God never promised perfect copying

Objection 3

Inerrancy is a presupposition, not a conclusion

Christians find a resolution to every contradiction — but only because they begin with inerrancy. Remove the presupposition and the problems reappear.

Jon Steingard

Strongest response

Don’t start there — bottom-up

Begin with the historical reliability of the gospels, meet Christ, and only then — “as a result of experiencing his trustworthiness” — revise upward to a high view of Scripture. Inerrancy is the destination, not the premise.

Cliffe Knechtle methodological reframe

Objection 4

The gospels are translated “treasured memories,” not transcripts — the exact words are lost

Jesus spoke Aramaic; every manuscript is Greek; nobody wrote anything down at the time. Inerrancy in the “exact words” sense is simply false.

Keith Ward (in-house dialogue)

Response — partial concession

Variation is evidence, not error

Personality-driven variation across gospels is precisely what rules out collusion: “if we all gave you the exact same details, you would say there’s some type of collusion going on.” Ward’s own positive view — the Word of God is the person of Christ, witnessed not transcribed — is conceded.

Cliffe Knechtle anti-collusion criterionWard person-of-Christ as Word

Objection 5

Scribal variants prove the text isn’t inspired

If the manuscripts disagree, the inspired text has been lost in transmission, so “inspiration” can no longer be claimed.

Popular skeptic

Strongest response

Inspiration ≠ preservation

Inspiration is a finite event at the moment of composition (2 Pet 1:21) — “a beginning and an end to this point of inspiration.” Transmission is a separate, ungoverned human activity Scripture never claims is supernaturally guaranteed. Critics conflate “verbal plenary inspiration” with “verbal plenary preservation.”

Mitchell bounded-event thesisHuff the two-doctrines contrast

Objection 6

Is the Bible dictated, like the Quran?

If God “breathed out” the words, the authors were mere stenographers, and the personalities in the text are an embarrassment.

Audience / Islamic parallel

Strongest response

Confluence, not stenography

Dictation passages (Exodus 20, Isaiah 38) are outliers. The norm is concursive: God uses the authors’ minds, styles and vocabularies. This is the precise contrast with Sunni doctrine, where Muhammad is God’s “stenographer.”

Huff Islam-contrast / outlier framing

Objection 7

The authors coordinated the “unity” deliberately

Of course the Bible looks unified — the writers (and later editors) engineered it that way.

Cameron Bertuzzi (relayed) / Mike Winger

Strongest response

The interlockings are sub-surface

The harmonies require connecting distant passages to surface (Isa 63 + Exod 23 + Mal 3 + Judg 2 to identify the Angel of the LORD). They are not coordinatable across ~40 authors and 1,500 years — “a masterpiece, a tapestry” assembled jigsaw-wise.

McLatchie intricate-harmonies defence

Objection 8

Why doesn’t the unity argument also prove the Quran?

If thematic unity evidences a divine Mind, the Quran — far more unified in voice — should qualify first.

Cameron Bertuzzi

Strongest response

Cross-author interlocking is the disanalogy

The Quran is one author over ~23 years; the Bible spans ~40 authors, multiple genres (narrative, poetry, wisdom, apocalyptic, legal) and ~1,500 years. The evidential weight is the cross-author, cross-genre, cross-century interlock — not a single voice’s thematic consistency.

McLatchie parity disanalogy

Objection 9

Imperfect men wrote it, so it can’t be true

Fallible human authorship is incompatible with a text that claims to be the word of God.

UPenn student

Strongest response

A non-sequitur — and a worldview misplacement

“What Jesus said was true” does not follow from the authors’ imperfection; Spirit-aided recall (John 14:26) supplies the mechanism. If you presuppose naturalism you’ll reject it — but challenge naturalism at the resurrection, “not the inspiration of scripture.”

Lennox non-sequitur + worldview placement

Objection 10

Taking the Bible “literally”

A high view of inspiration commits you to wooden literalism — snake-handling, refusing vaccination, every detail flat-read.

Recurring (Ward’s “fundamentalist” target)

Strongest response

“Literal” is hopelessly inadequate

Jesus “is not a literal door, but a real door.” “Milk and honey” is figurative language standing for a reality. A good literal reading honours genre and metaphor: “more than a book, not less.”

Lennox hermeneuticCliffe Knechtle genre-aware literalism

Objection 11

Galilee vs Jerusalem appearances — why does it need so much harmonising?

If the gospels are God’s inspired word, why is it even necessary to resolve so many things that appear contradictory? The need for harmonisation undermines the claim.

Jon Steingard

Gap — sidestepped

Deflected via the methodological reframe

Knechtle redirects to historicity rather than engaging the resurrection-appearance itineraries on the merits. The corpus never harmonises Galilee vs Jerusalem here — its hardest specific case goes unanswered. Cross-link Q-4.5.7.

Objection 12

Circularity of the inerrancy syllogism

How do we know (1) the Bible is God’s word and (2) God doesn’t err, without presupposing (3) that the Bible is inerrant?

Conference questioner

“It’s theological, not historical”

Persistent gaps in the corpus

Gap A

Bauckham’s eyewitness model never deployed

Jesus and the Eyewitnesses directly contests Ward’s “treasured memories passed down lovingly” framework, yet Knechtle — who has it in his repertoire elsewhere — never cites it in-conversation.

Corpus omission

Gap B

The Aramaic “lost words” claim goes uncontested

No one raises bilingual/trilingual first-century Palestine, or the preserved ipsissima verba (talitha cumi, abba, eloi eloi) that imply selective preservation rather than the wholesale loss Ward asserts. The redaction-critical objection to McLatchie’s harmonies, and the WLC teaching-vs-assertion / inductive-inerrancy models, are likewise never named.
