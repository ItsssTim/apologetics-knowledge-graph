Demons: nature, work, and the question of possession

A single-voice consolidation of William Lane Craig’s 2018 Defenders demonology lessons. The apologetic weight sits on one move: conceding that possession is empirically hard to tell from schizophrenia, while holding a bounded biblical category of indwelling agency.

The question

What are demons, what do they do, and is “possession” a real category — or a pre-scientific name for mental illness?

This is the question demonology actually has to answer. The descriptive theology — demons are fallen angels, ranked into powers, subject to Jesus’ name — is the easy part. The load-bearing question is whether the New Testament’s possession accounts pick out a genuine kind of thing at all, or whether they are first-century descriptions of epilepsy, dissociation and schizophrenia that a modern psychiatry would simply re-file.

The apologetic anchor is Craig’s handling of exactly that challenge. He concedes the empirical point to the naturalist — possession can be very hard to distinguish from schizophrenia — while holding a bounded biblical category of indwelling spiritual agency. The signature of the corpus is restraint: he repudiates the cartoon devil “with horns and a long tail” and is openly hostile to sensationalist deliverance. That restraint is also where the cracks show.

The cumulative case — converging scriptural descriptors

Not a syllogism. Craig assembles demonology the way the Defenders class always proceeds — a proof-text walk that stacks descriptors into a converging portrait. The seven-point nature sketch (Part 23) and the possession material (Part 24) form three evidence streams. The most apologetically important strand is the possession-vs-mental-illness handling in Stream C.

Stream A · Nature

  • Personal, intelligent (Acts 16; Rev 12:9)
  • Spiritual — “minds without bodies” (Matt 8:16; Luke 10)
  • Malevolent / unclean (Matt 12; 1 John 3:8)
  • Same kind as angels — differ only in moral alignment
  • The identity thesis: fallen angels

Stream B · Work / power

  • Possess and indwell bodies
  • Impart superhuman strength (Mark 5; Acts 19)
  • Ranked: principalities, powers, world-rulers (Eph 6:12)
  • Possession vs mere sin
  • Possession vs mental illness

Stream C · Authority over them

  • Subject to Jesus’ name = authority, not a magic word
  • Sons of Sceva: the empty-formula defeater (Acts 19)
  • They know their end (Matt 8:29; 25:41)
  • Possession vs oppression
  • Can a Spirit-indwelt believer be possessed?

How the expert deploys it

William Lane Craig 2 transcripts · 5 extractions
The restrained systematician — Defenders demonology, Parts 23–24
The identity thesis (nature)
  • A seven-point profile read off a proof-text walk: personal and intelligent; spiritual (“unembodied spiritual beings”); malevolent and unclean; ranked into authorities; able to possess and impart superhuman strength; subject to Jesus’ name; aware of their judgment.
  • The controlling conclusion: demons are fallen angels — the same sort of creature as angels, distorted in moral character. “The only difference really between angels and demons [is] their moral alignment.”
The anti-sensationalist signature
  • Attacks the “cartoons and popular culture” devil — “horns and a long tail… a buffoon that you can easily deal with” — and replaces it with Paul’s “incredibly evil and powerful being.”
  • Pivots the whole doctrine to sanctification: “when you stray off the path and sin gets into your life you open yourself up to the power of these demonic beings.” Demonology as a spur to holy living, not warfare spectacle.
Hierarchy — defended against Lewis’s “confederacy”
  • Argues from Ephesians 6:12 and the Michael datum (Jude / 2 Peter): even the archangel, “much greater in power,” would not revile these “glorious ones” — implying a real authority-structure.
  • Lay questioner Bill presses the C.S. Lewis line (“hell is more like a confederacy”) and “evil cannot trust evil.”
The name is authority, not incantation
  • The name varies by language (“Jesus is the English version”), so power cannot reside in the verbal form.
  • Scriptural defeater: the Sons of Sceva (Acts 19) used the name without the authority and were beaten by the demon. To act “in Jesus’ name” is to act with his delegated authority — exorcism is not a mechanical recipe.
  • Re-reads the Gerasene demon’s “Son of the Most High God” address as the ancient naming tactic, not praise; Jesus “turns the tables” by demanding the demon’s name.
Possession vs mental illness — the anchor
  • Concedes to the naturalist: “it can be very difficult empirically to know when a person is actually demon-possessed or is mentally ill, schizophrenic.”
  • Holds a distinct category: possession “involves an indwelling spiritual being in your body,” marked by the demon coming out (and entering the swine, Mark 5). Explicitly both/and, not either/or — but no diagnostic test for telling them apart in a given case.
  • Pivots from diagnosis to prevention: avoid the occult “like the plague.”
Can a Spirit-indwelt believer be possessed? (honest aporia)
  • Posed by Kashi, pressed by James: theologically it seems impossible (“God is stronger than a demon”); empirically, occult-deliverance workers report freeing possessed Christians.
  • Resolution A — possession vs oppression: the demon “is outside the person and is attacking him from the outside,” not indwelling.
  • Resolution B — indwelt but not filled: a believer living under the flesh, like the carnal Corinthians, “could also be indwelt by a demonic being.”
  • “Either one of those is a possibility… we don’t really know the answer.”
Partial concession (hierarchy)

Under Bill’s pushback Craig softens: “hell in that sense is a chaos of rebellious creatures… certainly would be plausible,” and falls back on prudential self-interest (“in their own best self-interests to submit… lest they be in some way reprimanded”). Genuine-authority and chaos-of-rebels are left side by side, unadjudicated.

Latent contradiction (flagged by aggregator)

Resolution A is built to preserve the premise that Spirit-indwelling excludes demon-indwelling. Resolution B concedes that very premise — it grants a believer can be genuinely demon-indwelt (possessed). Craig presents both as live without noticing that B gives up what A was crafted to protect. The most consequential internal tension in the corpus.

Method note — agency without a criterion

The testimony of occult-deliverance ministries is accepted at face value as evidence, never weighed against the documented risks of suggestion, false memory and misdiagnosis — despite his own concession that possession is empirically hard to distinguish from mental illness. No stated rule for when an event warrants a Satanic-agency explanation rather than a natural one.

Objections handled — cross-source master list

Objection 1 · the anchor

Possession is just mental illness

The NT accounts describe schizophrenia, epilepsy and dissociative disorders, not indwelling spirits. A modern psychiatry would re-file every “possession.”

Naturalistic / psychiatric (implicit in the questioner)

Strongest response

Grant the difficulty; hold a bounded category

Craig concedes empirical indistinguishability but keeps a biblical category: an indwelling spirit, evidenced by the demon coming out of the person and entering the swine (Mark 5). The move is both/and — mental illness is real and possession is real; neither reduces to the other.

Craig Part 24 — the canonical anchor

Objection 2

Possession is just sin / yielding to the flesh

What looks like possession is a person giving way to the desires of the flesh — a moral failure, not a metaphysical one.

The class questioner

Strongest response

An indwelling spirit, not mere sin

Possession “involves an indwelling spiritual being in your body” and so is “vastly different from mere sinning, yielding to the desires of the flesh.” Demarcated on the texts’ own terms; “much, much more serious.”

Craig internal critique

Objection 3

The demonic realm is a confederacy, not a hierarchy

C.S. Lewis: hell is “more like a confederacy” of competing rebels, not a military-style chain of command. There is no real authority-structure to read out of “principalities and powers.”

Lay questioner “Bill” (via C.S. Lewis)

Response — partially conceded

The Michael datum suggests structure

Even Screwtape (senior demon over junior) expresses tiered power; Jude / 2 Peter “despising authority” plus Michael’s refusal to revile the “glorious ones” “suggest powerfully… some kind of authority structure.” But Craig concedes the chaos-of-rebels picture is “plausible.”

Craig cumulative case (Q-22.4.3)

Objection 4

Evil cannot trust or respect evil

Revelation shows evil eventually turning on itself. So how could there be a stable authority-structure that demons genuinely respect?

“Bill”

Response — reconciling suggestion

Prudential self-interest, not loyalty

Craig grants it is “plausible” that hell is “a chaos of rebellious creatures,” and reconciles via self-interest: it may be in the demons’ own interest to submit “lest they be in some way reprimanded.” Not fully resolved.

Craig (Q-22.4.3)

Objection 5

“In Jesus’ name” is a magic formula anyone can wield

If casting out demons works “in Jesus’ name,” the name is an incantation — a power-word any speaker could deploy mechanically.

The incantation reading

Strongest response

The Sons of Sceva defeater

The itinerant exorcists used the name without the authority and were overpowered (Acts 19). The name varies by language, so it cannot be the locus of power. “In Jesus’ name” means acting with his delegated authority.

Craig internal critique (Q-22.4.5)

Objection 6

Naming a demon is a transferable control technique

The Gerasene episode — where the demon’s name (“Legion”) is demanded — shows a mechanical method later exorcists can reuse to gain control.

Sensationalist deliverance practice (Bob Larson style)

Strongest response

Jesus turns the tables; no recipe follows

The ancient naming tactic is real, but Craig reads the demon’s address to Jesus as failed one-upmanship and Jesus’ counter-demand as turning the tables — not a mechanical recipe any deliverance minister can deploy.

Craig (Q-22.4.5)

Objection 7

A Spirit-indwelt believer cannot be possessed

If a believer is indwelt by the Holy Spirit, and God is stronger than any demon, a demon could not also indwell and possess them. So reports of possessed Christians must be mistaken.

“Kashi” (theological)

Response — two resolutions, undecided

Oppression, or indwelt-but-not-filled

Resolution A: the demon attacks from outside (oppression), not indwelling. Resolution B: a believer not filled by the Spirit, living in the flesh, “could also be indwelt.” Craig declines to adjudicate — and does not flag that B concedes what A denies (see gaps).

Craig dilemma resolved by distinction (Q-22.4.5 / 16.4.2)

Objection 8

The Gerasene demon “praised” Jesus — so the man was a believer

The demon calls Jesus “Son of the Most High God”; a demon would not praise Christ, so the possessed man must himself have been a believer — proving believers can be possessed.

“James”

Strongest response

Naming, not praise

The confession is the ancient naming tactic — the demon attempting to exert control by naming Jesus — which is why Jesus then demands the demon’s name. It establishes nothing about the man’s faith. (A tight cross-lesson re-use of the Part 23 reading.)

Craig internal critique (Q-22.4.5)

Objection 9

Is oppression really different from possession?

If “the same things are happening to someone,” the oppression/possession distinction looks like a verbal dodge invented to save the theology.

The follow-up press

Strongest response

A locational / ontological difference

The distinction is real, not verbal: oppression is external attack (“outside the person… not indwelling him”); possession is indwelling. It was introduced precisely to answer the theological puzzle, not ad hoc.

Craig (Q-22.4.5)

Objection 10

Are demons immortal? When defeated, are they destroyed?

If demons can be “defeated” in spiritual battle, are they annihilated — and if not, what kind of existence do they have?

Questioner (James)

Strongest response

Eternal existence, not eternal life

They lack “immortality in the sense of eternal life” but have eternal existence — not annihilated but “punished forever” (Matt 25:41). When defeated they are “sent to a different location,” not destroyed.

Craig (Q-22.4.1) — an ECT-for-demons datum

Gap 1

No diagnostic criterion for possession

Having conceded that possession is empirically indistinguishable from schizophrenia, what marker could a Christian or clinician actually use to tell the two apart in a given case?

The anchor’s own limit

Gap — left open

Category grounded, practice not

Craig grounds the category’s reality in the NT exorcism texts but offers no contemporary diagnostic. The Part 23 superhuman-strength sign is never reprised as a marker. The practical question is unanswered.

Aggregation gap

Gap 2

The “ancient explanatory framework” hypothesis

NT authors, lacking modern psychiatric categories, would naturally describe mental illness as possession. So the “indwelling spirit” framing may itself be the pre-scientific interpretation, not a separate phenomenon.

The harder naturalistic objection

Gap — not engaged

Distinctness asserted, not defended

Craig asserts the biblical category is real and distinct without defending it against the hypothesis that the “indwelling” description is itself the ancient frame. This is the load-bearing version of the objection and it goes unmet.

Aggregation gap

Gap 3

Pastoral-harm risk

If possession cannot be told from schizophrenia, why should the church ever risk treating a treatable mental illness as a spiritual condition?

Live pastoral objection

Gap — not addressed

Prevention, not diagnosis

His occult-avoidance counsel is about keeping people out of exposure, not about adjudicating a presenting case — so it does not meet the harm objection at all.

Aggregation gap

Gap 4

The believer-possession contradiction is never flagged

Resolutions A and B cannot both be true: B grants a believer can be demon-indwelt, which is exactly what A was built to exclude.

Internal tension

Gap — unnoticed by Craig

Two options, no reconciliation

He presents both as live (“either one… is a possibility”) without seeing that B concedes the indwelling A denies. The aggregator flags it; the corpus does not.

Aggregation gap

Gap 5

Satanic agency over-attributed without a criterion

Across both lessons events are read as demonic agency, with deliverance-ministry testimony taken at face value, and no rule for when a Satanic explanation is warranted over a natural one.

Method-level gap

Gap — in tension with his own concession

Testimony unweighed

The reliability of deliverance testimony is never weighed against suggestion, false memory and misdiagnosis — despite Craig’s own admission that possession is hard to tell from mental illness. No criterion for attributing agency is offered.

Aggregation gap

Gap 6

The Nephilim-spirits alternative to the identity thesis

An older view (1 Enoch, some patristics) holds demons are the disembodied spirits of the Genesis 6 offspring — distinct from the fallen angels themselves.

Second-Temple / patristic alternative

Gap — not engaged

Identity thesis treated as settled

Craig takes “demons = fallen angels” as the settled view, though his own link of 2 Peter 2:4 to Genesis 6 (Part 22) makes the Nephilim reading an obvious unaddressed alternative.

Aggregation gap

Gap 7

The Reformed / cessationist “no believer can be possessed” position

A whole tradition holds that a regenerate believer can be afflicted or tempted but never possessed — closing the puzzle from the other side.

Reformed / cessationist theology

Gap — not engaged

A live option left aside

In the believer-possession discussion Craig does not consider the position that would dissolve the puzzle by denying believer-possession outright, nor the perseverance / eternal-security angle.
