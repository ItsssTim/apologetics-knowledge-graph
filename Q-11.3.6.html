Sacrament vs ordinance: the means-of-grace question

The definitional key beneath both baptism and the Lord’s Supper — is the rite a channel of grace or merely a sign of it? A thin, single-expert node that nonetheless unlocks three others.

The question

When the church administers baptism and the Lord’s Supper, are these means of grace that actually convey it, or merely signs that point to a grace given elsewhere?

This is the definitional question that sits underneath the two great Protestant sacramental disputes. A sacrament is a means of grace; an ordinance is a sign or evidence of grace. The answer here fixes the answers downstream — the meaning of baptism, who may receive it, and what happens at the Lord’s Supper all turn on this single fork.

Honest density note. This is a thin node: a William Lane Craig–only deep-dive drawn from two Defenders systematic-theology lectures (2014, 2021). It is a clean comparative taxonomy, not a defended-against-objection argument. Its value to the graph is structural, not evidential — it is the conceptual key, not a contested battleground.

The structure — a single fork, then a graded efficacy spectrum

This node is not a syllogism and not a cumulative case for a conclusion. It is a comparative taxonomy: one definitional fork, then a four-rung spectrum of positions ordered by how much causal work the rite is said to do. WLC presents it neutrally and defers all adjudication to the later baptism and Lord’s-Supper lessons.

The fork

A sacrament is a means of grace; an ordinance is a sign or evidence of grace.

The definition that carries the whole cluster. A sacrament is “an actual channel by which grace is imparted to the believer.” An ordinance is “not a means of grace; rather, it is a sign, or evidence, of grace.” In both cases the rite is “the word of God conjoined with some sort of visible element” — water in baptism, bread and wine in the Lord’s Supper.

The prior question

Which horn you take depends on your doctrine of the church.

Upstream dependency. The answer “will largely depend upon your view of what you think the church itself is.” Is the church “a sort of primary sacrament” — the means of salvation God has given mankind — or “simply the fellowship of those who, through faith in Christ, are united together”? This makes the node downstream of Q-11.1.1 / Q-11.1.2 and upstream of everything else in §11.3.

The number

Catholics recognise seven sacraments; Protestants recognise two.

The confessional divide. On the Catholic view the Council of Florence (1439) ratified seven — baptism, confirmation, the Eucharist, penance, marriage, ordination, and extreme unction. Protestants tend to recognise basically two: baptism and the Lord’s Supper. (Note: 1439 is the ratification date, not the doctrine’s origin.)

The four-position efficacy spectrum — strongest to weakest

The analytical heart of the node. WLC arrays the traditions on a single gradient, ordered by how much causal work the rite is said to do — from the Catholic view (grace infused) to the Baptist view (a bare confession). Read left to right as efficacy decreasing.

Catholic · strongest

  • The rite infuses grace into the person
  • “God’s grace is infused into the believer through these sacraments”
  • By baptism, mass, the Supper and other rites grace is imparted
  • Grounded in the Catholic infusion view of justification (Q-9.1.2 / Q-9.1.4)
  • Seven sacraments (Council of Florence, 1439)

Lutheran · slightly weaker

  • A means of grace by virtue of the word bound up with it
  • “It is in virtue of the word of God that they impart grace to those who partake”
  • Efficacy carried by word + element together
  • Still genuinely a means, not merely a sign

Reformed · still weaker

  • A visible confirmation of grace already imparted
  • “A visible sign confirming the grace which God has imparted to you”
  • The sign attests grace rather than channelling it
  • Aggregator caveat: this gloss flattens the Reformed Supper (genuine spiritual feeding on Christ — see Q-11.3.3)

Baptist · weakest of all

  • A bare confessional act of the believer
  • “Simply a confessional act … it is not a means of grace”
  • In baptism you give public confession of allegiance to Christ
  • The rite signifies; it does no causal work

Query 1

Extreme unction is no longer death-only

Bob reports having heard that Catholics now use the anointing in healing contexts, not only as last rites at death.

Bob · 2014 live class

Response — concede and update

Accepted as new information

WLC takes the correction generously — “that’s very interesting, I hadn’t heard that” — and attributes the broadened use to “the influence of the charismatic movement within the Roman Catholic Church, which would have a stronger emphasis on healing.” A concession, not a rebuttal; the correction stands as accurate.

Craig 2014 Defenders lecture

Query 2

What is extreme unction?

Drew asks for a plain definition (after a self-deprecating “is that like extreme skiing” joke).

A “last infusion of grace”

WLC: the priest administers the Eucharist to a dying person as “a kind of last infusion of Grace” to ensure they “die in a state of grace … and is therefore ready for meeting god.” Definitional, not defensive — and a neat tie-back to the Catholic infusion rung of the spectrum.

Persistent gaps in the corpus

This is where the node is genuinely thin. The gaps are substantial because the corpus offers exposition without defence.

Gap 1

No Protestant rationale for two-not-seven

WLC states the Protestant count of two but never gives the criterion that yields it. Without a test, “two” looks arbitrary against “seven.”

Deferred by WLC

Gap — criterion never stated

Dominical institution + visible sign

The standard Protestant criterion is dominical institution (a rite explicitly instituted by Christ) plus a visible sign — which yields exactly baptism and the Supper. WLC flags the deferral but never supplies the test.

Aggregation gap

Gap 2

“Council of Florence (1439)” is a ratification, not an origin

The transcript states 1439 twice and unambiguously — but a reader could mistake it for the birth date of the seven-sacrament doctrine.

Attribution flag

Gap — date misreadable

The seven predate Florence

Florence ratified the seven-sacrament number; it did not originate it. The seven were already settled in medieval theology (formalised earlier and reaffirmed later at Trent). Read 1439 as a conciliar endorsement, not a starting point.

Aggregation flag

Gap 3

The spectrum flattens the Reformed view

Presenting the four positions as a clean linear gradient of “efficacy” conceals deeper category differences between them.

Aggregator critique

Gap — useful map, not the territory

Reformed Supper is feeding, not mere sign

The Reformed view of the Lord’s Supper involves a genuine spiritual feeding on Christ by faith, not merely “a visible sign confirming” grace already given — arguably stronger, and different in kind, from WLC’s summary. See Q-11.3.3 for the detail the spectrum compresses.

Aggregation gap

Gap 4

No scriptural or historical justification for the count

Neither the Catholic seven nor the Protestant two is defended from Scripture or church history here. Both are simply reported.

Deferred by WLC

Gap — reported, not argued

Deferred to later lessons

The exposition is descriptive throughout. The substantive defence of why two rather than seven — or why the Catholic seven — is promised for the baptism and Lord’s-Supper lessons that follow, not delivered here.
