Can life arise from non-life?

The problem was never the molecules — it was the information. The claim that undirected chemistry cannot bridge non-life to a coded, homochiral cell, and that the only experiments which come close smuggle in the chemist. Stephen Meyer and James Tour dominate; five experts, nineteen substantive treatments.

The question

Can the first living cell have arisen from non-living chemistry by undirected natural processes, or does its origin require a designing intelligence?

This is the origin-of-life question — abiogenesis, or “chemical evolution” — and it is logically prior to the evolution debate. Darwin’s Origin of Species explained how new forms arise from pre-existing simpler forms, but never where the first form came from. Natural selection cannot be the answer here, because selection presupposes a self-replicating organism — the very thing whose origin is in question.

The experts converge on one diagnosis: the problem is not the molecules, it is the information. Getting from chemistry to a digital code is, in Meyer’s phrase, “quite a trick.” Their most distinctive move is the investigator-interference critique — that the only experiments which move chemistry in a life-friendly direction do so by the active intervention of an intelligent chemist, so what they actually demonstrate is the indispensability of mind in generating biological information, not its dispensability.

The convergent argument — the shared abductive structure

Premise 1

What must be explained is life as it actually exists — a membrane-bounded, homeostatic cell built from four homochiral classes of molecules running an information storage-and-processing system.

The explanandum, locked down first. Tour: a cell “is a system. It is a factory in a sense” — lipids, amino acids, sugars, and nucleic acids, not “a bunch of molecules inside a lipid.” Meyer (citing Oparin): “the question of the origin of life and the nature of life are inextricably linked.” Attempts to “redefine life down” to a simpler essence are flagged as goalpost-shifting.

Premise 2

No undirected mechanism — chance, self-organisation, or prebiotic natural selection — accounts for the sequencing of bases that constitutes biological information. The chemistry is indifferent to the arrangement.

The eliminative core. Meyer: “the chemistry does not force any particular arrangement of characters” — any base can bond at any site. Tour: prebiotically relevant chemistry has produced “zero” of the four classes in homochiral form, “not even the building blocks.” Lennox: evolution “assumes the existence of a mutating replicator” and so cannot explain it.

Premise 3

The only experiments that move chemistry toward information require the active guidance of the experimenter — so they covertly demonstrate the need for intelligence.

Investigator interference. Meyer: “to get the information, you need the intelligent input of the chemist. So what’s being simulated in these simulating simulation experiments is actually the importance of intelligence in generating information.” Against Peter Ward: “you’re missing the crucial element here, which is the role of the investigator.”

Conclusion

Therefore a designing intelligence is the best explanation for the origin of the first life and the information it contains.

Methodological warrant + scope. This is uniformitarian inference — “the present is the key to the past” — the same logic SETI, forensics and archaeology use. McLatchie frames it as a positive inference “based on what we do know,” pre-empting “God of the gaps.” From biology alone the inference reaches “a mind of some kind”; Meyer extends to cosmology against the alien-relocation move: “Where did the universe come from? No alien can explain that.”

How each expert deploys it

Stephen Meyer 14 transcripts
The architect of the origin-of-information argument
Signature delivery
  • The field is in admitted impasse — his dissertation supervisor: “everyone in the field knows that everyone else’s ideas aren’t working, but they’re not willing to admit it about their own.”
  • Chance fails by combinatorial inflation — a 150-residue protein is ~10195 possibilities; the bike-lock analogy and Stegosaurus dial-puzzle prop scale the intuition.
  • Prebiotic natural selection is question-begging — it “presupposes self-replicating organisms”; the absent-minded professor in the pit; Christian de Duve as hostile witness.
  • Self-organisation fails — “no chemistry forcing the arrangement”; the magnetic-letters-on-a-chalkboard prop.
  • The investigator-interference critique — the experiments simulate “the importance of intelligence in generating information.”
  • Miller-Urey is a “fudge” — wrong atmosphere, and amino acids don’t touch the sequencing problem.
  • Cambridge credential first — his doctorate was in origin-of-life biology; the indictment is insider, not outsider.
Concession / scope discipline

Meyer concedes some “life-relevant molecules” have been built — but “only with the help of intelligent design,” which he turns into the damning qualifier rather than a retreat. He also offers a rare on-record falsification: “if someone can get RNA to self-assemble to solve the sequencing problem without intelligent design I will resign the Discovery Institute.”

James Tour within the Meyer corpus, 2023–25
The synthetic chemist’s “zero”
Distinctive moves
  • The four-classes framework — lipids, amino acids (19 of 20 chiral), sugars (“a very difficult molecule to make”), nucleic acids — walked through as premise enumeration.
  • “Zero” — none of the four classes “have been made in homochiral form… using a prebiotic synthetic method”; “not even the building blocks.”
  • The homochirality bomb — every prebiotic experiment yields racemic mixtures; Ron Naaman’s spin-selectivity work shows biology needed homochirality “at the start.”
  • The public-perception gap — ~30% of the college-educated public think scientists have made frogs; the reality is zero.
  • “Chemicals don’t move toward life” — not without “a living biological entity, which could be a person, pushing them in that direction.”
  • “Just So Stories” (Cairns-Smith) — researchers as narrators, not demonstrators.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

“That’s an argument from personal incredulity”

“Life from life” is just an intuition, not evidence — and cells reduce to ordinary chemistry (pyruvate, redox reactions, proton-motive gradients), so the leap to a designer is bad reasoning.

PZ Myers (vs Knechtle)

Largely unanswered at the folk level

The real answer is technical, not intuitive

Knechtle re-asserts (“Evidence. You just said so”) rather than engaging the biochemistry — the fallacy charge sticks. The corpus’s actual reply is Meyer/Tour’s positive case from combinatorics and homochirality, which never reaches this conversation.

Knechtle re-asserts onlyNeeds Meyer-level engagement

Objection 2

The Miller-Urey experiment solved it / set the trajectory

The 1953 experiment heralded a series showing “exactly how life arose from non-life without any intelligent direction” — the rest is just filling in details.

Metaxas paraphrase; textbook public perception

Strongest response

A “fudge” that doesn’t touch the real problem

Meyer: Miller’s conditions “didn’t actually simulate the early Earth’s atmosphere,” and producing amino acids doesn’t address sequencing — “a fudge… ultimately now irrelevant.” Tour: there are “zero” demonstrating experiments — a “Just So Story.”

Meyer wrong atmosphere + sequencingTour “zero”

Objection 3

“Evolution determines that” — the sequence is naturally caused

Evolutionary processes account for the sequencing of the bases; the arrangement is not a mystery requiring a mind.

Peter Atkins (vs Meyer, on Unbelievable?)

Strongest response

That is just an assertion

Meyer: “you have to point to a physical process that is capable of doing the work of sequencing the bases.” Chemistry permits any base at any position — there is no chemical determinism, so “evolution determines that” names no actual mechanism.

Meyer name the physical process

Objection 4

“God of the gaps” / argument from ignorance

You can’t explain the origin of information, so you plug in a designer — a placeholder for present ignorance that science will eventually close, as it has closed every other gap.

Popular; Dover-style framing

Strongest response

A positive inference from known causes

McLatchie: ID is “not an inference based on what we don’t know… but on what we do know” — information always traces to a mind; SETI and forensics use the same logic. Meyer: it is “an argument from what we know about the cause-and-effect structure of the world.”

McLatchie based on what we know + SETIMeyer positive inference

Objection 5

The cell is a simple globule — give chemistry time

The 19th-century view that “the cell was a simple homogeneous globule of plasm” — complexity is modest, and undirected chemistry plus deep time will reach it.

Historical (Haeckel-era optimism)

Strongest response

The molecular-biological revolution overturned it

Meyer: from the 1950s — Watson-Crick (1953), Crick’s sequence hypothesis (1957) — the cell was revealed as an information-processing system “far more complex than 19th-century biology imagined.”

Meyer Crick’s sequence hypothesis

Objection 6

“At least Darwinian evolution generates new information”

Granted the origin of life is hard — but natural selection plus mutation can at least produce new genetic information, so the gap is not in principle unbridgeable.

Standard rejoinder to Signature in the Cell

Strongest response

Selection only operates after life exists

Meyer: “natural selection and random mutation only become relevant once you have living organisms” — they cannot explain the first genome. “After four years of reading responses… no one has really refuted that argument.”

Meyer selection presupposes life

Objection 7

OOL researchers redefine life down

Life can be defined to a simpler chemical essence — a minimal self-replicator — so the target is far easier than a modern cell with four molecular classes.

OOL field (vs Tour / Meyer)

Response (asserted as goalpost-shift)

The explanandum is the cell that exists

Tour: the same researchers who once defined life this way now “change the argument… a philosophical approach.” Meyer (via Oparin): actual cellular life with all four classes plus homeostasis is what any theory must deliver. (But a simpler first replicator is a substantive hypothesis, not merely a dodge — see gaps.)

Tour redefinition moveMeyer Oparin’s explanandum

Objection 8

Time will solve it / Harvard’s $100m OOL centre

Recreate exactly the early Earth, add millions of years of wet-dry cycling and impact-crater chemistry, and life will emerge naturally — “we will have artificial life in a decade.”

Peter Ward (vs Meyer, Town Hall Seattle)

Strongest response

Time without a mechanism doesn’t sequence anything

Meyer: time does not solve the sequencing problem; in every current simulation “you’re missing the crucial element — the role of the investigator.” Ward’s own example (Benner’s engineered “fake DNA”) illustrates design — “that’s designing it.”

Meyer investigator is the operative variable

Objection 9

Theistic evolution makes God’s activity invisible

“What sort of a God so disguises his activity that you can describe it in terms of chance processes and natural selection?” If God used Darwinian means, design is undetectable.

Veritas interlocutor (vs Lennox)

Strongest response

Chance processes can themselves evidence design

Lennox: the self-winding watch — “the chance movements of my arm wind it up. It’s a very intelligently designed watch… harder to make one of those than… an ordinary watch with a wind-up spring.”

Lennox self-winding watch

Objection 10

This is a fringe view — there’s no real scientific dissent

The debate is just “people who have the facts on one side and people who have an irrational faith on the other”; no serious scientist doubts chemical evolution.

Anticipated by Meyer; popular framing

Strongest response

The dissent is from inside the field

Meyer cites a current Inference journal critique by a leading Rice organic-synthesis chemist (Tour); Lennox cites Denis Noble; and the OOL field’s own rejection of chance from the 1960s (de Duve, Cairns-Smith) is hostile-witness dissent.

Meyer Inference critiqueLennox Noble

Objection 11

Directed panspermia — aliens seeded life

Even Dawkins, pressed in Expelled, allowed that “maybe aliens from an advanced society came and planted life on Earth” — a naturalistic designer that avoids God.

Richard Dawkins (raised by Slater)

Strongest response

It only relocates the problem

Meyer: the alien-designer hypothesis fails on cosmology — “the God hypothesis is a much better explanation of the overall ensemble of evidence… Where did the universe come from? No alien can explain that.”

Meyer cosmological extension

Objection 12

Credible naturalistic routes — borax/ribose, impact craters, synthetic life

Impact craters are natural “chemistry sets”; borax soap wet-dry-cycled yields ribose; Benner has engineered functional non-natural DNA. The field is making real progress.

Peter Ward

Strongest response

Engineered chemistry concedes the point

Meyer: intelligently steered synthesis is not unguided chemistry — Benner’s “fake DNA” is a designed molecule. Tour: every prebiotically relevant route still yields racemic mixtures and “zero” of the homochiral classes a cell needs.

Meyer “that’s designing it”Tour racemic / “zero”

Persistent gaps in the corpus

Gap 1

The RNA-world hypothesis is named but never critiqued

Across at least five transcripts Meyer explicitly defers the substantive RNA-world rebuttal to his book chapter. The popular corpus carries the conclusion (it fails) without the argument.

The standard naturalist reply to the sequencing problem — see Q-5.2.2

Deferred, not delivered

“In my book I have a whole chapter on this proposal called the RNA world and I give a very detailed critique” (Meyer) — but the detailed critique never appears in any transcript.

Left for the books

Gap 2

Metabolism-first / autocatalytic-set models (Kauffman)

The entire corpus assumes a replicator-first framing. The metabolism-first paradigm — self-sustaining reaction networks rather than a first replicator — is never engaged.

Stuart Kauffman; autocatalytic-set theorists

Not engaged

No transcript addresses the replicator-first vs metabolism-first split, leaving an entire alternative research programme untouched.

Aggregation gap

Gap 3

Hydrothermal / alkaline-vent chemiosmotic scenarios

Arguably the leading contemporary naturalistic programme — proton gradients across mineral membranes in alkaline vents — is absent from every transcript.

Mike Russell, Nick Lane, Bill Martin

Not engaged

The vent hypothesis directly targets the “chemistry doesn’t move toward life” claim by proposing a natural free-energy source; the corpus never tests itself against it.

Aggregation gap

Gap 4

“Investigator interference is just normal experimental method”

The obvious naturalist reply — that simulating a plausible natural pathway under controlled conditions is standard science, not a concealed design admission — is never tested.

Implicit; the strongest available counter to the central move

Untested

The argument would be stronger if it distinguished “the chemist supplies energy and conditions” (legitimate) from “the chemist supplies the sequence” (the real charge). No transcript draws the line explicitly.

Distinction not drawn

Gap 5

Protocell / minimal-genome research treated as a dodge

Szostak’s vesicle work and Deamer’s lipid-world variants are dismissed under “redefining life down” rather than rebutted on their merits.

Jack Szostak, David Deamer

Dismissed, not refuted

A minimal first replicator simpler than a modern cell is a substantive scientific hypothesis, not merely a philosophical goalpost-shift — but the corpus engages it only as a rhetorical move.

Treated as redefinition

Gap 6

Knechtle’s personal-incredulity charge is left standing

No folk-level resource in the corpus rescues the “life from life” argument from PZ Myers’s fallacy objection.

PZ Myers’s charge against Knechtle

Rescue exists only by importing Meyer/Tour

The technical case answers the charge, but it never reaches the popular deployment — so at the folk level the objection wins.
