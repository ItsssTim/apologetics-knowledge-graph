The question

What theories have the Church advanced to explain how Christ’s death atones — and is any one of them adequate on its own?

This is a historical-theology survey, not a single argument. The Church has produced four major families of theory: the patristic ransom theory (Christus Victor), Anselm’s satisfaction theory, Abelard’s moral influence theory, and the Reformation’s penal substitution. Each names a real mechanism, each has a chief proponent, and each has been pressed with serious objections.

The load-bearing verdict, carried here by William Lane Craig, is that the atonement is multi-faceted: penal substitution sits at the centre as the foundational facet, but it absorbs the valid residue of the rejected theories — the liberation / victory motif of ransom, and the subjective-appropriation motif of moral influence. No single theory exhausts the cross. A subsidiary but striking Craig thesis is revisionist: the Church Fathers held more than ransom, and even Abelard held more than moral influence.

The convergent case — four theories as facets, not a syllogism

Unlike the Kalam (a deductive syllogism), this Q-node is a historical survey resolving into a cumulative, multi-faceted account. The four theories are not stacked premises but successive answers to the same question — each contributing a facet that survives into Craig’s composite theory even where the standalone theory fails. The historical engine is a succession of internal critiques: Anselm and Abelard overthrew ransom; Abelard recoiled from satisfaction; the Reformers completed Anselm’s justice-logic with penal substitution.

Facet retained from ransom

Liberation and victory — metaphorical Christus Victor.

Indefensible as stated, valid as a facet. The literal ransom-to-Satan theory dies on the rocks of Satan’s having no rights and the atonement being misdirected away from God. But the motif of Christ’s triumph over sin, death and hell is a genuine residue, retained in metaphorical form. Lands at Q-8.2.1.

Facet from satisfaction

Divine justice must be satisfied — God cannot simply overlook sin.

Justice, not wounded honour. To remit sin without compensation or punishment is “nothing else than not to punish,” which is unjust; God cannot contradict his own just nature. This justice-logic is what penal substitution completes — not departs from.

Facet from moral influence

Subjective appropriation — how the cross’s benefits are received.

The thinnest standalone, a vital component. By itself it collapses into self-improvement; as one element it explains how Christ’s objectively-won benefits come to be appropriated in contrition and love.

Synthesis

A multi-faceted atonement with penal substitution at its centre.

The constructive verdict. The biblical data of Isaiah 53 and its NT use cannot be accounted for without penal substitution; and PSA is foundational for satisfaction of justice, redemption, and even moral influence. So a composite theory must place penal substitution at its centre while retaining the rest as facets.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

The ransom is paid to Satan — but Satan has no rights

If the ransom was paid to the devil, that implies the devil had a rightful claim over humanity that God was obliged to honour. He had no such claim.

Anselm, Abelard (against the Fathers)

Strongest response

Conceded — decisively, and fatally for the literal theory

“Man owes nothing to the devil… God owes the devil nothing but punishment.” This killed the literal ransom theory in the 11th century and it “has never come back.” Only the metaphorical victory motif survives.

Craig via Anselm

Objection 2

The demons recognised Christ’s deity — so Satan was not tricked

Scripture shows demons saying “we know who you are.” If Satan’s minions knew Christ was divine, the fish-hook divine ruse cannot have worked.

Defenders student

Response

Aware that he was divine, not what it meant

Craig concedes it is “hard to defend… that Satan was not aware that Christ was divine,” but Satan may have misjudged what it meant: Christ seemed so weakened by mortal nature that he looked limited (cf. 1 Cor 2:8, the rulers of this age did not understand).

Craig qualified concession

Objection 3

Wouldn’t God be the ransom-holder?

Isn’t God the one requiring death for sin? Then the ransom should be paid to God, not Satan — which makes the whole patristic picture incoherent.

Defenders student

Response

A ransom goes to whoever holds the captive

On the patristic theory God was not holding us captive; in the ancient world a ransom was paid to the captor. So payment went from God (sending his Son) to Satan. This very feature was later judged a perverse consequence.

Craig historical reconstruction

Objection 4

God would be more magnanimous simply to forgive

A truly gracious God would just forgive the insult, as we are told to forgive ours. So Anselm fails to show Christ’s death was necessary.

Anselm’s interlocutor / the “feudal lord” caricature

Strongest response

This confuses honour with justice

To remit sin without compensation or punishment is “nothing else than not to punish,” which is unjust. “Such compassion… is wholly contrary to the divine justice which allows nothing but punishment as the recompense for sin.” God “cannot be inconsistent with himself.”

Craig via Anselm — justice not honour

Objection 5

Isn’t satisfaction obvious from Romans 3:25–26?

If the biblical text already teaches it, why is Anselm called “groundbreaking”? Surely he is just restating Paul.

Dr. Bob (Defenders Q&A)

Response

Biblical antecedents are expected; the shift is historical

Of course there are biblical antecedents — “otherwise they could hardly be Christian theories.” The groundbreaking element is that Anselm ended 900 years of ransom dominance: a watershed between the patristic and medieval periods.

Craig historical framing

Objection 6

Moral influence is how cults see Jesus — a mere good example

A subjective-only cross makes Jesus a good moral teacher, not God incarnate — the root of “moralistic therapeutic deism.”

Cash (Defenders Q&A)

Response

True of the standalone view — hence its inadequacy

Agreed: “you don’t need Christ to be God on this theory” for his death to exert a subjective influence. But Abelard at least affirmed Christ’s deity, unlike Schleiermacher; and the view rightly recognises we are sinful before God. This is precisely why it works only as a facet.

Craig steel-man + boundary

Objection 7

PSA makes Christ literally a sinner / corrupt

If our sin was placed on Christ, he must have become sinful in himself — which is impious and incoherent for the sinless Son.

Standard PSA critique

Strongest response

Imputation is forensic, not ontic

God “looked upon him on the cross” and saw our wrongdoing, “no in the sense that he somehow became corrupted in his own nature.” The transfer is legal / reckoned, not a change in Christ’s nature — the believer is reciprocally clothed in Christ’s righteousness (Zech 3; 2 Cor 5:21).

McLatchie forensic-imputation anchor

Objection 8

If Christ were truly punished he would be “cut off without hope”

Our punishment is hopeless separation. If Christ bore exactly that, he too would be lost — which is absurd.

Steve (Defenders Q&A)

Response

He paid it and so could not be held

He endured the just desert but “having paid that punishment cannot be held any longer and is risen.” A burden the stronger “God punished Christ” version must carry; the weaker version (Christ endured what would have been our punishment) sidesteps it.

Craig two-versions move

Objection 9

“We did the crime, we still deserve it”

Punishment tracks just desert. We are the guilty parties; a substitute paying it does not erase that we are the ones who deserve it.

Don (Defenders Q&A)

Response

Granted — and that is the point

PSA theorists agree punishment revolves around just desert. Christ’s bearing of it is precisely why we no longer deserve it: “as a result of which we no longer deserve suffering.”

Craig definitional reply

Objection 10

The NT never says God is reconciled to us, only we to God

Reconciliation language always runs man-ward, never God-ward — so there is no divine wrath needing propitiation; moral influence is enough.

Implicit / liberal exegesis (Abelard’s heirs)

Response

An argument from silence; the concept is present

Craig concedes the lexical point but calls it “an argument from silence.” Romans 1–3 is all about God’s wrath on Jew and Gentile alike — wrath that “is propitiated” through Christ. The word’s absence does not erase the concept.

Craig wrath-propitiation reply

Objection 11

Penal substitution isn’t in the OT — Psalm 55:18, and Jesus wasn’t wicked

Psalm 55:18 says God does not accept a ransom from anyone; and “was Jesus wicked that he could be ransomed? No.” The whole substitutionary framework contradicts the Hebrew Bible.

Bashir Vania (Muslim) — cf. Ezekiel 18 family

Partial response — key gap on the central facet

Isaiah 53 against the priestly system — but Psalm 55:18 not engaged

McLatchie answers the “not in the OT” half by reading Isaiah 53 against the sacrificial system — the servant is the priest who “sprinkles many nations.” But he does not directly engage Vania’s Psalm 55:18 / “God accepts no ransom” objection within the debate — the standard Islamic critique (Q-6.2.14).

McLatchie Isaiah 53 priestly readingGap on Psalm 55:18

Objection 12

Atonement was at Yom Kippur with two goats, not by a Passover lamb

Leviticus 16 prescribes two goats (one slain, one scapegoat); nowhere does Leviticus atone for sin with a lamb. The “Lamb of God” / Passover language is a NT misreading of Jewish scripture.

Jim Majors (Jewish critic)

Gap — asserted but not exegeted

“Without shedding of blood, no remission” — left undeveloped

Knechtle answers that the blood-atonement principle runs throughout Leviticus and Isaiah, not just Yom Kippur. But he never exegetes it against Majors’ narrow Leviticus 16 point, and the Numbers 29:8 lambs (raised in a super-chat) go unaddressed. The exchange dissolves into cross-talk.
