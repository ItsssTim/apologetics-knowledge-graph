The question

Does the physical universe have a finite past — a literal beginning of space and time — and if so, what best explains it?

This is the empirical engine room beneath the Kalam (Q-1.1.1). Where the Kalam supplies the deductive frame “whatever begins to exist has a cause,” this node is the contested factual premise: did the universe begin? Twentieth-century cosmology answered “yes” — against a centuries-old Aristotelian and steady-state consensus, and against active resistance from scientists who saw the theistic leverage in the conclusion.

The mode is abductive — inference to the best explanation. None of the three experts run a tight syllogism. Each starts from an agreed datum (a robustly confirmed finite past) and argues that theism explains it better than the rival “something from nothing” cosmologies of Hawking and Krauss. The node’s signature feature: the establishment’s resistance to a beginning is itself repurposed as evidence.

The convergent argument — inference to best explanation

Observation

The universe has a finite past — a beginning of space and time.

The shared datum. Lennox: the 1960s singularity and the Borde-Guth-Vilenkin theorem. Meyer: “a definite beginning at a finite time in the past.” Craig: Hubble’s 1929 red shift confirming Friedmann-Lemaître, hardened by BGV (2003) and Vilenkin (2012).

Robustness

The result is the scientific mainstream, surviving a century of attempts to evade it — not a fringe theistic reading.

Survival under pressure. Craig: “the history of 20th century cosmology can be seen as a series of one failed attempt after another to avoid the absolute beginning.” Lennox: BGV constrains the past “whether you talk about a universe or a multiverse.”

Candidates

Either a transcendent cause beyond matter, space and time — or a self-creating / from-nothing physical account.

The explanatory fork. Meyer: a material origin cannot be explained by “something else material.” Lennox weighs Hawking’s self-creating universe and Krauss’s physical “nothing” as the only naturalist options on offer.

Elimination

The naturalist alternatives either equivocate on “nothing” or assert a flat contradiction.

Internal critique. Lennox: Hawking’s “self-creating universe” is “a flat logical contradiction”; Krauss’s “nothing must be physical” is “sheer rubbish.” Craig disambiguates the cosmologists’ own “nothing” so it cannot be smuggled in as a something.

Inference

Therefore a transcendent, non-physical cause — for Meyer, a conscious and rational one — is the better explanation.

Comparative explanatory force. Lennox: “it came from God, who’s not nothing.” Meyer: “a conscious and rational agent … God in the traditional sense.” Craig: the beginning stands, and the Kalam proponent “stands comfortably within the scientific mainstream.” The entailment step — beginning therefore cause — is carried at Q-1.1.1, not here.

  • Hawking / Krauss demolition: “X creates X” reduces to “nonsense remains nonsense”; laws don’t create things (Lewis); Krauss’s physical “nothing” is “sheer rubbish.”
  • BGV as a mathematician: privileges “what the mathematicians say” over popularisers (Brian Cox); “I’ve met two of them.”
Methodological hedge

“I word myself carefully here because science changes … we can only deal with what we see at the moment and not make our life depend on them.” A protective move against staking a worldview on a contingent result.

William Lane Craig 1 transcript
The technical anchor — BGV and the “either way” structure
Signature moves
  • The cosmological spine: Hubble’s 1929 red shift → Friedmann-Lemaître → “literally the beginning of space and time” → BGV (2003): no past-eternal cosmos, multiverse included.
  • The “either way” robustness: whether or not something lay on the far side of the boundary — even a quantum-gravity regime — “either way, the universe began to exist.”
  • Disambiguating “nothing”: cosmologists’ “nothing prior” means “it is false that there is something prior,” not a state of nothingness treated as a thing.
  • The Krauss/Carroll defence: Vilenkin confirmed by email “you have accurately represented what I’ve said”; Krauss “deliberately deleted portions” of the quotation.
  • “Nearly 100 years” framing: the prediction of a beginning has survived a century of attempts to overturn it.
Hostile witness

Vilenkin, in his own words: “None of these scenarios can actually be past eternal. All the evidence we have says that the universe had a beginning.” Craig leans on the secular cosmologist’s own conclusion.

Stephen Meyer 2 transcripts
The cumulative-case leg — convergence over depth
Distinctive moves
  • The transcendence inference: a material origin “cannot be explained by something else material … you’re looking at some kind of transcendent cause.”
  • Three-leg cumulative case: the beginning is one leg of cosmology + fine-tuning + DNA information; the strategy is to show convergence, not develop each line.
  • The load-bearing triad (vs Dowd): the universe is not eternal, not self-existent, not self-creating — “an evolutionary worldview affirms precisely that proposition.”
  • Stronger endpoint than Craig or Lennox: not a bare “cause” but “a conscious and rational agent … God in the traditional sense.”

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

The universe “creates itself from nothing”

“Because there is a law like gravity, the universe can and will create itself from nothing” — no creator required.

Stephen Hawking, The Grand Design

Strongest response

A flat logical contradiction

Because there is something (gravity), nothing creates the universe. Compound error: laws don’t create things (“the laws of arithmetic won’t create any money for you” — Lewis), and “X creates X” reduces to “nonsense remains nonsense.”

Lennox internal-critique demolitionCraig Hawking concedes a beginning

Objection 2

“Nothing” is itself physical, so no creator is needed

A quantum vacuum with zero net energy is a kind of “nothing” that can produce a universe. “Surely nothing must be physical … even if it is the absence of something.”

Lawrence Krauss, A Universe from Nothing

Strongest response

Equivocation on “nothing”

“That’s just sheer rubbish.” The universe came from nothing physical but not from nothing — “it came from God, who’s not nothing.” Craig adds: cosmologists’ “nothing prior” means “it is false that there is something prior,” not a state of nothingness.

Lennox verbatim quote demolitionCraig disambiguates the term

Objection 3

It was a 50/50 coin toss that the Bible got it right

Either the universe had a beginning or it didn’t — so there was a 50% chance the Bible guessed correctly. No big deal.

Richard Dawkins (Lennox debate)

Strongest response

Decided by argument, not statistics

“It wasn’t decided by statistics or tossing a coin. It was decided against enormous pressure” — pressure coming from atheism — “and at least it got it right.”

Lennox coin-toss rebuttal

Objection 4

Matter and energy are eternal and self-existent

The universe never began; it is self-existent and self-creating, with no need for anything beyond it.

Michael Dowd, evolutionary panentheism

Strongest response

A material origin needs a transcendent cause

“I don’t think the universe is eternal and self-existent and self-creating.” A material origin “cannot be explained by something else material — you’re looking at some kind of transcendent cause.”

Meyer transcendence inference

Objection 5

A multiverse or pre-Big-Bang state evades the beginning

Maybe ours is one universe in a multiverse, or there was a pre-Big-Bang “pure energy” state that manifested materially — pushing the beginning back indefinitely.

Brian Cox (BBC Universe), multiverse cosmology

Strongest response

BGV constrains any inflationary cosmology

Whether a universe or a multiverse, “you do have a beginning.” The theorem “applies to multiverse scenarios, too.” Lennox retreats from Cox’s specific scenario to the more authoritative mathematical result.

Lennox mathematician’s appeal to BGVCraig theorem applies to multiverse

Objection 6

Quantum gravity / inflation will modify the model and remove the beginning

The standard Big Bang model needs modification for quantum gravity, inflation and dark energy — those revisions could erase the absolute beginning.

Standard physicist response

Strongest response

Modifications don’t touch the prediction

Such adjustments “need not affect the fundamental prediction of the absolute beginning.” Even non-pointlike models — e.g. Hawking’s no-boundary proposal — “still have a finite past, not an infinite past.”

Craig only viable non-standard models begin

Objection 7

You misrepresent Vilenkin — he says it may be eternal

Krauss produced Vilenkin’s email and Carroll argued quantum effects could restore a past-eternal universe: “perhaps it’s eternal, who knows, we don’t know.”

Sean Carroll, Lawrence Krauss (Q&A)

Response — contested, partly resolved

Vilenkin confirmed the representation; the quantum regime is metastable

Vilenkin emailed “you have accurately represented what I’ve said”; Krauss “deliberately deleted portions” via ellipsis. The quantum regime “cannot endure through infinity doing nothing and then suddenly transition to classical spacetime.” Craig does not engage Carroll’s technical model on its own terms.

Craig email correspondence + metastabilityCarroll’s model not engaged

Objection 8

The Bible is pre-modern science and useless

Genesis is pre-scientific myth and contributes nothing to cosmology — getting a beginning “right” is meaningless.

Generic skeptic

Response — rhetorically strong, evidentially thin

It speaks of the same universe, and could have been used predictively

Pre-modern, yes — but Genesis describes “the same heavens and earth physicists study,” and taken seriously as a hypothesis “just maybe you’d have got to the standard model earlier than you did.” A counterfactual, not a demonstrated result.

Lennox folds Vilenkin’s and Krauss’s quantum-tunnelling-from-nothing into the “nothing is physical” rubbish move; Meyer does not engage it at all. The zero-net-energy bookkeeping goes unanswered.

No-boundary and pre-Big-Bang scenarios sidestepped

Gap 4

Does a beginning entail a cause?

This node establishes the beginning but offloads the causal step to Q-1.1.1. A reader who grants the beginning yet resists “therefore a cause” finds no answer here — and the Kalam’s own B-theory concession lives at Q-1.1.1, not on this page.

Gaps 5–6

Non-personal cause not closed off; Wall’s caveats untouched

Meyer leaps from “transcendent cause” to “conscious and rational agent” without ruling out an impersonal transcendent condition; the personhood inference (Craig’s freezing-water analogy) is not deployed in this node. Craig cites Aaron Wall (2013) as a source but engages neither his later qualifications nor any specific bubble-nucleation model.
