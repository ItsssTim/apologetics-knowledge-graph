Do the Gospels contradict each other on when Jesus died?

The textbook Gospel “contradiction” — Synoptic seder versus Johannine lamb-slaughter, third hour versus sixth — and the convergent defence that minor difference is not logical contradiction.

The question

Did Jesus die before or after the Passover meal — and at what hour was he on the cross?

This is the textbook contradiction skeptics use to dismantle inerrancy and, with it, the eyewitness apologetic. It comes in two interlocking forms. The day problem: Mark has Jesus eat the seder and be crucified the next morning on Passover (Mark 14:12–16; 15:25), while John 19:14 places the sentencing “on the day of preparation of the Passover,” read by skeptics as the day before the seder — so that John relocates the death to the slaughter of the lambs for a Lamb-of-God point. The hour problem: Mark crucifies at the third hour (9 a.m.), John still has Jesus before Pilate at the sixth (noon).

The stakes exceed a calendar detail. If John demonstrably bent the date for theology, the Gospels are tendentious construction rather than reportage. The convergent defence holds that on a correct reading of first-century idiom and ancient compositional norms there is no logical contradiction — and that even a genuinely unresolved tension no more discredits the Gospels than the routine errors of any other ancient source.

The argument structure — a two-stream cumulative defence

Unlike a deductive syllogism, this is a defensive, disjunctive case. Each stream defeats the objection on its own; the third stands even if the first two failed. The candid feature of the corpus is that its dominant scholarly voice declines to force a resolution at all.

Stream 1 · Lexical (the day)

  • “Day of preparation” (παρασκευή) = Friday
  • Defined in-text: Mark 15:42; John 19:31, 42
  • “Passover” (πάσχα) = the week-long festival
  • John 18:28 worry = the Chagigah midday meal
  • Carried by Jones & McLatchie

Stream 2 · Timekeeping (the hour)

  • Jewish reckoning (Mark) vs Roman (John)
  • Or: both estimate in quarters of the day
  • Third hour and sixth overlap at midday
  • A contradiction needs A and not-A at once
  • Carried by Huff

Stream 3 · Historical realism

  • Every ancient source contains mistakes
  • Minor error does not discredit
  • Plutarch parallel: condensing & combining
  • The non-Christian source could be wrong
  • Carried by Bauckham

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

“Day of preparation” means preparation for the seder — so John means Thursday

John 19:14 plainly calls it “the day of the preparation of the Passover.” Read at face value, John places the death a day before the Synoptic seder.

Skeptical reading; Ehrman via Ismail

Strongest response

παρασκευή is the standard idiom for Friday

The word is the common first-century term for Friday (preparing for the Sabbath), defined within the very same text — Mark 15:42 and John 19:31, 42. Reading three verses past the one skeptics stop at fixes the day as the day before the Sabbath.

Jones read-the-next-verseMcLatchie in-text definition

Objection 2

John relocated the death to the lamb-slaughter for a theological point

John moves Jesus’ death to the moment the Passover lambs are slaughtered to make a Lamb-of-God point — sacrificing history for theology.

Bart Ehrman, quoted by Yusuf Ismail

Strongest response

“Passover” in John is the week-long festival

The word πάσχα occurs eight other times in John and always denotes the festival as a whole, never the opening seder. Once the festival sense is granted, no relocation of the death is needed and the theological-motive charge dissolves.

McLatchie eight-fold πάσχα count

Objection 3

John 18:28’s defilement worry proves the seder hadn’t happened

The chief priests refuse to enter the praetorium so as not to be defiled but “eat the Passover” — so the seder must still be ahead, contradicting Mark.

Ismail / Ehrman

Strongest response

The worry is the Chagigah, not the seder

There is another ritual meal — the Chagigah — eaten at midday the following day. The defilement concern is about that meal, not the already-completed evening seder. John’s account fits perfectly with Mark’s.

McLatchie festival-mechanics resolution

Objection 4

Mark says 9 a.m., John says noon — the hours don’t match

Mark 15:25 crucifies at the third hour; John 19:14 still has Jesus before Pilate at the sixth. At face value the two cannot both be right.

Standard skeptical

Strongest response

Different reckoning, or ancient approximation

Mark uses Jewish timekeeping (day from 6 a.m.), John Roman (day from midnight) — compensate and they match. Or both estimate in quarters of the day, so Mark’s third hour spans 9 a.m.–noon and John’s sixth reads as “before midday.” Either explanation suffices.

Huff two independent harmonisations

Objection 5

If the Gospels contradict, they’re unreliable

Any genuine discrepancy on so central an event proves the Gospels are not trustworthy reportage.

Implicit / general skeptical framing

Strongest response

A face-value difference is not a contradiction

A logical contradiction requires affirming and denying the same proposition at once — which this is not. And minor error is normal in every ancient source; it brings the Gospels into the company of good ancient history rather than out of it.

Huff law of non-contradictionBauckham source parity

Objection 6

The contradiction is unresolvable — and Mark itself defines the term

Ehrman: reconciliation attempts fail because Mark itself says “day of preparation” means preparation for the Sabbath, conceding John’s wording while denying the chronology can be harmonised.

Bart Ehrman (verbatim, via Ismail)

Partial — the specific philology goes unrebutted

Answered in general, not on Ehrman’s own terms

Bauckham grants that even an unresolved tension does not discredit — error is normal and the non-Christian source could be wrong. But no expert directly dismantles Ehrman’s actual reasoning, and in live debate Ismail simply re-quoted Ehrman without engaging the lexical defence.
