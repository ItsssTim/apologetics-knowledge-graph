The three readings of the millennium

Premillennialism, postmillennialism, amillennialism — which reading of Revelation 20 survives its strongest objections? A single-voice node: two consecutive William Lane Craig lectures that weigh all three and decline to choose.

The question

Of the three classical readings of the thousand years — premillennialism, postmillennialism, amillennialism — which best fits Scripture, and does any survive its strongest objections?

This is an intra-Christian dispute, not a believer-versus-skeptic one. The axis that separates the three positions is the timing of Christ's return relative to the millennium: before it (premillennialism), after it (postmillennialism), or not as a literal earthly period at all (amillennialism). Only premillennialism reads the thousand years of Revelation 20:1–10 literally.

The coverage is single-voice. Both extractions are William Lane Craig, lecturing to a sympathetic adult-education class and following Wayne Grudem's Systematic Theology as his survey source. And — more notably — Craig reaches no verdict. He disclaims expertise and firm opinions and tells the class to make up their own minds, even though his assessments lean mildly against the premillennialist on coherence grounds. That professed neutrality stands in sharp contrast to his settled rejections of the rapture and preterism, and the tension between his accumulated negative verdicts and his refusal to conclude is the most distinctive feature of the node.

The convergent case — three frameworks, weighed against each other

There is no syllogism here and no cross-expert consensus to report — there is barely a second expert. What the corpus supplies instead is a fair-minded internal debate: Craig states each view's best case, then the objections, registering his own running verdicts. Four streams carry the weight. The dominant shapes are internal critique, a reductio (literal kingdom-prophecy entails renewed temple sacrifices, which Hebrews abolishes), and a dilemma (read literally and you get an absurdity; read non-literally and you have conceded the rival view).

The amillennialist case — and the premillennialist replies

The load-bearing exchange of Part 15. Craig steel-mans the amillennialist's five arguments so they “might seem surprisingly strong,” then offers the premillennialist responses — grading several as failures. His verdicts tilt against the premil replies on coherence grounds even as he disclaims a final view.

Amil 1 · reply ungraded

The millennium is taught in only one passage (Rev 20:1–10), and that in a book saturated with apocalyptic symbolism.

Premil reply: the OT pervasively hopes for an earthly kingdom of God, and first- and second-coming prophecies are not clearly distinguished. A separate sub-point cuts against the church-age reading: Rev 20 says Satan is bound, yet Satan is “very much on the prowl” now (1 Peter 5:8; 1 John 5:19). Craig grades neither — he lets the Satan-binding point stand.

Amil 2 · reply rejected

Scripture teaches one resurrection of just and unjust together, not the two a literal Revelation 20 requires (Daniel 12:2; John 5:28–29; Acts 24:15).

Premil reply: John 5 itself describes two resurrections, possibly temporally separated. Craig rejects it: Jesus is describing “one resurrection of two sets of people — what he differentiates is not the resurrections but rather the subjects.” He concedes only the weaker fallback that two resurrections “might be temporarily separated.” (See gaps — he never engages Rev 20:5–6.)

Amil 3 · reply rejected

Mortal sinners living alongside glorified, resurrected saints is “an intolerable thought.”

Premil reply: Christ himself was on earth in his glorified body for 40 days before ascending, so the mixture is not absurd. Craig rejects as off-point: a whole planet of ordinary mortals living among — even married to — glorified immortals “really is quite unlike saying that Jesus was temporarily with the disciples for 40 days.”

Amil 4 · reply rejected

If Christ is present and reigning, how can people persist in sin? The point of the kingdom being established is to do away with sin.

Premil reply: people resisted the earthly Christ and resist the Spirit today, so continuing sin under Christ's presence is not unprecedented. Craig rejects via the humiliation/exaltation distinction (Phil 2): Christ's earthly reign was in his “state of humiliation, not his state of exaltation … not the same as the glorified risen reigning Christ.”

Amil 5 · reply ungraded · no verdict

The millennium serves no purpose — why insert a strange earthly kingdom rather than pass straight to the eternal state?

Premil reply: the millennium shows God's plan for redeeming human society and its social structures — a worthwhile end in itself. Craig leaves this for the listener. He closes the session with explicit non-commitment, having tilted against the premil replies on arguments 2, 3 and 4 without drawing the amillennial conclusion.

Postmillennialism & premillennialism — pro and con

Part 16 weighs the two remaining views. Craig defends postmillennialism against a popular objection, then raises three of his own; against premillennialism he deploys his sharpest single move — the renewed-animal-sacrifices reductio.

Postmil · pro

Christ gave the church a Great Commission it will successfully fulfil, with Scripture indicating a triumphant, world-transforming spread of the kingdom.

Supported by Matthew 28:18–20 and the growth parables — the mustard seed (Matt 13:31–32) and the leaven (Matt 13:33). Postmillennialists use the same anti-literal arguments as amillennialists but add the note of a great triumph through the church's obedience.

Postmil · WLC defends it

The popular charge — that postmillennialism is naively optimistic and was “refuted” by the 20th century — has no scriptural merit.

A deliberate model of charity — Craig defends a view he does not hold. A single century is “a tiny blip” on the timescale of possible church history; in fact the church has grown from “the most ignominious beginnings” into “the largest most successful movement in the history of mankind,” with the last 25 years of the 20th century an “unprecedented” period of global growth.

Postmil · WLC's three objections

But three serious problems remain: the growth parables under-determine size; Scripture predicts the opposite (apostasy); and postmillennialism can only handle the tribulation by adopting preterism.

(1) Mustard-seed and leaven don't say how large the kingdom grows, so a great harvest is compatible with no earthly millennium. (2) An end-times falling away is predicted (2 Timothy 3:1–5; Luke 18:8, “will he find faith on earth?”). (3) The dependency transfer: the tribulation forces postmil into preterism, which Craig has already refuted (Parts 5–6) — so postmil inherits preterism's fatal problem with the resurrection of the dead.

Premil · pro

OT prophecies describe an earthly kingdom still involving mortality, sin and enemies; and believers are promised they will reign with Christ on earth, which is nowhere a present reality.

A kingdom not yet free of death and sin matches a millennium rather than the final sinless state — so John's millennium is “right in line with these old testament prophecies.” And against postmil's “we reign now,” the reign-with-Christ texts (the twelve thrones) describe no present reality.

Premil · the reductio · no verdict

Take the OT kingdom-prophecies literally and you get a rebuilt temple with renewed animal sacrifices — contradicting Hebrews' final sacrifice. “That seems crazy.”

The dilemma's other horn: read the prophecies non-literally and “you've taken a step toward the other views of the millennium.” Craig also reframes the second premil argument: the twelve-thrones saying “certainly envisions that kind of earthly kingdom,” but why not the new heavens and new earth rather than a temporary thousand years? He ends, as in Part 15, with explicit non-commitment — “I don't have any final verdict.”

How the one expert treats it

William Lane Craig 2 transcripts
The fair-minded surveyor who will not commit
Across the two sessions
  • Defines all three views (Part 15): amillennial (Rev 20 non-literal, often the church age); premillennial / chiliasm (Christ returns first, the historic patristic position); postmillennial (Christ returns after a gospel-driven triumph). What separates amil from postmil is triumphalism.
  • Premil ≠ rapture: a load-bearing clarification — “premillennialism is not bound up with rapture theology”; pre-1800s premillennialists held no rapture. Keeps this node distinct from Q-12.2.4.
  • Steel-mans the amillennialist five-argument case so it “might seem surprisingly strong,” then offers the premil replies — rejecting the John 5 two-resurrection reply, the 40-days reply, and the “people resisted Christ” reply.
  • Defends postmillennialism (Part 16) against the “20th century refuted it” objection — though he is not a postmillennialist — then raises three objections of his own (parables under-determine size; predicted apostasy; preterism-dependence).
  • The renewed-animal-sacrifices reductio against literal premillennialism — his sharpest single move across both sessions, delivered with uncharacteristic bluntness.
  • The preterism-dependency transfer: he defeats postmil not on its own terms but by showing it must borrow preterism, then importing that defeat — an economy of argument that requires the listener to have followed Parts 5–6.
Honest finding — the non-commitment

Craig reaches no verdict. He disclaims expertise — “I don't have any final verdict” — and tells the class to make up their own minds. His assessments lean mildly amillennial (he rejects the premil replies on arguments 2, 3, 4 and presses apostasy + preterism-dependence against postmil), but this is a lean, not an endorsement. It contrasts sharply with his settled rejections of the rapture (Q-12.2.4) and preterism (Q-12.2.3). He must not be represented as holding any millennial position.

Density / register note

A WLC-only doctrinal node — two consecutive catechetical lectures to a sympathetic adult-education class, following Wayne Grudem's Systematic Theology as the neutral survey source. There is no cross-expert consensus to report because there is no second expert. The content is, however, unusually apologetic-in-structure: a sustained, fair-minded internal debate weighing each view pro and con. Treat as a single-voice deep dive, not a multi-expert convergence.

Objections handled — the views against one another

Objection 1

The millennium rests on only one passage of a symbolic book

A doctrine found only in Revelation 20:1–10 — in a book of dragons, beasts and bowls of wrath — ought to give serious pause. Why read it literally?

Amillennialist case, argument 1 (vs premil)

Premil reply — ungraded

The OT pervasively hopes for an earthly kingdom

The Jewish hope was for God's kingdom established here on earth, not in an afterlife, and first- and second-coming prophecies are not clearly distinguished — so unfulfilled OT kingdom-prophecy supports an earthly millennium.

Craig reports the reply but does not grade it

Objection 2

Scripture teaches one resurrection, not two

There is a single resurrection of the just and the unjust together at Christ's return (Daniel 12:2; John 5:28–29; Acts 24:15) — not the two that a literal Revelation 20 requires.

Amillennialist case, argument 2 (vs premil)

Premil reply — Craig rejects this form

“One resurrection of two sets of people”

The premil reply appeals to John 5 as describing two resurrections. Craig is “not convinced”: Jesus differentiates the subjects (good vs evil), not the resurrections. He concedes only the weaker fallback that two resurrections “might be temporarily separated.”

Craig Part 15 — rejects the John 5 form

Objection 3

Mortals living among glorified saints is intolerable

After the return the dead in Christ have immortal, incorruptible, sinless bodies. Imagining them in a society with mortal, corruptible sinners “seems inconceivable.”

Amillennialist case, argument 3 (vs premil)

Premil reply — Craig rejects as off-point

Christ's 40 days is not analogous

The premil reply notes Christ spent 40 days on earth in his glorified body. “A fair enough point,” says Craig, “but it really doesn't go to speak to the issue” — a whole planet of mortals among immortals is “quite unlike” Jesus' temporary 40 days.

Craig Part 15 — disanalogy

Objection 4

How can sin persist under a present, reigning Christ?

The whole point of the kingdom of God being established is to do away with sin and with the enemies of God. A reigning king with continuing sin is incoherent.

Amillennialist case, argument 4 (vs premil)

Premil reply — Craig rejects

Humiliation vs exaltation

The premil reply notes people resisted the earthly Christ and resist the Spirit now. Craig: this “fails to convince” — Christ's earthly reign was in his “state of humiliation, not his state of exaltation,” which is “not the same as the glorified risen reigning Christ.”

Craig Part 15 — Phil 2 distinction

Objection 5

The millennium serves no purpose

Why insert a strange earthly kingdom at all, rather than pass straight from resurrection-and-judgment into the eternal state of heaven or hell?

Amillennialist case, argument 5 (vs premil)

Premil reply — ungraded

Redeeming social structures

The millennium shows God's plan for social structures, for redeeming human society — a worthwhile end in itself rather than going straight to the eternal state. Craig leaves this one explicitly “for the listener.”

Craig reports the reply but does not grade it

Objection 6

The present age is the millennium (the church-age reading)

Amillennialists and postmillennialists identify the thousand years with the present church age — the millennium is already under way.

Amil / postmil church-age reading

Premil reply — Craig lets it stand

Satan is not bound now

Revelation 20 says Satan is bound, but 1 Peter 5:8 (“your adversary the devil prowls”) and 1 John 5:19 (“the whole world is in the power of the evil one”) show Satan unbound and the world under his power — so the present age is not the millennium. Tells especially against postmil.

Craig lets the Satan-binding point stand un-rebutted

Objection 7

Postmillennialism is naive — the 20th century refuted it

This view is overly optimistic, a reading of history that “came to a shattering end with the 20th century” — two world wars and the horrors that followed.

Popular objection (vs postmil)

Craig rejects the objection

No scriptural merit — a century is a blip

Defending a view he does not hold: “I'm not persuaded that this kind of argument has any sort of merit.” A century could be “a tiny blip” in the whole scheme; the church is “the largest most successful movement in the history of mankind,” with the last 25 years of the century an “unprecedented” period of growth.

Craig Part 16 — charitable assessment

Objection 8

Scripture predicts end-times apostasy, not triumph

The growth parables don't say how large the kingdom grows; and a good number of passages predict the opposite — a falling away from the truth in the last days.

Craig's own objection (vs postmil); 2 Tim 3:1–5, Luke 18:8

Craig presses it

Apostasy cuts against triumphalism

2 Timothy 3 (“in the last days there will come times of stress”) and Jesus' open question — “when the Son of Man returns will he find faith on earth?” — tell directly against postmil's expectation of a world-transforming triumph before the return.

Craig Part 16 — counter-prophecy

Objection 9

Literal kingdom-prophecy entails renewed animal sacrifices

The OT prophecies, read literally, envision a rebuilt Jerusalem temple where animal sacrifices are once again offered to God.

Craig's own — the reductio (vs literal premil); Hebrews

Strongest response — the dilemma

“That seems crazy” — or you concede the symbolic reading

Hebrews teaches Christ is the final sacrifice who abolished the animal sacrifices permanently. So either read the prophecies literally (absurd: renewed sacrifices) or non-literally — which “takes a step toward the other views of the millennium.” Craig's sharpest single move.

Craig Part 16 — renewed-sacrifices reductio

Objection 10

The tribulation forces postmillennialism into preterism

Postmillennialism “doesn't really deal very well with the tribulation that is going to precede Christ's return” — it must see those predictions fulfilled in the AD 70 destruction of Jerusalem.

Craig's own — dependency transfer (vs postmil)

Strongest response — transferred defeat

Postmil inherits preterism's fatal flaw

To preserve the view, postmil must adopt preterism — which Craig has already refuted (Parts 5–6): it “can't make sense of the biblical data particularly concerning the resurrection of the dead.” So postmil “becomes implausible for the reasons that I already laid out.”

Craig Part 16 — imports the preterism defeat

Objection 11

“Reign with Christ” requires a temporary earthly millennium

Believers are promised they will reign with Christ on earth (the twelve thrones), yet this is nowhere a present reality — so it requires a future earthly millennial kingdom. Tells against postmil's “we reign now.”

Premil argument 2 (vs amil / postmil)

Craig reframes — raised, not pressed

Why not the new earth?

Craig grants it “an interesting argument” and that the twelve-thrones saying “certainly envisions that kind of earthly kingdom” — but asks why it must be this present earth rather than the new heavens and new earth, “rather than just for this thousand-year period.”

Craig Part 16 — new-earth reframing

Persistent gaps in the corpus

Gap 1 — most notable

Revelation 20:5–6 “first resurrection” is never exegeted

The strongest premil textual datum is Rev 20: “the rest of the dead did not come to life until the thousand years were ended … this is the first resurrection,” where the same Greek ezēsan (“came to life”) governs both groups — urged as decisive that two bodily resurrections, separated by a thousand years, are in view.

Premillennial exegesis; the standing flag from the sub-series

Partially closed

He engages only the John 5 form, not the passage that grounds it

Craig finally engages the two-resurrection argument in Part 15 — but rebuts its John 5 version, not its Revelation 20 version. He reads Rev 20 aloud in the opening yet never addresses 20:5–6 in the assessment. So the flag is only partially closed: the argument is now met, but not via the verse that most directly grounds it.

Aggregation gap — carry forward

Gap 2

He adjudicates everything except his own position

Craig has rejected the rapture and preterism, tilts against literal premillennialism (the sacrifices reductio) and against postmil triumphalism (apostasy + preterism-dependence) — which would seem to push him toward amillennialism.

Internal tension across Parts 15–16

Tension — he commits to nothing

Yet he refuses to draw the amillennial conclusion, repeating “I don't have any final verdict.” The tension between his accumulated negative verdicts and his professed neutrality is genuine. Record the non-commitment precisely — the lean is not an endorsement.

Aggregation gap

Gap 3

Several exchanges left ungraded “for the listener”

Amillennialist arguments 1 and 5, and the premil Satan-binding point, receive no positive verdict from Craig.

The internal debate is left open at multiple junctures. This is consistent with the catechetical, “make up your own minds” framing, but it means the node settles fewer of the exchanges than its analytic structure might suggest.

Beyond the premil-≠-rapture clarification, the dispensational distinctives — a separate Israel/church programme, staggered resurrections, distinct judgments — are not treated here.

The “reign with Christ → new earth” point is not pressed

Craig raises the reframing — that the twelve-thrones reign could belong to the eternal new earth rather than a temporary thousand years — but treats it as a question, not a developed argument.
