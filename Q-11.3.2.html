Infant baptism vs believer’s baptism

A genuine intra-Protestant fork — not believer versus sceptic, but Christian versus Christian over the proper subject of baptism. William Lane Craig steel-mans the paedobaptist case, then answers it.

The question

Is baptism rightly given to the infant children of believers, or only to those who have themselves consciously professed faith?

This is a genuine intra-Protestant fork, not a believer-versus-sceptic dispute. The disputants all affirm baptism; they divide over its proper subject. On one side, the paedobaptists — Catholic, Reformed, and Lutheran churches that baptise the infants of believing families. On the other, the credobaptists (believer-baptists) — Baptist and free-church traditions that reserve the rite for those who have consciously believed.

The fork is doubly subtle because it crosses a second variable: what baptism is taken to accomplish. A church may hold it sacramental (it conveys regenerating grace) or non-sacramental (a sign or seal of a grace received by faith). Subject and efficacy are independent axes — and, as WLC shows, the most consequential question in this node is what happens when paedobaptism and sacramentalism are combined.

The argument structure — two cumulative cases, head-on

This is not one shared syllogism but two cumulative cases pointed at each other. WLC first stacks the paedobaptist case “as fairly and convincingly as I can,” then answers it stream-by-stream and lays his own positive case alongside. Each strand is contested independently.

The paedobaptist case · engaged

  • Household solidarity — the family is baptised as a unit (Acts 16:30–33)
  • Jesus welcomes the children — “to such belongs the kingdom” (Mark 10:13–16)
  • Circumcision–baptism parallel — the new-covenant sign succeeds the old (Col 2:11–12)
  • Infant / surrogate faith + monergistic regeneration (Luther, Calvin; Anglican rite)

The credobaptist response · WLC

  • No infant baptism recorded anywhere in the NT — an argument from silence
  • These are children showing faith, not infants; blessing is not baptism
  • You enter the covenant by faith, not a rite; Col 2 “circumcision” = Christ’s death
  • Confession and faith are prerequisites (Acts 2:38; 1 Pet 3:21) an infant cannot meet

The second axis · efficacy

  • Sacramental — baptism conveys regenerating grace (Catholic)
  • Covenantal / non-sacramental — a sign or seal of covenant membership (Reformed)
  • WLC’s matrix: either practice alone is survivable
  • The combination — sacramentalism × paedobaptism — is “disastrous”

The dominant voice — steel-man, then rebut
Method & structure
  • Part 6 presents the case for infant baptism “as fairly and convincingly as I can,” bracketing his verdict; Part 7 delivers the believer’s-baptism case and rebuts each paedobaptist argument in the same order. One argument, split across a week.
  • The organising move — a two-axis analysis. He distinguishes paedobaptist views by what baptism effects: the salvific / regenerational version (Catholic) vs the covenantal sign / seal version (Reformed). This distinction is load-bearing for his verdict.
The four arguments he steel-mans (Part 6)
  • Household solidarity — the family is a unit; the jailer “baptized at once with all his family” (Acts 16).
  • Jesus and the children — “to such belongs the kingdom of God” (Mark 10); bring them in baptism.
  • Circumcision–baptism parallel (the distinctively Reformed argument) — the new-covenant sign succeeds the old (Col 2).
  • Infant faith + monergism — regeneration precedes faith and works “apart from the knowledge and will of the infant”; Luther / Calvin affirm infant faith; the Anglican rite supplies parental “surrogate faith.”
His positive case (Part 7)
  • Confession and faith are essential to salvation and so to baptism; Acts 2:38 “repent and be baptized” is something an infant “simply cannot do.”
  • Baptism is “an appeal to God for a clear conscience” (1 Peter 3:21) — a conscious decision an infant cannot make.
  • Precision: the alternative is not adult baptism but believer’s baptism — legitimate candidates may include children who have consciously believed, never infants.
Signature move — the two-variable matrix

Either practice alone is survivable: a sacramentalist who baptises only believers (he names G.R. Beasley-Murray) does no great harm; a non-sacramentalist who baptises infants treats baptism as a sign, so the infant must still later believe. It is the combination — sacramentalism with paedobaptism — that is “disastrous”: a church filled with people who falsely think a ritual done to them as infants made them regenerate.

Verdict (recorded precisely)

Believer’s baptism, non-sacramentalist. Paedobaptism is “very weak”; the confession-and-faith argument is “powerful.” Legitimate candidates may be children who have consciously believed — never infants with no conscious faith.

The four arguments, answered — the paedobaptist case engaged

Here the “objections” are the four paedobaptist arguments — the position being engaged. WLC steel-mans each in Part 6 and rebuts each in Part 7, in the same order.

Argument 1

Household solidarity — the family baptised as a unit

In the NT the family is treated as a unit; when the head turns to Christ the whole household is baptised — which would include infants. The Philippian jailer was “baptized at once with all his family” (Acts 16:30–33).

Reformed & Catholic paedobaptism · steel-manned by WLC (Part 6)

WLC’s rebuttal (Part 7)

Individual retribution + an argument from silence

Even in the OT the law of individual retribution stands (Ezekiel 18; Jeremiah) — household membership does not override personal responsibility. The gospel in fact divided households (1 Cor 7:12–16). Decisively: there is no baptism of infants recorded anywhere in the NT — the case rests on silence.

Craig Ezek 18 / 1 Cor 7 + the silence argument

Argument 2

Jesus welcomes the children (Mark 10:13–16)

Jesus rebuked the disciples for hindering the children, took them in his arms, blessed them, and said “to such belongs the kingdom of God.” This encourages bringing little children to Christ — which one might do in baptism.

Paedobaptism generally · steel-manned by WLC (Part 6)

WLC’s rebuttal (Part 7)

Children showing faith — and blessing is not baptism

These “seem to be little children who are exhibiting love and faith in jesus,” not infants; the lesson is childlike trust, not infant baptism. And even if infants were brought, prayer and blessing are not the same as baptism — nothing suggests they were to be baptised.

Craig faith-of-the-children reading + blessing ≠ baptism

Argument 3

Circumcision–baptism covenant parallel (Col 2:11–12)

Circumcision was the old-covenant sign, applied to infants days after birth; baptism is the new-covenant sign. Just as covenant infants received the old sign, infants of believing families should receive the new — the distinctively Reformed argument.

Reformed paedobaptism · developed by WLC (Part 6); granted by Knechtle

WLC’s rebuttal (Part 7)

Covenant entry is by faith; and the type is Christ’s death

One enters and stays in the covenant by faith, not by a rite. Empirically the parallel fails — Jewish believers kept both circumcision and baptism on their children. Exegetically, in Col 2 what corresponds to circumcision is Christ’s death (“putting away the body of flesh in the circumcision of christ”), not infant baptism.

Craig covenant-by-faith + re-assigned typology

Argument 4

Infant / surrogate faith + monergistic regeneration

On the Reformed ordo, regeneration precedes faith and works “apart from our knowledge and will,” so baptising an infant simply extends what happens to every believer. Luther and Calvin held infants do exercise faith; the Anglican rite has parents answer as a “surrogate faith.”

Luther, Calvin; Reformed / Lutheran theology; Anglican liturgy · steel-manned by WLC (Part 6)

WLC’s rebuttal (Part 7)

Faith is a prerequisite an infant cannot meet

“Repentance and faith are prerequisites for christian baptism”; an infant “cannot exercise confession and faith” (Acts 2:38; 1 Pet 3:21), so is not a legitimate candidate. The claim of infant / surrogate faith is rejected for failing the personal-profession condition — though by direct denial rather than by engaging Luther’s and Calvin’s case on its own terms (see gaps).

Craig Acts 2:38 / 1 Pet 3:21 prerequisite

Persistent gaps in the corpus

Unusually symmetrical here: a one-expert steel-man-then-rebut leaves the paedobaptist rejoinders un-voiced. These are points the corpus does not adequately settle.

Gap 1

The argument from silence cuts both ways

WLC presses “no baptism of infants recorded anywhere” as decisive — but the household-baptism texts (Acts 16:15, 16:33; 1 Cor 1:16) are silent the other way too. They neither include nor exclude infants.

Paedobaptist rejoinder (un-voiced in corpus)

Gap — symmetry not addressed

The silence he wields is the silence the paedobaptist wields

The argument from silence is deployed against paedobaptism without confronting the obvious reply that it cuts equally for it. WLC does not address the symmetry.

Aggregation gap

Gap 2

The broader covenant-theology framework is bypassed

The Reformed paedobaptist grounds the circumcision parallel in a new-covenant-membership theology (Jer 31 / Heb 8). WLC rebuts the Col 2 verse but treats “you enter the covenant by faith” as settled rather than argued.

Reformed covenant theology (un-engaged)

Gap — framework not engaged

Bypassed, not confronted

The exegetical rebuttal of one proof-text does not reach the systematic framework that motivates the parallel. The covenant-membership theology is never met head-on.

Aggregation gap

Gap 3

“Infant faith” is denied, not refuted

The fourth paedobaptist argument — that infants do in some sense exercise faith (Luther, Calvin) — is met by the bare assertion that “an infant cannot exercise confession and faith.”

Luther / Calvin’s case for infant faith (un-engaged)

Gap — answered by assertion

The contradicted premise is restated, not argued

WLC does not engage Luther’s or Calvin’s actual case for infant faith; he restates the credobaptist premise it contradicts. The dispute is decided by definition, not by argument.

Gap 4

Mode of baptism is untouched

Immersion versus sprinkling / pouring — the third great baptismal dispute — is never addressed in either lecture.

Mode dispute (out of scope here)

Gap — not in scope
