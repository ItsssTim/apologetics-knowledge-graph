Do humans have free will?

Not a proof of God so much as a pressure point against naturalism — four experts run a multi-stream case that free will is undeniable in practice, grounded in a soul, and that consistent determinism is self-undermining and unliveable.

The question

Do humans possess genuine freedom of the will — and if naturalism is true, can that freedom survive?

The apologists in this corpus rarely try to prove libertarian free will. They run a two-handed strategy: free will is a datum of lived experience that no one actually surrenders, and consistent naturalistic determinism is self-undermining — it vacates the very moral judgements and rational claims its defenders go on making.

The argument is therefore defensive and parasitic by design: it lets the determinist’s own moral indignation, rational claims, and lived practice become the rebuttal of his metaphysics. Downstream it is load-bearing — the free-will defence, the moral argument (ought-implies-can), the argument from reason, and the doctrines of sovereignty-and-freedom all inherit from it.

This is the whether question. Its technical sibling Q-17.1.2 asks which account of freedom survives — and carries the two pieces of distinctive machinery (Fischer’s semicompatibilism, and the full two-pronged Libet rebuttal) that this page deliberately cross-references rather than re-litigates. What this page adds, beyond the original treatment, is Craig’s soul-grounding from the Doctrine of Man lectures: why free will is thought to need a soul, and what it costs theology to deny one.

The argument structure — multi-stream, not one syllogism

The corpus does not converge on a single syllogism. It converges on a strategy — undermine determinism from several independent angles — across five distinct streams. Each is contested separately; the cumulative effect is that hard determinism becomes very expensive to hold. Craig’s ought-implies-can critique (Stream C) and his soul-grounding (Stream D) are the dominant deployments.

Stream A · Lived practice

  • “No one lives as if free will doesn’t exist” (Lennox)
  • Holding others responsible presupposes their choice (Knechtle)
  • Reasoned deliberation is neither randomness nor compulsion
  • Without freedom there is no love (Lennox)

Stream B · Self-undermining

  • “How would you know it was determined if it was determined?”
  • Pre-programmed utterances would be meaningless
  • Genetic-fallacy diagnosis (Lennox)
  • Laplacian predictability “went with quantum mechanics”

Stream C · Moral collapse

  • Ought-implies-can: no control = no responsibility
  • “Puppets have no moral obligations” (Craig)
  • Coyne & Harris keep moralising while denying responsibility
  • Causal closure → electrochemical machine (vs Wielenberg)

Stream D · Soul-grounding

  • Materialism makes libertarian freedom impossible (Craig)
  • “Just electrochemical machines” — Quine’s irritation of the surfaces
  • Deny the soul & four doctrines collapse (God, free will, resurrection, Incarnation)
  • van Inwagen: a Christian materialist concedes freedom is unintelligible

Stream E · Reconciliation

  • Libertarian vs compatibilist conceptions
  • Molinism — middle knowledge
  • Simple foreknowledge
  • Freedom grounds love & relationship by design

How each expert deploys it

William Lane Craig6 transcripts
Architect of the internal critique — and the soul-grounding
Distinctive moves
  • In debate, rarely argues for libertarian freedom — runs internal critique via endnote-mining and lived inconsistency.
  • Ought-implies-can: “a person is not morally responsible for an action which he is unable to avoid” (the shove illustration).
  • The kill-shot: Harris admits in his own endnotes that moral responsibility is “a social construct, not an objective reality.”
  • Let the indignation be the rebuttal — catalogues every moral judgement Coyne & Harris make (jihad, Hirsi Ali, the Catholic Church, the Taliban parent), then voids it: “robots are not moral agents.”
  • Tiger Woods equivocation — Harris’s “should” is a capability-given-machine “should,” not a moral one.
  • Wielenberg variant: causal closure + Humean bundle-self = “electrochemical machine” with no enduring moral subject.
The soul-grounding (new — Doctrine of Man)
  • Why materialism precludes freedom: “if we are just physical electrochemical machines then there isn’t any room for free agency” — everything is fixed by Quine’s “irritation of your surfaces.”
  • Four-consequence reductio — deny the soul and you lose the doctrine of God (an unembodied mind), free will, the resurrection (a replica, not you), and the Incarnation.
  • Duplicate-podium device for the personal-identity point; the soul is what “ensures personal identity with the person who died.”
  • Libet (Part 11) turns the brain-before-awareness lag into confirmation of the soul-uses-brain model — fully treated at Q-17.1.2.
Tactical signature

Craig flags that the opponent never answers: “the ought implies can problem — none of these have been responded to. So if you want a really desperate moral system, try atheism.” The silence is part of the argument. And the soul-grounding prices out materialism rather than only arguing for dualism — even a reader unmoved by the biblical case feels the systematic pressure.

John Lennox2 transcripts
Transcendental + lived-practice case
Distinctive moves
  • Self-undermining reductio: “How would you know it was determined if it was determined?” Pre-programmed utterances would be meaningless.
  • Genetic-fallacy diagnosis (named explicitly): denying free will commits the same error as dismissing a belief by its causal history.
  • Lived practice: “No one lives as if free will doesn’t exist.”
  • Physics: the Laplacian predictability model “went with quantum mechanics.”
  • Axiology: “If there were no free will then there’d be no such thing as love.”
  • Hostile witness: piggy-backs on naturalist neuroscientist Peter Tse’s indeterminism case + Polkinghorne.
Self-positioning

“I am not a determinist — not an atheist determinist, but neither am I a Christian determinist.” Grounds freedom in Genesis and in Jesus’s lament over Jerusalem, where even God respects the creature’s no.

Cliffe Knechtle1 transcript
Folk lived-experience deployment
Signature moves
  • False-dichotomy move: choosing is neither blind randomness nor compulsion — “I consult my experience, my taste buds, health issues — I use my reason.”
  • Responsibility presupposes freedom: “you hold others responsible — that’s why some relationships blow up in animosity and others blossom with love.”
  • Soul inference (folk Stream D): “the only way there can be a soul and a real self is if there is more to reality than matter and energy.”
Known weakness

Treats materialism as straightforwardly entailing hard determinism and never engages compatibilism — the exact position his interlocutor Tom Jump holds (“we can make choices without having free will”). He fights the harder target and leaves the live one untouched.

Max Baker-Hytch1 transcript (McLatchie panel)
Foreknowledge reconciliation
Distinctive deployment
  • The only voice treating Stream E — clean philosophical taxonomy.
  • Two conceptions: libertarian (“power to do otherwise”) vs compatibilist (“so long as you do what you want to do”).
  • Molinism — God’s middle knowledge of “what we freely would do in any possible situation,” combined with which situations obtain, deduces the future without causal predetermination.
  • Simple foreknowledge — God “just knows in a kind of immediate fashion” with “no mechanism we can unpack.”
  • Stevenson’s relational supplement: God “wants us to love him through choice.”

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

Every choice is either determined or random — both are unfree

An exhaustive dilemma: a choice is “either determined by previous reasons, which is determinism, or not determined by anything, in which case by definition random.” Neither is free.

Tom Jump (vs Knechtle)

Weakly answered

False dichotomy — reframed, not defeated

Knechtle: reasoned deliberation (consulting experience, taste, health) is a third option, neither randomness nor “I have no choice.” But he reframes the dilemma rather than rebutting its logic — he never shows why reasons-responsiveness escapes both horns.

Knechtle false-dichotomy reframeRandomness horn never defeated

Objection 2

Compatibilism — we make choices without libertarian free will

“It’s compatibilism — we can make choices without having free will.” Choice and determinism are compatible; nothing in materialism rules out meaningful deliberation.

Tom Jump, Daniel Dennett; described neutrally by Baker-Hytch

Engaged at Q-17.1.2, not here

The substantive engagement lives next door

On this page Craig dismisses it (Harris and Wielenberg reject it anyway) and Knechtle never addresses it. The corpus’s real engagement — Fischer’s semicompatibilism and the ledger-vs-accountability critique — is carried at Q-17.1.2, and even there is not met on its strongest Frankfurt / Strawson merits.

See Q-17.1.2

Objection 3

Determinism is forced on us by physics and neuroscience

fMRI readiness-potential studies predict decisions in advance; Sapolsky, Libet and Soon are cited as showing the brain decides before “you” do.

Tom Jump, popular determinism

Partial response

Quantum mechanics killed Laplacian predictability; Libet turned

Lennox: the total-predictability model “went with quantum mechanics,” and borrows naturalist Peter Tse’s own indeterminism case as hostile witness. Craig (Part 11) turns the classic Libet result into confirmation of dualism (full treatment at Q-17.1.2). But no expert engages the Soon et al. / Schurger successor literature (Q-5.6.8).

Lennox physics + TseCraig Libet reframeSoon / Schurger unrebutted

Objection 4

Moral responsibility is a social construct, not objective

“In neuroscientific terms, no person is more or less responsible than any other for the actions they perform” — so moral responsibility is a useful fiction, not a real feature of the world.

Sam Harris (endnotes), Jerry Coyne

Strongest response

Ought-implies-can, turned on the speaker

Craig: if no one can do otherwise, no one is responsible — yet Coyne and Harris fill their interviews with moral judgements, all of which their own metaphysics vacates. “Puppets do not have moral responsibilities.”

Craig endnote-mining + lived inconsistency

Objection 5

Behaviour modification still works under determinism

Determinism doesn’t preclude punishment-as-deterrent, rehabilitation, or behaviour modification — you can still “reprogram the robots” without invoking moral responsibility.

Jerry Coyne, Sam Harris

Strongest response

Then morality is gone — only engineering remains

Kevin Harris (with Craig): if it is all reprogramming, “morality is gone completely out the window — just a matter of making the machines function in a different way.” A Hitler or Stalin in charge of the reprogramming has done nothing “wrong” on this view.

Kevin Harris / Craig reductio on reprogramming

Objection 6

Introspection shows the self dissolves

In close attention to his mental life, Harris finds thoughts “spring into view” without his choosing them — undermining any sense of authoring his actions.

Sam Harris (“noticing mode”)

Strongest response

The observer smuggles back the transcendental ego

Kevin Harris: Harris’s very capacity to “review the influences” and “pick and choose what’s going to influence me” presupposes a self that “stands above the train of experiences and surveys them” — the ego he is trying to deny.

Kevin Harris / Craig transcendental-ego reply

Objection 7

A Christian can be a materialist about humans and still keep God and free will

Nancy Murphy: “I’m a materialist” but “I make an exception in God’s case.” Peter van Inwagen: a Christian materialist who affirms libertarian freedom outright while granting he cannot reconcile it with his materialism.

Nancy Murphy, Peter van Inwagen (Christian materialists)

Strongest response

Special pleading, and a hostile witness against materialism

Craig: Murphy’s God-exemption is “a rather ad-hoc move” — if God can be an unembodied soul, why not created souls in His image? And van Inwagen affirming freedom while conceding it is unintelligible on his own view is itself a tell: “if you can’t make sense of free will on a materialist anthropology then perhaps this ought to lead us to question physicalism.”

Craig ad-hoc charge + van Inwagen hostile witness

Objection 8

Deny the soul and you can keep the rest of Christian theology

A Christian could be a physicalist about human persons and still hold orthodox doctrine — the soul is dispensable theological furniture.

Implicit Christian-materialist position

Strongest response

Four-consequence reductio — the cost is systematic

Craig: denying the soul undermines the doctrine of God (an unembodied mind), free will, the resurrection (God raises a replica, not you — the duplicate-podium device), and the Incarnation (the Logos needs an immaterial constituent to assume). The price is the whole system, not one doctrine.

Craig “price out materialism” structure

Objection 9

Causal closure does not entail epiphenomenalism

“Any physical event that has a cause has a complete physical cause” renders mental states inert only if you add that no mental state is also a physical state — an identity-theory premise Craig “simply asserts.”

Erik Wielenberg

Live dispute — unresolved

Closure + bundle-self vs identity theory

Craig: closure plus a Humean bundle-self leaves “no enduring subject who can be held morally accountable” — an electrochemical machine. Wielenberg replies that reductive / identity theories allow mental causation and the philosophy of mind is “far from settled” in Craig’s favour. Neither side closes it.

Craig closure + bundle reductioWielenberg identity-theory rebuttal

Objection 10

Substance dualism has the very same problem

“No one has the slightest inkling as to how an immaterial soul can supposedly possess intentionality and agency.” If matter can’t ground agency, neither can a soul — it’s “nothing more than a wild guess.”

Erik Wielenberg

Gap — unanswered

The symmetry charge goes unmet

Craig does not reply to the dualism-symmetry objection in this transcript. The soul-grounding case (Stream D) asserts the soul as the seat of free agency but supplies no positive mechanism — it relocates the mystery rather than dissolving it.

Aggregation gap

Objection 11

How is libertarian freedom compatible with God’s foreknowledge?

If God infallibly knows what you will do, then you cannot do otherwise — so foreknowledge and libertarian freedom seem incompatible.

Standard theological objection

Strongest response

Molinism or simple foreknowledge

Baker-Hytch: God’s middle knowledge of what we would freely do in any situation, combined with which situations obtain, deduces the future — which “really is flowing from us and is not causally predetermined.” Or simple foreknowledge: an unlimited being need not run an inferential procedure.

Baker-Hytch Molinism / simple-foreknowledge taxonomy

Persistent gaps in the corpus

Where the corpus is honestly weak — the objections it does not adequately resolve on this page.

Gap 1 · Compatibilism at full strength

The live naturalist position is not defeated here

Craig waves compatibilism away because his opponents reject it; Knechtle treats materialism as entailing hard determinism and misses the target. The substantive engagement (Fischer, ledger-vs-accountability) lives at Q-17.1.2 — and even there is not met on Frankfurt / Strawson grounds.

Gap 2 · Libet’s successors

Calibrated to ~550ms, not to Soon / Schurger

Craig (Part 11) engages only Libet’s classic readiness-potential. The dualist time-lag reply scales for ~550ms (plausibly signal-propagation) but is strained by the Soon et al. ~7–10-second window, and does not touch Schurger’s stochastic-accumulation reinterpretation. Sound against the named experiment, weaker against the literature it omits.

Gap 3 · The randomness horn

Reframed, never logically defeated

The apologists assert reasoned deliberation is a third option but never show why reasons-responsiveness escapes the determined-or-random dilemma rather than collapsing into a horn. Craig names the agent-causation solution at Q-17.1.2 but never develops it.

Gap 4 · The dualism-symmetry charge

The soul-grounding relocates the mystery

Wielenberg’s charge — no one knows how a soul grounds agency either — goes unanswered. Stream D asserts the soul as the seat of free agency without the positive account the symmetry objection demands.

Gap 5 · The constitution view

The replica argument targets the cruder physicalism

Craig’s four-consequence reductio assumes “you just are your body.” Lynne Baker’s “constituted by but not identical to your body” is neither named nor engaged — and on it the replica/duplicate-podium argument is arguably begged.

Gap 6 · The atheist internal tension

Noted as a rhetorical gift, not theorised

Aron Ra holds a “percentage” view yet says he feels “fully determined”; Coyne and Harris moralise while denying responsibility. The corpus flags these splits but never develops why determinism characteristically produces them, nor turns them into a wedge.
