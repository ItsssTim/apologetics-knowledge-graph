The question

Are the moral values modern secular Westerners take for granted a Christian inheritance — or do they have an independent source?

Universal human dignity, compassion for the weak, the wrongness of slavery, the equal worth of women: this is the apologetic deployment of Tom Holland’s Dominion thesis (and Glenn Scrivener’s The Air We Breathe) — that secular humanism is, in Knechtle’s phrase, “Christianity light.” It keeps the Christian values while discarding the Christian story that grounds them.

The argument is overwhelmingly genealogical and abductive, not deductive, and it leans hard on hostile witnesses — Holland, Habermas, Ehrman, and Parris are all non-Christians conceding the Christian root. Its hidden fault line: as history it claims Christianity caused the moral revolution; as metaphysics it claims only the Christian story can ground those values. Several experts blur the two, and the strongest objections live in that seam.

The argument structure — converging evidence streams

This is a cumulative historical case, not a syllogism. No two experts run identical premises, but the shared shape holds: the pre-Christian world lacked these values; Christianity introduced them through documented practice; the modern secularist retains them while sawing off the branch they grow on.

Stream A · Slavery

  • Paul calls slave & master “brothers” (Philemon)
  • Galatians 3:28 / Colossians 3:11 — “no slave nor free”
  • Christians buy slaves to set them free
  • Basil, the Quakers, Wilberforce, Granville Sharp
  • Abolition as the late fruit of the Christian root

Stream B · Plague & the weak

  • 3rd-century Christians stay and nurse the dying
  • “Most who could simply fled”
  • Self-sacrifice for outsiders, at risk of life
  • The ancient world despised the poor
  • Compassion-as-an-idea (Ehrman)

Stream C · Women & dignity

  • Pre-Christian: “women were dirt, kids were dirt”
  • Imago Dei → intrinsic, unearned worth
  • Egalitarian transformation of Rome
  • Habermas: source of human rights & democracy
  • Forgiveness from the Cross of Christ

How each expert deploys it

Richard Bauckhamvia Knechtle interview
The historian’s measured genealogy — dominant voice
Distinctive moves
  • Early Christianity was potentially inclusive — “open to the inclusion of everyone” though it “didn’t actually include everyone obviously.” A disposition, not a finished achievement.
  • The third-century plague as a concrete, documented anchor: Christians nursed the dying “at the risk of their own lives” while others fled.
  • Slavery changed from the inside — no first-century abolition campaign, but Paul calls slave and master “brothers,” and later Christians bought slaves to free them.
  • Abolition is “the root of the Christian tradition… very much later.”
Signature move — the candid concession

“Along the way there were Christians who defended slavery; the Christian tradition is very varied.” Owning the dark chapters is integral — the genealogical argument is only credible when it concedes the worst while holding the core.

Wes Huff2 transcripts
The metaphysical upgrade — only Christianity can ground dignity
Distinctive moves
  • Pushes past Holland’s historical claim to a grounding claim: dignity “don’t spawn from natural materialistic evolution.”
  • Hostile-witness stack: John Gray (Darwinism undercuts truth-knowing), Solovyov (“descended from apes, therefore love one another”), Keller (selection logic licenses hate as readily as love).

William Lane Craig2 transcripts
“Parasitic upon Western Christianity”
Distinctive move
  • Deploys the argument as internal critique of Coyne and Harris, who invoke “Enlightenment values” to attack Islam but cannot ground them on naturalism.
  • “Where do you think the Enlightenment got its values? They came from the Christian worldview.”

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

Christianity defended slavery / has a bad track record

For centuries Christians justified slavery; some key abolitionists (Paine, Franklin) were deists, not Christians. The genealogy launders a bloody record.

Christopher Hitchens; implicit throughout

Strongest response

Concede the dark chapters, hold the root

Own it outright — “the Christian tradition is very varied” — while showing the root of abolition is Christian (Basil, Quakers, Wilberforce). The concession is what makes the argument credible.

Objection 3

Secularisation drove gender egalitarianism — not Christianity

The most secular societies (the Nordic countries) have the best gender outcomes; intensely religious societies tend to be misogynistic. The Enlightenment, not the Cross, drove the gains.

Ben Burgis

The available reply — the Nordic countries are post-Christian, living on inherited capital — is never made. The direct empirical challenge stands.

Objection 4

The Roman Empire wasn’t secular — it had many gods

If pre-Christian Rome was deeply religious, then “too secular” was never the problem — so secularism per se isn’t the variable the genealogy needs.

Ben Burgis

Gap — unaddressed

Not engaged

A clean disanalogy the corpus simply does not answer. The genealogical claim is about Christianity vs paganism, not religion vs secularism, but no expert clarifies this in response.

Objection 7

Humanist values can be grounded on flourishing without God

Moral norms can be derived from human flourishing or social contract (Harris, Singer) — no theistic premise required.

Gap — treated as settled

Hostile witnesses stacked, counter not met

Objection 8

Atheism is only a denial of the supernatural — it owes no positive hope

“Being an atheist is only to say that one doesn’t believe there is a supernatural dimension.” It carries no moral philosophy to indict and no debt to repay.

Christopher Hitchens

Unresolved standoff

Guthrie’s “philosophy of the strong” stands

Guthrie’s challenge — what does anti-theism offer the weak? — lands; Hitchens concedes atheism offers no positive hope but denies it owes any. The exchange ends in standoff, not refutation.

Guthrie internal-critique challenge

Objection 9

Historical cause ≠ metaphysical ground

Holland argues Christianity historically produced these values. Huff and Craig also claim only Christianity can ground them. An opponent can grant the first and deny the second — or vice versa.

Aggregator (latent tension)

Gap — conflated, never separated

No expert cleanly distinguishes the two claims

The corpus slides between the genealogical and grounding claims. Huff in particular wants both and presents the atheist witnesses’ concession of one as if it settled the other.
