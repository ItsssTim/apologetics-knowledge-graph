The question

Once you grant that the church recognised rather than imposed the canon, what tests did it apply — and were they principled or just the politics of the winners?

This is the criteria question sitting one level beneath the “lost gospels” debate (Q-4.3.6) and the “recognition, not imposition” thesis (Q-4.3.1 / Q-4.3.2). If the church simply received what it was handed, a sceptic can still press: by what standard did it sort an apostolic gospel from a Gnostic one — and isn’t any such standard reverse-engineered from the books it had already decided to keep?

The apologetic answer names three historic criteria — apostolicity (does it trace to an apostle or a known associate?), catholicity (is it received across the universal church?), and orthodoxy (does it teach what was handed down, without embellishment?) — and argues these were recognition tests, not selection tests. They are shown doing real work: explaining why the four gospels were never in question, why two near-misses (the Shepherd of Hermas, the Epistle of Barnabas) fell short on a single criterion, and why the apocryphal contenders (Thomas, Peter) fail all three.

The convergent argument — a three-criterion screen

This is a single-expert node, so “convergent” means the stable framework Wes Huff repeats across four years (2016–2023), not agreement between rival voices. Read it as a criterion / cumulative case: the three tests are applied jointly, and a book has to clear all three to be recognised as canonical. Huff’s signature move is to define each term by its Greek etymology, so that a lay audience does not hear “catholicity” and “orthodoxy” as “the Roman Catholic and Eastern Orthodox churches decided.”

Premise 1

Apostolicity — does it trace to an apostle, or to someone who knew an apostle?

The first flag. “Can we trace it back as early as we can go into the early Jesus community?” Mark→Peter and Luke→Paul satisfy the “or someone who knew an apostle” clause. This is the criterion that does most of the disqualifying work — it is the single leg on which the Shepherd of Hermas and the Epistle of Barnabas fall.

Premise 2

Catholicity — is it received across the universal church, not just one faction?

Greek katholikos, not Roman Catholic. “A compound word from kata meaning concerning or about and holos meaning the whole — in other words, universal.” The test looks across the breadth of the Christian world, at the apostolic foundations like Antioch and Ephesus. “We believe in a holy catholic church — that’s a small-c catholic; we’re not talking about the Roman Catholic Church.”

Premise 3

Orthodoxy — does it teach the rule of faith handed down, with no known embellishment?

Greek orthodoxia, not Eastern Orthodox. Orthos (straight / right) + doxa (opinion) = “the right opinion, the right teaching... AKA, are there known embellishments?” For this leg Huff simply reads the texts aloud — Thomas’s logion 114, Peter’s 90-foot Jesus — and lets them disqualify themselves.

Premise 4

These are recognition tests, not a menu the church chose from.

The anachronism point. Ask a second-century Christian “why did you choose Matthew, Mark, Luke and John?” and “they would look at you strangely and say choose? We didn’t choose any of these — they were the ones handed down to us.” The criteria articulate, after the fact, why inherited books carried authority. Grounded in covenant theology: every prior covenant came with written documentation, so a new covenant generating new texts is a natural progression.

Conclusion

Books that clear all three were never in serious question; those that fail one (Hermas, Barnabas) or all three (Thomas, Peter) are excluded on objective grounds, not by power-play.

The principled verdict. The four gospels and Paul’s letters clear apostolicity, catholicity and orthodoxy and “were never in question.” The contenders fail the historical-theological tests the church already used to recognise authoritative texts — so their exclusion is principled, not the politics of the winners.

The screen applied — four worked examples

The criteria are deployed negatively — to disqualify contenders — far more often than positively, and the worked examples are where the framework earns its keep. The Hermas and Barnabas cases are the clever ones: Huff concedes that both passed catholicity and orthodoxy and failed only apostolicity, showing the criteria discriminate rather than functioning as a blunt instrument.

Near-miss

Shepherd of Hermas — fails apostolicity alone.

Passes catholicity and orthodoxy. The Muratorian Fragment names a recent author: Hermas wrote it “very recently, in our times, in the city of Rome, while Bishop Pius his brother was occupying the chair.” “We know the guy who wrote it, so it’s not really scripture” — out on the first criterion.

Near-miss

Epistle of Barnabas — fails apostolicity alone.

Passes catholicity and orthodoxy. The chain to Paul’s companion Barnabas is unreliable — “a friend of a friend of mine told me.” “The early church recognised this... it’s not a reliable chain, so it too failed the first criterion. Interestingly enough, it passed catholicity and orthodoxy.”

Contender

Gospel of Thomas — fails all three.

Apostolicity: a second-century gnostic text; “Thomas was dead by the time the Gospel of Thomas was written.” Catholicity: “not accepted by the wide-range universal church” and condemned whenever the fathers cite it. Orthodoxy: logion 114 — Jesus makes Mary male to enter the kingdom. “We’ve already flown way off the rails... it fails all three criteria.”

Contender

Gospel of Peter — fails all three.

Apostolicity: late — “it doesn’t fit the script for going back to either an apostle or someone who knew an apostle.” Catholicity: “it wasn’t accepted by the universal church.” Orthodoxy: “all these crazy docetic heresies” — a 90-foot Jesus and a talking cross at the resurrection. (Huff dates it 4th c.; scholarly consensus is mid-to-late 2nd c. — see footer note.)

Signature moves
  • The etymology inoculation — the most recognisable habit on this node. Catholicity “has nothing to do with the Roman Catholic Church” (katholikos, universal); orthodoxy “has nothing to do with the Greek Orthodox church” (orthos + doxa, right teaching). Extended to the framing terms: canon = kanon, “rule or list” (“canon with one n is a ruler / list; cannon with two ns goes boom”); apocryphal = “strange, foreign.”
  • Recognition screen, not selection menu — “in one way there was no formal criteria, because it wasn’t a choosing as much as it was a recognising.” The criteria articulate why inherited books carried authority.
  • Covenant-theology grounding (Dig & Delve) — every divine covenant came with written documentation (Sinai→tablets; the prophets→“write this down”); the Old Testament was “a story in search of a conclusion” (N. T. Wright); the new covenant (2 Cor 3:6, “ministers of the new covenant”) naturally generates new written texts. The deepest layer of his case.
  • The Mark/Luke authorial-appropriation point — “there is no reason to put Mark’s name on Mark and Luke’s name on Luke unless that’s who wrote it.” The lesser names are evidence of authenticity (cf. Q-4.1.3).
  • The careful-not-careless reframe — the books that “took a little bit longer for the dust to settle” (2 Peter, 2–3 John, Revelation) were scrutinised because so many forgeries circulated under those names, not because the criteria were unstable.
The “REAL” acronym — with Tim Barnett (2020), and its one gap

On the Stand to Reason livestream, Tim Barnett offers a popular mnemonic — the NT books are Recognised, Early, Apostolic, and Lack embellishment — which Huff develops with manuscript detail. Note the mismatch with the three-criterion framework: “Recognised” is the recognition frame, “Early” + “Apostolic” do the work of apostolicity, “Lack embellishment” gestures at orthodoxy — but catholicity drops out of the acronym entirely. Huff credits Michael Kruger as the source of his canon-recognition concepts.

Delivery caveats (preserved honestly)

In 2016 Huff dates the Gospel of Peter to the fourth century (consensus: mid-to-late second). In 2023 he enumerates the criteria as “first... second... fourth,” skipping “third” — a verbal slip, not a hidden fourth criterion. The framework stays three.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

This is a one-expert node, so every response below is Huff’s, and the objections are the ones he raises and answers inside the argument.

Objection 1

If apostolicity is the test, why are Mark and Luke — non-apostles — in the canon?

Neither Mark nor Luke was one of the Twelve, yet both gospels are canonical — so apostolicity cannot really be the criterion.

Standard skeptical

Strongest response

“Or someone who knew an apostle” — and the lesser names prove authenticity

Mark’s connection to Peter and Luke’s to Paul satisfy the “apostle or associate” clause. And the very inclusion of these lesser names is itself evidence: “if you are going to make that up, why on earth not put an apostle’s name on it? There is no reason to put Mark’s name on Mark... unless that’s who wrote it.”

Huff authorial-appropriation (cf. Q-4.1.3)

Objection 2

Some canonical books (2 Peter, 2–3 John, Revelation) wrestled to be accepted

If books inside the canon were themselves disputed for centuries, that shows the criteria were unstable and the boundary arbitrary.

Standard skeptical

Strongest response

The struggle shows care, not chaos

Because so many forgeries circulated under “Peter” and “John,” books bearing those names drew stricter scrutiny — “hold on, we need to make sure we do our homework” — so “some of those books took a little bit longer for the dust to settle.” The four gospels and Paul’s letters were never in question.

Huff careful-not-careless reframe

Objection 3

There was a formal “choosing” — a council that voted the canon into being

A pile of competing books was placed before a council, which voted some in and branded the rest heretical — the canon was imposed top-down.

Da Vinci Code / popular

Strongest response

There was no vote — it was a recognising

“In one way there was no formal criteria, because it wasn’t a choosing as much as it was a recognising.” Where there was struggle (Revelation), it was protracted and prayerful: “it wasn’t because they got a pile of books and said, okay, we have one book with dragons, let’s see if we can get a couple more — let’s have a vote. That’s not how it worked.”

Huff recognition frame (see Q-4.3.7)

Objection 4

The criteria are reverse-engineered — circular

“Apostolic,” “catholic” and “orthodox” were defined to fit the books the church had already decided to keep, so the test is rigged: it can only ever validate the chosen list.

Implicit / sophisticated

Gap — never confronted

Gestured at, not answered

Huff’s recognition framing — the books carried innate authority the church merely acknowledged — is the beginning of a reply, but the circularity charge is never confronted head-on. The orthodoxy leg is especially exposed: “the rule of faith handed down” is never specified independently of the canon it is meant to validate.

Aggregation gap

Persistent gaps in the corpus

Single-expert coverage makes the gaps sharper than on a multi-voice node. The framework is stable, but several live pressure-points go untouched.

Gap 1

The circularity objection is never confronted.

The sharpest sceptical move — that the three criteria are derived from the books already accepted, so the test is rigged — is not addressed anywhere in the corpus. The recognition framing is asserted, not argued against the circularity charge.

Gap 2

Catholicity is the unstable leg.

Fully defined in three transcripts, but it drops out of the 2020 “REAL” acronym altogether, replaced by the vaguer “the literature betrays a different worldview.” The corpus never reconciles the three-criterion framework with the four-letter mnemonic, and never specifies how much universal reception counts as catholicity.

Gap 3

The Constantine / Nicaea imposition myth is not engaged here.

Huff dismantles “there was a vote” in general terms, but the specific Nicaea / Da Vinci Code claim is handled at Q-4.3.6 / Q-4.3.7, not on this node.

Gap 4

No dates, councils, or the Athanasius letter.

The framework is presented a-historically. Athanasius’s 367 Festal Letter (the first list of exactly the 27), and the synods of Hippo (393) and Carthage (397), are never named — the hearer gets the criteria but no timeline for when recognition crystallised.

Gap 5

The Old Testament / deuterocanon boundary is untouched.

As at Q-4.3.6, the Protestant–Catholic–Orthodox disagreement over Tobit, Judith and Maccabees never surfaces. The three criteria are presented as a New Testament screen; their application to the OT canon edge is not addressed.

Gap 6

“Orthodoxy” risks question-begging without a fixed rule of faith.

Orthodoxy is defined as conformity to “the oral teaching handed down,” but the corpus never specifies the content of that rule independently of the canon it is meant to validate — leaving the orthodoxy test exposed to the same circularity worry as Gap 1.
