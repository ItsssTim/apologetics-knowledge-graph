The question

Do the four Gospels contradict one another on the death and resurrection — and if they do, does that collapse their reliability?

This is the sceptic’s single most-rehearsed reliability attack, and in this corpus it is almost always raised by the sceptic — Bart Ehrman, Alex O’Connor, Paulogia, Yusuf Ismail, Ahmed Sraa, “The Inquisitor” — not by the apologist. The standard list is fixed: which day Jesus died (Mark’s after-Passover vs John’s before), what hour (third vs sixth), how many women went to the tomb, whether they saw one figure or two, men or angels, whether the disciples were sent to Galilee or Jerusalem, and whether the ascension was same-day, eight-days-plus, or after forty days.

The apologetic burden is therefore defensive and methodological, not constructive. The live dispute is not over any single text but over a prior question: what counts as a contradiction, and what is the evidential weight of a discrepancy in independent testimony? Two rival rules collide — the sceptic’s courtroom rule (“witnesses who differ on a central fact are impeached”) and the apologist’s historiographical rule (“independent witnesses who differ in peripheral detail are corroborated, not impeached”). Whoever’s rule the audience adopts decides the exchange before any text is examined.

The convergent case — one reframe, four moves

This is not a syllogism. It is a defensive cumulative case in which every expert runs the same reframe and then deploys some subset of four harmonising moves. The reframe is load-bearing; the moves are interchangeable ammunition.

Move 1 · Angelology / idiom

  • Angels appear as men (Gen 18)
  • Heb 13:2 — “entertained angels unawares”
  • Luke 24:23 equates the “two men” with angels
  • Focal narrowing ≠ denial of a second
  • Omission is not denial

Move 2 · The plural verb

  • John 20:2 — “we do not know”
  • Greek verb in the plural
  • Presupposes a group Mary had left
  • John’s lone Mary ↔ Synoptic women
  • O’Connor conceded this instance

Move 3 · Parity & reframe

  • Caesar’s assassin named four ways
  • Yet no historian doubts the killing
  • Argument from silence is weak
  • Earlier source > later for reliability
  • Reconciliation → independence marker
Reframe

Difference is not contradiction. A contradiction requires A and not-A.

The definitional move. None of the Gospels asserts “one and only one” angel, or “Mary and no one else.” One woman named in one account is fully consistent with three women present. Huff states it formally; Knechtle, McLatchie and Craig assume it. “A cannot be non-A. I cannot be a married bachelor.”

Mechanism

The harmonisations arise from each text’s own words, not from a fabricated “fifth gospel.”

How the moves combine. Luke 24:23 internally equates “two men” with “a vision of angels.” John 20:2’s plural verb presupposes the Synoptic group. Angels appear as men throughout Scripture. No charge of incompatibility survives a close reading that uses only the texts themselves.

Conclusion

Reconcilable variation is positive evidence: accounts that first seem irreconcilable, then harmonise without forcing, are almost certainly independent.

The asymmetry verdict. Confirmations are stronger evidence for reliability than discrepancies are evidence against it — there are more ways to be honestly mistaken in peripheral detail than to fabricate consistent peripheral detail. “You turn the argument on its head to become a positive argument for the veracity of the Gospels” (McLatchie).

Distinctive moves
  • “Scene of the accident with my wife” analogy (Stuart) — a Habermas staple; wins difference ≠ contradiction but does no specific harmonisation.

William Lane Craig1 transcript
The three-assumptions filter
Distinctive moves
  • Three-assumptions diagnosis — the contradiction argument silently assumes the inconsistencies are (1) irresolvable, (2) at the heart not the periphery, and (3) equally weighted across earlier and later sources.
  • The agreed-core move — all four Gospels agree on crucifixion under Pilate at Passover, death within hours, burial by Joseph of Arimathea, the empty tomb found by women including Mary Magdalene, and appearances to the disciples including Peter.
Caveat — the unanswered yes/no

Ehrman twice presses: do you think the embellished later accounts (John) are historical or not? Craig does not answer, defaulting to “earlier is more credible.” The corpus’s clearest unanswered methodological challenge on this node.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

How many women at the tomb? One in John, two in Matthew, three+ in Luke

The four Gospels give incompatible counts of who discovered the empty tomb — a fundamental forensic detail on which honest witnesses should not differ.

Ismail, O’Connor, Ehrman, The Inquisitor, Sraa

Strongest response

John 20:2’s plural verb presupposes a group

Mary’s “we do not know where they have laid him” is plural in Greek — she had left a larger group. John’s lone Mary is consistent with the Synoptic women. Omission is not denial; none of the accounts says “and no one else.” O’Connor conceded this instance.

McLatchie John 20:2 plural verbHuff focal-point variation

Objection 2

One figure or two? Men or angels?

Mark has one young man, Matthew one angel, Luke two men, John two angels. The number and the ontology both clash.

Ismail, The Inquisitor, Ehrman

Strongest response

Luke 24:23 equates “two men” with angels

The same author who reports “two men” has the Emmaus travellers describe “a vision of angels.” Angels appear as men (Gen 18, Heb 13:2). Matthew and Mark narrow focus to the one who spoke — not a denial of a second.

McLatchie in-Luke harmonisation + angelology

Objection 3

If Mark knew Greek and wrote “man,” not “angelos,” he meant a man

Mark could have written “angelos” and chose “man” — the lexical choice is deliberate and the texts use the words distinctly.

Ismail (criminal-defence-attorney frame)

Strongest response

Description vs interpretation of one referent

The angelological idiom plus Luke’s own equation defeats the lexical wedge: Mark/Luke describe what the women saw (men in human form); Matthew/John interpret who they were (angels). Same beings, two registers.

McLatchie men/angels equivalence

Objection 4

Eyewitnesses who contradict on a central fact are legally impeached

As a working defence attorney: a witness who says “shot” while another says “stabbed” is impeached. You can only reconcile by inventing a fabricated “fifth gospel.”

Yusuf Ismail

Strongest response

Wrong rule for the case — this is peripheral, not central

The discrepancy orbits a fixed agreed core (crucifixion, burial, empty tomb, appearances). Independent honest variation in peripheral detail corroborates. The harmonisation arises from each text’s own words, not a fabricated harmony.

McLatchie reconcilable variationCraig peripheral, not heart

Objection 5

What hour? Third hour (Mark) vs sixth hour (John)

Mark says Jesus was crucified at the third hour; John has him still before Pilate at the sixth. The timelines are incompatible.

The Inquisitor, Ehrman

Strongest response

Roman reckoning (John) vs Jewish reckoning (Synoptics)

John writes to a Roman audience using Roman time; the Synoptics use Jewish time, where the day begins at sunset (grounded in Genesis 1’s evening-then-morning). The only direct engagement with this charge in the corpus — from Huff alone.

Huff lexical-temporal reckoning

Objection 6

Matthew-only earthquake and angel rolling the stone

Only Matthew reports a great earthquake and an angel moving the stone. The other writers would surely have noticed and recorded so dramatic an event.

Alex O’Connor

Strongest response

Argument from silence is notoriously weak

Josephus is the only first-century Jewish source for the AD 70 destruction of Jerusalem; the Claudian expulsion of Jews from Rome is attested in Acts plus a single line of Suetonius — yet all historians grant both events. Non-mention proves nothing.

McLatchie parity + silence rebuttal

Objection 7

Lazarus: raised in John 11 vs “won’t be raised” in Luke 16

John has Lazarus raised to prove Christ’s identity; Luke 16 has Jesus say a Lazarus should not be raised because the unpersuaded won’t believe even then. A blatant contradiction.

Alex O’Connor

Strongest response

Two different Lazaruses; a parable is reported, not enacted

We have multiple Marys, Johns and James’ — why not two Lazaruses? In Luke 16 Jesus reports a parable; he does not act. Only “fringe” scholars (Price, Carrier) identify the two. The exchange muddied when O’Connor pivoted to “philosophical inconsistency.”

McLatchie disambiguation + genre

Objection 8

It’s the most important event in history — God should have ensured consistency

If the Bible is the inspired word of God reporting the single most important event, internal consistency is the very least we could expect.

Alex O’Connor, Jon Steingard

Strongest response

Category mistake about inspiration

Historic Christianity does not hold that God dictated the Bible. “This is the sort of variability we see in historical reportage when you have multiple eyewitnesses.” Variation is what eyewitness testimony looks like.

McLatchie doctrine-of-inspiration reframe

Objection 9

The unnamed young man (Mark 14:51-52) is a later metaphorical insertion

The naked young man who flees is read as symbolic embroidery added to the passion narrative.

Implicit (campus questioner)

Strongest response

No name, no narrative function — a historicity marker

A fiction writer would not invent a figure with no name and no plot role. Random incidental detail that doesn’t advance the story is a literary marker of reportage. “I would turn it more so historical evidence for the reliability of the gospels.”

Stuart Knechtle incidental-detail criterion

Objection 10

What day did Jesus die? Mark after Passover, John before

Mark explicitly has Jesus die after the Passover meal was eaten; John explicitly has him die the day before. The chronologies are flatly incompatible.

The Inquisitor, Ehrman

Gap — unengaged

No expert in this corpus harmonises the Passover-day charge

The standard scholarly harmonisations (different calendars; John’s theological Passover-lamb timing; “preparation of the Passover” as the Friday of Passover week) appear nowhere. The single largest hole.

Objection 11

Galilee vs Jerusalem resurrection appearances

Depending which Gospel you read, the disciples were told to go to Galilee to see Jesus, or to stay in Jerusalem. Steingard names this “the biggest one to me.”

Jon Steingard, Ehrman

Gap — deflected

Knechtle reframes the method rather than harmonising

No expert offers the standard sequence (Jerusalem appearances → Galilee appearances → return to Jerusalem for the ascension). Knechtle answers with bottom-up historicity, which sidesteps the charge.

Objection 12

Ascension timing: same-day (Luke 24) vs forty days (Acts 1)

Luke 24 reads as a same-evening ascension; John 20 has a second appearance eight days later; Acts 1 says forty days. The same author (Luke) appears to contradict himself.

The Inquisitor

Gap — asserted, not shown

“Orthodoxy has always said forty days” — but the mechanism is missing

Stuart asserts the forty-day reading without explaining how Luke 24 telescopes a forty-day span into what reads as one evening. The Luke 24/Acts 1 harmonisation mechanism is left unstated.

Aggregation gap

Objection 13

You can’t claim unembellished accounts are more historical and that embellished John is historical

If unembellished accounts (Mark’s burial scene) are probably historical because unembellished, then embellished John cannot also be historical — or the criterion has no weight.

Bart Ehrman

Gap — unanswered

Craig declines the yes/no on John’s reliability

Pressed twice, Craig defaults to “earlier is more credible” without committing on John. The methodological inconsistency Ehrman alleges stands unrebutted in the transcript.
