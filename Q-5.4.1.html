Does the Bible give an age of the Earth?

Young earth or old? The skeptic and the young-earth Christian share an assumption the experts reject — that Genesis pronounces on the question at all. Lennox dominates; five voices, seven substantive treatments.

The question

Does the Bible actually state an age for the Earth, such that the scientific age of ~13.8 billion years contradicts Scripture?

This is a §5 science-and-faith question with an unusual double structure: it is at once a science-versus-Bible apology aimed at the skeptic and an in-house dispute between young-earth and old-earth readings of Genesis. The corpus is striking because every substantive expert refuses to defend young-earth chronology and instead dissolves the question — the Bible, they argue, simply does not pronounce on the age of the Earth.

The apologetic pay-off is defensive minimalism. Rather than win the scientific argument or the in-house exegetical argument, the experts deny there is a contradiction to win. The clash of “science says billions, the Bible says thousands” is treated as a category mistake resting on a misreading of genre and grammar, not a genuine collision of authorities.

The convergent argument — inference to best explanation

Premise 1

The Bible nowhere states an age for the Earth — so one premise of the alleged contradiction is simply invented.

Observed phrasings. Knechtle, as a burden-of-proof reversal: “would you please show me in the Bible where the Bible says the Earth is 4–8,000 years old.” Lennox, as a flat criterion: “What does the Bible say about the age of the Earth? Nothing whatsoever. Full stop.”

Premise 2

Genesis 1:1 sits before and outside the sequence of days — the grammar itself blocks the young-earth chronology.

Supports. Lennox: the days each open “And God said”; verse 1 does not, and is written in a different past tense, so “in the beginning” is not Day One. McLatchie: the heavens and earth already exist before the first “and God said” in verse 3. Craig: vegetation “bringing forth” fruit trees on day three implies elapsed time, not a film on fast-forward.

Premise 3

Different literary forms answer different questions; Genesis answers why, not how old.

Supports. Knechtle: “if I want to find out how old the Earth is I turn to science… the same way if I want to learn about photosynthesis… I don’t read the Bible.” Baker-Hytch: Genesis is theological accommodation to a pre-scientific audience and a subversion of Ancient Near Eastern myth, not a chronology. Lennox: the Hebrew yom carries four distinct meanings in the chapter — a warning sign of stylised language.

Conclusion

The best explanation is that the text never set out to date the Earth — so the “billions vs thousands” conflict has the wrong object. The skeptic commits a category mistake; the young-earth Christian over-reads.

Methodological note. This is abductive, not deductive: no single premise is a proof, but silence + grammar + genre + the Galileo precedent together make “the text was never dating the Earth” the best explanation. Lennox names the error: “a danger of trying to protect scripture by overkill by claiming it says something which it doesn’t actually say.”

How each expert deploys it

John Lennox 4 transcripts
The dominant voice — grammar, Galileo, and pastoral framing
Signature moves
  • The grammatical wedge — Genesis 1:1 is not Day One. The days each open “And God said”; verse 1 does not, and the first two verses use a different past tense. Checked with the Hebrew professors at Cambridge and Oxford.
  • Tactical narrowing — brackets the meaning of the days entirely (“has got nothing to do with what you believe about the days”); defends only the narrow claim he needs.
  • The yom lexical move — four distinct meanings in Genesis 1, “a wonderfully interesting warning sign that this is highly sophisticated language.”
  • The Galileo parallel — for 1,700 years the church read Scripture as teaching a fixed earth, then learned it “could be interpreted otherwise without losing its authority.” Made visceral with a live hands-up vote.
  • Pastoral framing — the only expert to foreground the human cost; “the saddest thing is to see Christians fighting one another.” Stays inside a high view of inspiration; from Ussher’s home city, rejects YEC from the inside.
Named diagnosis

“Overreading what the Bible says” / “protecting scripture by overkill” — Lennox’s coinage for the error of claiming the text fixes an age it never fixes.

Cliffe & Stuart Knechtle 1 transcript
The folk burden-reversal
Signature delivery
  • Burden-of-proof reversal as the opening move — “show me in the Bible where the Bible says the Earth is 4–8,000 years old.” Grounded autobiographically: “I’m 68 years old… never once have I read where the Bible says the Earth is this many years old.”
  • The parity reductio — the Bible’s silence on the Earth’s age no more impugns it than a biology textbook’s silence on US history impugns its biology.
  • Declining to pick a side — like Lennox, reframes rather than entering the YEC/OEC quarrel.
  • Stuart’s genealogies supplement — genealogies telescope generations (“Jesus was not directly after David”), so they cannot be summed to date the Earth; the Torah is about “why are we here,” not a scientific textbook.
William Lane Craig 1 transcript
The internal-textual indicator & the boxed-in naturalist
Distinctive moves
  • Internal critique of YEC — indicators within Genesis 1 against a literal week; the day-three vegetation requires growth-time, “not a film being run on fast-forward.”
  • Theological / scientific decoupling — his doubts about evolution are “scientific doubts, not theological doubts… I have no theological or biblical objection to the theory.”
  • The boxed-in naturalist reversal — “if naturalism is true then evolution has to be true… but the Christian can be open-minded and follow the evidence.” Inverts the “religion is closed-minded” trope.
  • The evangelistic-obstacle anecdote — a young man “prevented from giving his life to Christ because he thought he had to jump through the hoop of young Earth Creationism.”
  • Barron concurring — theistic evolution permissible, with a non-reductionist proviso: there is “something in us… not reducible to the merely biological or physical.”
Jonathan McLatchie 1 transcript
Exegetical precision (with Max Baker-Hytch on accommodation)
Distinctive moves
  • The Genesis 1:1 / 1:3 hinge — heavens and earth already exist before the first “and God said” in verse 3, so “by the time you even get to the first day of creation week you already have the heavens and the earth.” Only the biosphere age is at issue.
  • More concessive on the days than Lennox — yom is “accurately translated day in an ordinary sense,” with the symbolism located in the number seven.
  • Baker-Hytch’s accommodation frame — God communicates to a people who could not grasp “the Doppler effect… a Big Bang singularity.”
  • ANE subversion — Genesis takes Enuma Elish imagery and inverts it; sun, moon and stars become “greater and lesser lamps,” humans become stewards.
  • Hostile-witness confirmation — Fred Hoyle and atheist cosmologists resisted the Big Bang “because it seemed too friendly to that kind of biblical theistic creation narrative.”

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

“Science says ~5 billion years, the Bible says 4–8,000 — so the Bible is false.”

The headline contradiction: modern cosmology and the Bible give incompatible ages, so Scripture cannot be trusted.

Skeptic (Knechtle’s street interlocutor)

Strongest response

One premise is simply invented

Burden-of-proof reversal: show me where the Bible states an age — it never does. The conflict is fictitious because one of its two premises is not in the text.

Knechtle show-me-the-verseLennox “nothing whatsoever, full stop”

Objection 2

“If the Bible is silent on the Earth’s age, it’s unreliable.”

A text that fails to answer such a basic factual question is defective and should not be trusted on anything.

Skeptic

Strongest response

Silence is not a defect — genre parity

A biology textbook’s silence on US history says nothing about its biology; a history book’s silence on stellar motion does not invalidate its history. Different genres answer different questions.

Knechtle biology-textbook reductio

Objection 3

“The genealogies sum to a young earth (Ussher’s chronology).”

Adding up the begats from Adam pins creation to roughly 4,000 BC, fixing the age short regardless of Genesis 1.

Young-earth tradition / skeptic

Strongest response

Genealogies telescope, and the chain is already broken

Genealogies telescope generations — “Jesus was not directly after David. There was space between.” And the literary structure puts Genesis 1:1 outside the day-sequence, breaking the chain before it starts.

Stuart Knechtle telescopingLennox grammar

Objection 4

“Genesis 1 teaches a literal six 24-hour-day creation week.”

The plain reading is sequential 24-hour days; to deny it is to evade the text.

Young-earth reading

Strongest response

The text itself argues against it

Day-three vegetation “bringing forth” fruit trees requires growth-time, “not a film being run on fast-forward” (Craig). The grammar shows verse 1 precedes Day One (Lennox, McLatchie), and yom’s four meanings warn against a flat reading (Lennox).

Craig day-three vegetationLennox / McLatchie v.1 precedes the days

Objection 5

“You’re just reinterpreting Scripture to dodge science — capitulating to liberalism.”

Old-earth readings look like the text being bent to fit whatever science currently says.

Young-earth Christian / skeptic

Strongest response

The Galileo precedent, from inside a high view of inspiration

The church once read Scripture as teaching a fixed earth and learned it “could be interpreted otherwise without losing its authority.” Said explicitly from inside “I believe in the authority and inspiration of scripture” — not liberalism but better reading.

Lennox fixed-earth parallel

Objection 6

“Don’t you have to believe in six-day creation to be a Christian?”

YEC is treated as a test of orthodoxy, so doubting it is doubting the faith.

Earnest believer (Craig’s anecdote)

Strongest response

No — and making it a hoop is an evangelistic obstacle

Explicit denial that YEC is a Christian requirement. The young man Craig met “had been prevented from giving his life to Christ because he thought he had to jump through the hoop of young Earth Creationism.”

Craig not a requirementBarron concurs

Objection 7

“Origin-of-life — life from a warm pond — is the textbook story science requires.”

Abiogenesis is the established account, and a metaphysics built on “only the provable is true” rules out a creator.

Skeptic / scientism

Engaged rhetorically, not evidentially

Ridicule plus a self-refutation aside

Knechtle ridicules the unevidenced leap (“do you know how big of a joke that is?”) and notes that “only the scientifically provable is true” is itself unprovable. But no actual abiogenesis or ID argument is engaged — the dismissal is rhetorical.

Cliffe Knechtle ridicule + scientism reductioNo evidential engagement

Objection 8

“Genesis 1 is just borrowed Ancient Near Eastern myth.”

The creation account is a derivative of Enuma Elish and its neighbours — no more authoritative than any other myth.

Critical scholarship

Strongest response

It subverts the myths, and cosmology confirms its core

Genesis takes ANE imagery and inverts it — stars demoted from deities to “greater and lesser lamps,” humans elevated from slaves to stewards. And modern Big Bang cosmology confirms its creation-from-nothing claim, which atheist cosmologists resisted as too theistic.

Baker-Hytch accommodation + subversionMcLatchie ex-nihilo confirmation

Persistent gaps in the corpus

Gap 1

The Romans 5 / death-before-the-fall argument

The strongest theological case for young earth — that animal death and predation before Adam’s sin conflicts with “death entered through sin” — goes unanswered.

Young-earth theological case

Unanswered

Deferred, never engaged

Lennox explicitly defers it; no expert in the corpus engages it. The experts win the grammar while leaving the YEC camp’s deepest motivation untouched — the most serious gap in the node.

Aggregation gap

Gap 2

No positive case for any old-earth reading

The corpus shows what the days are not, but no one argues positively for day-age, framework, analogical-day, or cosmic-temple readings.

Constructive hermeneutics

Deferred to the books

Almost entirely negative

Lennox repeatedly defers the constructive case to Seven Days That Divide the World. The reader is left knowing the conflict is dissolved but not what Genesis 1 affirmatively means.

Gap 3

Where the soul enters an evolutionary lineage

Barron insists on a non-reducible human soul and Craig is open to common descent, but the join is never made.

Historical-Adam problem

Named and dropped

No reconciliation offered

Neither expert explains how a special, non-material human soul is reconciled with biological continuity from ancestral hominids. The historical-Adam question (Q-13.4.1) is named and abandoned.
