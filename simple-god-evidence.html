A case built one beam at a time

Is there any good reason to think God is real?

Plenty of people assume faith is a leap in the dark, believing because you were raised to or because it feels nice. But there are real arguments here, and no single one has to carry the whole weight. Think of it like a courtroom case. You take the evidence one strand at a time, then ask what best explains all of it together.

First / The beginning
Step 1 of 6
Why is there anything at all

The universe began. Beginnings need a cause.

Start with the plainest fact there is. Something exists rather than nothing. The philosopher J. P. Moreland puts the next step in three simple moves.

It had a start
The past cannot stretch back forever. You can never count to the end of an endless list, so if the chain of past events were truly infinite, this present moment could never have arrived. Yet here we are. So the universe began. The slow loss of usable energy points the same way.
It was caused
Nothing in all our experience simply pops into being out of nothing. Things come from other things. A beginning that real therefore calls for a cause that brought it about.
The cause is personal
Time, space and matter started with the universe, so its cause sits outside all three. It is timeless, spaceless and immaterial. And only a free agent could choose to bring a world into being, which means the cause is more like a mind than a mechanism.
The theory you will hear
The universe could have come from nothing, or just always existed. Both run into walls. Coming from literally nothing has no parallel anywhere in science. And an actual infinite past cannot be crossed to reach today. The honest options keep narrowing to a beginning with a cause beyond the physical (Genesis 1:1).
✓
A universe that began, from a cause outside space and time that chose to act, already sounds a great deal like the God people have always described.
Second / The design
Step 2 of 6
Order that asks to be explained

When you find a watch, you infer a watchmaker.

William Paley said it centuries ago. Stumble on a watch in a field and you do not assume wind and rain assembled it. The mathematician William Dembski shows the same reasoning still holds.

The watch
A watch has parts arranged for a purpose, gears fitted to tell time. That arrangement is the signature of a designer. The human eye, parts fitted together to produce sight, makes the point even more sharply.
The update
Modern intelligent design takes Paley further using information theory and molecular biology. It is not a gap in knowledge. It is a positive inference from the kind of order that, everywhere else, only minds produce.
Stay modest
Dembski is careful here. This argument shows a designing intelligence behind nature. On its own it does not name that intelligence or prove the gospel. It clears the ground. The other strands fill in who.
The theory you will hear
Darwin explained design away, an unguided process can mimic a designer. It can mimic some of it. The deeper claim was that random variation and selection account for all biological order. Notice what is being granted even by critics: nature looks so designed that you need a whole theory to explain the appearance away.
✓
You do not need to settle every biology debate. The order in nature is real enough that even its sceptics spend their energy explaining the design, not denying it.
Third / The fine-tuning
Step 3 of 6
The settings behind the scenery

The dials are set for life with eerie precision.

Step behind the universe to the constants that govern it. The physicist Walter Bradley catalogues just how finely balanced they are.

Fifty needs
At least fifty distinct requirements must all be met for life to exist anywhere: the right elements to build molecular machines, carbon that bonds and releases, water as a solvent across earthly temperatures, energy sources matched to the chemistry. Every one is satisfied here.
Nineteen constants
Nineteen universal constants, the speed of light, the gravity force, the mass of the electron, the unit charge, sit at life-permitting values. The strong force and electromagnetism must balance to within about five percent, or you get no stable sun and no rich variety of elements.
A blunt verdict
The astronomer Fred Hoyle, no friend of religion, concluded that a super intellect had monkeyed with the physics, chemistry and biology, and that there are no blind forces worth speaking about in nature.
The theory you will hear
Maybe there are countless other universes, and we just live in the one that works. Notice this is a leap of faith too, an unobserved sea of universes invoked precisely to avoid a designer. Given one universe, the one we can actually see, fine-tuning this exact is far more at home with a mind behind it than with luck.
✓
When the settings of reality are tuned this carefully, and a sceptic of Hoyle's standing calls it the work of a super intellect, design has stopped being a religious hunch.
Fourth / The code
Step 4 of 6
A message written into matter

Every living cell runs on written code.

Zoom all the way in, to the molecule inside you. Bradley points out that life is not just chemistry, it is information, and information behaves like a message.

DNA is text
DNA is a storage system. It encodes the instructions for building and running a living thing, written in a four-letter chemical alphabet. We know of no other source for coded information like this except a mind.
The odds
The chance of one key protein, the cytochrome-C molecule, falling into the correct sequence by accident is about one in ten followed by sixty zeros. That is not a long shot. It is a number that makes blind chance stop being a serious answer.
Built to work
The biochemist Michael Behe adds that some molecular systems only function once every part is in place, with no advantage until assembled. Information plus that kind of all-or-nothing machinery points hard toward design, not gradual accident.
The theory you will hear
Given enough time, chance and natural selection will write the code on their own. Selection can only refine what already lives and reproduces. It cannot author the first set of instructions, and the odds against that first sequence forming by accident are staggering. Time does not help when the target is that small.
✓
Wherever else you find coded language, you find an author behind it. The code at the heart of life is the same kind of clue, written small.
Fifth / The moral law
Step 5 of 6
The clue inside you

Some things are really wrong, not just unpopular.

Leave the telescope and microscope for a moment and look inward. The philosopher Paul Copan argues that our sense of right and wrong is itself a signpost.

You know it
Torturing a child for fun is not merely frowned upon, it is wrong. You know that by conscience, before any argument. People across every culture recognise the same core virtues, as if a law were written on the heart (Romans 2:15).
The argument
It runs cleanly. If real, binding moral duties exist, they need a ground beyond human opinion. They plainly do exist. So there is a source of moral law above us, the kind of anchor a good God provides (Romans 2:14-15).
Real worth
This is also why every person carries dignity that no government grants and none can revoke. If we are made in God's image, human worth is built in, not handed out. Take God away and that footing gets very hard to keep.
The theory you will hear
Morality is just evolution and culture, no God required. But that only explains why we have moral feelings, not why some acts are genuinely wrong whether we feel it or not. The atheist who calls cruelty evil is borrowing the very moral law the view denies. The objection leans on the thing it tries to remove.
✓
If right and wrong are more than personal taste, and almost everyone lives as though they are, then the moral law you already trust is pointing somewhere.
Sixth / The encounter
Step 6 of 6
The witness of experience

Across every culture, people say they have met God.

The arguments so far work from the outside in. This last strand is more personal. The philosopher Douglas Geivett asks whether the experience of God can count as evidence too.

The reports
Scripture and history are full of direct encounters, Moses at the burning bush among the most famous (Exodus 3). And it is not an ancient or local thing. Reports of meeting the divine turn up in every age and across the world.
A fair rule
We trust our experiences unless we have good reason not to. You believe your own eyes and ears without a proof for each one. Geivett says we should extend that same rational trust to experiences of God, rather than rule them out from the start.
Weight in numbers
No single experience compels everyone, and it should not have to. But the sheer breadth of these reports across cultures adds real weight, and it lands hardest when it joins the other lines of evidence rather than standing alone.
The theory you will hear
Experiences can be mistaken or invented, so they prove nothing. Some surely are, and an experience can be misread. But the bare possibility of error does not discredit all experience, or you would have to throw out ordinary perception too. You weigh experiences, you do not dismiss a whole class of them in advance.
✓
Treated as fairly as any other experience, the worldwide testimony of people who say they have encountered God is evidence worth taking seriously, not waving away.

So is there any good reason to think God is real? Not one knockdown proof, but six independent strands that all lean the same way: a beginning, a design, a fine-tuned home, a written code, a moral law, and a worldwide weight of experience. Any one might be talked around. Together they make the question worth your honest attention. The reasonable next step is not to settle it from a distance, but to look into it for yourself.
