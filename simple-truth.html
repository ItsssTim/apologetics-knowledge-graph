The foundation under everything

Isn't truth just relative, true for you but not me?

It is the line that ends a thousand conversations: that is true for you, but not for me. It sounds humble and tolerant. But it quietly muddles three very different things, and once you separate them the idea falls apart. Let us untangle subjective, relative and objective truth.

First / Three meanings
Step 1 of 4
Objective, subjective, relative

Truth comes in three very different kinds.

Most of the confusion clears the moment you separate three things people lump together under the one word true.

Objective
Objective truth is true no matter what anyone thinks, because it matches reality. As Lennox puts it, objective means person-independent, unbiased by anyone's feelings or opinions. Water is made of hydrogen and oxygen whether you like it or not; the earth orbits the sun even if everyone believed otherwise. It is simply true, for everyone.
Subjective
Subjective truth is the opposite: person-dependent, genuinely about you. Chocolate is delicious, that song is beautiful, I feel cold. These are real, but they are reports about the subject, not claims about the world. There is no point arguing whether mint ice cream is objectively the best; by its nature, it is a matter of taste.
Relativism
Then there is the contested one: relativism, the claim that all truth, even about God, history and right and wrong, is only ever subjective, true for you but not for me. This is the move people reach for to quietly retire the hard questions. And it is the one that does not survive a second look.
Where faith sits
Christianity stakes itself on the first kind. Scripture presents itself not as one community's taste but as objectively true revelation from God: let God be true, though everyone is a liar (Romans 3:4), Scripture given so we are equipped for every good work (2 Timothy 3:15-17). Jesus is Lord is not Christian cultural language; it is a claim about the way things actually are.
The theory you'll hear
This is just splitting hairs over words. It is the opposite of hair-splitting. It is the difference between I prefer tea and arsenic is poison. One is about you; the other is about the world and will kill you whatever your opinion. Blur that line and you can dodge any uncomfortable claim by relabelling it a preference. The distinction is the whole game.
✓
Confidence: three different things hide under the word true. Objective (matches reality, true for all), subjective (genuinely about you), and the relativist claim that everything is the second. Keep them apart.
Second / Really subjective?
Step 2 of 4
Some things, but not everything

Yes, some things are subjective. Not everything is.

The relativist is half right, which is exactly why the idea feels true. Some things really are a matter of you. The mistake is stretching that over everything.

Some, yes
Plenty of things genuinely are subjective: taste, preference, what you find beautiful or funny. Nobody should argue those as if they were facts about the universe. So when someone says that is true for you, they are sometimes exactly right, and you can happily agree. The trouble only starts when they apply it where it does not belong.
Reality
Because most of the big questions are not about taste at all. Whether a dose is poison, whether an event in history happened, whether God exists, whether Jesus rose, these are claims about reality, and reality does not bend to opinion. They are either objectively true or objectively false. There is no true for me about whether a bridge will hold your weight.
The mix-up
That is the category mistake at the heart of relativism: treating a claim about reality as if it were a claim about taste. Did Jesus rise from the dead is simply not the same kind of question as do you like jazz. One has an answer that is true for everyone or no one. Filing it in the taste box is how people avoid ever facing it.
The theory you'll hear
But everyone sees reality through their own lens, so isn't it all just perspective? Our perspectives certainly differ; the facts underneath them do not. Two witnesses describe a crash differently, yet only one car actually ran the red light. Where you were born shapes what you believe, it does not shape what is true. Perspective is real. It is just not the same thing as truth.
✓
Confidence: some things really are subjective, and that is fine. But reality-claims, about God, history, right and wrong, are not matters of taste. Treating them as if they were is the basic error.
Third / Does it hold?
Step 3 of 4
Two ways it collapses

Relativism collapses the moment you test it.

Set aside whether relativism is appealing and simply ask whether it can stand up. It cannot, on two counts: it refutes itself, and nobody actually lives by it.

It eats itself
Here is the fatal problem. The statement there is no objective truth is itself put forward as an objective truth. When Nietzsche declared there are no facts, only interpretations, he meant that as a fact, not as one more interpretation we may ignore. The claim all truth is relative must, by its own rule, be only relatively true, in which case you are free to reject it. As Lennox says, it refutes itself, so we can simply discard it.
The courtroom
And nobody truly lives it. Lennox's picture: imagine you are on trial for a crime you did not commit, and the judge announces there is no objective truth, so he will decide by his own prejudices. You would be outraged, and you would protest in the name of truth and fairness. The instant it costs you, you believe in objective truth absolutely.
No one lives it
Notice too how selectively it is applied. As Copan points out, nobody is a relativist about gravity, the dosage on a drug label or the balance in their bank account; the relativism is reserved for God, morality and cheating. Cut in front of a relativist in a queue, or take what is his, and he will insist on his rights as objective facts. Has anyone ever truly thought torturing children for fun is wrong only as a matter of taste? The relativist is bluffing, including to himself.
The theory you'll hear
Well, all truth is relative isn't meant as an absolute, it's just how I see it. Then it carries no weight at all. If it is only your private perspective, I am perfectly free to set it aside and keep looking for what is actually true. Either it is an objective claim, in which case it sinks itself, or it is a mere preference, in which case it cannot tell anyone else what to think. It dies whichever way you take it.
✓
Confidence: relativism cannot be stated without assuming the objective truth it denies, and no one lives as though cruelty were just a matter of taste. As a theory, it eats itself.
Fourth / Why it matters
Step 4 of 4
And the escape hatch that fails

Why it matters, and the escape hatch that fails.

This is not a word-game for philosophers. What you believe about truth decides whether the most important question of all is even allowed to be asked.

The real question
Relativism sounds gracious, but it is really a way of never having to ask is it true. If every belief is just your-truth and my-truth, then God, the resurrection, the meaning of your life, all get quietly filed under personal taste and never actually examined. Even the atheist philosopher John Searle conceded that the appeal of relativism is a will to power, a refusal to be at the mercy of the real world. That is not open-mindedness; it is a polite refusal to look.
Not your truth
Which is why the gospel is never offered as my truth. The claim that Jesus rose from the dead is either objectively true, for everyone, or objectively false, for everyone. It cannot be true for you and false for me, any more than the earth can orbit the sun for some people only. Saying so is not arrogance; it is just honesty about what kind of claim it is.
Enough to act
But you might protest that you cannot be one hundred percent certain. True, and you do not need to be. Outside pure mathematics, Lennox notes, nobody has absolute proof of anything; you still trust the surgeon and board the plane on strong evidence, not certainty. Faith works the same way: not believing without evidence, but trusting on enough of it to act.
The theory you'll hear
Since you can't prove it beyond all doubt, you can't claim it's true. By that standard you could claim almost nothing: not that the sun will rise, not that your memories are real, not that your friends exist. We act on excellent evidence rather than mathematical proof in every part of life. Demanding total certainty of God alone, while happily accepting good evidence everywhere else, is a double standard, not a high one.
✓
Confidence: how you treat truth decides whether the real question gets asked at all. The gospel is a public claim, true or false for everyone, and you can weigh it on the same solid-enough evidence you trust your life to daily.
