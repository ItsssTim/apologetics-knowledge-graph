The question that feels like checkmate

If God made everything, who made God?

It feels like the move that ends the game. If everything needs a maker, then God needs one too, and if God can exist uncaused, why not just the universe? It sounds airtight. It rests on a quiet misunderstanding of the one word in the sentence that matters: God.

First / The principle
Step 1 of 3
The principle, stated correctly

Only things that begin need a cause.

The objection smuggles in a rule nobody actually holds. Fix the rule, and the question changes.

Not everything
The principle was never that everything has a cause. It is that everything that begins to exist has a cause. Things that come into being need something to bring them about. That is the careful version of the idea, the one behind the cosmological argument that thinkers like William Lane Craig and John Lennox defend.
The universe began
And the universe looks very much like a thing that began. The evidence for a beginning, a finite past with a starting point, is exactly why it needs a cause. Something that starts to exist does not bring itself into being out of nothing.
God did not begin
God, by definition, is not that kind of thing. The God of the Bible has no beginning and no end; he simply is, before all things (Psalm 90:2). So asking what caused God is asking what caused the one thing that never came into being.
The theory you'll hear
Everything needs a cause, so God needs one too. But that is not the principle. The principle is that everything which begins to exist needs a cause. God did not begin; the universe did. Applying a rule about things-that-start to the one thing that never started is not a fair use of the rule. It is a misuse of it.
✓
The rule is not everything has a cause; it is everything that begins has a cause. The universe began. God did not. So who made God is asking after the maker of the one thing that was never made.
Second / The chain
Step 2 of 3
Why not causes all the way down?

The chain has to stop somewhere.

If you refuse a first cause, the alternative is an endless chain of causes, and that turns out to explain nothing at all.

Passing the bill
Imagine every cause needs an earlier cause, with no end. Each link just passes the bill to the one before it, forever. A debt endlessly deferred is never actually paid. An explanation endlessly postponed never actually explains. The chain that never stops never accounts for why anything is here.
The unmoved mover
So reason pushes towards a stopping point: something that is not itself caused, that holds everything else up without needing to be held. Aristotle called it the unmoved mover. It is not a gap in the explanation; it is the floor the whole explanation stands on.
Eternal something
Which leaves a simple choice. Either there is an eternal, uncaused something that grounds it all, or there is an infinite regress that grounds nothing. The everlasting God, Maker of all, who does not grow faint or weary, is the first option named (Isaiah 40:28). He is where the chain rests, not one more link in it.
The theory you'll hear
Then the universe can be the eternal, uncaused thing instead of God. It is the right move to consider, but the universe is a poor candidate. The evidence points to it having a beginning, and a thing with a beginning is precisely what cannot be the uncaused starting point. The buck stops at something that did not begin. The universe is not that something.
✓
An infinite regress of causes explains nothing; it just defers the question forever. Reason lands on an uncaused first cause, a floor rather than another link, and a universe that began cannot be it.
Third / A category mistake
Step 3 of 3
A category mistake

It is the wrong question, not a hard one.

Once you see what God means, who made God stops being a tough question and becomes a confused one, like asking what the colour blue smells like.

Blue and Tuesday
Some questions are grammatical but meaningless. What does blue smell like? How tall is Tuesday? Each takes a property from one category and forces it onto something it cannot apply to. Who made the uncreated maker does the same: it demands a beginning for the one thing defined as without beginning.
The simulation twist
The modern version is simulation theory: maybe a programmer built our world, so that is our god. But it only moves the problem back a step. Who made the programmer's world? You still need an ultimate ground that was not itself made. As Wes Huff puts it, the idea just kicks the can down the road; you still arrive at the need for a God.
Not an extra thing
So God is not one more item in the universe that we have failed to find a cause for. God is the answer to why there is a universe at all, the one in whom all things hold together (Colossians 1:17). He does not need a maker, because being the uncaused Maker is the whole job description.
The theory you'll hear
You are just defining God so the question cannot touch him; that is cheating. Not quite. We are following the logic to where it leads: a chain of causes that has to end in something uncaused. The only question is what that something is. A thing with no beginning, holding everything else in being, is not a trick definition. It is simply what the word God has always meant.
✓
Who made God is a category mistake, like asking the smell of blue. The simulation version only defers it. God is not an unexplained thing inside the universe; he is the uncaused ground of there being a universe at all.
Putting it together
The bottom line

Nothing made God.

So who made God? Nothing did, and that is not special pleading, it is the entire point. Only things that begin need a maker. The chain of causes has to stop at something that never began. And a thing that never began, holding all else in being, is exactly what the word God has always meant. The question is not answered by hunting for God's maker. It dissolves the moment you see that the Maker of everything is, by definition, the one thing that was never made.
