The anatomy of “not enough evidence”

What is a sceptic actually claiming when he says the evidence for God falls short – and does anyone, including him, really live by that standard? Five experts diagnose the complaint across eight substantive treatments.

The question

When a sceptic says “there isn’t enough evidence to believe in God,” what is actually being claimed – and is it a defensible standard?

This is a meta-apologetic question. It does not ask what the evidence for God is; it asks what the unbeliever’s evidence-complaint is doing. The corpus treats “not enough evidence” not as a neutral verdict on the data but as a claim that can be diagnosed, decoded, and turned back on the person making it.

The shared instinct across all five experts is that the complaint usually conceals something – an unexamined standard of proof, a category error, a will that does not want to be convinced, or a buried biographical wound. The task is less to pile on more evidence than to expose the standard the sceptic is silently applying, and to show that nobody actually lives by it.

The convergent case – five diagnostic streams

This is not a deductive syllogism but a cumulative diagnostic case: five accounts of what “not enough evidence” really is, which reinforce rather than depend on one another. Each shifts the burden from the quantity of evidence to the disposition and standards of the person assessing it.

Stream A · Apatheism

  • “Not enough” means “not enough to coerce me”
  • Coercion was never the goal
  • Live question: is belief rational?
  • Knowledge of God is morally conditioned
  • Lead: Craig

Stream B · Everyone has faith

  • You trust the pharmacist
  • You trust the oncoming driver
  • You trust the earth keeps spinning
  • Faith = evidence + commitment
  • Lead: Knechtle

Stream C · Category error

  • “What colour is middle C?”
  • A spirit measured by instruments
  • Demanding a sign = manipulation
  • Genesis 1:1 grounds time/space/matter
  • Lead: Knechtle / Perry

Stream D · Testability + will

  • Induction (lives) + abduction (events)
  • Jesus made testable promises
  • People believe against evidence
  • Judas, the Pharisees, the smokers
  • Lead: Lennox / Huff

Stream E · Buried cause

  • Hostile “no evidence” is often anger
  • Name the anger, gently
  • Novosibirsk academic; Cambridge student
  • Befriend before you debate
  • Lead: Lennox

The shared move

  • Stop adding evidence
  • Expose the standard being applied
  • Show no one lives by it
  • Relocate the bottleneck to the will
  • Reframe: rational, not coercive

How each expert deploys it

William Lane Craig1 transcript · architectural
The dominant voice – apatheism & the wrong success-criterion
Signature sequence
  • Russell anecdote as the representative academic posture: “not enough evidence, God.”
  • Diagnosis: most great atheists lost their faith at 11 or 12 – “most of them reject Christianity based on the objections of a 12-year-old.”
  • Decoding the complaint: “not enough to coerce me out of my indifference” – the evidence “isn’t going to grab me by the lapels.”
  • Knowledge of God is sui generis – morally and spiritually conditioned; a spiritually indifferent person can master theology but cannot know God.
  • Biblical warrant: Jeremiah and Jesus ground knowing God in honest seeking.
  • Pascal calibration: enough light for the willing, enough obscurity for the closed – hiddenness is a feature.
The reframe

The evidence is admittedly not coercive – but coercion was never the criterion. The live question is whether there is enough evidence for faith to be rational. Craig is the only expert who supplies the underlying theory; everyone else operates downstream of it.

Cliffe Knechtle3 transcripts
Everyone already lives by faith + the category error
Two signature moves
  • Faith is universal: no one verifies every bottle, every oncoming driver, the earth’s rotation. “All of us have faith.” Good faith is “evidence of reliability plus commitment” – evidence, not proof.
  • The girlfriend-sex analogy: demanding a sign to “prove” God is like demanding sex to prove love – manipulation, not openness.
  • The middle-C analogy: demanding a spirit be scientifically detected is a category error – “what colour is middle C?”
  • Demanding proof rather than evidence is “a dishonest game.”
  • Stuart’s pivot: not sign-waiting but relationship – prayer, fellowship, Scripture.
Counter on the record

Matt Dillahunty (2012): trust is evidence-justified; faith is “the excuse people give for believing something when they don’t have a good reason.” Per the raw extraction, Knechtle restates his own definition rather than refuting the distinction.

John Lennox2 transcripts
Testability + diagnosing the anger
Distinctive moves
  • Christianity is testable: science reasons by induction (repeatable) and abduction (best explanation, for unique events – Poirot). Resurrection and miracles are abductive; transformed lives are inductive.
  • Jesus made testable promises – forgiveness, peace, new power – “I have tested in life and seen it to be true.”
  • Rule: “don’t reject Christianity until you discover what it says – don’t reject on the basis of no knowledge.”
  • Name the anger: hostile resistance is often emotional. Befriend first; then, gently, “you’re angry about it” – the Novosibirsk academic and the Cambridge student.
  • Frames Nagel’s “I hope there is no God” as costly intellectual honesty.
Preston Perry1 transcript
God is not confined to science – Genesis 1:1
The set piece
  • Kelly defines her belief: “evidence that is touchable… matter that’s repeatable… proven by constant tests.”
  • All of science reduces to three categories: time, space, and matter – “you can’t have science without those three things.”
  • The first ten words of Genesis establish all three: “in the beginning” (time), “the heavens” (space), “the earth” (matter).
  • Therefore the Creator precedes and grounds the categories science uses – to demand God fit inside verification is to demand the Creator fit inside his own creation.
  • An internal critique: the sceptic’s own definition of science becomes the wedge.
Wes Huff1 transcript
People believe and disbelieve against the evidence
Distinctive moves
  • Answers “if the evidence is so good, why isn’t everyone convinced?” by denying evidence is the operative variable.
  • People act against clear evidence: a father loses his tongue to cancer; both sons take up smoking anyway.
  • Inside-the-story counterexamples: Judas saw the dead raised and still betrayed Jesus; the Pharisees plotted to kill Lazarus – “what’s killing them gonna do if they could get raised?”
  • Theological turn: “you can’t argue… pray… preach… or heal anyone into the kingdom of God.” Only the Holy Spirit replaces a heart of stone (Ezekiel 36:26).
  • Even Dawkins is not “outside” – “if a Paul can come to Christ… he sure knows the facts.”
Why the concession helps

Conceding that arguments alone do not convert strengthens the evidential case: it locates the failure in the will, not in the data.

Objections handled – cross-expert master list

Objection 1

Faith just is belief without evidence; trust is the evidence-backed thing

“Trust is something that is justified with evidence. And faith is a confidence level that is not supported by evidence… the excuse people give for believing something when they don’t have a good reason.”

Matt Dillahunty, 2012 debate

Strongest response

Good faith is evidence plus commitment

Everyone already lives this way – the pharmacist, the oncoming driver, the earth’s rotation. The demand for proof rather than evidence is a standard the sceptic abandons everywhere else.

Knechtle universal-faith moveRestates, does not refute the trust/faith distinction

Objection 2

Why not just leave God as a matter of faith, not rationality?

“Why can’t we just leave it as a matter of faith whether or not we believe in God?”

Audience, 2012 debate

Strongest response

You have no choice – you already live by faith

The only live question is whether that faith has evidence behind it. “We don’t have a choice. We all have to live by faith.”

Knechtle faith-is-unavoidable

Objection 3

Science keeps replacing supernatural explanations with natural ones

Weather, disease, astronomy were once explained supernaturally and now are explained naturally – making the sceptic “suspicious of anything we explain supernaturally now.”

Jon Steingard

Strongest response

Theism grounds science; and a “naturalism-of-the-gaps”

Galileo and Kepler framed science theistically (“thinking your thoughts after you”); science by nature cannot answer whether a being above the natural order exists. Stuart adds the symmetrical “science-of-the-gaps.”

Knechtle Galileo / KeplerStuart Knechtle naturalism-of-the-gaps

Objection 4

Give me a sign / scientifically prove yourself to me

Belief is reasonable only once God appears on demand or submits to scientific measurement.

Sceptic, long-form podcast

Strongest response

Category error – and manipulation, not openness

Demanding a spirit be physically detected is like asking the colour of middle C. Demanding a sign as proof-of-love is the manipulation of a man who says “prove you love me, have sex with me.”

Knechtle middle-C & girlfriend-sex analogies

Objection 5

Christianity isn’t testable

“Everything’s testable – how could you be a Christian, it’s not testable.”

UPenn audience

Strongest response

It is – abductively and inductively

Unique events (resurrection, miracles) are reasoned abductively; transformed lives are reasoned inductively. Jesus made testable promises. “Don’t reject on the basis of no knowledge.”

Lennox induction/abduction + “just look at me”

Objection 6

God contradicts / can’t be proven by science

“If something doesn’t fit within the frames of time, space and matter… it cannot be proved logically.”

Kelly, street conversation

Strongest response

The Creator cannot be confined to his creation

Science is time + space + matter; Genesis 1:1’s first ten words establish all three; therefore God precedes and grounds the very categories science uses. “God could not be confound to science because he is outside of these things.”

Perry Genesis 1:1 internal critique

Objection 7

If the evidence is so good, why aren’t people convinced?

Widespread unbelief is itself evidence that the case is weak.

Implicit / general

Strongest response

People believe and disbelieve against evidence

Conversion is not purely intellectual: Judas, the Pharisees, and the smokers all acted against clear evidence. The will and the Spirit are required. Craig: “not enough evidence” means “not enough to coerce me.”

Huff will + pneumatologyCraig coercion-decode

Objection 8

Isn’t Russell right – there’s simply not enough evidence?

“I should say not enough evidence, God. Not enough evidence.”

Bertrand Russell, judgment-day anecdote

Strongest response

Rational, not coercive

The evidence is admittedly not coercive – but coercion was never the criterion. It is sufficient to make belief rational for the willing seeker, “if you’re willing to look… with an open mind and an open heart.”

Craig the reframe

Objection 9

Schellenberg’s divine hiddenness – willing seekers still doubt

If a loving God exists, non-resistant non-belief should not occur. But it does: sincere, open seekers remain in doubt – which Pascal’s “obscurity for the closed” does not cover.

J. L. Schellenberg, hiddenness literature

Gap – not engaged

Gestured at, never stated or answered

Craig’s Pascal calibration assumes the doubter is closed; the formal argument from non-resistant non-belief is never put. The amputee/hiddenness case is hand-waved with “God has reasons I don’t understand.”

Aggregation gap

Persistent gaps in the corpus

Central gap

The trust / faith distinction is never refuted

Dillahunty’s claim – that “faith” names confidence exceeding the evidence while “trust” is evidence-proportioned – is the strongest atheist move in the node. Knechtle redefines faith as “evidence plus commitment” and re-asserts it; he never engages the charge of equivocation.

The one objection the corpus most needs to answer

Further gaps

Reformed epistemology, sui generis warrant, scientism

Plantinga’s properly-basic-belief framework would dissolve the whole “not enough evidence” objection but is never invoked – the corpus stays evidentialist throughout. Craig asserts that knowledge of God is uniquely disposition-conditioned but the argument for why (rather than special pleading) is thin. And the anti-scientism premise (science cannot reach the supernatural) is asserted by Knechtle and Perry, never argued against a committed verificationist.
