The nature of saving faith

Knowledge, assent, and trust — the anatomy of what saves. Five experts converge on the same three-part structure, then divide over where the third part comes from. Nine substantive extractions.

The question

What is saving faith made of — intellectual agreement that Christian claims are true, or something more; and if more, where does that “more” come from?

Everyone in the corpus agrees that something called faith is the instrument of salvation. The live question is what that faith consists of. The classical Protestant answer — given here most precisely by William Lane Craig — is that saving faith has three components: knowledge (notitia: understanding what is to be believed), assent (assensus: agreeing it is true), and trust (fiducia: personally relying on Christ). The Latin labels are not used in the corpus, but the tripartite structure is.

Each component, taken alone, is a recognised counterfeit. Knowledge without assent is the theologian who studies a faith he does not hold. Assent without trust is the figure James warns of — “the demons believe, and shudder.” The apologetic payoff is sharp: evidence can compel knowledge and even assent, but it cannot, by itself, produce trust — which is why the evidentialists (Strobel, Lennox) and the Reformed evangelist (Perry) arrive at the same place by different roads, and then disagree about where trust comes from.

The convergent structure — three components, not a syllogism

Only Craig states it as a formal taxonomy, but all five experts instantiate the same anatomy. It is best read as deductive in its core (genuine faith requires all three components) with an abductive test (since trust is invisible, visible fruit is the best evidence it is present).

Component 1 · Knowledge

  • Notitia — grasping the content
  • Craig: “knowledge of the Gospel”
  • Strobel: “reaching the verdict”
  • The evidential route in
  • Necessary, never sufficient

Component 2 · Assent

  • Assensus — agreeing it is true
  • Craig: “he will give his agreement”
  • Strobel: “believing the evidence”
  • Lennox: believing he is God-made-ransom
  • The demons get this far

Component 3 · Trust

  • Fiducia — personal reliance
  • Craig: “trusting in Christ”
  • Strobel: “received… as forgiver and leader”
  • Lennox: “personal commitment”
  • Perry: “the gift of believing”
Premise

Saving faith is by definition knowledge plus assent plus trust.

The componential core (Craig). “First a person will understand what there is to be believed… he will assent to it… he will trust in what he believes to be true.” The three are sequential, and the third is the load-bearing one.

Therefore

Bare assent — knowing and agreeing the claims are true — is necessary but not saving.

The shared negative claim. Strobel reached the historical verdict and still felt “let down”: “it’s not enough, it’s not enough — believed plus receive.” Intellectual conviction is the second component, not the whole.

Conclusion

Genuine trust is invisible — so visible transformation is the best evidence that it is real.

The abductive coda. The Knechtles’ “botanical growth out of gratitude”; Perry’s “tree by its fruit” and “Christianity is not behaviour modification… it’s a heart change.” Fruit is the test of fiducia, not the price of it. The remaining controversy is entirely about the third component: what trust is, and what generates it.

How each expert deploys it

William Lane Craig 1 transcript
The componential anchor — notitia · assensus · fiducia
Signature moves
  • The only formal taxonomy in the corpus — saving faith as knowledge, assent, and trust, stated as three sequential elements. This is the classical Protestant notitia–assensus–fiducia schema, unlabelled with the Latin.
  • Calvinist ordering — the definition is embedded in regeneration-prior-to-faith: “this kind of saving faith is something that only a regenerate person can exercise.”
  • Conversion as fruit, not cause — Bible-reading, prayer, baptism, Christian identity follow later as the fruit of regeneration, not the root of faith.
  • Monergistic epistemology of trust — the third component is not manufactured by the unaided will. This is the deepest seam in the node: Craig grounds fiducia in prior divine regeneration; the evidentialists ground assent in public evidence.
Scope note — the classical, not the analytic, anchor

Craig presents the tripartite definition as common ground within the Calvinist scheme, not a contested point. He does not engage the contemporary refinements — faith-as-trust-without-belief, Stump’s negative-act account, Evans on trust vs propositional assent — that live at Q-9.5.2 / Q-9.5.3. Cross-reference, but do not conflate.

Lee Strobel 1 transcript
“Believe + receive = become” — assent vs trust, made lay
Signature moves
  • Assent alone is insufficient — lived, not theorised. Having concluded the resurrection was historical, he felt “let down”: “shouldn’t an angel appear about now… it was kind of a let-down after two years.”
  • John 1:12 as the structural verse — “believed plus receive”: belief (knowledge + assent) plus reception (trust) yields the status of child of God.
  • Transactional / legal-imputation register — the free gift “Jesus purchased for me… when he died as my substitute to pay for all of my sin.”
  • Inverts the apologist’s own frame — the evidence-builder’s sermon closes by declaring evidence necessary but not sufficient; the appeal is decisional. “You might believe but you’re not sure if you’ve ever received.”
Preston Perry 3 transcripts
Faith-as-gift — the epistemology of trust
Signature moves
  • Faith is a gift, not a self-generated belief. Against “it’s hard to believe” he does not push proof; he diagnoses the difficulty biblically (the natural man cannot discern; Satan veils the mind) and reframes faith as a divine grant.
  • Diligent seeking as the prescribed method — modelled on his own testimony: “God, I don’t believe you’re real, but if you are, show yourself to me” (Jer 29:13).
  • Pre-emptive biographical credentials — South Side, gang-banging, drug-dealing, getting shot at — deployed against the “you were brainwashed” objection before it is raised.
  • The object: penal substitution as rescue — “he who knew no sin became sin so that we can become the righteousness of God”; the cross is not cosmic cruelty but the resolution of righteousness and love.
  • The test: heart-change, not behaviour-modification — the fake track-star who never ran impeaches the liar, not the faith (Matt 7). Closes with homework, not a sinner’s prayer.
Convergence with Craig

Perry’s faith-as-gift lands him alongside Craig’s monergism and against the evidentialists: trust is granted, not extracted. His refusal to push a decision (“read Galatians and Romans… if it doesn’t challenge you, walk away”) is the practical expression of that conviction.

John Lennox 1 transcript
The object and structure of saving faith
Signature moves
  • Commonality before difference — establishes moral common ground first (“the golden rule… is found in every single religion on earth”) before drawing any distinction.
  • The radical inversion — merit-systems are “very like the University of Pennsylvania… it depends on your merit.” True Christianity accepts you “at the beginning of the path”; the first step “is not a rite, a ritual or a ceremony… it’s a step of personal commitment to Jesus Christ.”
  • The content of trust is specified — that commitment “involves believing that he is God who has come… to give his life as a ransom.” Knowledge and assent feed directly into the act of trust.
  • Resurrection as evidential warrant — the only expert to tie saving faith explicitly to the historical apologetic: “the evidence… is that God raised Jesus from the dead.” The cleanest contrast with Perry’s Spirit-granted account.
  • Assurance built in — John 5:24: whoever believes “has eternal life — that is, has it already.”
Generous concession (signature Lennox)

“Even non-religious friends can put professing religious people to shame by their moral behaviour.” He concedes moral parity to disarm the “Christians think they’re superior” objection before drawing the grace-versus-merit line.

Cliffe & Stuart Knechtle 3 transcripts
The fruit-test — telling fiducia from bare assensus
Signature moves
  • “Botanical growth” — sanctification is the organic outgrowth of trust, “always botanical growth out of gratitude.” Fruit, not root. Recurs across two transcripts.
  • The “putting Jesus back on the cross” reductio — a works-based reading “did not die for your sins… it’s by your own works,” denying the atonement it professes (assent mistaken for merit-keeping).
  • Paul-and-James synthesis — complementary, not contradictory: Paul on grace, James on “the company you keep” that produces good works (against cheap grace, i.e. assent without transformation).
  • Pharisee-fate diagnostic — they “were probably tithing more than the majority of Christians” and were not saved; external works without grace doesn’t save.
  • Cliffe’s adjacent forgiveness criterion + the prison-inmate imago Dei anecdote anchor the costliness of the grace that faith receives: “nothing so bad… that God can’t forgive… but he’s not going to force his forgiveness on me.”
Distinctive gloss (Stuart)

His idiosyncratic anxiety-as-idolatry reading of the “anxious” among the Lake-of-Fire passages — anxiety as an idol made of something other than God. A Stuart-distinctive psychological-theological move (cross-ref Q-16.6.4).

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

Assent is enough — if I’m convinced Jesus rose, that’s saving faith.

If I am intellectually persuaded by the historical evidence, surely that conviction is the faith that saves. Faith just means agreeing the claims are true.

Implicit in evidentialist apologetics

Strongest response

Believe plus receive

John 1:12 distinguishes concluding the evidence from receiving the gift. Strobel reached the verdict and still felt “let down” — assent is only the second of three components; trust is the third.

Strobel John 1:12 formulaCraig componential definition

Objection 2

“It’s hard for me to believe” — belief can’t be willed.

I can’t just make myself believe something that doesn’t feel real. So how can faith be required for salvation?

Sabrina (Perry)

Strongest response

Faith is a gift; the method is diligent seeking

Scripture predicts the difficulty (the natural man cannot discern; Satanic veiling). Faith is a gift, not self-generated. The response is not to wait for proof but to ask God to reveal himself.

Perry 1 Cor 2:14, 2 Cor 4:4, Jer 29:13

Objection 3

Cheap grace — “just say sorry and do whatever you want.”

Christian grace is too easy: sin freely, ask forgiveness, repeat — and you still go to heaven. Assent costs nothing.

Folk Christianity; the “Kiran-nightly syllogism” (Stuart)

Strongest response

Paul and James together

Grace saves, but Paul himself warns against grieving the Spirit and stresses sanctification. Cheap grace is a caricature, not Pauline grace — genuine trust bears fruit.

Stuart Knechtle Paul-and-James synthesis

Objection 4

Works-righteousness — “be good enough and you get in.”

Surely salvation comes down to whether your good deeds outweigh your bad — just like every other religion.

Every merit-religion; folk Christianity

Strongest response

A works-faith puts Jesus back on the cross

If works save, the atonement was unnecessary — the position denies the very cross it professes. Merit-systems guarantee nothing: “it depends on your merit.” Trust, not merit, is the instrument.

Stuart Knechtle reductioLennox UPenn analogy

Objection 5

Christianity is arrogant — it looks down on other faiths.

Christians treat themselves as morally superior and everyone else as lost.

Sabrina (Perry); Veritas audience (Lennox)

Strongest response

Concede moral parity; the difference is structural

Establish commonality first; concede non-religious people can morally shame Christians. The distinction is the structure of faith — grace received at the beginning vs merit earned at the end — not a claim of moral superiority.

Lennox commonality-before-differencePerry dignity-first

Objection 6

The cross is cosmic cruelty — why crush an innocent son?

If God is love, how could it “please” him to crush his own sinless son? The object of this trust sounds monstrous.

Anticipated by Perry (Matt, Trayvon)

Strongest response

The resolution of righteousness and love

Not arbitrary cruelty: divine righteousness must judge sin; divine love will not destroy us. So the Father judges sin in the Son rather than in us — which is what trust rests on.

Perry Isaiah 53:10 / 2 Cor 5:21

Objection 7

Won’t God just forgive everyone? (universalism)

My aunts and grandmothers have always said God forgives everybody in the end — so a faith-response can’t really be necessary.

Trayvon, citing church-going relatives

Strongest response

The good-judge analogy

A judge who fails to judge crimes is a bad judge. God’s judgment of sin is an expression of goodness. Repentance and trust are here-and-now — so you meet him as father, not only judge.

Perry good-judge analogy

Objection 8

Hypocrites discredit the faith they profess.

Christians preach one thing and live another — that discredits the whole religion and its claim to transform.

Sabrina (Perry)

Strongest response

Tree-by-its-fruit / track-star analogy

A fake track star who never ran impeaches the liar, not running. A professed Christian who lives like an atheist impeaches himself, not the faith — he had assent without trust. The absence of fruit is the diagnostic.

Perry Matt 7 fruit criterion

Objection 9

God’s forgiveness must have a limit (Hitler; blasphemy of the Spirit).

Surely some acts are unforgivable — and the Bible itself names an “unforgivable sin.” So trust can’t be all it takes.

George (Knechtle interview)

Strongest response

No act too bad — but God respects free will

“There is nothing so bad… that God can’t forgive.” The “unforgivable” is persistent voluntary refusal of trust (Romans 1), not a discrete act — God finally gives you up to your own desires.

Cliffe & Stuart Knechtle Romans 1 reframe

Objection 10

How do I know I’ve truly received? (assurance vs presumption)

If the Pharisees thought they were righteous and were not, how can I be sure my own “reception” is genuine trust and not the same self-deception?

Implicit in the Pharisee-fate argument

Gap — largely unaddressed

The fruit-test is asserted, never turned on the believer

“Believe + receive = become” and the botanical-fruit test are asserted, but no expert engages how the believer distinguishes genuine fiducia from self-deception — the very worry the Pharisee argument raises against everyone else. The deepest internal gap in the node.

Aggregation gap

Objection 11

If faith is a sovereign gift, what is the evidential apologetic for?

Perry and Craig say trust is God-granted; Strobel and Lennox build the case on public evidence. If the Spirit must grant fiducia, why marshal evidence at all — and if evidence suffices, why call faith a gift?

The monergism–evidentialism seam (internal)

Gap — present but never adjudicated

Both accounts sit side by side, unreconciled

The corpus contains both a gift-of-faith account (Perry, Craig) and an evidence-warrants-assent account (Strobel, Lennox) without anyone addressing how they fit. The reconciliation lives at the seam of Reformed epistemology (Q-17.5.x) and classical evidentialism, and is not attempted here.

Aggregation gap

Objection 12

What of those who never had the knowledge component — the unevangelised?

If saving faith begins with knowledge of the gospel, what happens to my Buddhist Cambodian mother, or the good person who never heard, and so could never assent or trust?

Sabrina (Perry); George re “Sarah” (Knechtle)

Gap — meta-question answered, pastoral question dodged

Why Christianity is exclusive, not what happens to my mum

Both interlocutors raise it; neither gets a direct answer. The experts defend why Christianity is exclusive but never the specific case of someone who never received the content to assent to. Primarily a Q-9.6.x / Q-9.7.x concern, but it bears on the necessity of the knowledge-component here.

Aggregation gap

Persistent gaps in the corpus

Gap 1

The assurance / self-deception problem is the largest internal gap.

The Pharisee-fate argument turns the worry against everyone else — they thought they were righteous and were not — but no expert then turns it on the believer. How do I know my reception is genuine fiducia? “Believe + receive = become” and the fruit-test are asserted, never stress-tested against the sincere self-deceiver.

Gap 2

The classical–analytic split is unbridged.

Craig gives the classical tripartite definition and explicitly does not engage the contemporary debates — faith-as-trust-without-belief, Stump’s negative-act account, Evans on trust vs propositional assent — that live at Q-9.5.2 / Q-9.5.3. The corpus has the textbook answer but not the modern refinements.

Gap 3

The monergism-vs-evidentialism tension is present but never adjudicated.

Perry and Craig hold that trust is a Spirit-granted gift (regeneration prior to faith); Strobel and Lennox present a route in which public evidence warrants the assent the will then commits to. Both sit in the corpus side by side, and no one addresses the obvious seam between Reformed epistemology and classical evidentialism.

Gap 4

The deathbed / last-second-repentance edge case is raised and dropped.

It directly tests whether trust can be genuine without the fruit the Knechtles make the test of genuineness. Cliffe gestures at “I have to repent” but never engages the case that most pressures a fruit-as-evidence account.

Gap 5

The unevangelised question is answered at the meta-level only.

Two interlocutors surface the question of saving faith for those who never had the knowledge component. The experts answer why Christianity is exclusive but never the specific pastoral case. Primarily Q-9.6.x / Q-9.7.x, but it bears on whether the knowledge-component is strictly necessary.
