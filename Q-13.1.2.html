What does the image of God consist in?

Substantive, relational, or functional? A single-voice deep-dive: William Lane Craig’s three-part case that the image is man’s ontological resemblance to God as a personal being — and that dominion is grounded in that ontology, not a rival to it.

The question

When Scripture says man is made “in the image of God,” what does the image consist in — a set of properties man possesses, a relationship he stands in, or a role he performs?

Genesis 1:26–27 asserts the image but never defines it, so the doctrine has been systematised in competing ways. The apologetic stakes sit downstream: which construal is correct determines whether the imago Dei can ground inherent human dignity — and a purely functional image, in particular, looks unable to do that work for the human who cannot yet (or can no longer) function: the foetus, the comatose, the senile.

This is a single-voice node — an honest deep-dive into one teacher’s sustained treatment rather than a convergence of many. The whole page is built from Craig’s Doctrine of Man sequence (Defenders class, Parts 3–5, 2020): catalogue → verdict → application. It is correspondingly strong on the resolution and thin on adversarial pressure-testing of the rivals; both are flagged below.

The three construals — the menu to be evaluated

Craig (Part 3) sorts the historic systematisations into three families — cross-cutting the older Catholic / Reformation split over whether image and likeness are two things or one. Each answers “what is the image?” differently.

Substantive · Ontological

  • The image is a feature of man’s constitution
  • Classically: the rational soul
  • Aristotle’s “rational animal” / the Logos (John 1)
  • The shared Catholic & Reformation root
  • Content (Part 5): self-consciousness, rationality, freedom of the will

Relational

  • Not a thing man has but a relation he stands in
  • The I–Thou (first-person) relation
  • With other persons and with God
  • Sub-variant: our relative freedom of the will
  • Sub-variant: our answerability / accountability to God

Functional

  • Neither a component nor a relation but a role
  • Man’s God-given lordship / dominion over the earth
  • Functioning as God’s royal representative
  • The reading with “virtual unanimity” among OT scholars (Middleton)
  • Grounded in ANE royal ideology

The convergent case — WLC’s verdict for the substantive view

Not a single syllogism but a cumulative case: several independent considerations, exegetical and philosophical, each insufficient alone, converging on the verdict that the substantive construal is “practically unavoidable” — with the functional reading grounded in ontology rather than opposed to it.

Stream 1 · The fall datum

The image is not lost in the fall — so it is not original righteousness.

With the Reformers, image and likeness are synonymous (Hebrew parallelism, not two tiers). But against the Reformers, Genesis 9:6 calls fallen man God’s image — clearing away the construal of the image as the righteousness Adam lost.

Stream 2 · The prepositions

The Hebrew grammar cannot settle the question.

Whether the image is ontological, relational or functional turns partly on be (“in”) and ke (“according to”). These are not decisive: ke cannot bear an identity sense, and Genesis 5:1–3 reverses the prepositions for Seth. The grammar will not adjudicate.

Stream 3 · The functionalist’s own admission

The functional reading presupposes the substantive one.

Middleton concedes in a footnote (p. 27) that functional and relational readings are themselves metaphysical — they ground the image in personal agency. But personal agency is a property of personal agents, not a function. The functional reading collapses back into the substantive one.

Stream 4 · The asymmetry

Functions are grounded in ontology, not vice-versa.

The relation between ontology and function is asymmetric. A thing’s having an essential function — even an essential one — is still not the same as the thing’s definition.

Stream 5 · The ANE parallels mis-fire

The strongest Near-Eastern parallels do not yield a functional reading.

The best Egyptian/Mesopotamian texts are incarnational (the king or idol embodies the deity) — a metaphysical claim irrelevant to anti-iconic Judaism. The absent-king-statue parallel fails too: Genesis never depicts an earth from which God is absent, and a statue merely represents authority rather than functioning in the king’s place.

Stream 6 · Seth — the decisive point

Seth was “in Adam’s image” without being Adam’s co-regent.

Genesis 5:1–3 says Seth was born in Adam’s image and likeness — yet Seth held no dominion over Adam. So the image cannot be the dominion-function. The functionalist rejoinder (that “image of” is transitive) fails: resemblance is not transitive — a granddaughter need not resemble her grandmother.

Stream 7 · The phenomenology of personhood

Reflection on personhood supplies the content: self-consciousness, rationality, freedom of the will.

These three are sufficient for personhood. Man has all three (finitely); higher animals lack rationality (no language / syntax) and therefore freedom. This is what the ontological resemblance to God is.

Conclusion

The image of God is man’s ontological resemblance to God as a personal being. Dominion is a real, essential, God-given function — but grounded in that ontology, not identical with it.

Man is like God precisely in being personal; in his finitude he is like the rest of creation. So the three construals are “not mutually exclusive alternatives”: design / ontology grounds relationship and function alike. The substantive view is “practically unavoidable.”

How the case is built — one voice, three movements

William Lane Craig 3 transcripts · Defenders, 2020
Catalogue → verdict → application — a single argument in three lessons
Part 3 · Systematising the data (the catalogue)
  • Two layers: the confessional split (Catholic two-tier — image = rational soul, impaired but not lost; likeness = original righteousness, lost in the fall) vs the Reformers (image just is likeness = righteousness, so lost in the fall).
  • Flags the Reformers’ consistency problem: image declared lost, yet a “general image” preserved (Luther: man “almost lost” it; Calvin: “a relic of the image of God remains”).
  • Lays out the cross-cutting trio — substantive (rational soul; Aristotle; the Logos), relational (I–Thou; freedom; answerability), functional (dominion).
  • Defers the verdict but signals the integrative lean: these are “not mutually exclusive alternatives.”
Part 4 · Evaluating the construals (the verdict)
  • Agrees with the Reformers that image = likeness; against them that the image survives the fall (Genesis 9:6).
  • Signature internal critique of Richard Middleton’s The Liberating Image: quotes him at length, grants the royal flavour of Genesis 1, then turns Middleton’s own footnote (p. 27) against the thesis.
  • Taxonomy-engineering: carves off a fourth “incarnational” category for the strongest ANE parallels precisely to quarantine it as irrelevant to anti-iconic Judaism; shows the two ANE parallel-sets support contradictory readings.
  • The rhetorical hinge: the Seth / non-transitivity-of-resemblance point (Genesis 5:1–3), treated as “decisive.”
Part 5 · Man as a personal being (the application)
  • The infinite-personal-God frame (borrowed from Schaeffer): like God in the personal dimension, like the beasts in the finite dimension — “a hominid body akin to that of the great apes.”
  • The three attributes sufficient for personhood: self-consciousness, rationality, freedom of the will.
  • The contrast case: chimps lack abstract thought (no language) — “even if they have souls they do not have rational souls” — and so lack freedom.
  • Methodological candour: names his method as phenomenology, grants Genesis never defines the image, and twice disclaims certainty.
Signature move

The whole verdict rests on an internal critique: Middleton’s own footnote — that functional and relational interpretations are “strictly speaking metaphysical” — is used to detonate Middleton’s functionalist thesis from within. Craig grants the exegesis and lets the footnote do the work.

Concede-and-redirect

Dominion is conceded as a real and even essential function: “God has then given man this responsibility to rule the earth.” But it is redirected, not surrendered — the image is the ontological resemblance that enables the function. An essential function is still not the definition of the thing.

Objections handled — cross-source master list

Single-voice, so the source line tracks which lesson fields the objection and who raises it — Craig anticipates some; students raise others in live Q&A.

Objection 1

The image just is the dominion-function

Rule is “a necessary and inseparable purpose and therefore virtually constitutive of the image.” The functional reading enjoys virtual unanimity among OT scholars.

Richard Middleton, The Liberating Image

Strongest response

An essential function is not a definition

A thing’s having an essential function is just not the same as the thing’s definition; the function is still grounded in what the thing is. “Middleton’s conclusion is over-drawn.”

Craig Part 4 — concede-and-redirect

Objection 2

The functional reading has “virtual unanimity” among OT scholars

The weight of Old Testament scholarship, on ANE comparative grounds, has converged on a functional construal of the image.

Middleton (sociological appeal)

Strongest response

The consensus admits it is metaphysical

Middleton’s own footnote concedes the functional and relational readings are “strictly speaking metaphysical” — grounding the image in personal agency, which is a property, not a function. By its own admission the consensus presupposes ontology.

Craig Part 4 — internal critique

Objection 3

The ANE royal-image texts make the image functional

Mesopotamian and Egyptian texts call a king or idol “the image of a god” in a royal / representative sense — so Genesis means dominion.

Middleton’s comparative evidence

Strongest response

Those parallels are incarnational, and irrelevant

The strongest parallels mean the king / idol embodies the deity — better called “incarnational,” a metaphysical claim an anti-iconic religion like Judaism “would have recoiled at.” The absent-king-statue parallel fails because Genesis never depicts an earth from which God is absent.

Craig Part 4 — taxonomy-engineering

Objection 4

“Image of” is transitive — so Seth-in-Adam’s-image entails Seth-in-God’s-image

If Seth is in Adam’s image and Adam is in God’s image, then Seth is in God’s image — the functional reading survives the Seth verse.

Functionalist rejoinder

Strongest response

Resemblance is not transitive

A daughter may resemble her mother, and the mother her own mother, yet the granddaughter need not resemble the grandmother. Seth being “in Adam’s image” just means Adam brought forth a being like himself.

Craig Part 4 — the decisive point

Objection 5

Doesn’t the image include man’s God-given responsibility?

Scripture ties the image to the expectation that man will carry out God’s plan — so the responsibility to rule must be part of the image.

Student, Part 4

Strongest response

Real and essential — but not the definition

Conceded: the responsibility is real and even essential. God created man in order that he might represent God’s reign. But the image is the ontological resemblance that enables the responsibility; an essential function is not the definition of the thing.

Craig Part 4 — concede-and-redirect

Objection 6

How could the obviously-mortal Pharaoh be regarded as divine?

The Egyptians embalmed and entombed the Pharaoh — so how could they have thought a manifestly mortal man was a god?

Student “Steve,” Part 4

Strongest response

Because he incarnates the deity

From Latin in + caro, “in the flesh”: the cult statue embodies the god (being stone), the Pharaoh incarnates it in flesh. That is precisely why a mortal man could be called divine — and why the parallel is incarnational, not functional.

Craig / Middleton Part 4

Objection 7

John Walton’s temple-creation view parallels the image

Walton reads Genesis 1 as the inauguration of the cosmos as God’s temple — doesn’t that support a functional / representative construal?

Student (re John Walton), Part 4

Strongest response

Walton actually cuts against the statue parallel

If the world is God’s temple in which he resides, he needs no surrogate cult-statue — “he’s there himself.” Craig is “not persuaded by Walton,” but the two ANE parallel-sets appealed to for the functional reading support contradictory interpretations.

Craig Part 4 — reductio-style pressure

Objection 8

The image is a “Trinity of man” — body / soul / spirit

Man images God by being tripartite: the body like the Son, the soul like the Spirit, the spirit like the Father — a mirror of the Trinity.

Student, Part 3

Strongest response

Two discussions conflated — and it folds into the substantive view

The vestigia Trinitatis question is distinct from the image question, and the tripartite mapping “would have a hard time” finding biblical warrant. But if adopted, it is simply a version of the substantive view — the image rooted in human structure.

Craig Part 3 — concede-and-redirect

Objection 9

“Image” means man physically looks like God

Is there a view on which the image is corporeal — God has a human-like face and man resembles it physically?

Student, Part 3

Strongest response

The final author rules it out

Some hold this as the “crude” original meaning, but the final author of Genesis 1 portrays God as transcendent — “in the beginning God created the heavens and the earth” — so he cannot have meant a physical, anthropomorphic likeness, whatever the oral prehistory.

Craig Part 3 — final-author hermeneutic

Objection 10

Isn’t a soul by definition rational?

If having a soul just means being rational, then the talk of animals having non-rational souls is incoherent.

Student, Part 5

Strongest response

Not on the classical view

For Aristotle and the medievals, sentient beings — horses, dogs, cats — have souls but not rational souls: “poorer,” less richly endowed souls than the uniquely rational human soul. Craig cites Eccles and Moreland and declines a settled position (“not my area of expertise”).

Craig Part 5 — appeal to authority, with disclaimer

Objection 11

Doesn’t this match Boethius — and resolve abortion?

A person is “an individual substance of a rational nature” (Boethius). Doesn’t that mean a foetus, as a rational substance, is already a person?

Student “Cody,” Part 5

Endorsed — but with an unflagged shift

“A rational substance by nature”

Craig affirms it as “a wonderful clarification”: even though a foetus is not currently functioning rationally, it is “still a rational substance by nature.” But this quietly shifts the criterion from actual functioning (the lecture line) to capacity / by-nature — a shift left unreconciled.

Craig / Beckwith Part 5Criterion shift — see Gap 2

Objection 12

Are the attributes from Scripture — and can we even know our own image?

Where do self-consciousness, rationality and freedom come from — the text or observation? And since “we see through a glass darkly,” can we really know what our image is?

Student, Part 5

Strongest response

Phenomenology, openly — not proof-texting

From observation and reflection, not Scripture: “the scripture isn’t a philosophy book,” and the image is never defined in the text. “You reflect on the phenomena of what it is to be a person.” The “glass darkly” verse is not about what it is to be a person; certainty is disclaimed.

Craig Part 5 — methodological candour

Persistent gaps in the corpus

Gap 1

The relational construal is barely evaluated on its own terms

The whole argument is substantive vs functional. The relational view (I–Thou; freedom; answerability) is dispatched only obliquely, via Middleton’s footnote lumping relational with functional as “metaphysical.”

Structural omission across Parts 3–5

Gap — must be inferred

No standalone verdict against the relational view

Craig never separately argues why the image is not constituted by relationship rather than by the ontology that makes relationship possible. A reader wanting that verdict has to reconstruct it from the function-grounded-in-ontology move.

Aggregation gap

Gap 2

“Sufficient for personhood” is asserted, then quietly switched

Part 5 calls self-consciousness + rationality + freedom “sufficient for personhood” — an actual-functioning criterion. The abortion rescue then shifts to “a rational substance by nature even if it can’t yet think” — a capacity account.

Internal tension; secular bioethics (Singer, Tooley)

Gap — the most important unreconciled tension

The switch is never flagged. It matters because the same functional marks are used by secular bioethics in the opposite direction — to deny personhood to the foetus and the severely impaired. Craig does not engage that literature, and the actual-vs-potential criterion is left unreconciled.

Aggregation gap

Gap 3

A philosopher overturning a biblical-studies consensus on philosophical grounds

Craig reports Middleton’s claimed “virtual unanimity among Old Testament scholars” for the functional reading and answers it philosophically.

Methodological asymmetry

Gap — asymmetry never flagged

He never addresses why the OT guild converged where it did, nor whether a philosophical mode adequately answers a consensus built on ANE comparative grounds. He argues as a philosophical theologian against a biblical-studies consensus; the class is not told.

Aggregation gap

Gap 4

The chimp-language premise is dated and load-bearing

“Chimps cannot learn a language / cannot learn syntax” is presented as “generally agreed among primatologists” — and it carries the weight of the animals-lack-rationality conclusion.

Empirical premise; the Kanzi disputes

Gap — asserted, not defended

The well-known ape-language disputes (and the bonobo Kanzi, alluded to by a student) are met anecdotally rather than argued, even though the premise is doing the real load-bearing work for the contrast with personhood.

Aggregation gap

Gap 5

The Eastern Orthodox position is outside his notes

Asked about the Orthodox view of the image, Craig concedes “I didn’t have that as part of my notes.”

Live Q&A, Part 3

Gap — low confidence

He only suspects it resembles the Catholic (original-righteousness-vs-rational-soul) view. The Orthodox construal is genuinely unrepresented in the corpus.

Aggregation gap

Gap 6

The naturalism contrast is named, not argued

Atkins and Dawkins are invoked as the foil — “what an exalted view of man this is compared to the naturalistic view.”

Part 5 close

Gap — rhetorical only

No argument against the naturalistic anthropology is offered here; the contrast functions as a closing flourish rather than a defended claim.
