The question

What does Christian theology hold angels to be — and on what basis?

This is the descriptive core of §22 Angelology: the existence, ontological nature, and functional work of the unseen created order. William Lane Craig supplies the systematic spine across two 2018 Defenders lectures — a seven-attribute nature catalogue and a five-office work catalogue, each built as a cumulative proof-text walk.

But the corpus is lopsided, and the honest framing must say so up front. This material is catechesis, not apologetics: WLC argues the nature and work of beings whose reality is simply assumed within the biblical frame. He never defends the existence of the spirit-world against a naturalist who grants none of it. The single genuinely apologetic move is narrow and internal — the “third man” infinite-regress refutation of angels-as-mediators.

The cumulative case — converging scriptural descriptions

This node is not a syllogism. Like the Christology case, it is a descriptive cumulative synthesis — angelology is assembled by stacking converging biblical descriptions into a composite doctrine. Strands A and B are WLC’s pure Defenders catechesis (no opponent in view); only Strand C holds material with an adversary.

Strand A · Nature (WLC)

  • Created & contingent (anti-dualism)
  • Innumerable (“Lord of hosts”)
  • Ranked / hierarchical
  • Powerful
  • Incorporeal / invisible
  • Spatially unbound
  • Wise (finite, creaturely)
  • Able to assume human form

Strand B · Work (WLC)

  • Guide the destiny of nations (Dan 10)
  • Minister to God’s people (Heb 1:14)
  • Execute God’s justice (2 Kgs 19; Acts 12)
  • Gather the living elect at the return (Matt 24)
  • Accompany the dead in Christ (1 Thess 4)

Strand C · Identity & defence

  • The “third man” reductio (WLC)
  • Angel of the Lord: deity (McLatchie)
  • … vs law of agency (Ismail / Xavier)

How each expert deploys it

William Lane Craig 2 transcripts (Defenders 19 & 20)
The systematic spine — nature & work
Definition
  • Angels are a distinct created order — “unembodied minds who serve the Lord,” not part of the physical universe yet dependent on God (Col 1:16).
  • Malakh / angelos both mean messenger: “angel” is a function-word, not an ontology-word.
The seven-attribute nature catalogue
  • Created & contingent, innumerable, ranked, powerful, incorporeal, spatially unbound, wise, and able to assume human form.
  • The created/contingent attribute is an explicit anti-dualism guardrail, pre-positioning the doctrine he needs against Satan-dualism in Part 22.
The five offices of angelic work
  • Over nations — “special connections with various nations” who “actually do battle with one another” (Dan 10).
  • Ministry to believers — minister means “to serve food and drink” (Elijah, the wilderness, Gethsemane; Ps 91). The pastoral payoff.
  • Executing justice — the destroying angel (185,000 Assyrians; Herod; the bowls of wrath).
  • Gathering the living at the second coming (Matt 24) and accompanying the dead in Christ at the resurrection (1 Thess 4).
Deliberate minimalism

WLC’s signature texture here is restraint: he refuses to speculate beyond the text on the national-princes material and carefully separates the rationales for angels he endorses from those he rejects, rather than swallowing the tradition whole.

Jonathan McLatchie 2 transcripts (cross-filed)
The adversarial strand — the Angel of the Lord
The contested identity
  • The malak of Yahweh in many texts is identified as Yahweh himself — “the visible Yahweh who reveals the invisible Yahweh” (John 1:18 pattern).
  • Cumulative stack: the Angel bears Yahweh’s Name (Exod 23:21), is “the God of Bethel” (Gen 31), removes iniquity (Zech 3), accepts worship (Josh 5), and appears as two Yahwehs in Gen 18–19.
  • Strongest move: the “we shall surely die, for we have seen God” reaction-texts (Judg 13, Gen 32) read against the Exodus 33 rule — no one sees God’s face and lives — turning the agency reading into a contradiction.
  • Hostile witness: the Aramaic Targum (Pseudo-Jonathan on Gen 19) shows ancient Jewish reception of two divine persons (the Memra tradition).
Cross-filed

This strand’s fuller home is Q-22.7 and Q-3.4.1. The agency rebuttal (Ismail / Xavier: “the sent one cannot be the sender”) is the standard biblical-Unitarian reply — and McLatchie does not directly dismantle the agency framework itself.

The “third man” argument — the one genuinely apologetic move

The only properly philosophical manoeuvre in the WLC corpus on angels, and the only place he argues against a position rather than merely describing the biblical data. A classic internal-critique reductio, glossed for a lay Sunday-school class.

Target rationale

A transcendent, immaterial God needs angels as mediators to act on the material world.

The Platonic / Demiurge logic: because God is “beyond time and space ... immaterial ... pure spirit,” he must create an intermediary tier of beings to communicate with and work in the physical realm.

The reductio

A mediator would itself require a mediator — an infinite regress.

If God needs a mediator to act on matter, he equally needs one to act on that mediator: “there would have to be some sort of mediator between him and the angels ... and that leads to an infinite regress.” This is Plato’s own “third man” problem turned against the rationale — “and so on and so on ad infinitum.”

Positive defeater

No mediating tier is needed — God already acts immediately in nature.

“When we looked at miracles we saw that this is exactly what miracles are” — direct divine action in the spatio-temporal world. The miracles doctrine does the work the mediation rationale was invented to do.

Corrective conclusion

Angels are manifesters of God to man, not mediators.

A weaker surviving rationale (Lovejoy’s “great chain of being”) is allowed — angels express the fullness of creation — but explicitly not because God needs them to bridge the gap. The double edge: the same regress logic sharpens the parsimony objection against this surviving rationale, which WLC does not then defend (see gaps).

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

A transcendent immaterial God needs intermediary beings to act on matter

Because God is beyond time, space, and matter, he must create angels as mediators between the immaterial realm and the spatio-temporal world he wishes to act upon.

Plato / Demiurge rationale (WLC’s own setup)

Strongest response

The “third man” infinite regress + immediate divine action

A mediator would itself need a mediator, ad infinitum. And no mediating tier is required, because God already acts immediately in nature — “this is exactly what miracles are.” Angels are manifesters, not mediators.

WLC internal-critique reductio

Objection 2

“Deity is never an angel; the sent one cannot be the sender”

An angel is by definition a messenger of someone. The agent represents the principal but is never identical with him — the Jewish law of agency explains every “Angel = Yahweh” identification.

Ismail; Carlos Xavier (agency view)

Strongest response

The Angel exercises divine prerogatives agency cannot grant

The Angel bears Yahweh’s Name (Exod 23:21), forgives / removes iniquity (Zech 3), and accepts worship (Josh 5). Agency does not extend to the divine acts the texts assign him.

McLatchie name-bearing & divine-act stack

Objection 3

Theophanies cannot be literal — “no one sees God and lives”

If theophanies were literal sightings of Yahweh, Isaiah would have died in Isaiah 6. So the “Angel of the Lord” encounters must be read non-literally — not as appearances of God himself.

Carlos Xavier

Strongest response

The same criterion turned the other way

The “we shall surely die, for we have seen God” reactions (Judg 13, Gen 32) prove the witnesses perceived Yahweh himself. The Exodus 33 rule makes the agency reading incoherent, not the deity reading.

McLatchie parity-with-Exodus-33 hermeneutic

Objection 4

Genesis 19:24 is a Hebrew subject-repetition idiom, not two Yahwehs

“Yahweh rained from Yahweh” is the same idiom as Gen 17:23 (“Abraham ... from Abraham’s house”) or 1 Kgs 8:1 (“Solomon ... unto Solomon”) — one subject named twice, not two divine persons.

Carlos Xavier

Standoff — not decisively closed

Targum reception vs the idiom counter

McLatchie cites Pseudo-Jonathan reading the Word of the Lord descending from the Lord — ancient Jewish reception as two persons. But the subject-repetition idiom is the standard Unitarian reply, and the corpus does not decisively settle it.

McLatchie Targum hostile witnessIdiom counter unresolved

Objection 5

Prophets are also “messengers of the Lord” — yet not divine

Haggai and Malachi are called “the messenger of the Lord” (Mal 2:7) — the same Hebrew phrase — without being the second person of the Trinity. The title alone proves nothing.

Carlos Xavier (parity argument)

Strongest response

Function-word agreed — but the divine acts are not shared

Both sides grant malak is a function-word (“messenger”). But the Angel-of-Yahweh texts assign divine acts — name-bearing, sin-forgiving, worship-receiving — that the prophetic “messenger” texts never do.

McLatchie lexical concession + divine-act distinction

Objection 6

The “Angel is Jesus” claim breaks at Matthew 1:24

Joseph meets the Angel of the Lord while Jesus is in Mary’s womb. If the Angel were the pre-incarnate Christ, he could not be appearing to Joseph at the same moment Christ is in utero.

Ismail

Gap — largely unanswered

McLatchie does not engage the simultaneity

A real difficulty for the Angel-as-pre-incarnate-Son reading, left on the table. (It bites less against an Angel-as-self-manifestation-of-Yahweh reading, but McLatchie does not draw that distinction here.)

Persistent gaps in the corpus

Gap 1 — the largest

The threshold naturalist objection is never raised

Why think any incorporeal beings exist at all, rather than that “angel” reports are legend, vision, or psychological projection? The whole question of the spirit-world’s reality is bypassed.

Naturalist / sceptic (unrepresented in the corpus)

Gap — not engaged

The node establishes the doctrine, not its truth

WLC’s audience is sympathetic, so he argues entirely within the biblical frame — which data describe angels, what their nature is — and at no point defends the existence of the spirit-world against a sceptic who grants none of it. This is catechesis, not apologetics. Surface it prominently.

Honest existence-gap

Gap 2

The parsimony / Ockham objection — the flip side of WLC’s own reductio

If God can and does act immediately (WLC’s own defeater for the mediation rationale), the explanatory work angels do is nil. Why posit a whole additional created order at all?

Ockham-style parsimony (implied by WLC’s argument)

Gap — undefended

“Manifestation / fullness of creation” left exposed

WLC’s surviving rationale (angels express the fullness of creation) is never defended against the charge that it is explanatorily idle. His own third man argument arguably sharpens this objection against him.

Aggregation gap

Gap 3

The literal-here / figurative-there asymmetry

The work-catalogue (national princes, destroying angels, the Gethsemane angel) is read literally; yet WLC reads Isaiah 14 / Ezekiel 28 / Revelation 12 figuratively in Part 22.

Critical / literary reading (unrepresented)

Gap — unjustified

No principle for which texts get which treatment

The corpus never supplies a criterion distinguishing the literal angelology texts from the figurative ones — the same demythologising reading WLC applies elsewhere is never turned on the work-catalogue.

Aggregation gap

Gaps 4 & 5

The Angel of the Lord is left ontologically unresolved

The corpus never settles whether the theophany Angel is (a) a created angel, (b) the pre-incarnate Son, or (c) literary idiom for direct divine action. And the Matthew 1:24 simultaneity (objection 6) is not answered.

Agency view (Ismail / Xavier) — the standard biblical-Unitarian reply

Gap — agency framework not dismantled

Divine-act texts stacked, but the framework stands

McLatchie stacks divine-act texts against the agency reading but never directly rebuts the “sent one ≠ sender” framework itself. The live seam between this node and Christology stays open.
