Objective truth & relativism

A consolidated topic tree pooling seven sub-questions — what truth is, whether it is objective, why “there is no truth” refutes itself, what “your truth” conceals, and how Christianity owns its exclusive claim. Five experts in thirteen substantive treatments.

The question

Is there such a thing as objective truth — truth that holds independently of who believes it — or is “that’s true for you but not for me” the last word?

The relativist move — “there’s no objective truth,” “that’s your truth, not mine” — is the default disarming reflex of the secular West. It feels humble and tolerant, and it absorbs every particular claim, the gospel included, into a private-preference frame where nothing can be asserted as true for everybody.

The task here is not one syllogism but a cluster of linked sub-questions: define what truth is, survey the theories, show that objective truth exists, expose the self-refutation at the heart of relativism, diagnose the “your truth” idiom and the defence mechanism it often conceals, and finally own Christianity’s exclusive truth-claim without flinching.

The sub-questions — seven pooled strands

This topic is rendered as a sequence of sub-questions, not a single deductive chain. Each carries its own core answer and a signature move. The load-bearing strand is Q-17.4.6 — the self-refutation of relativism (four of the thirteen treatments); the sharpest is Q-17.4.21 — Christianity’s exclusive claim.

The sub-questions in sequence

Q-17.4.1

What is truth? — Truth is correspondence to reality, not plausibility or preference.

Key move (Huff). Approached through AI hallucination: a fabricated quote “sounds just right but not right enough.” The operative test is whether a claim can be traced to a real, identifiable source — “if we’re not actually seeing the sources the AI is pulling from, it could be anything.” Truth is what survives verification against the world, not what sounds convincing. Correspondence is assumed, not named.

Q-17.4.2

Theories of truth — correspondence + coherence (Lennox) against a deflationary view (Craig).

Lennox: truth corresponds to a fact “outside the statement”; coherence is a necessary condition; rigorous proof belongs only to pure mathematics, while elsewhere we act on sufficient evidence (the surgeon, the trans-Atlantic flight). Craig: truth is no heavyweight property — semantic descent lets us say “dinosaurs existed” rather than “it was true that…”; “we truly assert something if and only if reality is as we have asserted it to be.” His motive is to protect divine aseity from uncreated abstract propositions. The tension is real but latent — the two are never deployed against the same audience.

Q-17.4.3

Does objective truth exist? — Yes; even those who deny it believe in it when it matters.

Key move (Lennox — the courtroom). If you are wrongly accused and the judge announces “there’s no such thing as objective truth” and proceeds to judge on his own prejudices, “you would react with outrage and protest strongly in the name of truth and objective fairness.” Extended to morality: “I have never met anybody who thinks it’s right to torture infants.” Conclusion: “there is truth, we can know it, but never absolutely certainly — certain enough to rely on it.”

Q-17.4.6

Is “there is no objective truth” itself an objective claim? — Yes, and that is fatal. The hinge of the topic.

Lennox (Penn): conventionalism — “all meaning is relative” — must itself be relative, so it refutes itself. Lennox (Yale): “the person that says [there is no truth] expects me to believe it’s true”; the “no authorial intention” book (“I’m not going to read it”); people are “only postmodern in areas of no importance to them” (Everest rope, bank teller). Huff (McGill): the subway encounter — “even saying that there are no absolutes is an absolute statement” — then the law of non-contradiction and the insulin / gravity analogies. Perry (street): the same self-defeating structure applied to scientism — science is time, space, and matter, all three established in Genesis 1:1, so the Creator is the precondition of science, not falsifiable by it.

Q-17.4.10

“Your truth / my truth” — selectively applied relativism, abandoned everywhere it would cost something.

Key move (Perry — the yellow wall). Against “everybody believes in their own things,” Perry runs the colour analogy: call a red wall yellow and “you’re gonna say it’s red” — an objective standard regardless of opinion. The interlocutor concedes: “it’s my opinion, but it still doesn’t change facts.” Knechtle’s parallel: when the disputed word is worship, he re-routes — “we’re going to use a different word… who or what do you trust?” The denial collapses under the person’s own practice (they trust gravity, they trust the department chair).

Q-17.4.13

Why “your truth” is often a defence mechanism — a way to keep the ultimate question as private preference.

Key move (Knechtle — name the idol). “I don’t trust anything” is itself a stance of ultimate trust: “you have an idol, and that idol is yourself.” The question is never whether you live for something but what, and “what’s the evidence that whatever it is I’m living for is reliable?” The honest counter-current (Steinhart): the raw material records that the interlocutor pushes back with content — real worship “involves practices of submission and fealty,” and worshipping a personal God is itself the projection (“you make it something in your image… that’s idolatry”). The two definitions slide past each other.

Q-17.4.21

Christianity as an exclusive truth claim — exclusivity is the structure of truth itself, not a Christian peculiarity.

Knechtle: every worldview, pluralism included, is a truth-claim — “all paths lead to heaven” excludes “some paths” and “one path,” so the open/closed-minded asymmetry collapses; only after the structural point does he introduce John 14:6. Huff: logically exclusive (John 14:6) yet filter-free in its invitation — “the most inclusive exclusive truth in the world.” Perry: if there is one God, competing claims cannot all be true. Ward (via Knechtle): a Logos-Christology that preserves exclusivity (“only the love of God could change your heart”) while widening the scope to the never-heard — inclusivism, though the term is never used.

William Lane Craig 1 treatment
The deflationary outlier — in-house philosophical register
Distinctive move

Latent tension

Craig’s deflationism pulls against the popular consensus — Lennox, Huff, Perry, Knechtle all run substantive correspondence. The two views reach the same practical place (truth tracks reality) but disagree on whether “truth” names a real property, and the corpus offers no adjudication.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

“There is no objective truth”

Truth is a construct; no claim holds independently of who believes it.

Popular relativism / postmodern slogan

Strongest response

Self-refuting — the claim is offered as true

The statement is itself an objective truth-claim. “Even saying that there are no absolutes is an absolute statement.” Logical chaos.

Huff names the contradictionLennox performative contradiction

Objection 2

“All meaning is relative” (conventionalism)

There is no fixed meaning; every interpretation is relative to its frame.

Postmodern literary theory

Strongest response

The claim must itself be relative

“The statement that all meaning is relative refutes itself, because it itself must have relative meaning — so we can discard it.”

Lennox Penn courtroom dialogue

Objection 3

“There is no authorial intention”

A text means whatever the reader takes it to mean; the author’s intent is inaccessible and irrelevant.

Postmodern hermeneutics

Strongest response

Performative contradiction

The author of that book expects you to grasp his intention. “Do you mean if I read your book I’ll know there’s no authorial intention? — So I’m not going to read it.”

Lennox Yale lecture

Objection 4

“That’s your truth, not my truth”

Truth is indexed to the person; what is true for you need not be true for me.

Everyday relativist idiom

Strongest response

Selectively applied — abandoned where it costs

Nobody says it at the bank when shown the red figures, about the strength of a climbing rope, or about the colour of a wall. The idiom survives only where nothing is at stake.

Lennox bank-teller testPerry yellow-wall analogy

Objection 5

Diversity of belief proves there is no one truth

So many cultures and views disagree that there cannot be a single objective truth behind them.

Cultural pluralism

Strongest response

Diversity of opinion ≠ absence of fact

Disagreement does not entail that there is no fact of the matter; the law of non-contradiction still holds — “I cannot be a married bachelor.”

Huff McGill lecture

Objection 6

“Truth is relative — you’re out of date”

Everyone modern knows truth is relative; insisting otherwise is just being behind the times.

Cultural-authority appeal

Strongest response

Even commercial bodies affirm objective truth

“Creativity is subjective, the truth isn’t” — Advertising Standards Canada. The “everyone knows” framing is simply false.

Huff billboard counter-example

Objection 7

“You’re closed-minded; I’m open-minded”

The Christian asserts one exclusive way; the pluralist generously holds them all open.

Tolerance framing

Strongest response

Symmetry — pluralism is also exclusive

“All paths lead to heaven” is itself a truth-claim that excludes “some paths” and “one path.” Both parties make truth-claims “the same way”; the asymmetry collapses.

Knechtle the symmetry move

Objection 8

Christianity’s exclusivity is arrogant and intolerant

“No one comes to the Father except through me” is a narrow, intolerant claim.

Pluralist objection to John 14:6

Strongest response

Exclusivity is the structure of truth

Truth is exclusive everywhere — the exam answer, 2+2=4. And the Christian invitation is filter-free: “the most inclusive exclusive truth in the world.”

Knechtle grading & 2+2=4Huff filter-free invitation

Objection 9

“All religions are valid for those who hold them”

Nobody is wrong; everyone believes in their own god, true within their own frame.

Soft pluralism

Strongest response

If there is one God, someone must be wrong

Competing god-claims cannot all be true at once. “If there is only one God, there’s only one truth, and God has spoken for himself.”

Perry yellow-wall extension

Objection 10

The 2+2=4 analogy is “an intellectual system created by a human”; God goes beyond it, so the analogy cannot carry over to faith.

Objection 11

Pragmatism — “truth has shades, a spectrum”

Truth-claims are not simply true or false; there is “a spectrum of false and truth.”

Reflective interlocutor, Knechtle dialogue

Strongest response

A spectrum presupposes the distinction

Concedes a real difference between truth and falsehood — which the “spectrum” itself assumes — then pivots: “what does the evidence, not proof, point to?”

Objection 12

Hick-style pluralism — one noumenal “Real”

The religions are culturally conditioned responses to one ineffable Real; they are not strictly contradictory, so “someone must be wrong” does not follow.

John Hick (implied)

Gap — not engaged

The corpus assumes univocal claims

Perry’s yellow wall and Knechtle’s symmetry both presuppose religions make univocal, competing claims about the same object. The phenomenal / noumenal distinction is nowhere addressed — the strongest relativist position is left untouched.

Objection 13

“Worshipping a personal God is itself idolatry”

To make the ultimate a person is to project your own personhood onto reality — “you make it something in your image… that’s idolatry.”

Eric Steinhart (Tillich, Eckhart, Altizer)

Gap — definitions slide past

Knechtle does not engage the stipulation

Whether worship is best defined as “practices of submission and fealty” (Steinhart) or “anything ultimately worthy” (Knechtle) is never adjudicated, so the idolatry-reversal stands unrefuted within the exchange.

Persistent gaps

Gap 1

Hick-style noumenal pluralism is never engaged.

Every treatment assumes religions make univocal, competing claims about the same object. The sophisticated pluralist reply — that the religions are culturally conditioned responses to one ineffable “Real,” so not strictly contradictory — is nowhere addressed. The most serious gap in the topic.

Gap 2

The metaphysics-of-mathematics objection is dropped.

When the interlocutor says “math is a human system,” Knechtle declines to concede but never defends mathematical realism (cross-ref §1.8). The 2+2=4 analogy is left exposed.

Gap 3

The correspondence-vs-deflationary tension is latent, not resolved.

Lennox treats truth as substantive correspondence; Craig treats it deflationarily to protect aseity. They never meet, and the corpus offers no adjudication of whether “truth” names a real property.

Gap 4

Steinhart’s definitional pushback on worship is unanswered.

“Worship = practices of submission and fealty” vs Knechtle’s “anything ultimately worthy” never engage, so the projection-of-personhood idolatry reversal stands unrefuted within the exchange.

Gap 5

The exclusivism texts are under-deployed.

Perry and Huff lean on John 14:6 pastorally but never marshal the exegetical or historical case for it; Perry omits Acts 4:12. The inclusivist / exclusivist distinction (Ward’s actual position) is never named.

Gap 6

“Your truth as defence mechanism” is asserted more than demonstrated.

The diagnosis that relativism is psychologically protective rather than philosophically held is implicit in Knechtle’s “idol is yourself,” but the corpus never develops the pastoral or evidential case for why people retreat into it.
