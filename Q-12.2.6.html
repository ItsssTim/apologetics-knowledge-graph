The question

Will the dead be raised bodily, and is that hope grounded in Scripture rather than merely asserted by the church?

This is the eschatological counterpart to §3.3 (the historical resurrection of Jesus). Where §3.3 asks whether Jesus rose, this node asks whether we will — and on what basis. The corpus answers with a cumulative case running from the Old Testament’s late-emerging resurrection hope, through Jesus’ own affirmation against the Sadducees, to Paul’s first-fruits typology.

The apologetic centre of gravity here is method. The two cleanest data points — Jesus arguing with the Sadducees (Matt 22) and Paul arguing with the Corinthians (1 Cor 15) — are both internal critiques: each defeats the denier on the denier’s own conceded premises.

The argument structure — a cumulative case, not one syllogism

No single expert runs one syllogism. The hope is built from converging evidence streams treated in different registers — exegetical, doctrinal, typological. Each stream is contestable alone; together they make bodily resurrection the best reading of the scriptural data and pre-empt the standard deniers on their own terms.

Stream A · OT trajectory

  • Isaiah 26:19 (“your dead shall live”)
  • Daniel 12:2 (awake to life or contempt)
  • Exodus 3:6 latent in the Pentateuch
  • Hope genuinely scriptural, not late invention

Stream B · Jesus’ affirmation

  • Sided with the Pharisees (Matt 22)
  • Levirate trap’s false premise exposed
  • Positive argument from Exodus 3:6
  • “God of the living,” not the dead

Stream C · First-fruits typology

  • Christ the “first fruits” (1 Cor 15:20)
  • Paul’s three-step modus tollens
  • Witness-list secures premise (2)
  • His resurrection grounds ours

A fourth stream — embodied, transformed finality (Schreiner / Huff) — specifies what is raised: a body physical but transformed (perishable → imperishable), on a renewed earth, distinct from the disembodied intermediate state, and emphatically not a Gnostic escape from embodiment.

How each expert deploys it

William Lane Craig2 transcripts
Method-forward exegete — two internal critiques
Jesus vs the Sadducees (Matt 22:23–33)
  • Stages the live fault-line with Acts 23: Paul splits the Sanhedrin — “what a clever move… Paul adroitly exploits” the Pharisee / Sadducee divide.
  • Thesis, stated bluntly: Jesus sided with the Pharisees on bodily resurrection.
  • Two-step refutation. First clear the false premise — resurrection life has no marriage (“like angels in heaven”), so “the riddle was simply beside the point.”
  • Then the positive argument from the Pentateuch the Sadducees accepted: Exodus 3:6, “the God of the living,” implying the patriarchs “are in some sense still alive.”
Paul vs the Corinthians (1 Cor 15:12–34)
  • Identifies a formal modus tollens: if the dead are not raised, Christ is not raised; but Christ is raised; therefore the dead are raised.
  • The witness-list is piled up deliberately — it is the evidence for premise (2).
  • First-fruits typology: Christ’s resurrection is “the harbinger… a representative sample of the harvest that will come.”
Signature move

The denier’s objection to the general resurrection collapses the particular one too. The Corinthians recoiled at materiality — “but that is exactly what happened to Jesus… the same objections would apply.”

Wes Huff1 transcript (carrying Patrick Schreiner)
Anti-Gnostic embodiment supplement
Schreiner’s doctrinal treatment
  • The resurrection body is physical but transformed: “spiritual body” means animated by the Spirit, not disembodied — “physical like Jesus’s body… but… imperishable… glorious… full of power.”
  • Distinguish the intermediate state (disembodied, with Christ) from the final state (bodily, on a renewed earth) — Christians routinely conflate them.
  • The ascension did not undo the incarnation: “the dust of earth is on the throne room of heaven.”
  • All are raised — “one is for judgment and one is for life.”
  • The new earth is concrete (eating, nature, recognisable persons) and never static: stasis-on-arrival is the Platonic picture, not the Christian one.
Huff’s reinforcement
  • Corrects a Christian-internal misframe — on hearing “you’re a spirit that has a body,” he “winced… nope, that’s not how it works.”
  • The hope is not Platonic / Gnostic escape: “we’re” not “stuck in meat prisons.”

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

The levirate scenario makes resurrection absurd

Seven brothers each marry one woman in turn (levirate duty). “In the resurrection therefore to which of the seven will she be wife, for they all had her?”

The Sadducees (Matt 22:28)

Strongest response

Expose the false premise

Jesus does not deny the data; he denies the hidden assumption that resurrection replicates earthly marriage. “They neither marry nor are given in marriage but are like angels in heaven” — so “the riddle was simply beside the point.”

Craig Jesus’ first move, Matt 22:29–30

Objection 2

Resurrection isn’t really in (their) Scripture

The Sadducees accepted only the Pentateuch as authoritative and held it taught no resurrection — so the doctrine is an unscriptural innovation.

The Sadducees (Pentateuch-only canon)

Strongest response

Argue from the opponent’s own canon

Jesus reasons from the Pentateuch itself — Exodus 3:6, “the God of Abraham, Isaac and Jacob… not the God of the dead but of the living” — a textbook internal critique on conceded ground.

Craig Jesus’ second move, Matt 22:31–32

Objection 3

Christ could be raised without a general resurrection

A critic could grant Christ’s resurrection yet deny premise (1) of Paul’s syllogism — why should his rising entail that everyone rises?

Corinthian-style critic; raised by a student in WLC’s class

Strongest response

The objection cuts both ways

The Corinthians’ real revulsion was at materiality. “But that is exactly what happened to Jesus… Christ rose physically and bodily.” Any reservation about bodily resurrection in general applies equally to Christ’s — so premise (1) holds.

Craig response to the materiality objection

Objection 4

“Spiritual body” means disembodied

Paul’s phrase “spiritual body” (1 Cor 15) sounds like an immaterial, ghostly survival — not a physical resurrection at all.

Popular ethereal-heaven imagination; Gnostic-leaning readers

Strongest response

“Spiritual” = animated by the Spirit

Schreiner: the contrast is perishable → imperishable, weakness → power — not physical → immaterial. The body is “physical like Jesus’s body was physical but… imperishable… glorious… full of power.”

Schreiner / Huff exegesis of 1 Cor 15

Objection 5

The hope is escape from the body

“You are not a body that has a spirit, you’re a spirit that has a body” — salvation is the soul’s release from a material prison.

A prominent (unnamed) apologist; Platonic / Gnostic instinct

Strongest response

A Christian-internal misframe

Huff: “nope, that’s not how it works.” God “cares about the physicality of this world and the restoration is going to make all things new” — the hope is not “an ancient platonic gnostic one” of escaping “meat prisons.”

Huff internal critique of the misframe

Objection 6

Won’t a finished heaven be static and boring?

An eternal, completed state sounds inert — pop culture pictures the afterlife with death still clinging to it (zombies, Stephen King), as though nothing good could last without decay.

Popular intuition; zombie / horror imagery

Strongest response

Stasis is the Platonic picture, not the Christian one

Schreiner flips the charge: “it’s actually more platonic to think that… you arrive at something and then it’s static.” Christian finality is continual growth toward an infinite God — “further up, further in.”

Schreiner / Huff growth-not-stasis reframe

Objection 7

“God of Abraham” proves survival, not bodily resurrection

Exodus 3:6 need only assert God’s covenant faithfulness to a memory; even granting present survival of the patriarchs, the inference to bodily resurrection is under-determined by the text.

Standard skeptical rejoinder (ancient or modern)

Gap — reported, not defended

Craig narrates the argument but does not shore it up

Craig recounts Jesus’ appeal and the crowd’s astonishment but never engages the obvious objection that the text, taken at face value, proves at most the patriarchs’ survival. The sharpest pressure point on the node — must be answered from elsewhere in §12 / §3.

Aggregation gap

Persistent gaps in the corpus

Gap A

The Exodus 3:6 inference is undefended

Survival-vs-resurrection under-determination (see Objection 7) is never engaged on its own terms.

Both Craig sessions

Gap B

“Like angels” vs the Sadducees’ denial of angels

Acts 23:8 reports the Sadducees rejected “angel nor spirit,” yet Jesus answers them by appealing to angelic existence. The tension is untouched — links to §22 angelology.

Aggregation gap

Gap C

No modern objection is engaged

Identity / continuity of the resurrection body, the disability-and-the-resurrection-body sub-question, and the physics of reassembly are all absent — expected, since both Craig sessions expound first-century debates, but coverage must come from another node.

Scope limitation

Gap D

The intermediate-to-final mechanism is left as “mystery”

Schreiner is candid that how the ascended body relates to the present heavens is unresolved — “that’s the mystery.” Honest, but a gap rather than an answer.
