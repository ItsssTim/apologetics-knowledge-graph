The accident of birth

Isn't my worldview just an accident of birth?

It looks like a lottery. Born in Riyadh, you will almost certainly be Muslim; in Bangkok, Buddhist; in Alabama, Christian. So if where you happen to be born largely decides what you believe, how could it be fair for your eternal future to hang on it? The same goes for when in history you were born. That part has a card of its own; here we take on the where.

First / The genetic fallacy
Step 1 of 4
Where the objection backfires

The argument cuts both ways.

Start with the logic itself, because there is a problem with it before we even reach God. The claim is that you only hold your beliefs because of where you were born. But look at what that does to the person making it.

Both ways
If being raised somewhere discredits what you believe, it discredits the objector just as fast. The secular Westerner only doubts because they were born into a doubting age and place. The atheist in a university town absorbed that as surely as anyone absorbed a religion. If birth invalidates belief, it invalidates the objection along with everything else. The boomerang comes straight back.
The origin
This is an old mistake with a name, the genetic fallacy: confusing where a belief came from with whether it is true. As Lennox replies when told he is only a Christian because he is Irish, that is absurd. Giving the cause of how someone reached a view tells you nothing about whether the view is correct. You learned your language and your arithmetic from your culture too. That does not make two plus two a local custom.
Is it true
So the real question was never how you arrived at a belief. It is whether the belief is true. Those are completely different questions, and only the second one finally matters. A person born into the right answer for cultural reasons still holds the right answer, just as a person born into a wrong one can examine it and walk out of it.
The theory you'll hear
You're only a Christian because you were raised in a Christian country. If that discredits a belief, it discredits yours just as much, since you only doubt because you were born into a doubting age. The objection is a boomerang. How a person came to a view tells you nothing about whether it is true, otherwise we would have to bin the mathematics and the language we also soaked up from our surroundings.
✓
Confidence: where you first met a belief has no bearing on whether it is true, and the objection refutes the sceptic as quickly as the believer. How you got here and whether you are right are two separate questions.
Second / Not actually trapped
Step 2 of 4
Is the premise even true?

Born into it isn't stuck in it.

The objection also leans on a claim about people that is shakier than it sounds: that we simply stay inside whatever worldview we were handed. As a hard rule, that is just not so.

A tendency
It is true that belief often runs along the lines of birth. But a strong tendency is not a locked cage. Hundreds of millions of people across history have converted, deconverted, migrated, and changed their minds, many of them at enormous personal cost. The pattern bends all the time. It is an influence on where you start, not a sentence on where you finish.
People change
And the map is moving as we speak. Christianity is fading in the European heartland that once defined it, and growing fastest in places with no Christian past at all, among them Iran and China, where conversion can cost a person dearly. Lennox makes the point from experience: he has watched people change their worldview again and again, which is exactly what you would not see if belief were truly fixed by birth.
So test it
There is also an honest challenge buried in here. If you suspect you believe only what you were raised to believe, the answer is not to shrug, it is to test it. Lennox says this very objection is the reason he has spent his life deliberately exposing his faith to its strongest opponents. A belief you have actually examined is no longer just an accident of birth.
The theory you'll hear
But statistically, religion tracks birthplace almost perfectly. A strong correlation is not a locked door. Hundreds of millions have changed, often at real risk, and Christianity is collapsing in the Europe that cradled it while surging in Iran, China and Nepal, places with no Christian heritage to inherit. People are shaped by where they begin. They are not imprisoned there.
✓
Confidence: people are influenced by the worldview they are born into, not chained to it. Conversion, migration and honest doubt move millions across those lines every generation. The cure for inherited belief is to examine it.
Third / Not a trivia exam
Step 3 of 4
What is actually being judged

It isn't a religion exam keyed to your postcode.

Underneath the objection sits a picture of God grading everyone on whether they landed on the correct religion, with your birthplace having pre-filled the wrong answer. But that is not how the Bible says it works.

Not an exam
The picture assumes salvation is a test of getting the right religious system, performed well enough to pass. If that were true, then yes, being handed the wrong textbook at birth would be a disaster. But Christianity is not, at its heart, a system you perform. Lennox puts it sharply: most religions hand you a rule book and assess you at the end on merit. Christianity does the very opposite.
A free gift
Acceptance comes at the start, as a free gift, received by trusting the God who is reaching towards you, not earned by mastering the religion of your region. Lennox compares the merit version to proposing marriage by handing someone a forty year list of rules to keep first. Real relationship does not work that way, and neither, he says, does grace. What matters is your response to God, not the label you were issued at birth.
Your light
And you are answerable only for the light you actually receive. No one is judged for failing to grasp a message that never reached them. Everyone, everywhere, has some genuine light, in the created world around them and in conscience within (Romans 1:20; Romans 2:14-15), and the question is what they do with it. As one summary of the Christian view puts it, holding that Christ is the only way does not require condemning people by historical accident.
The theory you'll hear
So the person born Hindu or Muslim was simply handed the wrong religion, through no fault of their own. Only if salvation is an exam on the correct system. It is not. It is a gift, received by trusting the God who reaches towards every person, and people are judged on the light they were genuinely given, never on a gospel that never arrived. The accident of your birthplace does not set the syllabus.
✓
Confidence: salvation is not a culture-specific exam you might have been born studying the wrong book for. It is a gift received by faith, and people are judged on the light they truly had. The brand you inherited is not the thing in question.
Fourth / No accident to God
Step 4 of 4
The deepest answer

Your birthplace is no accident to God.

Here is the part the objection never quite considers. It calls your origin an accident of birth. But to the God of the Bible, nothing about where and when you were born is accidental at all.

No accident
In Acts, Paul tells the philosophers of Athens that God marked out the appointed times in history and the boundaries of where each people would live, and he did it with a purpose: so that they would seek him and perhaps reach out and find him (Acts 17:26-27). Your spot on the map is not a dice roll that God then judges you for. It is something he himself oversees, and aims at your finding him.
Times & places
So the very thing the objection treats as a cruel lottery, Scripture treats as part of God's care. The seasons and the borders of every life are arranged so that people in every corner of the earth have a real chance to seek and to find. That is the opposite of a God who scatters souls at random and then punishes them for landing badly.
He placed you
Some Christian thinkers, like William Lane Craig, press this further. An all-knowing God, who knows how each person would freely respond in any setting, can order history so that those who would receive him are born where the light will reach them, and no one who would have said yes is lost merely for want of the chance. That you are even asking this, that the gospel has reached you enough to trouble you, may be part of the same providence.
The theory you'll hear
Calling it providence is just a comforting story to dodge the unfairness. Then weigh the alternative it assumes, that your birthplace sits outside God's care, a random throw he later judges. Scripture claims the reverse: he set the times and places where people live so that they might seek and find him (Acts 17:26-27). An all-knowing God arranging the world so that the most people possible are saved is not dodging the problem. It is the problem dissolving.
✓
Confidence: to God your birthplace is not an accident but a setting he oversees, so that people everywhere might seek and find him. Far from a lottery he ignores, where and when you were born is a tool he uses to reach you.

So is it fair that your beliefs lean on where you were born? First, that fact says nothing about whether they are true, and it boomerangs straight back on the sceptic. Second, no one is locked in by birth; people cross those lines all the time. Third, salvation was never a culture exam, but a gift received by trusting God on the light you genuinely have. And fourth, your place on the map is no accident to a God who set the times and borders of every life so that people everywhere might seek him and find him. The accident of birth turns out to be no accident at all.
