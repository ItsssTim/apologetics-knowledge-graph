The question

What is the final state of the redeemed and of creation itself — and what does Christian hope actually picture at the end of history?

This is the closing question of eschatology, and the corpus treats it less as a problem to be defended than as a hope to be described accurately. Two distortions are corrected rather than two objections refuted. Lennox corrects the idea that the Christian future is catastrophe — the end of history full stop. Schreiner, reinforced by Huff, corrects the idea that the Christian future is escape — disembodied souls in an ethereal, static heaven.

The positive claim both halves converge on: the end of history as we know it is not the end of history, but the beginning of the new heavens and the new earth — a renewed and embodied creation governed by the Lamb, where every tear is wiped away and growth toward an infinite God never ceases.

The convergent case — two dimensions of one hope, not a syllogism

Unlike the Kalam (a deductive syllogism), this node is descriptive and cumulative. There is no premise-to-conclusion chain to defend; two experts each supply a dimension of the same final state, and a third register — the pastoral posture — frames how the doctrine is meant to land. Honest density note: two transcripts, no sustained sceptical pressure. This is a consummation summary, not a contested battleground.

Stream A · Throne & Lamb

  • Rev 4 — the throne alone, august magnificence
  • Rev 7 — an uncountable throng, every nation
  • Rev 5 — the Lamb who made it possible
  • Rev 21–22 — the Lamb wipes every tear away
  • Death is not the end

Stream B · Embodied earth

  • Body: perishable → imperishable, transformed
  • “Spiritual body” = animated by the Spirit
  • Intermediate state ≠ final state
  • A new home, new bodies, a new vision of God
  • Not Gnostic escape; not static stasis

Stream C · Pastoral posture

  • Given to steady believers, not terrify
  • Faced “in a balanced way”
  • Lampstand-witness, not retreat
  • Increase the light of Christ
  • Hope produces courage, not fear
Reframe 1

The coming of Christ is not the end of history — it is the end of history as we know it.

Lennox’s inversion. The phrase “the end of history” is deliberately qualified: what ends is the present order, but what begins is “the new heavens and the new earth which will go on eternally.” Catastrophe is recast as consummation.

Reframe 2

The final state is physical and transformed, not ethereal and disembodied.

Schreiner’s correction. The resurrection body is “animated by the spirit” — physical like Jesus’s risen body, but imperishable and glorious. The intermediate state (with Christ) must not be confused with the final state: bodily resurrection on a renewed earth, with eating, beauty, and continual growth.

Consummation

A renewed, embodied, unending creation under the Lamb — every tear wiped away.

The convergent picture. Not logical compulsion but a corrected imagination: the hope is concrete, embodied, restorative, and endless, governed “not by algorithms but by the lamb of God” — and meant to produce courage, not fear.

How each expert frames it

John Lennox 1 transcript
The throne, the Lamb & “the end of history as we know it”
Signature moves
  • Throne-multitude-Lamb architecture. Rev 4 (the throne alone) → Rev 7 (an uncountable throng from every nation) → the obvious question how did they get there? → Rev 5 (“you discover there is a lamb”).
  • The Lamb wipes every tear away. At the book’s close the Lamb “in the midst of the throne” shepherds the redeemed — “terrific hope. Death is not the end.”
  • The “end of history” inversion (his signature here): not the end of history but “the end of history as we know it” — the beginning of a new creation “which will go on eternally.”
  • Christ as the Logos. The theological capstone: “his intelligence is not artificial. He is the logos, the intelligence” — and he loves persons individually, which no machine can.
  • The lampstand vocation. The proper posture toward a darkening culture is witness — to “increase our light” and illuminate Christ “who is the light of the world.”
Pastoral key

Eschatology is given to steady believers, faced “in a balanced way because although there are things that are scary … there always have been.” Saunders crystallises it: Revelation was written “not to terrify believers, but to study them.”

Wes Huff 1 transcript (Schreiner substantive)
The embodied, non-boring new creation
Distinctive moves
  • Schreiner: the spiritual body is physical. Perishable → imperishable, weakness → power; “spiritual body” means “animated by the spirit” — physical “like Jesus’s body was physical but … imperishable.”
  • Schreiner: intermediate state ≠ final state. Many Christians think death lands them “immediately in the new heavens and new earth”; rather, Christ returns bodily, then judgment, then the new creation — “a physical reality.”
  • Schreiner: the ascension as pledge. The ascension did not undo the incarnation — “the dust of earth is on the throne room of heaven.”
  • Schreiner: heaven is not static. A static endpoint is the Platonic picture; Christianity promises “always growth … even … growth in heaven” toward an infinite God.
  • Huff’s anti-Gnostic supplement. “You’re a spirit that has a body” is exactly wrong; the restoration makes “all things new” — not escape from “meat prisons.”
Attribution note

The substantive theological voice in this transcript is Patrick Schreiner; Huff reinforces the anti-Gnostic, embodiment point. The manifest counts two experts (Lennox, Huff); the new-creation body of this framing is Schreiner’s.

Distortions corrected — cross-expert master list

This node faces distortions more than adversaries. The “objections” are mainly popular misimaginations the experts correct from within — an internal-critique register rather than a debate.

Distortion 1

Revelation is fear-mongering — eschatology terrifies believers

The apocalyptic imagery of the end — beasts, plagues, judgment — reads as a catalogue of terrors designed to frighten the faithful into compliance.

Popular reading / implicit

Strongest response

Given to steady, not to terrify

Lennox: the book is faced “in a balanced way because although there are things that are scary … there always have been.” Its closing image is not catastrophe but the Lamb on the throne wiping every tear away.

Lennox balanced postureSaunders “to study them, not terrify” (faithful summary)

Distortion 2

The final state is disembodied — souls in an ethereal heaven

Christian hope is for the soul to leave the body behind and float in a non-physical heaven — clouds, harps, and no earth.

Popular Christian imagination

Strongest response

A physical-but-transformed new earth

Schreiner: the resurrection body is “animated by the spirit” — physical like Jesus’s risen body but imperishable and glorious. The final state is bodily life on a renewed earth, with eating, beauty, and recognisable persons.

Schreiner spiritual-body exegesisHuff embodiment supplement

Distortion 3

Christian hope is a Platonic / Gnostic escape from the body

“You’re a spirit that has a body” — salvation is liberation from the material world, getting the soul out of its “meat prison.”

Gnostic / dualist intuition (a named-but-unnamed apologist)

Strongest response

The restoration makes all things new

Huff: that line is exactly wrong — God “cares about the physicality of this world,” and the hope is not “an ancient platonic gnostic one” of escaping embodiment but the renewal of the material order.

Huff anti-Gnostic correctionSchreiner new-creation physicality

Distortion 4

Won’t heaven be boring — eternal life with nothing left to do?

Even in the after-lives fiction imagines — zombies, Stephen King — death clings on, because an endless static existence sounds either dreadful or dull. Arrival at perfection means nothing more to seek.

Popular intuition; secular imaginings of the afterlife

Strongest response

Continual growth, not stasis

Schreiner: a static endpoint is the Platonic picture. Christianity promises “always growth … even … growth in heaven” toward an infinite God — unending ascent, “further up, further in,” never a frozen terminus.

Schreiner anti-Platonic growth argument

Distortion 5

How can heaven be physical if the ascended Jesus is bodily there now?

If “the heavens” is a non-physical place, yet Christ lives there as the risen God-man with a body, the categories seem to clash.

Huff poses it

Partial response — honestly incomplete

Intermediate vs final state, then “the mystery”

Schreiner distinguishes the intermediate from the final state and suggests the present session “at the right hand” may be symbolic of reign, “pointing to how one day he will manifest that in a physical sense” — but candidly concedes “that’s the mystery.”

Persistent gaps in the corpus

Because this hope-node is carried by only two transcripts, the gaps are wide and are flagged honestly rather than disguised.

Gap 1

Renewal vs annihilation is never adjudicated

The whole continuity claim — a new earth, not no earth — depends on a renewal reading over against the “destroyed by fire” reading of 2 Peter 3. Neither expert engages the fork; renewal is simply assumed.

Continuity-vs-destruction exegetical debate

Gap — unaddressed

Assumed, not argued

The renewal view is taken for granted. A sceptic favouring annihilation-then-recreation gets no engagement at all.

Aggregation gap

Gap 2

The content of the new creation is asserted from imagery

“You will eat,” “a new vision of God,” cherry-blossom-as-foretaste — evocative readings of resurrection appearances and creation beauty, not defended inferences.

Methodological

Gap — unaddressed

A corrected picture, not an argument

The sceptic is offered a richer imagination of the new earth, but no inferential case that it will be so. Persuasive devotionally; thin evidentially.

Aggregation gap

Gap 3

No timing / millennium engagement

Premillennial, amillennial and postmillennial framings of how the new creation arrives are entirely absent. The node treats “return → judgment → new creation” as a flat sequence.

Millennial-position debate

Gap — unaddressed

Out of frame entirely

Neither transcript touches the millennium; the “when” and “how” of the consummation are simply not in view. See Q-12.2.1 for the parousia material that would anchor it.

Aggregation gap

Gap 4

“Every tear wiped away” vs continuity of the person

How memory, loss, and the identity of the resurrected person survive into a state with “no more tears” is never raised. The comfort is asserted, not examined.

Philosophical-theological

Gap — unaddressed

Comfort without analysis

The pastoral promise is offered whole; the puzzle of how grief is healed without erasing the self who grieved is left untouched.

Aggregation gap

Gap 5

The Lamb-on-the-throne image is given devotionally, not defended

Lennox’s Revelation 4-5-7-22 architecture is a faithful reading delivered as proclamation; no objection to apocalyptic-as-genre, or to a non-literal heavenly throne, is entertained.

Genre / hermeneutical

Gap — unaddressed

Proclaimed, not exegeted

The image carries devotional weight but no defence against a critic who reads Revelation’s throne-room as symbolic vision rather than a literal final-state description.

Aggregation gap
