Does naturalism undermine reason?

The argument from reason — from Darwin’s “horrid doubt” through Lewis to Plantinga’s EAAN. If the mind is the unguided product of mindless matter, why trust it? Lennox’s signature argument, in 30 substantive extractions.

The question

If the mind is the unguided end-product of mindless processes, do we have any reason to trust it — including the reasoning that produced naturalism itself?

This is the argument from reason: the move that turns naturalism back on itself. It does not claim naturalists cannot reason — they manifestly do. It claims naturalism gives them no grounds for trusting that they reason reliably.

Its strategic value is that it is self-defeating to deny: any argument the naturalist mounts against it is itself a deliverance of the very faculties under suspicion. And its pedigree runs straight through the atheist tradition — Darwin’s 1881 “horrid doubt,” Haldane, C.S. Lewis’s Miracles, Plantinga’s formal EAAN, and atheist Thomas Nagel’s concession in Mind and Cosmos.

The convergent argument — the self-defeat structure

Premise 1

On naturalism, the mind reduces to the brain — and the brain is the end-product of a mindless, unguided process.

Lennox’s Socratic set-piece. “What do you do science with? — your brain. What’s the brain? — the end product of a mindless unguided process. And you trust it?” Knechtle’s folk version: the mind is “an accidental collection of atoms” or “a highly developed monkey’s mind.”

Premise 2

Such a process selects for survival and reproduction — not for truth. And the two are importantly different.

The Plantingan core. Brains track survival value, not truth. Lennox via John Gray: “the human mind serves evolutionary success, not truth.” This is the defensible narrow claim — distinct from Knechtle’s stronger Lewisian claim that the rational simply cannot come from the non-rational.

Premise 3

Therefore there is no reason to trust any belief those faculties produce — including the belief in naturalism itself.

The computer analogy. “If you knew your computer was the end product of a mindless unguided process, would you trust it? — No.” Lewis’s form: “any theory that renders thinking invalid cannot be true, because it was generated by thinking.”

Conclusion

Naturalism is self-defeating — it “shoots itself not in the foot but in the brain.” Theism grounds reason: the mind in here and the universe out there trace to the same rational Creator.

Transcendental, not deductive. The argument does not directly prove God. It shows that denying a rational ground for reason is self-cancelling, then offers theism as the worldview that supplies the missing ground — corroborated by Einstein’s “the only incomprehensible thing about the universe is that it is comprehensible.”

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

Natural selection would track truth — true beliefs aid survival

The reliabilist reply (Plantinga–Fodor–Dennett): in survival-relevant domains, true belief and adaptive behaviour substantially coincide, so selection would favour roughly reliable faculties.

Texas A&M questioner, PZ Myers, Aron Ra, Dillahunty

Gap — the corpus’s weakest point

“Survival, not truth” — stated, not won

Lennox’s best reply: selection optimises for survival not truth (cites John Gray ridiculing the defence), and asserting otherwise without a mechanism is an “evolution of the gaps” just-so story. But no expert engages the detailed overlap reply. Lennox states the worry; he does not refute Fodor or Dennett.

Lennox “evolution of the gaps”Reliabilist case left unanswered

Objection 2

Naturalism has a rational structure / naturalists reason fine

“How does naturalism counteract reason?” — naturalists do excellent science, so naturalism can’t be hostile to rationality.

Yale audience questioner

Strongest response

Structure vs warrant — the technical point

Lennox: the point is not that naturalism lacks rational structure (it has one, “because it is created by minds made in the image of God”) but that it gives “no grounds for trusting reason. That’s the technical point I’m making.”

Lennox warrant-not-ability distinctionLennox / Plantinga imago Dei as warrant

Objection 3

Trust science for its predictive success, not its “rationality”

You trust science because it makes predictions validated by observation — not because it’s “rational” or “intelligent.” Verification, not reason, is the warrant.

Lawrence Krauss (vs Lennox)

Strongest response

Verification itself presupposes intelligibility

Lennox: “it’s the fact that we can understand those claims — that’s at a higher level than that.” Prediction-verification only works if nature is rationally accessible to mind in the first place.

Lennox higher-level intelligibility

Objection 4

Brain and universe co-evolved — “amazing but not miraculous”

Our minds arose within the natural world, so of course they can grasp it. The mind-cosmos fit is amazing but needs no creator.

Lawrence Krauss

Partial response

The deeper feature still points beyond

Lennox: even granting co-evolution, the deeper feature — rationally intelligible laws plus rational minds able to grasp them — “reasonably points in the direction of a rational creator.” (Does not fully close the naturalist’s reply.)

Lennox intelligibility-of-laws move

Objection 5

Consciousness and rationality can emerge from chemistry

“Only chemicals reason — the only thing in the universe we can show reasons is chemicals.” Rationality is an emergent property of complex matter; no immaterial mind is needed.

PZ Myers, Aron Ra

Gap — asserted, not argued

Knechtle asserts the contrary; emergence never rebutted

Knechtle: “chemicals don’t reason, they react” — but asserts rather than argues it, and Aron Ra immediately catches him stepping “outside the evidence.” Lennox/Huff supply the better reply (immaterial information is fundamental; reduction empties the universe of thinking), yet the emergence claim is never decisively answered.

Objection 6

Intelligibility is just a brute fact

Why not simply assert the universe is intelligible and stop there? Invoking God adds an unnecessary explanatory step.

Strongest response

Brute-fact stopping is un-scientific

Lennox: treating intelligibility as a brute fact is “a very surprising statement for a scientist, because we’re always looking for explanations.” Even non-theist Paul Davies criticises scientists who “lose their curiosity about the biggest thing of all.”

Lennox Davies as confirming non-theist witness

Objection 8

Objection 9

Humans aren’t fully rational anyway

We hold many irrational beliefs that are “spontaneously generated” — so the picture of cognition as truth-tracking is false from the start, and the argument has no target.

PZ Myers

Gap — largely dodged

Knechtle pivots to abiogenesis

Knechtle does not engage; he redirects to “there is no creative mechanism in evolution” and the non-life-to-life problem. PZ correctly flags this as “a tremendous leap of logic.”

Objection 10

Compatibilism saves the determinist (Dennett)

Determinism is compatible with a meaningful kind of free will once “free will” is properly defined — so the self-undermining charge dissolves.

Jerry Coyne / Daniel Dennett (vs Craig)

Strongest response

Target is determinism itself

Craig notes the in-house dispute but holds his target is hard determinism, not the compatibilist redefinition — which Coyne himself dismisses as “a semantic trick.” A belief physically mandated, not reflectively reached, still cannot be rationally justified.

Craig determinism-not-compatibilism

Persistent gaps in the corpus

The objections the corpus does not adequately answer — flagged honestly rather than papered over:

1. The reliabilist / proper-function reply to EAAN is never properly engaged. The most serious gap. Every expert states the “survival not truth” worry; none refutes the detailed claim that fitness and truth-tracking substantially overlap in survival-relevant domains.

2. Plantinga’s actual probability structure is never laid out. The formal claim — that P(R | N&E) is low or inscrutable — is paraphrased repeatedly but never unpacked, leaving the strongest version unstated.

3. The AI disanalogy is unaddressed. The 2026 AI-as-experiment move ignores that AI has an explicit non-truth-tracking objective function, whereas evolved brains have no specified objective function at all.

4. Knechtle’s Lewisian “impossibility” claim is asserted, not argued. “The rational cannot come from the non-rational” is a strong modal claim his interlocutors directly contest with emergence accounts; he replies “that is ludicrous” and pivots.

5. Functionalist accounts of intentionality — how physical states can be about anything — are load-bearing here but treated only at Q-1.9.2.
