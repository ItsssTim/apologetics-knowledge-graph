The heart of the gospel

Why did Jesus have to die? Why not just forgive?

It is a fair question, asked by sceptics and seekers alike. If God is all-powerful and all-loving, why the cross, the blood, the death? Why not simply forgive and move on? The answer is the most important thing Christianity has to say, and it turns on what forgiveness actually is.

First / Why not just forgive?
Step 1 of 4
The hidden assumption

The question assumes forgiveness is free. It never is.

The objection sounds obvious: an all-powerful God could just wave sin away. It stays obvious right up until you ask what forgiveness actually costs.

The snap
The sharp version, as one debater put it, is that an all-powerful God could simply Thanos our sins away, so the whole crucifixion looks unnecessary, even irrational. If he can do anything, why not just snap his fingers and forgive? Hold that thought, because the answer is hiding inside the word forgive.
Never free
Here is what nobody notices: forgiveness is never free. When someone wrongs you, you can make them pay, or you can forgive, which means you absorb the cost yourself. As Knechtle puts it, to forgive is to take the hit you did not deserve. Cancel a debt and the money does not vanish into thin air; you eat the loss. Forgiveness always lands on somebody.
The debt
So if I smash your window, forgiving me does not mean the window magically un-breaks. It means either you pay for it or I do. Sin is a real debt, a real wrong done to God and to people, not a clerical error to be quietly deleted. The question is never whether it gets paid, only who pays it. That single shift reframes the whole thing.
The theory you'll hear
An all-powerful God could just forgive by decree, no cross required. But raw power does not dissolve the problem, because forgiveness is not a power problem, it is a cost problem. Even God, in forgiving, must do what forgiveness always does: absorb the wrong rather than pretend it never happened. To simply wave evil away is not forgiveness at all. It is calling evil nothing.
✓
Confidence: the real question is not why didn't God just forgive, but how does anyone forgive. The answer is always the same, someone absorbs the cost. Keep that in view.
Second / Love and justice
Step 2 of 4
A good God can't shrug at evil

A God who just ignored evil wouldn't be good.

Behind why not just forgive sits a picture of God as nothing but indulgent. But a God who simply overlooked evil would not be loving. He would be unjust.

Not just kind
God is love, but he is also just and holy, and those are not in competition; both belong to being good. A father who laughed off his child's cruelty would not be more loving, he would be less. Love that never takes a wrong seriously is not love at all. It is indifference dressed up as niceness.
The judge
Picture a judge who let every criminal walk free because he was just so loving. You would not call him good; you would call him corrupt. We actually want a God who takes the abuser, the tyrant, the genocide seriously, who refuses to file the world's evil under no harm done. A God who simply forgave everything would be morally asleep.
The dilemma
So here is the real tension, and it is a beautiful one. God is perfectly just, so evil cannot just be waved away; and he is perfectly merciful, so he longs to forgive. How can he be both at once, the judge who must not ignore the wrong, and the Father who wants to pardon the wrongdoer? The cross is the answer to exactly that question.
The theory you'll hear
If God is so loving, justice shouldn't matter to him. But strip justice out of love and you are left with sentimentality, a God who shrugs at the Holocaust. The people most wounded by evil are the last to want a God who lets it slide. Real love and real justice both refuse to call evil acceptable. The cross is where they stop pulling apart and finally meet.
✓
Confidence: a God who merely overlooked evil would be neither good nor just, and we would not want him. The problem the cross solves is how God can be both perfectly just and freely merciful.
Third / The cross
Step 3 of 4
Judge and rescuer in one

So God paid it himself, as both judge and rescuer.

Here is the resolution. God does not ignore the debt, and he does not make us carry it. He pays it himself. That is what the cross actually is.

Both roles
Knechtle frames it perfectly: God is the judge, so the penalty must be paid; but he is also the justifier, gracious and merciful. (Romans 3:25-26) So he becomes a man in Christ, lives a sinless life, and on the cross absorbs the penalty himself. (2 Corinthians 5:21) Justice is fully satisfied, the wrong really is paid for, and mercy is fully extended, you go free. Both at once, in a single act.
Steps down
Imagine a judge who hands down the rightful fine, then steps off the bench, takes off the robe, and pays it out of his own pocket for the guilty defendant. Justice is not cheated, the penalty is real and met, but the debtor walks free. That is the strange arithmetic of the cross: God upholding the law and rescuing the lawbreaker in the very same motion.
In person
And this is the crucial part: God does it himself, he does not make someone else suffer. Christ is not a hapless third party dragged in; he is God in person, stepping into our place. The one who sets the penalty is the very one who pays it. That detail matters enormously for the objection in the next step.
The theory you'll hear
How can one person's death pay for everyone, or for an infinite wrong? Because of who is paying. The worth of a payment tracks the worth of the one who makes it; an apology from a stranger and from your dearest friend are not equal in weight. This is not a finite man dying, it is God himself, of infinite worth, which is exactly why one act can cover what no amount of our own effort ever could.
✓
Confidence: the cross is God as both judge and justifier, upholding justice by paying the penalty himself. Not evil ignored, not us crushed, but God absorbing the debt in person.
Fourth / Barbaric?
Step 4 of 4
The cosmic-child-abuse charge

It's not barbarism. It's the costliest love there is.

The modern ear hears blood sacrifice and recoils: barbaric, primitive, even cosmic child abuse. But every piece of that reaction gets the cross backwards.

Not child abuse
The cruellest version calls it divine child abuse, an angry Father punishing an innocent Son. But that misses the very heart of it: the Son is God. This is not God taking it out on a third party; it is God taking it onto himself. Self-sacrifice, not scapegoating. Once you see that the Son is God in person, the abuse charge simply dissolves.
Not barbaric
And the blood is not God needing gore to calm down. It is the honest measure of what evil actually costs. As Knechtle notes, every age before ours grasped that grave wrong is grave; it is the modern West, having trivialised sin, that finds the price shocking. The cross is not too violent for our wrongs. We have simply stopped taking them seriously.
Costliest love
So far from barbaric, it is the deepest love imaginable. God could have ignored evil, and stopped being good; or made us pay, and lost us forever; instead he absorbed it himself. (Romans 5:8) That is what love does at full stretch: it pays. The cross is not a stain on God's character. It is God's heart, laid utterly bare.
The theory you'll hear
Why couldn't God just forgive the way we do, with no cross at all? But this is exactly how we forgive, only on a tiny scale: we absorb the hurt instead of passing it on. The cross is that same move, made by God, for the whole weight of human evil. Some faiths have God forgive by sheer decree, but that leaves justice unmet. Only here do perfect justice and perfect mercy both come out whole.
✓
Confidence: it is not barbaric and not child abuse; it is God himself absorbing what we owed, holding justice and mercy together at infinite cost. The cross is the measure of the love, not a stain on it.
