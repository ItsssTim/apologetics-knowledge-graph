Sharing your faith

How do I share the gospel with an atheist friend?

The instinct is to win the argument. The better aim is to win a hearing, and then a person. Here is how to talk about Jesus with someone who does not believe in God, in a way that respects them and actually opens the door.

First / Understand them
Step 1 of 4
Who you're actually talking to

Start by understanding what they actually reject.

Before a single argument, find out who is in front of you. An atheist is not one thing, and assuming the wrong one is how most conversations die in the first minute.

Not one type
Some atheists are angry at the church, some are quietly indifferent, some have read more theology than most Christians. Ask before you assume. Your first job is to learn what THIS person believes and why, not to argue with the cardboard cut-out in your head. A good question shows more respect than a rehearsed answer.
Head and heart
Most people do not reason their way out of God on a whiteboard. As John Lennox observes, the resistance is often emotional underneath: an unanswered prayer, a hypocrite who wounded them, a suffering they could not square with a good God. The intellectual objection is real, but it frequently sits on top of a story. Listen for the story.
Common ground
Most atheists prize reason and evidence, and that is shared ground, not a threat. Christianity is not afraid of evidence; it invites it. So you can meet them exactly where they stand, as someone who also wants to believe what is actually true and is willing to follow the evidence wherever it goes.
The mistake to avoid
Assuming they secretly know God and are just rebelling. It is a popular line, and it is usually both untrue and insulting. Plenty of atheists are sincere, thoughtful, and genuinely persuaded, and many were pushed there by real pain. Treat them as a fool or a rebel and the conversation is over before it starts. Treat them as an honest person worth listening to, and it can actually begin.
✓
Remember: you are talking to a person with a story, not a position to be defeated. Find out what they actually believe before you say a word in reply.
Second / Where to begin
Step 2 of 4
Where to actually begin

Begin with the questions their worldview can't answer.

You do not have to prove everything at once. A few honest questions, gently asked, do far more than a wall of arguments fired all at once.

Three questions
Three questions carry a lot, asked, not preached. Why is there something rather than nothing? Why is the universe so finely tuned for life (Romans 1:20)? And where does real right and wrong come from, if we are only atoms? Those are the cause, design and morality arguments in plain clothes, the heart of the science and morality cards.
Morality
The moral one usually lands hardest. Most atheists are deeply moral people who believe some things are genuinely wrong, not just unfashionable. Gently asking where that objective right and wrong comes from, if there is no God, opens a real conversation, because their own worldview struggles to ground the very morality they live by every day.
Resurrection
And you have a historical event to point at, not only philosophy. The resurrection is a claim you can investigate, exactly the kind of evidence an atheist says they want. It shifts things from abstract God-talk to a specific, checkable question: what actually explains the empty tomb and the transformed disciples?
The mistake to avoid
Leading with the Bible says, or firing every argument at once. To someone who does not accept the Bible's authority, quoting it as proof just sounds circular, so save it for later. And a barrage of arguments feels like an ambush. As Preston Perry models, ask one good question and then truly listen. A question invites someone to think; a lecture invites them to defend.
✓
Remember: you are not proving God like a sum. You are opening the door with the questions a godless world leaves hanging, and letting them walk through it themselves.
Third / Point to Jesus
Step 3 of 4
Arguments aren't the destination

Arguments clear the path. Jesus is the point.

The arguments can get someone to maybe there is a God. They cannot get someone to Jesus. So do not get stuck forever in the foothills of God's existence; move toward the person of Christ.

Not just God
Once the door is open even a crack, steer toward Jesus himself: who he claimed to be, how he lived, how he died, and the evidence he rose. Endlessly debating whether some vague God exists can become a way of never reaching the actual question, which is what you do with Christ.
Your story
You are not only an arguer; you are a witness. What has Jesus actually done in your life? A changed life is hard to argue away, because it is not a syllogism, it is you, in front of them. Lennox's line is that when you watch two and two make four thousands of times, you start to believe it really does.
Grace
And when you reach the gospel itself, lead with the thing no other worldview offers: grace. Not be good enough to earn your way up to God, but a God who came down because you never could. To someone who assumes religion is just rules and guilt, grace is genuinely surprising, and genuinely good, news.
The mistake to avoid
Winning the argument and losing the person. You can be completely right and still drive someone away by being smug about being right. The goal was never to make them feel stupid; it was to make Jesus worth a second look. Hold your points lightly and the person carefully. Nobody was ever argued into the kingdom by being humiliated at the door.
✓
Remember: the aim is not a defeated atheist, it is a person who has met Jesus. Arguments open the door; only Christ is worth walking through it for.
Fourth / The posture
Step 4 of 4
How to carry the whole thing

Play the long game, with love and patience.

How you say it matters as much as what you say. The Bible's own instruction is to give your reasons with gentleness and respect, not to win at any cost.

Listen first
The old line holds: people do not care what you know until they know that you care. Lennox makes a point of befriending the people he debates, sharing a meal, getting to know them as people first. Ask more than you tell. A real friendship where someone feels genuinely heard will do more than a dozen clever comebacks.
I don't know
You will not have every answer, and that is fine. I do not know, let me look into that is far more credible than a slick non-answer, and faking it loses trust on the spot. Honesty about the hard questions, including the ones you yourself still wrestle with, is what earns you the right to be heard.
It's God's job
Finally, relax. You are not the Holy Spirit. Your job is to love, to live it, to give a reason (1 Peter 3:15) and to plant seeds; changing a heart was never on your shoulders. That frees you to be patient and kind and unworried, and to stay someone's friend whether they believe this year, in ten years, or never.
The mistake to avoid
Treating it as a single debate to win today. Most people come to faith slowly, through a relationship, not one knockout conversation, and pushing for a verdict on the spot usually backfires. Be the friend who is still there long after the argument has cooled, still praying, still living something they cannot quite explain away.
✓
Remember: love them, listen, give your reasons honestly, and leave the rest to God. You plant the seed; he is the one who makes it grow.
Putting it together
The whole approach
Four steps, taken together

So, how do you share it?

1 · Understand them
Find out what THIS atheist actually believes and why. There is usually a story under the argument.
›
2 · Find the bridge
Begin with the questions a godless world can't answer (cause, fine-tuning, morality) and a resurrection they can investigate.
›
3 · Point to Jesus
Arguments only reach a vague maybe. Move on to Christ, your changed life, and grace.
›
4 · The posture
Listen more than you talk, admit what you don't know, play the long game, leave the heart to God.
›

So you are not out to crush an atheist in a debate. You are out to love a person, take their questions seriously, point past the arguments to Jesus himself, and trust God with the timing. Win the person, not the point.
