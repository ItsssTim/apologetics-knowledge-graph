Mind, matter, and reason

If I'm just atoms, can I trust my own mind?

It is the modern creed in a sentence: you are your brain, your thoughts are chemistry, and the self is a story the neurons tell. It can sound hard-headed and scientific. But follow it all the way down and it turns on itself, because if your mind is only physics, the case for that very claim collapses along with everything else.

First / Just atoms?
Step 1 of 4
What is being claimed

You are nothing but atoms. Supposedly.

Start by stating the view fairly, because it is genuinely the default assumption of a great many clever people. It just turns out to be far less stable than it looks.

Nothing but
On the strict naturalist picture, a human being is nothing but matter in motion. Your thoughts are brain states, your choices are chemistry, and your sense of being a self is a useful trick the neurons play. There is no you over and above the wiring. There is just the wiring, firing.
The claim
It is a tidy, confident story, and it wears the lab coat of science. Mind is reduced to brain, brain to chemistry, chemistry to physics, and physics to particles bumping in the dark. Reason itself becomes one more chemical reaction, no more about truth than a kettle coming to the boil is about truth.
No you?
And that is the quiet cost. If you really are nothing but atoms obeying physics, then love is a chemical state, your decisions were never truly yours, and the person you call yourself is a kind of afterglow thrown off by the machinery. Almost no one who says they believe this actually manages to live as though it were true.
The theory you'll hear
Neuroscience shows thoughts are just brain chemistry; the self is an illusion. Brain activity accompanies every thought, and no Christian denies it. But that a thought has a physical side does not show it is nothing but its physical side, any more than the message of a letter is nothing but ink. And notice: the self is an illusion is itself a thought. If no thought can be trusted, that one cannot be either.
✓
Confidence: the reductionist claim is that a human being is nothing but atoms and chemistry, with mind, reason and the self as mere by-products. It sounds scientific, but as the next step shows, it cannot account for the reasoning used to reach it.
Second / The self-defeater
Step 2 of 4
The branch it's sitting on

It saws off its own branch.

Here is the problem the view rarely notices about itself. If your reasoning is just blind physics, then so is the reasoning that led you to this view, and that turns out to be fatal.

Wired to survive
On naturalism, your brain was shaped by evolution, and evolution does not select for true beliefs. It selects for beliefs and behaviours that help you survive and reproduce. The trouble is that useful and true are not the same thing; plenty of false beliefs would keep you alive perfectly well. So blind evolution gives you no guarantee that your mind tracks reality at all.
Darwin's doubt
Darwin himself felt the chill of this. He confessed a horrid doubt: whether the convictions of a mind developed from the lower animals could be trusted when it ventured into the deepest questions. If our thinking is just the residue of survival, why believe it delivers truth about anything grand, including the truth of evolution itself?
The branch
The geneticist Haldane drew the conclusion cleanly. If his mental processes were wholly the motion of atoms in his brain, he had no reason to suppose his beliefs were true, and therefore no reason to suppose his brain was made of atoms. The view destroys itself. Used consistently, it removes the very trust in reason you need in order to argue for it.
The theory you'll hear
But evolution wired our brains, and they obviously work; we survive. They work for surviving, which is exactly the point. Evolution rewards beliefs that keep you alive, not beliefs that are true, and the two regularly come apart. A belief can be useful and false. So natural selection offers no guarantee that your reasoning tracks reality, which is why a worldview betting everything on reason cannot afford to make reason a blind accident.
✓
Confidence: if the mind is only physics shaped by survival, there is no reason to trust that it delivers truth, including the truth of naturalism itself. As Darwin worried and Haldane spelt out, the view undercuts the very reasoning used to reach it.
Third / Where reason fits
Step 3 of 4
Where reason is actually at home

Reason is at home with God.

Turn it around. The strange thing is not that a believer trusts reason. It is that the naturalist does, while having no account of why it should work.

Science assumes it
Science itself runs on two unprovable gifts: that the universe is rationally intelligible, and that the human mind is fit to read it. Lennox makes the point often. The scientist has to assume both before running a single experiment, yet naturalism can explain neither. They are simply taken on trust, because without them nothing works at all.
Mind from mind
But those two gifts are exactly what you would expect if a rational Mind made the universe, and made us in his image. On that view our minds track truth because they were designed by truth, to know a world that was designed to be known. Reason coming from Reason makes sense. Reason as a fluke of mindless matter does not.
Where reason fits
So the very act of reasoning, following an argument, grasping a proof, knowing that you have understood, fits a universe with Mind at the bottom and sits homeless in a universe with only matter at the bottom. Far from belief being the unreasonable option, it is naturalism that has to borrow a confidence in reason its own story can never pay for.
The theory you'll hear
You're just inventing a God to explain the mind. It is the other way around. Naturalism has to assume the very things it cannot explain: that the universe is rationally intelligible and that our minds can be trusted to read it. Theism does not assume them; it explains them. A rational world and a truth-seeking mind are precisely what you would expect if a rational God made both.
✓
Confidence: science assumes a rational universe and a reliable mind, and naturalism can ground neither. Both are exactly what theism predicts: minds made by a rational God to know a world he made knowable. Reason is at home with God, a stranger in a mindless cosmos.
Fourth / More than parts
Step 4 of 4
The category mistake

The ink is not the message.

Underneath it all is a simple error of logic, hidden in the little phrase nothing but. Spot it, and the whole reduction loses its grip.

The love letter
Take a love letter. You could give a complete physical account of it: the chemistry of the ink, the wavelengths of light off the page, the trees the paper came from. Every word of that account would be true, and it would miss the entire point. The message is real, and it is found nowhere in the chemistry. The chemistry carries the message; it does not explain it.
Category mistake
That is the mistake buried in nothing but. Describing the parts of a thing is not the same as exhausting it. A symphony is not nothing but vibrating air, and a person is not nothing but firing neurons. You can give a full account of the mechanism and still have said precisely nothing about the meaning, the mind, or the truth being grasped.
The more
And that more, the mind that means things, reasons, loves and knows, is the very thing crying out to be explained. It does not look like an accident thrown off by particles. It looks like what the Christian says it is: the mark of a Maker who is himself Mind, in whose image we can think and know at all. You are not less than your atoms. You are gloriously more.
The theory you'll hear
Isn't the claim that you're more than atoms just wishful thinking with no evidence? The evidence is the thing you are doing this very moment: weighing an argument, grasping a meaning, caring whether it is true. None of that is the sort of thing atoms, left to themselves, do. And to explain it away as nothing but chemistry, you have to use the very reasoning your explanation says you cannot trust. The wishful thinking runs the other way.
✓
Confidence: nothing but is a category mistake; describing a thing's parts never exhausts it, just as the ink never explains the message. Mind, meaning and reason are real and point beyond mere matter to a Maker who is Mind. You are more than your atoms, not less.

So if you are just atoms, can you trust your own mind? On naturalism, not really, and that is the quiet disaster at the heart of it. A mind that is only blind physics, wired by survival rather than by truth, gives you no reason to trust a single conclusion it reaches, including the conclusion that you are only atoms. The very fact that you can follow this argument, and care whether it is true, is the thing crying out for explanation, and it fits a universe made by a rational God far better than one made by no one at all. You are not nothing but matter. You are a mind, made by Mind, and that is exactly why your reason can be trusted in the first place.
