The silence question

Why does God seem so hidden?

If he is real and wants to be known, why is it not obvious? Honest people feel this, not just sceptics. The worship leader Jon Steingard put it plainly before he walked away from faith: if a relationship with God is the most important thing, why has he not made it plain as day?

First / What is hidden?
Step 1 of 4
Hidden, or just not on demand?

He is quieter than you'd expect, not absent.

First, get the complaint right, because it is usually sharper than people state it. The issue is rarely that there is no evidence at all. It is that the evidence does not corner you.

The complaint
Listen to what is actually being said. Not there is zero trace of God, but there is no proof I cannot argue my way out of. Those are very different complaints. There is a real gap between God leaving no trail and God simply declining to force himself on you. The honest grievance is about the second one.
Not an object
Part of the trouble is a category mistake. God, if he is real, is not one more object inside the universe, a very large man somewhere you could photograph. He is the reason there is a universe at all, outside space and time. You do not find that kind of being by scanning the sky, any more than you find an author by searching her novel for a character with her name.
The trail
And he is not trace-less. The beginning and the fine-tuning of the universe, the moral law you feel pressing on you, the stubborn reality of beauty and love (Romans 1:20), and above all a man in history who claimed to be God. You can read every one of those another way if you want to. That you are free to do so is exactly the point, and it is step two.
The theory you'll hear
If God were real he would just come down to the lake and say hello. That he doesn't is proof he isn't there. But that pictures God as a man who could wander over on request. Demanding that the author step onto the page and wave is a misunderstanding of what an author is. The deeper reply is that, on the Christian account, he did show up, just not on tap. Hold that for step four.
✓
Confidence: the honest complaint is not no evidence, it is no proof you cannot dodge. God seems hidden because he does not overwhelm, not because he left no trail.
Second / Why not overwhelm?
Step 2 of 4
Enough light, and enough shadow

Overwhelming proof would crush what he wants.

So why not remove all doubt? He could presumably blaze across the sky tonight. The reason he does not is the same thread that ran through the suffering question: he is after love, and love cannot be forced.

Pascal
Blaise Pascal put it in a line that has steadied a lot of doubters: God has given enough light for those who want to see, and enough shadow for those who would rather not. Enough to find if you are genuinely looking, not so much that it bludgeons the unwilling into agreement. That mix is not an accident. It is the design.
No force
If God's reality were as unavoidable as a tax bill, you would get compliance, not love. People would bow the way you obey an armed guard, out of fear and prudence. That is all stick and no heart. A God who wanted real children rather than cowed subjects would, precisely, not make himself impossible to refuse.
The step
Knechtle compares it to marriage. There is only so much you can know about a person before you commit; at some point you take a step in order to fully know them. That is what faith actually is. Not believing with no evidence, but trusting enough, on good evidence, to take the step that lets you know the rest.
The theory you'll hear
A decent father would never let his own child grow up doubting he even exists. A sharp point, and worth two honest replies. Loving parents do raise children who, grown, insist they were never loved, so perceived distance is not always the parent's fault. And a father who erased all doubt by sheer overpowering force would secure a frightened child, not a trusting one. The analogy quietly assumes God's goal is to be believed in. His goal is to be loved, and that he will not force.
✓
Confidence: God could overwhelm you and chooses not to, because coerced belief is not the relationship he is after. Enough light to seek by, not so much that it compels.
Third / Head or heart?
Step 3 of 4
We are not neutral about the answer

Whether we find him isn't only about evidence.

Here is the uncomfortable part, and it cuts at everyone. We like to picture ourselves as cool juries weighing the facts. On a question this personal, nobody is quite that detached.

We lean
On the biggest questions people believe and disbelieve partly according to what they want to be true. And few questions are more loaded than this one, because a real God would have a claim on your life. It is worth asking yourself honestly: would I rather he were there, or rather he were not? That lean quietly shapes what you will even accept as evidence.
Seek and find
The Bible's repeated promise is not come and I will overwhelm you. It is seek and you will find (Matthew 7:7), you will find me when you look with your whole heart (Jeremiah 29:13). That is the logic of hide-and-seek with a findable God, not the silence of an empty house. The posture of the looking changes what gets seen.
Honest doubt
And this has to be said carefully. Some people genuinely wish God were real and still do not find him. The sincere doubter is not a myth, and it would be cheap and false to brand every questioner a rebel. But a God who is just, as the earlier questions argued, deals rightly with each heart and meets the real seeker. Nobody honestly looking is turned away at the door.
The theory you'll hear
But I would prefer a loving God existed, and I still don't find him. I am not resisting anything. That is the most honest form of the objection, and it deserves respect, not a slogan. Two gentle things. Wanting a comforting higher power is not the same as wanting the particular God who would reorder your life; the heart can say yes on one level and flinch on another. And the promise still holds: keep genuinely seeking. Many who said exactly this found the door opened later, not on demand, but it opened.
✓
Confidence: finding God is not a pure evidence problem, the will is in it too. The promise is that honest, whole-hearted seeking is met, even when it is not met instantly.
Fourth / He came looking
Step 4 of 4
The silence God broke himself

The Christian claim: God broke his own silence.

Here is the turn that sets Christianity apart from a vague deity behind the clouds. It does not say God is forever hidden and you must guess well. It says he stepped out of hiding, on purpose.

He showed up
Knechtle's answer to the demand show yourself is simply: he did. He became a Jewish man two thousand years ago, spoke a human language, lived a human life, so that people with finite minds could look at him and begin to understand God. That is why the gospels matter; they are the record of God making himself find-able. As Jesus said, whoever has seen me has seen the Father (John 14:9).
Like a person
And you come to know God the way you come to know a person, not the way you prove a theorem. By encounter, by trust, by walking with him over time, not by a knock-down equation on a whiteboard. Vast numbers of people, hard sceptics among them, describe meeting a reality that then changed them. That is the kind of evidence a person gives you, from inside the relationship.
The cost
And the deepest answer to where is God when I call is the cross. God's proof of love is not a light show on demand. It is that he entered the very worst of human life and died inside it. A God willing to do that is not an absentee landlord. He is the opposite: present at the exact point where it feels most like he is missing.
The theory you'll hear
Then why not give me what he gave Thomas, and let me touch the wounds? Fair, and notice Jesus did exactly that for Thomas, he met the doubt rather than scolding it (John 20:24-29). But he added that those who come to trust on good evidence, without a private miracle, are not second-class believers. The evidence he has left, a real life, death and resurrection in history, changed lives, the way it all fits together, is the kind you weigh and then step toward, the way you trust a person, not the kind that flattens you into agreement.
✓
Confidence: Christianity's whole answer to hiddenness is that God did not stay behind the clouds. He showed up in person, and he is known the way a person is known.

So the quiet is not the silence of an empty universe. It is a God who will not overwhelm you into love, who has left more than enough light for anyone genuinely looking, and who, on the Christian claim, has already broken cover, in a manger, on a cross, out of a tomb. The real question may be less where is God, and more whether we have wanted to find him.
