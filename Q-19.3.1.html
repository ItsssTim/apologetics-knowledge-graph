The question

How did the doctrine of original sin and inherited guilt fare from the Reformation through the Enlightenment and into the modern period?

This is a historical-theology survey, not a stand-alone apologetic argument. It tracks one claim — Augustine’s doctrine that all humanity is guilty for Adam’s sin and so under condemnation by nature — across four eras: its medieval default, its Reformation reclamation and intensification, its Enlightenment demolition, and its liberal-modern recasting.

Its apologetic value is genealogical. The Enlightenment objection to original sin — that you cannot be guilty for an act you did not personally commit — is, Craig insists, the same objection Faustus Socinus raised against penal substitution. The debate over original sin and the debate over the atonement are one debate: whether guilt and merit can transfer between persons.

This is a single-expert node (William Lane Craig). Density is honest rather than convergent — one voice, one lecture — and Craig explicitly defers his own rebuttal to a later session.

The historical arc — four strands, not a syllogism

This is not a deductive argument but a chronological cumulative case: each era is a strand, and the force of the page is the trajectory across them — reclamation, intensification, critique, dissolution. Each strand inherits and reacts to the one before it.

Strand 1 · Medieval default

  • Augustine’s inherited guilt for Adam’s sin
  • Drift into Semi-Pelagian compromise (will cooperates with grace)
  • Infant baptism to deal with the guilt
  • The position the Reformation reacts from

Strand 2 · Reformation

  • Luther & Calvin reclaim Augustine’s doctrine
  • Inherited guilt stressed even more strongly
  • Federal headship / imputation replaces biology
  • Tied to forensic justification; paedobaptism retained

Strand 3 · Enlightenment

  • Voltaire & the rationalists
  • “Guilt is non-transferable”
  • Linked to Socinus on substitutionary atonement
  • Doctrine judged an incoherence, a moral impossibility

Strand 4 · Liberal / modern

  • Schleiermacher: sin as weakened God-consciousness
  • Ritschl: a systemic “kingdom of evil”
  • Social-reform, ethical Christianity
  • The word “sin” kept, the content replaced

The historical-theology survey — one lecture, four eras
Strand 2 · The Reformation reclamation
  • Reclaim, then intensify. Luther and Calvin took over the main elements of Augustine’s doctrine and “stressed even more strongly the guilt inherited from Adam.” Not mere preservation — sharpening.
  • The retained-paedobaptism puzzle. Despite their stress on personal faith, the reformers kept infant baptism, following Augustine, “as a means of dealing with the guilt of Original Sin imputed to us from Adam.”
  • From biology to law — federal headship. Imputation “avoided the need to see Original Sin as something transmitted biologically.” Adam is “the federal head of the human race. He represented us before God.”
  • The legal analogy. “Adam did not act for himself alone” — his guilt is imputed to us, and we are “found guilty of being vicariously liable for his crime.” A forensic category, paired with forensic justification.
Strand 3 · The Enlightenment demolition
  • Non-transferability. Voltaire and the rationalists held it “impossible that we could be held guilty or punished for another person’s sins.” If another sinned, “he alone” bears it.
  • The genealogical link to Socinus. Craig’s signature move: the argument “is very similar to Fausto Sozzini’s earlier criticism of the doctrine of substitutionary atonement.” One non-transferability principle, two doctrines.
  • The verdict. It would be “manifestly unjust” for God to punish you for another’s sin; the doctrine was judged “an incoherence, a moral impossibility.”
Strand 4 · The liberal-modern recasting
  • Schleiermacher recast original sin as a weakened God-consciousness — a diminished receptivity to God carried in humanity’s corporate life, not a forensic verdict from Adam.
  • Ritschl relocated sin to the systemic: a corporate “kingdom of evil” answered by the moral and social reform of the kingdom of God, not by satisfaction of a legal penalty.
  • The pattern: keep the human predicament the doctrine named; discard inherited guilt; pour new content into the old word.

Objection 1

Non-transferability of guilt

It is impossible to be guilty for, or punished for, an act you did not personally perform. If some other person sinned, “he alone is the one that is guilty and bears the responsibility.” To punish you for it would be manifestly unjust.

Voltaire and the rationalists; genealogically, Faustus Socinus

Deferred — not answered here

Federal headship stated, not defended

Craig reports the objection at full force (“how in the world could you be guilty for something that you didn’t do?”) and explicitly defers evaluation to the next session. The federal-headship reply is given as the Reformation position, not as a defence. The parallel Socinian critique of penal substitution is answered at Q-8.2.8.

Objection 2

Original sin is biologically incoherent

How could guilt be inherited like a physical trait, passed from parent to child? Treating sin as biologically transmitted makes the doctrine unintelligible to a scientific age.

Implicit Enlightenment / scientific-era worry

Answered indirectly

Imputation was already the move away from biology

The Reformation’s imputation model “avoided the need to see Original Sin as something transmitted biologically from parents to children.” Guilt is reckoned legally through Adam’s federal headship. The objection lands on a biological-transmission model the reformers had already abandoned.

Craig federal-headship / imputation

Objection 3

The doctrine is morally repugnant, not merely false

Even granting coherence, a just God would never condemn the innocent for another’s act. The objection is moral as much as logical: original sin impugns the justice of God.

Voltaire / the rationalists

Deferred — not answered here

Granted as the rationalists’ framing, then postponed

Craig voices the moral charge in the rationalists’ own terms (“It would be manifestly unjust of God…”) and again defers his evaluation. No theodicy of inherited guilt is offered in this lecture.

Persistent gaps in the corpus

Honest deficiencies of this node as built, several flowing from its single-expert, deferred-rebuttal character.

Gap 1 · most serious

The rebuttal is offline

The strongest objection (non-transferability) is presented without the response. Craig’s actual answer lives in the unextracted follow-up lecture. As built, the page documents the attack on the Reformation doctrine more fully than the defence.

Gap 2

No second voice

One expert, one lecture. No Reformed systematician defending federal headship in his own terms; no historian contesting the narrow doctrine-of-sin framing. Density is honest, not convergent.

Gap 3

Reformation reduced to soteriology

Framed through original sin and grace alone. The five solas, sola scriptura, ecclesiology, the political and printing-press causes never appear — a feature for this doctrinal thread, a gap for the Reformation whole.

Gap 4

The liberal recasting is asserted, not evidenced

The Schleiermacher / Ritschl strand names the recasting (weakened God-consciousness; systemic kingdom of evil) but neither theologian is quoted or critiqued. It is a summary, not an engagement.

Gap 5

Editorial overreach at the close

WLC’s mapping of Ritschl’s systemic sin forward onto modern progressive politics is his interpretive read, not established intellectual history. It should be marked as Craig’s extension, not presented as reportage.

Net assessment

A clean chronology and a sharp genealogy

Despite the gaps, the node earns its place: a lucid four-era arc of one doctrine, and the load-bearing observation that the Enlightenment critique of original sin and the Socinian critique of the atonement are one objection about the transfer of guilt and merit.
