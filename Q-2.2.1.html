Does the amount of evil make God unlikely?

The evidential problem of evil — not “evil contradicts God” (that version is dead) but “evil is evidence against God.” Three apologists meet five atheists across ten substantive treatments.

The question

Does the sheer quantity, intensity, and seemingly random distribution of suffering make it improbable that an all-powerful, all-good God exists?

This is the evidential (probabilistic) problem, to be distinguished from the older logical problem. By near-universal agreement among the experts the logical version is dead: as Craig repeats across four debates, “no philosopher in the history of the world” has identified the hidden premise that makes the contradiction explicit, and Plantinga’s free-will defence supplies a compossible third proposition that dissolves it.

The live argument is therefore not that evil is incompatible with God but that evil is evidence against God. Burgis states the upgrade: the world is “soaking in evidence” against a morally perfect God, licensing atheism, not mere agnosticism. The whole battle now lives in whether finite humans are in a position to judge that any given evil is gratuitous — pointless, serving no greater good.

The atheist case — the syllogism the theist must break

This Q-node is unusual: the atheist carries the argument, and the theist runs a defensive structure aimed at one premise. Sinnott-Armstrong gives the canonical deductive form; Burgis upgrades it to inference-to-best-explanation.

Premise 1

If an all-powerful, all-good God existed, there would be no evil except evil logically necessary for some adequately compensating good.

Variant. Sinnott-Armstrong’s deductive frame. Burgis re-casts it probabilistically: a morally perfect God makes pervasive gratuitous suffering improbable, so the evidence licenses an inference, not a proof.

Premise 2

Some evil exists that is not logically necessary for any compensating good — gratuitous evil.

Anchored by paradigm cases. The dying infant in terrible pain (Sinnott-Armstrong), childhood cancer (Smalley), Batwa child mortality before age five (Steingard), and cruel predator-prey suffering with no free agent involved (Burgis, Atkins). This is the premise the entire defence targets.

Conclusion

Therefore no all-powerful, all-good God exists — or its existence is improbable.

The asymmetry. The atheist needs P2 to be known; the theist needs only to show it is not known. That asymmetry is the whole game — and it is why every defender converges on attacking the human capacity to judge an evil gratuitous.

The convergent theist response — five moves against Premise 2

R1

Concede the logical problem — God and evil are logically compatible.

Craig (all five transcripts), Knechtle. Adding “God has morally sufficient reasons for permitting the evil” — as long as it is possibly true — proves compatibility. Craig cites van Inwagen as hostile witness: the incompatibility thesis “is no longer defended, not even by atheists.”

R2

Skeptical theism — finite knowers cannot establish that no reason exists.

Craig (Wykstra, Maxwell, butterfly), Knechtle (St Bernard, two-year-old), Barron (Augustinian). A being “big enough to make the heavens and the Earth” gives a high probability we’d regularly fail to discern its purposes — so our failure to see a reason is not evidence none exists. P2 is therefore unprovable.

R3

Re-aim the teleology — the goal of life is knowledge of God, not happiness.

Craig (Molinist optimal-saved). Evils “utterly pointless with respect to producing happiness” may not be pointless “with respect to producing the knowledge of God.” Maybe only a suffering-suffused world is the feasible world in which the most people freely come to God. The atheist must show a better-feasible world exists — “pure speculation.”

R4

Internal critique — the outrage at evil presupposes the God it denies.

Knechtle (C.S. Lewis reversal), Lennox (Dawkins on Dawkins). The only reason evil is evil is that one knows the good, which requires an objective standard atheism cannot ground. So “the problem of evil” is re-deployed as evidence for God.

R5

Cruciform theodicy — God enters the suffering, and the resurrection grounds justice.

Lennox (canonical), Knechtle, Craig (emotional dimension). Not a deductive theodicy but a person: “God on a cross” means God has not stayed distant from suffering, and the resurrection grounds the ultimate justice and hope “atheism is literally hopeless” to supply.

How each expert deploys it

William Lane Craig 5 transcripts
The skeptical-theist architect
Signature delivery
  • Partition into three problems. Logical (dead), evidential (over-ambitious probability judgements), emotional (the cross).
  • Logical — dead. One compossible proposition dissolves it; van Inwagen as hostile witness: “no longer defended, not even by atheists.”
  • Evidential — skeptical theism. Quotes Wykstra: a maker of heaven and Earth gives high probability we’d fail to discern its purposes. Packages chaos theory as science via the butterfly effect and a long Maxwell “singular points” quotation.
  • Molinist optimal-saved. The purpose of life is knowledge of God, not happiness; burden-shifts the atheist to prove a better-feasible world exists.
  • Eschatological reframe. Eternity will reduce all suffering to “an infinitesimal moment.”
Notable concession from the foil

Hitchens largely conceded the philosophical point: “If I wasn’t a believer I would not feel God owed me an explanation. I’m not one of those atheists who thinks you can go around complaining” — a striking admission from a New Atheist.

Cliffe & Stuart Knechtle 4 transcripts
Moral-grounding reversal + cruciform pivot
Signature moves
  • The C.S. Lewis reversal. Convert the objection into evidence for theism: “Why am I so bothered by it to begin with?” Objective good requires “some type of god who creates and defines valuable human beings.”
  • The evolutionary-indignation challenge. “Why does it bug you so much from an evolutionary perspective?”
  • Cruciform pivot. Christianity is “the suffering religion” — “an amazing suffering God” rather than a deductive theodicy.
  • Plantinga “no-seems” + the St Bernard / pup-tent and two-year-old in time-out analogies for finite epistemic access.
Known weakness

Against Smalley, Stuart never answers the options-menu point (God set the menu and put childhood cancer on it) nor the omnipotence-vs-required-tool point. He deflects to the evolutionary challenge, and Smalley repeatedly calls it “sidestepping and deflecting.” A clean case where the atheist reductio outruns the defence on the day.

John Lennox 1 transcript
The cruciform anchor — richest single treatment
Signature moves
  • Atheism doesn’t solve it. “It removes the suffering’s meaning but leaves the suffering intact and removes all hope.” To Dawkins’s “bleak but not false”: “it doesn’t mean it’s true either.”
  • Dawkins on Dawkins. Naturalism abolishes the very categories of good and evil needed to state the problem.
  • The cross + resurrection. God “has not remained distant from the problem of suffering but himself has become part of it”; the resurrection grounds ultimate justice (C.S. Lewis appetite-fits-object).
  • Socratic re-framing. Not “why does God permit this?” but “are there grounds for trusting God with it?”
Canonical close

Delivered with “I almost hesitate to say this”: “If you could see what God does with the innocent who have suffered, you’d have no more questions.” Anchored by the disclosure of his niece, 22, killed by a brain aneurysm the year surgery saved his own life.

Robert Barron 1 transcript (with Craig)
Augustinian greater-good (concurring voice)
Distinctive move
  • “The best argument against God” — but its strength is emotional, not logical.
  • Standard Augustinian theodicy: God permits evil to bring about a greater good we cannot see from “this little tiny swath of space and time.”
  • Refuses to trivialise the pastoral register; supplies the pedigree to Craig’s chaos-theory analogy — a Catholic-Evangelical convergence.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

Logical PoE — God and evil are contradictory

An all-good, all-powerful God would eliminate evil; evil exists; therefore no such God exists. A flat contradiction.

Epicurus, popular atheism

Strongest response

One compossible proposition dissolves it

“God has morally sufficient reasons” is possibly true, which proves logical compatibility. No atheist has identified the hidden premise that yields a contradiction.

Craig van Inwagen hostile witnessKnechtle Plantinga “no-seems”

Objection 2

Evidential PoE — gratuitous evil makes God improbable (IBE)

The world is “soaking in evidence” against a morally perfect God. Mere logical possibility of a reason doesn’t rescue theism; inference to the best explanation favours atheism.

Ben Burgis, Walter Sinnott-Armstrong

Strongest response

Skeptical theism — the probability judgement is unprovable

Finite knowers in a tiny slice of space-time cannot establish that no reason exists; the judgement that any evil is gratuitous is “very, very ambitious.” The sufficient reason for your lupus “might not emerge until hundreds of years from now.”

Craig Wykstra + Maxwell ripple-effectKnechtle St Bernard, two-year-old

Objection 3

Natural evil — cruel predator-prey suffering, no free will

You can run the problem from natural laws alone: animals kill and eat each other in cruel ways inflicting enormous suffering, and no free agent is involved. The free-will defence cannot touch it.

Ben Burgis, Peter Atkins (“unconscious evil of evolution”)

Gap — largely deflected

Subsumed under cognitive limits, never answered

Craig folds it into skeptical theism + Molinist optimal-saved; nobody directly engages the predator-prey case. Plantinga’s speculative demonic-free-will move is named by Burgis but no expert defends it.

Objection 4

The options-menu — God put childhood cancer on the menu

Even granting free will, cancer in a three-year-old “wouldn’t have been an option unless God created it.” God set the menu of possible human experiences and chose to include it.

David C. Smalley

Gap — unanswered

Knechtle deflects to evolutionary indignation

Stuart never addresses the menu-setting point, pivoting instead to “why does it bug you on naturalism?” Smalley repeatedly names the deflection. The clearest case in the corpus where the atheist reductio is sharper than the reply.

Aggregation gap

Objection 5

Isaiah 45:7 — “I create evil,” so God authored evil

“I form the light and create darkness, I make peace and create evil, I the Lord do all these things.” If God created everything that exists and cancer exists, God created cancer.

David C. Smalley

Strongest response

Out of context; the Hebrew is calamity, and evil is privation

The “evil” of Isaiah 45:7 is judgement / destruction language, not metaphysical evil. Evil is the privation of good; God is the source of goodness, and free will introduces the brokenness.

Knechtle context + privation reply

Objection 6

Omnipotence vs the required tool

Saying God needs to permit evil to accomplish a good is to say he is not all-powerful — a prerequisite tool is something only the non-omnipotent require.

David C. Smalley

Gap — unanswered

Conceded without articulation

Knechtle replies “I agree with you actually, but again you’re totally missing it” without ever stating what the agreement is or how it rescues omnipotence. The argument is left standing.

Objection 7

The pre-emption stack — seven theodicies disarmed in advance

Punishment-for-sin (babies haven’t sinned), original sin (we call collective punishment barbaric), heaven-compensation (why not send the infant straight to heaven?), soul-making (an omnipotent God could instil compassion without real suffering), and worship-maximising (“awfully narcissistic”).

Walter Sinnott-Armstrong

Gap — largely unrebutted

The framework is answered; the specifics are not

Craig answers with skeptical theism but never returns to refute the individual defeaters. The straight-to-heaven and soul-making points in particular land unanswered — Craig re-aims to knowledge-of-God rather than character, sidestepping rather than engaging.

Aggregation gap

Objection 8

Skeptical-theism symmetry — the evil-god challenge

If God can hide behind “mysterious morally sufficient reasons,” so can an evil god hide behind mysterious malevolent reasons. The defence proves too much.

Stephen Law

Strongest response

Grant the inductive symmetry; goodness comes from elsewhere

Craig concedes symmetry at the inductive level but argues God’s goodness is established by the moral argument, not by induction over observed evils. The asymmetry is restored upstream.

Craig moral-argument grounding

Objection 9

Moral outrage has an evolutionary explanation

The sense that suffering is wrong can be explained by the evolutionary origins of altruism, so it need not presuppose an objective standard or God.

Jon Steingard, David C. Smalley

Strongest response

Press the grounding gap (partial)

Knechtle presses that evolutionary explanation describes why we feel outrage, not whether the outrage tracks a real objective wrong. Steingard himself concedes he is “not totally sold” on the evolutionary story — but the point is never decisively closed.

Knechtle partialNot decisively closed

Objection 10

Where was Jesus? — the concrete intervention-expectation

A Christian organisation worked in Jesus’s name among the Batwa, where most children die before five. “I didn’t see Jesus doing the work, I saw people doing the work.” An intervening God would surely have acted here.

Jon Steingard

Gap — unanswered

Cruciform theodicy answers the wrong question

The cross answers “is God absent from suffering?” but not “why didn’t God act here, where his own people were begging him to?” Knechtle never engages the specific empirical expectation.

Aggregation gap

Objection 11

Atheism need not entail despair

Hume died cheerfully without belief in an afterlife; Boswell came to watch the atheist’s resolve shatter and was himself shaken. Life is “tough” but not nihilistic — like a play, wonderful even though it finishes.

Julian Baggini

Theist response weakest here

The despair-hammer is out-narrated

Knechtle’s “the only logical response is despair — case closed” is absorbed and re-framed by Baggini as “tough but not inevitably miserable.” One of the few exchanges where the atheist out-narrates the apologist on suffering.

Aggregation gap

Objection 12

Logical possibility vs evidential weight (the unengaged distinction)

“It could be that God has a reason” only establishes logical possibility. Burgis explicitly distinguishes this from the evidential weight of pervasive gratuitous evil — and charges the defender with never defending a positive theodicy beyond “it’s possible.”

Ben Burgis

Gap — unengaged

The distinction is never addressed

Knechtle replies with more possibility-statements and the C.S. Lewis reversal, never engaging Burgis’s precise point that IBE governs once compatibility is granted. The corpus answers the logical problem and treats it as having answered the evidential one.

Aggregation gap

Objection 13

Skeptical theism corrodes ordinary moral knowledge

If we cannot judge God’s reasons for permitting any evil, can we judge moral situations at all? Sinnott-Armstrong’s neighbour analogy: a man who lets his children starve gives us evidence he is bad — we don’t infer hidden good reasons.

Walter Sinnott-Armstrong (neighbour analogy)

Gap — unaddressed

The standard worry never surfaces

Craig never addresses the objection that skeptical theism, taken consistently, undermines our capacity to make ordinary moral judgements. The neighbour analogy is left standing as a parity defeater.
