The New Perspective on Paul

Did the Reformers misread Paul — is justification a marker of covenant membership rather than imputed righteousness? A single expert, William Lane Craig, expounds the view across three lectures and then argues it “should now be abandoned.”

The question

Did the Reformers misread Paul? Is justification the declaration that one belongs to God’s covenant people — marked out by faith — rather than the imputing of a forensic, normative righteousness to the believer?

The New Perspective on Paul (NPP) is, in Craig’s framing, a “very recent” third option set against both the Catholic (infused-righteousness) and Reformation (imputed-righteousness) accounts. It grows out of E.P. Sanders’ Paul and Palestinian Judaism (1977) and its thesis of covenantal nomism: Second Temple Judaism was not the works-righteousness caricature the Reformers attacked — you get into the covenant by grace and stay in by doing the works the law requires. On that reading Paul is “not really all that different from what the Judaism of his day was saying.”

The stakes are the whole Protestant doctrine. If the righteousness of God (dikaiosynē theou) means God’s covenant faithfulness rather than a normative right standing reckoned to the believer, then sola fide as the Reformers understood it is not what Paul taught. This is the node where the corpus tests whether the Reformation read its own Bible correctly.

Coverage — a single-voice node

Read this as one wing of the NPP, not the movement entire

Everything below is William Lane Craig expounding and then dismantling the Sanders / Dunn version of the NPP. The corpus’s most important limitation — flagged prominently in the gaps section — is that N.T. Wright, the NPP’s strongest living exponent, is never named, quoted, or engaged across any of the three lectures. Craig refutes the lexical-reductionist wing and treats the case as closed; the page is thorough on that wing and silent on Wright.

The convergent case — exposition, then a cumulative refutation in three strands

This is not a deductive syllogism. Craig first steelmans the NPP (Part 13), then assembles a cumulative case against it in two movements: a logical / exegetical critique of the soteriology (Part 14, following Gundry) and a lexical demolition of its defining word-claim (Part 15, following Seifrid and Irons). The strands are independent — each could stand even if the others failed.

Exposition · Part 13

Covenantal nomism — grace to get in, works to stay in.

The steelman. Craig grants the NPP its strongest statement: “the traditional Reformers have seriously misunderstood Paul.” Sanders’ view is “very subtle” — works “don’t earn your salvation” but are “the means by which you stay in.” Assessment is explicitly deferred to the next two lectures.

Soteriology · Part 14

Concede the Jewish half; deny the Pauline half — for Paul works are evidential, not instrumental.

Gundry plus logic. Sanders’ own data show works are a condition of staying-in for Judaism; for Paul they are “only… a sign.” Craig’s refinement: “If one has genuine saving faith, one will do good works” is P ⊃ Q — works are a necessary byproduct of saving faith, not a sufficient or instrumental cause. Sanders and Gundry are both salvaged.

Lexicon · Part 15

“Righteousness of God” is normative, never “covenant faithfulness” — six independent lines say so.

The cumulative refutation. Translation reductio; Phil 3 (“availed him nothing”); the opposite of righteousness is wickedness, not unfaithfulness (Seifrid); the Gentile / Job reductio; the 7-of-283 / 500+ category mistake; and Irons’ lexical study finding no relational sense. Dikaioo is a forensic, soteriological term.

Verdict

The NPP “has run its useful course and should now be abandoned”; the Reformers were on target.

Hostile-witness close. Even Dunn now concedes the forensic / punitive dimension (wrath and judgment, Rom 1:16–18; 3:3–6). Justification is “God’s declaration of a new legal status for believers involving a normative right standing before the holy God.”

How the argument is built — William Lane Craig across three lectures

William Lane Craig3 transcripts · Parts 13–15
The sole voice — exposition front-loaded, a hard verdict at the close
The exposition (Part 13)
  • Scrupulous steelman. Introduces the NPP neutrally as “a contemporary alternative, indeed a very recent alternative” and defers assessment.
  • Covenantal nomism defined: grace to get in, the works the law requires to stay in — non-meritorious yet instrumental.
  • Even defines “Palestinian” Judaism (homeland vs Diaspora) for the lay audience — a fair-minded prelude (cf. Part 15’s blunt close).
The soteriological critique (Part 14) — Gundry
  • Concede the Jewish half, deny the Pauline half. Jews emphasised obedience “as the way of staying in the covenant” — but Paul is “un-Jewish” here.
  • For Paul obedience is “proportionately slight,” takes the form of exhortation not “legal interpretation, extension, and application.”
  • Evidential, not instrumental — the load-bearing distinction: good works are “a sign of staying in,” not the means of it.
  • Internal critique: Sanders’ own cited evidence shows works are a condition for Judaism; 2 Cor 13:5 (“Examine yourselves”) marks the evidential / self-diagnostic role.
The distinctive contribution — necessary vs sufficient
  • Teaches propositional logic to the lay audience, then applies it: “If one has genuine saving faith, one will do good works” has the form P ⊃ Q.
  • Saving faith is sufficient for good works, so good works are necessary for saving faith — affirming Sanders and Gundry at once.
  • The load-bearing synthesis: works are “the necessary byproduct or concomitant of saving faith” — the classic Lordship / Reformed fruit-of-regeneration position (Q-9.2.3).
The lexical refutation (Part 15) — a cumulative case
  • Translation reductio — the reading implies translators “for generations” mistranslated dikaiosynē theou, since “righteousness” does not mean “faithfulness.”
  • Philippians 3 — Paul was already blameless as to covenant faithfulness and “it availed him nothing”; faithfulness cannot be what justifies.
  • Opposite-of-righteousness — the opposite is wickedness (Rom 1:18) or lawlessness (2 Cor 6:14), not unfaithfulness; faithlessness is one sin among many (Rom 1:29–31). Seifrid: not all righteous behaviour is covenant-keeping.
  • Gentile reductio — if unrighteousness just is covenant-unfaithfulness, Gentiles can be neither unrighteous (Rom 1:18) nor, like Job, righteous; this turns the NPP’s own aim (justifying Gentiles) against it.
  • Category mistake — only 7 of 283 “covenant” and 500+ “righteousness” occurrences co-occur; one keeps or breaks a covenant, one does not act righteously with respect to it.
  • Irons’ study — The Righteousness of God (2015), “the definitive work,” finds sedek / dikaiosynē normative across the Hebrew OT, the LXX and extra-biblical Greek; dikaioo is forensic and soteriological for Paul.
The hostile-witness kill-shot — Dunn against himself

The leading NPP proponent James D.G. Dunn (cited as “the late James D.G. Dunn”) has himself backed away from the reductionism, conceding that God’s righteousness “includes wrath and judgment as well as faithfulness and salvation… clearly implicit in the sequences Romans 1:16-18 and 3:3-6.” Craig caps it with Blocher’s exclamation over Rom 4:5 — “the staggering audacity of Paul’s combination of words: God who justifies the ungodly!” — the forensic doctrine the relational reading cannot accommodate (Q-8.2).

Density / deep-dive note

Part 15 is unusually academic for a lay catechetical lecture — lexical statistics, Septuagint usage, word-frequency counts — and the verdict (“run its useful course and should now be abandoned”) is out of character against Craig’s eirenic close in Part 13. Treat the node as a one-camp settlement: decisive against the lexical-reductionist NPP, but reached on the strength of Reformed-sympathetic specialists (Gundry, Seifrid, Irons) plus a single Dunn concession, without testing the strongest opposing case.

Objection 1

The Reformers misread Paul; correctly read, he is “not really all that different” from his Judaism

Justification by faith as a law/grace antithesis is a sixteenth-century anachronism imposed on a first-century Jew who shared his contemporaries’ covenantal framework.

E.P. Sanders; the NPP programme

Strongest response

Concede the Jewish half, deny the Pauline half

Covenantal nomism may describe Second Temple Judaism — but Paul’s extension of faith and grace to staying in is “un-Jewish,” and the defining lexical claim about dikaiosynē is independently false.

Craig Parts 14–15Gundry the disanalogy

Objection 2

“Works are the condition of remaining in” — so Paul keeps the Jewish pattern

Salvation is by grace but judgment is according to works; works are the condition of staying in even though they do not earn salvation. Paul holds fast to this Jewish mode of thinking.

Sanders, covenantal nomism

Strongest response

For Paul works are “only… a sign”

Sanders’ cited evidence shows works are a condition for Judaism, but for Paul they are “only, but not unimportantly, a sign of staying in.” The whole of Galatians shows “both getting in and staying in is a matter of faith in Christ. It is faith from beginning to end.”

Gundry / Craig Part 14

Objection 3

Paul says people are judged according to their works — so works condition staying-in for Paul too

The judgment-according-to-works texts (Rom 8:13; Gal 5:16–18 and the warnings against the deeds of the flesh) show works conditioning final salvation for Paul, just as for Judaism.

Sanders

Strongest response

Evidential, not instrumental — 2 Cor 13:5

Judgment-according-to-works is satisfied if works are the necessary evidence of genuine faith rather than its instrumental means. “Examine yourselves… Test yourselves” (2 Cor 13:5) points to exactly this evidential, self-diagnostic role — not the means or condition of salvation.

Gundry / Craig Part 14

Objection 4

Doesn’t requiring good works for salvation just collapse into works-righteousness?

If good works are genuinely necessary for salvation, the difference from a works-based gospel looks merely verbal — the condition / evidence distinction seems to be a distinction without a difference.

The apparent either/or (lay worry)

Strongest response

Necessary ≠ sufficient / instrumental

Craig’s distinctive move: “If one has genuine saving faith, one will do good works” is P ⊃ Q. Works are a necessary byproduct or concomitant of saving faith, not a contributing cause. Sanders (works are necessary) and Gundry (works are not instrumental) are both right.

Objection 6

The reductionist reading is needed to explain how God justifies Gentiles

The point of construing justification as a declaration of covenant faithfulness is to explain God’s justifying of the Gentiles — declaring them to be members, faithful to the covenant.

NPP intent (per Craig)

Strongest response

Self-defeating — the Gentile reductio

Gentiles stand outside the Israelite covenant, so a covenant-faithfulness account cannot ground their justification at all. If unrighteousness just is covenant-unfaithfulness, a Gentile could be neither unrighteous (Rom 1:18) nor righteous (Job). The motivating aim tells against the view.

Craig reductio ad absurdum, Part 15

Objection 7

The NPP is the current scholarly consensus; the Reformers are outdated

Pauline scholarship has moved on from the Lutheran reading; the burden is now on the Reformation view, not the NPP.

“Evidently not” — hostile witness

Objection 8

N.T. Wright’s mature account: justification as the law-court declaration of covenant membership

Wright reframes (rather than denies) imputation, and reads dikaiosynē theou as God’s covenant faithfulness worked out through the “single plan through Israel for the world.” This is the NPP’s strongest, most nuanced form.

N.T. Wright

Gap — never engaged

The node’s principal limitation

Wright is not named, quoted, or answered in any of the three lectures. Craig refutes the Sanders / Dunn lexical version and treats the case as closed; Wright’s reframing of dikaiosynē theou and of imputation is left entirely untouched. A reader could finish believing the NPP refuted without ever meeting its best case.

Objection 9

“Works of the law” are Jewish boundary markers, not works-righteousness

Paul opposes circumcision, food laws and Sabbath as ethnic badges dividing Jew from Gentile — not human merit per se. The polemic is sociological, not soteriological.

James D.G. Dunn (the sociological NPP)

Gap — not addressed

Logically independent of the lexical claim refuted

The boundary-marker reading of erga nomou is independent of the “righteousness = faithfulness” lexical claim. Craig answers the lexical Dunn (and uses Dunn’s concession as hostile witness), but never engages Dunn’s positive reading of “works of the law.”

Objection 10

James 2:24 — “justified by works and not by faith alone”

The single most direct biblical objection to the works-are-only-evidential reading, and the verse most plainly in tension with sola fide. Any account of works and justification must harmonise it.

Persistent gaps in the corpus

Gap 1

N.T. Wright is never engaged — the decisive gap.

Craig refutes only the Sanders / Dunn lexical-reductionist wing (“righteousness = covenant faithfulness”) and on its defeat pronounces the whole NPP fit to be “abandoned.” But the movement’s strongest living exponent is Wright, whose mature account — justification as the law-court declaration of covenant membership; dikaiosynē theou as God’s covenant faithfulness through the single plan begun with Israel; imputation reframed rather than denied — is never named, quoted, or answered. Any canonical rendering must foreground this.

Gap 2

James 2:24 is never raised.

“Justified by works and not by faith alone” is the most obvious text a critic would press against Gundry’s works-are-only-evidential reading, and the verse most directly in tension with sola fide. It is not quoted or harmonised anywhere. Cross-ref Q-9.5.5.

Gap 3

Dunn’s boundary-marker thesis is untouched.

The sociological NPP claim that “works of the law” means ethnic identity badges (circumcision, food laws, Sabbath) rather than meritorious works is logically independent of the lexical claim Craig refutes. Craig uses Dunn’s concession on righteousness but never engages Dunn’s positive reading of erga nomou.

Gap 4

Covenantal nomism is conceded but never independently assessed.

Craig grants (via Gundry) that Sanders may correctly describe Second Temple Judaism, and stakes everything on Paul being disanalogous. He does not weigh whether covenantal nomism survives as a reading of the Second Temple sources even if the lexical reduction fails — whether the historical core of the NPP can be true while its lexical claim is false.

Gap 5

The verdict rests on one camp’s specialists.

Gundry, Seifrid and Irons are all Reformed-sympathetic; Dunn supplies only a single conceding line. The “should now be abandoned” verdict is reached without weighing NPP-sympathetic lexical work (e.g. responses to Irons), so the settlement is more one-sided than its confident register implies.
