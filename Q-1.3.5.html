The question

If there is no God, can a purely naturalistic story — evolution, cultural convention, or “human flourishing” — supply objective moral values and binding obligations?

Q-1.3.1 asks whether objective moral values exist; Q-1.3.2 asks whether they require God. This node is where the apologist tests the secular alternatives directly. The recurring claim is that each naturalistic grounding either collapses into description that never reaches prescription (the is–ought gap), grounds morality in something that yields monstrous conclusions (eugenics, the strong eating the weak), or quietly borrows Judeo-Christian moral capital while denying its source.

It is the most field-tested apologetic move in the corpus. Craig himself rates it “far more effective” than cosmology, because “every day you get up and answer how you live the question of whether other people have intrinsic moral worth.”

The convergent argument — all five experts

Premise 1

Naturalism reduces morality to a descriptive by-product of biology and social conditioning.

Observed variants. Craig: “a sort of herd morality” selected for like baboon cooperation. Knechtle: “we're all just matter — there's no difference in value.” Lennox (quoting Ruse): “morality is an illusion fobbed off by our genes.”

Premise 2

Description cannot generate prescription — you cannot derive ought from is (Hume).

Supports. Lennox stacks Einstein (“no scientific foundations of ethics”) and Feynman (“the laws of nature don't come with rules on what we ought to do”). Against Harris, Craig diagnoses circularity: Harris “puts the ought in at the beginning” by defining the good as well-being.

Premise 3

Every concrete naturalistic candidate — evolution, social contract, cultural conditioning, flourishing — either licenses atrocity or is bare stipulation.

Supports. Knechtle's “n-options” enumeration (the mafia kills social contract; eugenics kills survival-of-the-fittest; “are you a Nazi or part of a monarchy?” kills cultural conditioning). Craig's Darwin hive-bee passage: under different evolution, infanticide would be a “sacred duty.”

Conclusion

Therefore objective moral values and binding obligations are best explained by a transcendent personal source — the imago Dei, a “law above the law.”

Deductive in Craig's hands (“if God does not exist, objective moral values do not exist; some exist; therefore God exists”); abductive in the popular hands (Knechtle, Perry: the best explanation of the moral intuitions everyone actually has). All controversy lives in P2 and P3.

The architect — the analytic syllogism
Signature delivery

  • Moral-agency disanalogy: “A lion kills a zebra, it doesn't murder the zebra” — on naturalism human behaviour is ingrained, not binding.
  • Lady Gaga reductio: the rapist who flouts herd morality “is doing nothing more serious than acting unfashionably.”
  • Diagnoses Harris's circularity: conflates “good” with “well-being” by stipulation; the axioms move “is the same as saying you've got to take it by faith.”
  • Genealogical move: “Enlightenment humanism is parasitic upon Western Christianity.”
Signature conclusion

“Take God out of the picture, and all you seem to be left with is an ape-like creature on a speck of dust beset with delusions of moral grandeur.”

Most technical extraction

Vs Wielenberg's atheistic moral realism: “robust causation” selecting abstract moral properties without agency is “a sort of voodoo metaphysics” — Wielenberg's own divine-willing analogy turned against him.

Cliffe & Stuart Knechtle 10 transcripts
The “n-options” street dialectic (dominant voice)
Signature delivery
  • Options enumeration, each dispatched by one vivid counter-example: social contract → the mafia; survival of the fittest → eugenics; cultural conditioning → “are you a Nazi or part of a monarchy?”
  • Blunt value reductio: “if there is no God, there is no difference in value between a rat, a roach and Cliff.”

  • Candour: “if I find out God didn't exist, I'd be a total moral relativist.”
  • Literary anchors: Brave New World, Silence of the Lambs (“you just can't handle that I'm evil”), Dostoevsky, Nietzsche-as-honest-atheist.
Recurring weakness

In hostile settings the “n-options” dialectic is repeatedly interrupted before completion — Aron Ra never lets Cliffe get past “the fifth option.”

The naturalist interlocutors across the corpus
The positions the experts answer
Strongest secular framings on record
  • Aron Ra / Shermer / Hitchens / Atkins: morality is grounded in our evolution as a social species — cooperation, empathy, reciprocity; free-riders are removed by population mechanics.
  • Dillahunty / Harris: morality is about the well-being of conscious creatures; secular systems are superior because they self-correct, where religious morality is “moral fiat.”
  • Tom Jump / Arif Ahmed / Wielenberg: objective morality can be a priori (consent model) or a brute primitive fact in the fabric of reality — no God required.

Objections handled — cross-expert master list

Objection 1

Morality is grounded in our evolution as a social species

Cooperation, empathy and reciprocity gave early humans a survival advantage; free-riders are removed by population mechanics over generations. No God required.

Aron Ra, Michael Shermer, Christopher Hitchens, Peter Atkins

Strongest response

Evolution under-determines morality

Darwin's ants give you altruism; Spencer's struggle gives you predation — you can derive any ethic you like, so evolution cannot fix moral content. And these are descriptive patterns, not binding obligations: “a lion kills a zebra, it doesn't murder it.”

Lennox under-determination + HabermasCraig lion/zebra disanalogy

Objection 2

Morality is about the well-being of conscious creatures

Science can study well-being as it studies health. Secular systems are superior because they self-correct — religious morality is unilateral “moral fiat.”

Matt Dillahunty, Sam Harris — The Moral Landscape

Strongest response

Conflates “good” with “well-being” by stipulation

Harris smuggles the ought into his axioms — “there's no reason to identify pleasure/misery with good and evil, especially on atheism.” The axioms move “is the same as saying you've got to take it by faith.”

Craig circularity diagnosisCliffe “who defines well-being?”

Objection 3

The free-rider problem is solved by population mechanics

Selfish cheats get ostracised, imprisoned or killed; over generations they are removed from the gene pool. Cooperation is therefore stable without God.

Aron Ra

Strongest response

Modern cheats are not ostracised

In wealthy societies the rational cheat thrives: “I can cheat the IRS very easily in Fairfield County Connecticut.” Population mechanics over many generations never answer the individual rational free-rider in a single lifetime.

Stuart Knechtle hedge-fund counter-example

Objection 4

Objective morality can be grounded a priori without God

A Kantian / consent model — “the best of all possible worlds is one where all interactions are consensual” — gives an objective ground that needs no third moral agent.

Tom Jump

Partial response

Presses universality, but the contingency challenge is not met

Stuart pushes the cross-cultural reach of the consent model and its implication for non-consenting persons (Alzheimer's, infants — Singer's denial of personhood). But Tom's deeper point — objective morality “can't be contingent” even on God — is left unresolved.

Stuart Knechtle partialContingency / Euthyphro unmet

Objection 5

Moral reasons are brute primitive facts in the fabric of reality

As well as particles and people, the world contains moral reasons — binding on us as a brute primitive fact. Atheistic moral realism (Platonism) needs no God to ground them.

Arif Ahmed, Erik Wielenberg

Engaged only against Wielenberg

“Voodoo metaphysics” — selection without agency

Craig turns Wielenberg's own divine-willing analogy against him: without agency, how do physical situations “correctly select the right moral states of affairs to instantiate”? But Ahmed's plainer “brute moral facts” version goes unanswered in the popular corpus.

Craig supervenience critiqueAhmed unanswered

Objection 6

“Subjectivity is objectivity”

Billions of subjective perspectives existing simultaneously is the one objective fact; objective moral content (“don't kill your own kind”) is observable across all cultures as a DNA-level survival adaptation.

The agnostic philosophy major (Perry)

Strongest response

Self-defeating — exposed by a single question

“If I tell you that a white middle-class woman is the truth, that's not true, right?” The slaves knew slavery was wrong even when society endorsed it — which undercuts society-as-arbiter (cross-ref Q-17.4.11).

Perry Socratic exposure

Objection 7

Christian morality has itself changed, so it's also a social construct

Slavery was commanded then condemned; treatment of women and homosexuality shifted; Christians can't even agree among themselves. Right and wrong are social constructs — exactly what evolutionary biology predicts.

Robert Stovold

Weakest engagement in the corpus

Treated as a category error, not answered

Lennox distinguishes logical warrant from behavioural conformity but does not answer the historical claim on its own terms; he lets the conquest-passage framing stand as a rhetorical drive-by. The irony that biblical ethics is itself culturally situated is never reckoned with.

Lennox category moveHistorical claim unmet

Objection 8

Atheists behave morally — you don't need God to be good

“Name one moral act a believer can do that an atheist cannot.” Human solidarity is innate; religion merely “catches up to morality for a change.”

Christopher Hitchens, Matt Dillahunty

Strongest response

The claim is logical warrant, not psychological capacity

“Sometimes my atheist friends can put me to shame.” Dostoevsky's “everything is permissible” is a claim about justification, not behaviour. The question is not whether the atheist can be good but whether his worldview can call anything objectively good.

Lennox warrant vs conformity

Objection 9

The “no foundation” move is a dodge from the real question

Whether the atheist has a meta-ethical foundation is irrelevant to whether a thing is immoral. Cliffe walked in agreeing slavery is evil — so the foundations gambit just distracts from the textual question.

Matt Dillahunty

Gap — largely unrebutted

Knechtle does not resolve the dodge charge

Dillahunty's “how is this binding on anybody who's not a Bible-believing Christian?” is repeated without a clean answer. The experts assert that grounding matters but do not show why the topical question can't proceed without settling meta-ethics.

Aggregation gap

Objection 10

Morality is rooted in collective human nature / chimpanzee proto-morality

We share basic needs as social beings; we see “echoes of morality's biological origins” in chimpanzees, which our conscious minds built on. Human nature, not God, is the ground.

Andrew Copson, the agnostic interlocutor

Strongest response

The Holocaust wasn't “really” wrong on this view

These are descriptive sociobiological patterns, not prescriptive obligations. The naturalist must pay the cost: an atrocity “wasn't really wrong, it was just against the herd morality” — and to deny it would be “guilty of speciesism.”

Craig herd-morality reductio

Persistent gaps

Gap 1

The strongest secular meta-ethics is rarely engaged

The experts overwhelmingly attack biological reductionism (Ruse, Dawkins, “the strong eat the weak”). Non-natural moral realism, constitutivism, neo-Aristotelian naturalism and Kantian constructivism are named and waved past. Only Craig (vs Wielenberg) engages atheistic moral realism on its own terms.

Why it matters

The apparent strength of 27 extractions masks a narrow target. Against a sophisticated non-theist moral realist the corpus has little to say beyond “voodoo metaphysics.”

Highest-priority gap-fill

Gap 2

The is–ought gap is asserted, not defended

Lennox and Craig invoke Hume as settled. The corpus never engages the body of work arguing the gap is bridgeable — or that theism faces the symmetrical problem of deriving a human ought from a divine is.

Why it matters

P2 of the whole argument rests on this. If the gap is not load-bearing, the secular naturalist's flourishing account survives.

Aggregation gap

Gap 3

The Euthyphro shadow is never answered

Naturalists repeatedly gesture at it (“God's morality is just his subjective opinion” — Aron Ra; “for morality to be objective it can't be contingent” — Tom Jump). Stuart says “not to go Euthyphro on you” but the modern divine-command reply (Adams, Evans) never appears.

Why it matters

This is the symmetrical mirror of the entire argument — if God's commands are arbitrary, theism grounds morality no better than “Matt from Austin.” (Belongs to Q-1.3.6.)

Cross-node gap

Gap 4

Evolutionary debunking cuts both ways

Craig uses Darwinian contingency to debunk secular moral knowledge but never addresses Sharon Street's point that the same debunking targets theistic moral realism via reliability worries.

Why it matters

The hostile-witness Darwin quote is double-edged; an informed opponent can turn it back on the theist's own moral epistemology.

Aggregation gap

Gap 5

The genealogical claim is asserted, not historically defended

“Western values are borrowed Christian capital” (Habermas, Holland, Craig, Stuart) is stated as fact. The historical counter-pushback — Hitchens on the Christian justification of slavery; Stovold on the Anglican split — is never met head-on.

Why it matters

The move is rhetorically powerful but evidentially thin as deployed; it invites a historian's rebuttal the corpus is not equipped to answer.

Aggregation gap

Gap 6

Biblical morality is itself culturally situated

Knechtle's “cultural conditioning is a horrible basis” move never reckons with the fact that biblical ethics is also embedded in a particular ancient culture — the very objection he presses against the relativist.

Why it matters

An unaddressed self-application; the consistency challenge the experts level at naturalists can be levelled back.
