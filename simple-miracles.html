Miracles and the laws of nature

Hasn't science disproved miracles?

For a lot of people this is the quiet deal-breaker. We know how nature works now, so surely the walking-on-water, rising-from-the-dead claims belong to a pre-scientific age. Hasn't science simply closed the door on miracles? It turns out the door was never science's to close, and seeing why clears the path to everything else.

First / What a miracle is
Step 1 of 4
What a miracle actually is

A miracle isn't science breaking down.

Most of the objection rests on a picture of what a miracle is, and it is the wrong picture. Fix that, and the supposed conflict with science largely dissolves.

Not magic
A miracle is not magic, and it is not nature malfunctioning. On the Christian view it is the personal God who designed the natural order choosing, on rare and significant occasions, to act within it. As Lennox puts it, the regularities of nature are God's own handiwork, and now and then their Author does something special to draw attention to himself.
The Author
This is why a miracle does not break the laws of nature, any more than a composer breaks the rules of music by adding a new note. God feeds a fresh event into the system, and the regular order simply carries on around it. The laws describe what nature does when it is left to itself. They were never a cage around the one who made them.
Why it counts
And here is the part people miss: the regularity is exactly what makes a miracle a miracle. The reason the resurrection means anything is that everybody knows dead people stay dead. If bodies rose every Tuesday, nobody would call it a sign. A miracle leans on the ordinary rule. It does not deny it.
The theory you'll hear
Believing in miracles is just a primitive, pre-scientific cop-out. But the people in these stories knew the regularities as well as we do. When Joseph learned Mary was pregnant, his first instinct was not to cry miracle but to quietly call off the wedding (Matthew 1:18-19), because he knew perfectly well where babies come from. You have to know nature's normal course to recognise an exception to it. Miracle is not ignorance of the laws. It depends on them.
✓
Confidence: a miracle is not nature breaking down but its Author acting within it, a new event fed into the regular order. Far from denying the laws of nature, the claim depends on knowing them, which is what makes the event a sign at all.
Second / Science can't settle it
Step 2 of 4
Can science settle it?

Science can't actually rule them out.

Science is extraordinarily good at describing how nature behaves on its own. That is a different thing from telling you whether anything beyond nature can ever act, and the difference is everything.

Describe, not forbid
The laws of nature are descriptions of what regularly happens, not commands that forbid anything else. They tell you, with great precision, what the universe does when nothing interferes. They cannot tell you that nothing ever could interfere, because that is not a measurement. It is a guess about what exists beyond the reach of the instruments.
The drawer
Lennox uses a homely picture. If you leave money in a drawer and later find some missing, you do not conclude that the laws of arithmetic have been broken. You conclude that someone has been into the drawer. The laws of arithmetic no more stop a person from acting than the laws of nature stop their Author from acting. They simply describe what happens when no one does.
No instrument
So when someone says science has disproved miracles, ask which experiment did it. There isn't one, because science has no instrument that detects whether there is a God who can act. It can map the usual course of nature beautifully. It is simply silent on whether anything stands behind nature, which is the question that actually matters here.
The theory you'll hear
But no one has ever observed the laws of nature being suspended. A miracle does not suspend the laws; it adds an event to the system they describe. If someone slips extra money into your drawer overnight, arithmetic is not suspended, a person has acted. The laws carry on describing what nature does when left alone. They have nothing to say about what God may do within it on a particular occasion.
✓
Confidence: the laws of nature describe what happens on its own; they do not forbid an outside cause from acting. No experiment has disproved miracles, because science has no tool that could detect or exclude a God beyond nature. That question is outside its range.
Third / Hume and the slogan
Step 3 of 4
Hume and the famous slogan

The slogans don't hold up.

Two pieces of intellectual furniture get wheeled out here: David Hume's classic argument, and the line that extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence. Both are weaker than their reputation.

Circular
Hume argued that uniform experience establishes the laws, and so always outweighs any report of a miracle. But this quietly assumes the very thing in question. To know that experience is uniformly against miracles, you would have to have surveyed every event in all of history, which no one has. As Lennox says, Hume simply assumes what he set out to prove.
Self-defeating
Worse, Hume elsewhere denies that nature is uniform at all, his famous worry that the sun rising every day so far is no guarantee it rises tomorrow. You cannot lean on the uniformity of nature to bar miracles and also deny that nature is uniform. Tellingly, Antony Flew, long regarded as Hume's leading interpreter and once the world's most famous atheist, came to admit the case did not work.
The slogan
As for extraordinary claims requiring extraordinary evidence, it sounds wise and is mostly slippery. In practice the bar gets set so high that nothing could ever clear it. The fair question is not whether an event is unusual, of course a miracle is, but whether the evidence we actually have would be very hard to explain unless it had really happened.
The theory you'll hear
Extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence. A neat slogan, but a blunt instrument. Taken strictly it would force us to reject many rare but perfectly real events, and in debate it usually just means the bar sits wherever the sceptic needs it to sit. Probability does not work that way. An unusual event can be well established by ordinary evidence, as long as that evidence would be hard to account for any other way.
✓
Confidence: Hume's argument is circular and even self-defeating, and the demand for extraordinary evidence is usually a bar set too high to clear on principle. Neither shows that miracles cannot happen. They only show how to dismiss one without looking.
Fourth / The real question
Step 4 of 4
The real sticking point

It was always the God question.

Strip it all back, and the objection to miracles is almost never the finding it claims to be. It is a decision made before the evidence is ever examined.

An assumption
When someone says science rules out miracles, they usually mean that nature is all there is, and so nothing outside nature can act. But that is not something science discovered. It is a philosophy, naturalism, brought to the table in advance. As Lennox pressed Lawrence Krauss, the real issue is an a priori rejection of the supernatural, not any particular piece of evidence.
If God is there
And once you see that, the whole thing turns on one prior question. If there is no God, then of course there are no miracles, and no evidence could ever change it. But if God exists, a miracle is simply God doing something, and there is nothing irrational about it at all. The miracle question was never really about science. It was about God.
Go look
Which means the honest move is not to rule miracles out in advance, but to go and look. If God might be there, then the central Christian miracle, the resurrection of Jesus, is exactly the kind of claim worth examining on its evidence rather than dismissing on principle. That case has a card of its own.
The theory you'll hear
I'd believe in a miracle if science could verify one in the lab. But science is built to study the repeatable regularities of nature, and a one-off act of God is not the sort of thing its instruments are designed to catch. Asking for a miracle on demand in a laboratory misunderstands what is being claimed. The real obstacle is rarely the evidence. It is a prior ruling that the supernatural is not allowed, and that is an assumption, not a result.
✓
Confidence: science has not disproved miracles; a philosophy that rules out the supernatural in advance has. The question was always whether God exists, not whether a laboratory can summon a miracle. If he might, the reasonable move is to weigh the evidence for the resurrection, not refuse to look.

So has science disproved miracles? No, and it was never equipped to. Science describes the regular order of nature; a miracle is the God who made that order acting within it, which is exactly why everyone recognises it as exceptional. The old arguments turn out to be circular, and the famous slogan is mostly a bar set wherever it needs to be. What is really doing the work is a quiet assumption that nothing beyond nature exists, slipped in before any evidence is read. Drop the assumption, and the question becomes honest again: not has science closed the door, but is God there, and did he, on one particular morning, walk out of a tomb.
