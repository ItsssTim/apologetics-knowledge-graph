What is God doing on a cross?

John Lennox’s signature theodicy — not a solution to suffering but a window: the claim that God did not stay distant from human pain but entered it. A single-expert deep-dive across fourteen recorded deployments.

The question

If God is good and all-powerful, why is there so much suffering — and does Christianity say anything atheism cannot?

On Lennox’s own repeated admission this is “the hardest question I face as a Christian, without any hesitation.” It is the problem of evil — but Q-2.6.3 isolates one specific answer: the claim that the God of Christianity has not stood at a distance from human suffering but has personally entered it on the cross.

The node is unusual in the corpus for being almost entirely one voice. Lennox returns to the move across at least fourteen recorded events. The structure is remarkably stable — which lets us read the recurrence itself as evidence of a worked, road-tested argument rather than an ad-hoc reply. Two features mark it out: he refuses the word “solution” (he offers “a window”), and he reframes the question as a mathematician would.

The cumulative argument — Lennox’s recurring structure

Not a deductive syllogism like the Kalam, but a cumulative case that pivots from defence to evidence. The same five movements recur across the transcripts, almost always in this order.

Move 1

Concede the difficulty — the intellectual problem has no neat solution.

Could / would / should a good God have done otherwise? After centuries of debate, “none of us have ever come to a satisfactory conclusion.” Disclaiming a tidy answer earns credibility and separates the intellectual register from the pastoral one (the oncologist vs the young mother).

Move 2

Turn the problem on atheism — it solves nothing.

Atheism removes God but leaves the pain, removes ultimate hope and final justice, and (on Dawkins’s own admission — “DNA just is and we dance to its music”) abolishes the very category of “evil” the objection needs. “You can’t have it both ways.”

Move 3

Change the question — the mathematician’s move.

When a question yields nothing for centuries, change it. Not “could God have prevented this?” but “is there evidence anywhere of a God we can trust with it?” “Just as hard,” he says — but it admits of an answer. This is the pivot the whole case turns on.

Move 4

The cross is the evidence — God entered the suffering.

If the man on the cross is God incarnate, then “at the very least God has not remained distant from human suffering but has himself become part of it.” The atonement adds a layer no rival worldview offers: Christ “took upon himself the mess I’ve made” so forgiveness is possible.

Move 5

The resurrection seals it — tragedy becomes hope, and justice is guaranteed.

Without it the cross is mere tragedy — “if the cross were the end, we’d never have heard of Jesus.” With it: death is not the end; a final judgment ensures “fairness will be done and seen to be done”; God is “a God of compensation.” The case “hangs around something I believe happened in space-time and history.”

Two further premises recur often enough to count as standing parts of the case: a compressed free-will defence (a robotic universe has no love; love requires the possibility of its refusal), used to answer “couldn’t God have made a world without evil?”; and an empirical / pastoral confirmation — the transformed sufferer, the Christchurch widow, the Gulag prisoner — “when you see two and two make four thousands of times, you begin to believe that two and two make four.”

How Lennox deploys it — register, illustrations, named moves

One expert, so the analysis maps the internal architecture of Lennox’s treatment — the registers he moves between, the illustrations he reaches for, the named tactical moves — rather than contrasting voices.

The framing “beauty and bombs”
The mathematician’s pivot — signature move
What he does
  • Controlling image of the world-as-it-is: “beauty and bombs” (variant: “beauty and barbed wire”). No worldview faces a one-sided picture; all must account for the mixed one.
  • Stages the classical dilemma (Epicurus, Hume) and concedes — autobiographically — that “none of us have ever come to a satisfactory conclusion.”
  • The mathematician’s move: when a question resists for centuries, change it. The reframed question is “just as hard” but answerable. Unmistakably his.
The counter-attack internal critique
Atheism solves nothing
Three sub-moves
  • It doesn’t remove the suffering — “it could make it worse because it removes all hope.” Only the questioner is removed from the category of “problem.”
  • It removes hope and justice — on atheism “the vast majority of people who have ever lived will never get justice.”
  • It abolishes the category of evil — Dawkins’s “DNA just is” turned against his own outrage. When Dawkins said “it’s bleak but that doesn’t make it false,” Lennox: “it doesn’t mean it’s true either.”
The free-will defence by analogy
Answering “couldn’t God have made a world without evil?”
The pictures, not the modal logic
  • “Of course he could — but you wouldn’t have lived in it, nor would I,” because such a world would be robotic and empty of love.
  • The robotic wife — the “button marked kiss” that proves love requires the possibility of refusal.
  • The parental risk — holding his first child: “this little one could grow up to reject me. That’s a risk you take.”
  • Covers moral evil only. For natural evil he notes the tectonic plates that cause earthquakes are “necessary for life,” and cites Luke 13 (Tower of Siloam) against the karmic reading.
The cruciform pivot the canonical anchor
“What is God doing on a cross?”
The centre of the case
  • “The man on the cross was God incarnate” is “a colossal claim” — and if true, “it raises a very deep question: what’s God doing on a cross?”
  • The answer is deliberately minimal and always hedged: “at the very least it shows that God has not remained distant from human suffering but has himself become part of it.”
  • Repurposes Nietzsche’s mocking phrase “God on a cross” into the heart of the argument.
  • Atonement layer — not “simply solidarity in suffering” but “a taking upon himself of the mess I’ve made.” “No other religion or philosophy offers me what Christ offers me.”
The resurrection tragedy into hope
What turns the cross from defeat into evidence
Three functions
  • The cross alone would be empty: “if the cross were the end, we’d never have heard of Jesus.”
  • Validates the identity claim — “evidence that he was who he claimed to be.”
  • Guarantees a final judgment, framed as good news: “the bombers of 9/11 are going to face it.”
  • Grounds a God of compensation: “when we see what God has done with those who suffered innocently… we will be quiet and have no more questions.”
The pastoral confirmation the illustrations
Christchurch · the Gulag · Lazarus
The recurring set-pieces
  • The Christchurch widow — three days after the 2011 earthquake, a note pressed into his hand: “what you said about the cross has given me the first glimmer of hope.” His signature pastoral illustration.
  • The Gulag prisoner — “God comes in and he acts in the situation, not before it.”
  • Lazarus (John 11) — Martha’s intellectual response, Mary’s emotional one; Jesus reasons with one and weeps with the other. Doubles as the observed-vs-experienced distinction.
  • “Two and two make four” — transformation repeated across millions is cumulative evidence.
Personal stake — refusing cheap grace

Lennox pairs his own near-death heart episode with the death, the same year, of his 22-year-old niece from a brain tumour: “I was spared; she wasn’t. I need to be very careful how I describe my own reaction, because I need to be able to say that to her as well.”

Objections handled — master list

Single expert; “source” denotes the interlocutor or tradition raising the objection. The response is Lennox’s unless flagged as a gap.

Objection 1

The Epicurus / Hume trilemma

You cannot have a good God, an all-powerful God, and evil, all at once — so God is not good, or not powerful, or not there.

Epicurus, David Hume — classical problem of evil

Strongest response

Concede the dilemma, then change the question

The deductive route yields no satisfactory answer after centuries — “have you ever got through that argument satisfactorily? No.” So substitute the evidential / trust question: is there evidence of a God we can trust with it?

Lennox mathematician’s criterion shift

Objection 2

Couldn’t a good God have made a world without evil?

An omnipotent God could surely have created a universe in which these horrors simply do not happen.

Michael Tooley; standard

Strongest response

Yes — but it would contain no humans, only robots

A love-permitting world requires genuine choice, which can also refuse. The robotic-wife (“button marked kiss”) and parental-risk analogies: love that cannot be withheld is not love. “You wouldn’t have lived in it, nor would I.”

Lennox compressed free-will defence

Objection 3

Atheism’s own problem-of-evil objection

How can a good God allow such suffering? The atheist presses the contradiction as decisive against theism.

Richard Dawkins; the atheist objector

Strongest response

On atheism, “evil” is a non-existent category

“DNA just is and we dance to its music” — if there is no good, no evil, no justice, the objection presupposes what the worldview denies. “You can’t have it both ways, ladies and gentlemen.”

Lennox internal critique

Objection 4

“It’s bleak, but that doesn’t make it false”

The atheist concedes the picture is grim but insists grimness is no argument against its truth.

Richard Dawkins, verbatim to Lennox

Strongest response

“It doesn’t mean it’s true either”

Bleakness is not a truth-maker in either direction. “It could be bleak and false” — so the question must be pressed deeper, on evidence, not mood. Atheism, meanwhile, destroys ultimate justice.

Lennox symmetry reply

Objection 5

It’s just wish-fulfilment / a psychological cope

If religion is a comfort-mechanism, a suffering-God who consoles is exactly what we would invent — so the consolation is evidence of projection, not truth.

Marx, Freud; Eric Tse

Strongest response

Comfort cuts both ways; argue the rational case in parallel

“Something being comforting doesn’t mean it’s false — and atheism is found by many to be comforting; that doesn’t make it true either.” Lennox concedes pastoral testimony cannot stand alone, which is why the rational side must be argued elsewhere.

Lennox partial concession

Objection 6

Christianity isn’t testable — it’s mere subjective experience

“You’re a scientist — aren’t you embarrassed talking about Christianity? Christianity is not testable.”

Sceptical scientist (in Q&A)

Strongest response

It is testable — in the transformative-evidence sense

The narcotic-addict-to-radiance case, repeated across millions of lives, is real-world evidence: “when you see two and two make four thousands of times, you begin to believe that two and two make four.”

Lennox cumulative-experiential evidence

Objection 7

Survivor attribution is grotesque

If God spared you, did he not like the man who took your seat on the doomed plane? What kind of God prefers one family’s grief to another’s?

Michael Shermer (debate)

Full concession, then pivot

“He’s absolutely dead right”

Lennox concedes the point entirely — “this nonsense of saying ‘wonderful, I was spared.’” He refuses the providential boast and pivots to the cross, where the God-claim does not depend on who survives. “I’ve got to have something I can talk to my sister about.”

Lennox steelman + pivot

Objection 8

Karma — “you suffered because you sinned”

Suffering is deserved; the sufferer must have done something to bring it on.

Folk view (met in New Zealand)

Strongest response

Jesus explicitly denies it

In Luke 13 (Pilate’s slaughtered Galileans, the Tower of Siloam) Jesus rejects the inference from suffering to guilt outright. The cross severs suffering from desert.

Lennox Luke 13

Objection 9

Don’t insult me with a personal God

“There might be some intelligence out there, but don’t talk to me about a personal God — just look at what I’m going through.”

Lennox’s Oxford colleagues

Strongest response

Atheism removes the framing, not the pain

Conceding “there is no God” removes the problem at the intellectual level — but the suffering remains, now without hope or justice. The emotional pull is real; the relief it offers is illusory.

Lennox pastoral framing

Objection 10

Christians have themselves caused massive suffering

The name of Christ has been attached to violence and atrocity — how can his followers lecture on evil?

General; Northern Ireland

Strongest response

“I’m ashamed of it, utterly”

Lennox grants the charge and turns it against the offenders: Christ forbade violence to advance his kingdom (Peter and Malchus). “Wherever people take weapons to defend Christianity, they cut the ears off people… we’ve a big job to put the ears back on.”

Lennox concede + redirect

Objection 11

No amputee ever grows a limb; prayer studies show nothing

God heals only things that might have remitted anyway; the Templeton study of 1,800 heart patients found no benefit (slightly worse, if anything); salamanders regrow limbs but praying Christian amputees never do.

Michael Shermer (Templeton study, Galton)

Gap — not engaged

Lennox pivots away from the evidential challenge

He concedes the survivor-attribution point but never engages the amputee specificity or the prayer-study data, moving instead from the intellectual to the existential frame. Given how central these are to Shermer’s case, the omission is notable.

Aggregation gap

Objection 12

The evidential (Rowe-style) “amount of evil” argument

Even granting free will, the sheer quantity and apparent gratuitousness of suffering — the fawn dying slowly in the forest fire — is positive evidence against a loving, powerful God.

William Rowe, Michael Tooley (evidential PoE)

Gap — sidestepped

The whole strategy is to change the question

Lennox never takes the probabilistic argument on its own terms; he reframes to the trust question rather than weighing whether the amount of evil disconfirms theism.

Aggregation gap

Objection 13

Natural evil — the free-will defence doesn’t reach it

Earthquakes, childhood cancer, animal suffering across deep time long before humans existed — none of this is explained by the free choices of moral agents.

Structural gap in the corpus

Gap — named by anecdote, never theorised

Tectonic plates and Christchurch, not an argument

The free-will defence covers moral evil only. The cruciform/resurrection move implicitly extends to all suffering, but the asymmetry between moral and natural evil is named via anecdote (the life-giving tectonic plates) and never separately argued.
